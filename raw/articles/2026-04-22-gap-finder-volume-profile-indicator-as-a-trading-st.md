## 1. Context: What “Volume Profile as a Strategy” Really Requires

Before identifying gaps, it helps to be explicit about what a “volume profile indicator as a trading strategy” entails in a modern trading environment. Volume profile, in its simplest form, is a horizontal histogram that shows how much volume traded at each individual price level over a specified range.[1][3][6][10][13][16] Unlike traditional vertical volume bars that show total volume per bar or per unit of time, volume profile displays volume by price, revealing where the market actually did business, which prices attracted trading interest, and which prices were quickly rejected.[1][3][10][15] This structural view of where volume clustered is what allows traders to interpret the market in terms of acceptance and rejection, rather than simply price movement over time.

Central to almost every volume profile strategy are a set of canonical constructs: the **Point of Control (POC)**, the **Value Area** (commonly defined as the price range containing about 68–70% of the traded volume), the **Value Area High (VAH)** and **Value Area Low (VAL)**, and the pattern of **high‑volume nodes** (HVNs) and **low‑volume nodes** (LVNs).[1][3][6][9][10][13][16] The POC is the price with the largest horizontal bar in the profile and is often described as the market’s “fair value” or “bullseye,” around which price frequently rotates.[1][3][6][10] The value area represents a broader band of price acceptance and tends to behave like an extended magnet or balance region: markets often move more slowly inside value and more explosively through low‑volume “gaps” between value areas.[1][3][9][10][15][16]

In practice, volume profile trading strategies rarely exist in isolation. Modern practitioners blend profile constructs with **order flow analytics**, especially in the futures and crypto perpetual futures markets where full depth‑of‑market and tick data are accessible.[2][8][10][12][16][17] Tools like cumulative volume delta (CVD), volume footprint or cluster charts, and volumetric bars allow traders to see not only where volume transacted, but also whether buyers or sellers were more aggressive at those prices.[2][8][10][12][17][18] Platforms that specialize in this style of trading—Sierra Chart, NinjaTrader’s Order Flow suite, Bookmap, Quantower, and Exocharts—therefore play an outsized role in how volume profile strategies are implemented.[2][7][8][9][13][14][16][17]

### 3.1 Point of Control, Value Area High, and Value Area Low

The **Point of Control (POC)**, **Value Area High (VAH)**, and **Value Area Low (VAL)** merit their own pages separate from your general “value area” entry. In volume profile usage, POC is the price level with the tallest bar in the profile, representing the single price at which the most volume was traded over the chosen range.[1][3][6][10][13][16] Videos and documentation repeatedly describe POC as the market’s “fair value,” a price that often behaves like a magnet, attracting price back toward it when the market is not strongly trending.[1][3][6][10] Strategies built around POC typically involve mean‑reversion trades back to POC from the value area extremes, or breakout trades when price fails to revert and instead accepts a new POC in a different region.

VAH and VAL define the boundaries of the value area, typically the contiguous range of prices that contains about 70% of the total volume (or 68% in some implementations, approximating one standard deviation of a normal distribution).[1][3][9][10][13][15][16] The value area itself is already covered in your wiki, but VAH and VAL behave like dynamic support and resistance: inside the value area, price tends to rotate and consolidate, whereas excursions beyond VAH or below VAL are interpreted as potential breakouts or “imbalances” where the market is exploring new territory.[3][9][10][11][15] A dedicated entry on VAH and VAL could explain how traders interpret tests, rejections, and acceptances of these levels, and how they form the backbone of value area rotation, value area breakout, and “outside value” trend continuation strategies.

You might, for instance, describe how some traders define a **value area rotation trade** as entering long near VAL with a target near POC or VAH when the market is in balance, using the assumption that price is likely to remain within value in the absence of strong directional catalysts.[3][9][10][15][16] Conversely, when price auctions outside VAH or VAL with strong volume and order flow support, traders may look for continuation moves, treating the prior value area as a completed auction and the new region as an emerging area of acceptance.

### 3.2 High Volume Nodes and Low Volume Nodes

The concepts of **High Volume Nodes (HVNs)** and **Low Volume Nodes (LVNs)** are fundamental to how volume profile reveals “market acceptance” versus “rejection,” yet they are not explicitly covered in your wiki. In a volume profile, high volume nodes are price levels or zones where the profile bulges outward, indicating that a large amount of trading occurred there, while low volume nodes are the narrow “valleys” indicating price levels that the market passed through quickly with relatively little trading.[9][10][15][16]

Educational material on volume profile emphasizes that HVNs represent prices the market is willing to accept, which often act like magnets or balance zones, while LVNs represent prices that were rejected, where the market moved quickly through without building much volume.[9][10][15][16] For example, NinjaTrader describes how the widest portion of a profile shows where the market accepted price, and how the narrow sections show where price was rejected, with the overall shape revealing balance or imbalance.[10] Exocharts similarly explains that volume profiles show which prices the market is willing to accept and which it rejects, and that understanding this helps traders anticipate behavior when price revisits those ranges.[9]

An HVN/LVN page would allow you to articulate several key strategies: trading rotations between adjacent HVNs, expecting price to oscillate between accepted areas; using LVNs or volume “gaps” as fast‑move zones where price may move quickly if it reenters; and identifying “single prints” or thin areas in the profile that can act as binary zones of rejection or rapid acceptance.[9][10][15][16] This is particularly powerful when combined with order flow tools, where traders look for absorption or exhaustion at HVNs and for aggressive buying or selling as price attempts to traverse LVNs.

### 3.3 Volume Profile Shapes: D, P, b, and Double Distribution (B)

Another missing concept is the classification of **volume profile shapes**—often referred to as **D‑profile, P‑profile, b‑profile, and B‑profile (double distribution)**—which describe the overall distribution of volume over a session.[10] NinjaTrader, for example, teaches that these four shapes reveal whether a market is in balance, showing exhaustion, or likely to continue a trend.[10] A D‑shaped profile resembles a typical normal distribution, indicating a balanced or range‑bound day, while a P‑shaped profile reflects a short‑covering or short‑squeeze type day with volume skewed to the upper side, and a b‑shaped profile reflects long‑liquidation type behavior with volume skewed to the lower side.[10] A B‑shaped or double‑distribution profile consists of two distinct volume bulges separated by a low‑volume area, often indicating a session with two phases of auctioning at different price levels.[10]

These shapes are not merely descriptive; they are used prescriptively in strategy. For instance, a D‑profile suggests mean‑reversion and value area rotation strategies will be more effective, while P‑ and b‑profiles suggest directional continuation or fade strategies depending on context.[10] A double‑distribution B‑profile may suggest that the market has shifted its perception of value mid‑session, with the low‑volume area acting as a critical inflection point between the two distributions.[10] Documenting these shapes and their strategic implications would allow traders reading your wiki to understand not only where volume is, but what kind of day type the profile is expressing.

### 3.5 Cumulative Volume Delta and Volume Footprint / Cluster Charts

Perhaps the most glaring conceptual gap is the absence of **Cumulative Volume Delta (CVD)** and **volume footprint or cluster charts**. Cluster or footprint charts show the detailed breakdown of trades at each price within a bar or candle, often including volume, delta, and number of trades, and can be displayed as numerical grids or colored histograms.[8][18] Quantower, for example, describes its cluster chart as an advanced tool that shows OHLC prices and trading volume on a single chart, with options to visualize data as clusters or histograms and to color by delta, volume, or trades.[8] TradingView likewise introduced volume footprint charts as a powerful tool that overlays intra‑bar volume distribution onto price, making order flow accessible within its Supercharts interface.[18] A dedicated footprint/cluster chart page would allow you to explain how such charts connect the macro context of volume profile (where volume is) with the microstructure of order flow (how volume traded at each price).

CVD and footprint charts are particularly important for volume profile strategies because they resolve one of volume profile’s limitations: profiles show where volume accumulated, but not whether buyers or sellers were more aggressive at those prices. By combining profiles with CVD and footprint charts, traders can distinguish between high volume caused by absorption (large passive limit orders) versus high volume caused by aggressive initiatives, leading to more nuanced support/resistance and breakout/failed breakout strategies.[8][12][17][18]

### 3.6 Composite vs Session Volume Profiles
