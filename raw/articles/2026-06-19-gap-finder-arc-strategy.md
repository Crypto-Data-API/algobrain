### 3.2 Box And Swing Structures, Including Gap Logic

ARC?s level framework is built on two classes of levels: boxes and swings. Boxes are constructed from session-specific highs and lows; swings come from prominent turning points beyond those sessions. A proper conceptual page on box and swing structures should distinguish between several cases.

In the simplest case, where the new day opens within the prior day?s regular-session range, the box high is set at the previous day?s high and the box low at the previous day?s low; these define the main trading arena.[1][8] The nearest swing high is then found by scanning back left on the chart from the box until the next highest price is encountered; this might be a multi-day high, a news spike, or another relevant pivot.[1] Likewise, the nearest swing low is identified by scanning back for the next lowest price point beyond the current box low.[1][8] These swings act as extended resistance and support levels that become relevant when price breaks out of the box and travels into new territory.

On days where the asset gaps up above the prior day?s high, the ARC educator instructs traders to treat the prior box as obsolete and instead create a new box using the pre-market high and pre-market low.[1][8] This reflects the reality that overnight and pre-market trading can form a distinct liquidity zone, often with different participants and order flow characteristics than the prior cash session. The same logic applies to gap-down days: a new pre-market box better captures where early institutional flow has transacted, and box high and box low are thus redefined for the day.[1][8] A conceptual page should examine this gap-adjusted box construction in depth, discuss edge cases such as extremely thin pre-market volume, and contrast the ARC approach with alternative gap frameworks like ORB (Opening Range Breakout).

Swing levels merit their own detailed treatment. In ARC, the next swing high above the box high is not just any local maximum; it is framed as the ?very next highest price above the box high, no matter where or what time that price has occurred.?[1] This implies that traders should prioritize the closest major high as a likely area of supply and profit-taking, rather than overlaying dozens of historical highs and cluttering the chart. The same applies to swing lows, which are treated as the nearest major lows below the box low that might attract demand.[1][8] Comparing this approach to traditional support?resistance methods, Fibonacci retracements, or volume profile points of control will deepen readers? understanding of why ARC uses this minimalist level scheme.

### 3.4 Candlestick Microstructure: John Wick, Harami, Inside Bars, And Outside Bars

The ARC educator?s affection for ?John Wick? candles is grounded in a broader body of candlestick analysis that your wiki does not yet fully articulate.[8][11][14][16] In social media explanations, a typical John Wick scenario involves a series of strong green candles showing aggressive buying pressure, followed by a small red candle with a large upper wick.[11] If price in subsequent candles trades above the high of that wick, it signals that buyers remain in control; if it trades below the low of that wick candle, it suggests sellers have stepped back in and that the wick zone may act as resistance.[11][14] In ARC terms, when such a wick appears at or just above a box high or swing high after a sufficient upward range move, it can be interpreted as a rejection of higher prices?a footprint of institutional selling that signals a potential short entry.[8][11][14] Conversely, an inverted version at box or swing lows may signal exhaustion of selling and the emergence of strong demand.

Harami and inside bar patterns, while structurally different, also play a role in the broader ecosystem of candle-based strategies. A harami involves a long-bodied candle followed by a shorter real-bodied candle whose body is completely contained within the first, regardless of whether the second candle?s shadows extend beyond the first candle?s shadows.[12] An inside bar, by contrast, focuses on the total range: the second bar?s high must be lower than the first bar?s high and its low higher than the first bar?s low.[12] Both patterns signal volatility contraction and often precede breakouts or reversals. In an ARC context, an inside bar at a box high after a strong up move could be interpreted as a pause where the market is deciding whether to break through resistance or revert back into the box. A breakout of the inside bar?s low might align with a short entry, especially if confirmed by a wick rejection and volume delta imbalance.[5][8][12]

Outside bars or external candles form the basis of the Larry Williams strategy that CodeTrading backtested, where the current candle?s high is greater than the previous high, its low is lower than the previous low, and its close breaks out beyond the prior bar?s high or low.[13] These patterns represent a decisive expansion of range and directional commitment, often marking the start of a strong move. In ARC, a trader might look for outside bars coming off box lows (for longs) or box highs (for shorts) as confirmation that the level has held and that a new directional leg is underway. A conceptual page on outside bars should therefore reference both their stand-alone statistical properties as shown in the CodeTrading backtest and their potential integration into level-based frameworks like ARC.[13]
