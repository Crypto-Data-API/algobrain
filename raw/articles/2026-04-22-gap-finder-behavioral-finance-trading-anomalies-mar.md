### 1. Key Entities
- **Daniel Kahneman**: Nobel-winning psychologist who co-developed Prospect Theory; foundational for understanding loss aversion and irrational decision-making in trading[1][5].
- **Amos Tversky**: Collaborator with Kahneman on Prospect Theory; his work explains why traders overweight recent losses over gains, impacting risk management[1][5].
- **Richard Thaler**: Behavioral economist awarded Nobel for integrating psychology into finance; key for nudges against biases like overconfidence in portfolio strategies[5].
- **Hersh Shefrin**: Co-author of disposition effect research; his models quantify how traders irrationally hold losers, guiding sell rules[1][2].

### 2. Important Concepts/Strategies
- **Overconfidence bias**: Traders overestimate their prediction accuracy and control, leading to excessive trading volume and higher costs; matters for sizing positions and using stop-losses[1][2][4][6].
- **Herding (or herd behavior)**: Investors mimic crowd actions, amplifying bubbles/crashes via FOMO or panic; traders exploit via contrarian entries at sentiment extremes[1][2][5][6].
- **Momentum effect**: Stocks with recent strong performance continue outperforming short-term due to underreaction and trend-chasing; core anomaly for systematic trading strategies[2][3].
- **Anchoring bias**: Reliance on irrelevant reference prices (e.g., purchase price) distorts valuations; traders counter by focusing on current fundamentals[6].
- **Confirmation bias**: Seeking info that affirms beliefs while ignoring contradictions; causes holding biased views, fixed by diverse data checks[6].
- **Representativeness bias**: Judging assets by stereotypes or recent patterns, leading to poor extrapolation; relevant for avoiding false momentum signals[1].
- **Availability bias**: Overweighting vivid recent events for forecasts; explains recency-driven trends, countered by long-term data review[3][4].
- **Prospect theory**: Framework where gains/losses are evaluated relative to reference points with diminishing sensitivity; underpins asymmetric risk-taking in volatile markets[1][5].
- **Market bubbles and crashes**: Sentiment-driven price extremes from overconfidence/herding; traders profit from reversion strategies post-peak[3][4].
- **Value vs. growth outperformance**: Value stocks beat growth due to overextrapolation biases; anomaly for factor rotation in portfolios[3].

### 3. Data Sources/Tools
- **AAII Sentiment Survey**: Weekly poll of individual investor bullish/bearish views; contrarian signal when extremes predict reversals.
- **VIX Index (CBOE Volatility Index)**: "Fear gauge" measuring S&P 500 options-implied volatility; spikes signal panic for mean-reversion trades.
- **CNN Fear & Greed Index**: Composite of 7 sentiment indicators; extreme readings flag overbought/oversold for timing entries.
- **Bookmap**: Order flow visualization tool; reveals hidden sentiment via heatmaps, helping spot herding before price moves.

### 4. Recent Developments (2024-2026)
- **2024 AI-driven sentiment tools**: Platforms like Sentieo/AlphaSense integrated NLP for real-time bias detection in news/social; boosted momentum crash predictions during NVDA rally[1].
- **2025 Retail herding in crypto**: Post-ETF approvals, disposition effect amplified GameStop-like squeezes; studies linked overconfidence to 30% higher crash risk[2].
- **2026 Behavioral volatility models**: Post-Fed pivot, hybrid EMH-behavioral frameworks (e.g., ACR Journal updates) quantified herding's role in S&P flash crashes[1].
