Here are the **highest-value gaps** I would add for **ITAA 1997 Division 35 non-commercial losses for share traders**, prioritizing items that materially affect whether a share-trading activity is treated as a business, whether losses are deferred, and how disputes are resolved.

## 1) Key entities to add

| Name | One-line description | Why it matters for traders |
|---|---|---|
| **ATO** | Australia’s tax administrator and the issuer of rulings, private advice, and compliance guidance on Division 35 and share-trading classification. | It is the primary source traders rely on for whether losses are deferred and whether activity is a business.[1][2][3] |
| **Federal Court of Australia** | The court that decides tax appeals and has shaped the “business vs investor” line in share-trading cases. | Court decisions are the most authoritative source for when share activity is a business and when losses are capital or deferred.[1] |
| **Administrative Appeals Tribunal (AAT)** | The tribunal that has historically heard many taxpayer challenges to ATO decisions on trading classification and discretion. | AAT reasoning is often cited in private rulings and helps show how factual indicators are applied.[1][2] |
| **TR 97/11** | ATO ruling on indicators of carrying on a business. | This is the core reference for the “share trader vs investor” analysis, including repetition, turnover, records, and a business plan.[1] |
| **TR 2003/3** | ATO ruling on the non-commercial losses rules. | It explains how Division 35 operates, including separate business activities and deferral mechanics.[3] |
| **PCG 2021/2** | ATO practical compliance guideline for exercising discretion under s 35-55. | Traders use it to assess the likelihood of ATO challenge when seeking discretion for early-stage or exceptional loss-making activity.[5] |
| **Income Tax Assessment Act 1997 s 35-10** | Core operative provision that defers non-commercial losses unless an exception, test, or discretion applies. | This is the legal trigger for deferral; it is the main rule traders need to model.[3] |
| **Income Tax Assessment Act 1997 s 35-55** | The Commissioner’s discretion provision. | Important where a share-trading business fails the objective tests, especially around special circumstances or lead-time style arguments.[2][5] |
| **Income Tax Assessment Act 1997 s 35-30 / 35-35 / 35-40 / 35-45** | The four objective tests: assessable income, profits, real property, and other assets. | These are the main gateways for avoiding deferral if a trader’s activity is a business but loss-making.[3][8] |
| **Income Tax Assessment Act 1997 s 35-10(4)** | The exception provision for activities that are specifically excluded from Division 35. | Important because many traders confuse business classification with automatic exposure to Division 35; exceptions can remove the deferral rule.[7] |

## 2) Important concepts / strategies to document

| Name | One-line description | Relevance to trading |
|---|---|---|
| **Business vs investor classification** | The threshold issue is whether the taxpayer is carrying on a business of share trading or merely investing. | If the taxpayer is not in business, Division 35 usually does not apply; losses are then generally capital or on revenue account depending on facts.[1][3] |
| **Trading stock treatment of shares** | Shares held by a share trader can be trading stock rather than capital assets. | This affects income timing, loss treatment, and inventory-style accounting; it is central to trader classification.[1] |
| **Separate business activity analysis** | Division 35 applies to each separate business activity, not just the taxpayer overall. | Relevant where a person runs multiple strategies, entities, or side businesses and wants to isolate share-trading losses.[3][4] |
| **Assessable income test** | One of the four objective tests, focused on whether the activity has generated sufficient assessable income. | Traders often miss that the test is activity-specific and may be affected by partnership mechanics.[3][4] |
| **Profits test** | Another Division 35 test asking whether the activity has made a profit in at least three of five years. | Important for mature trading operations with uneven returns.[3][8] |
| **Real property test** | Tests whether real property used in the activity meets the threshold value. | Usually less common for share traders, but still relevant where premises or land are used in the business structure.[3][8] |
| **Other assets test** | Tests whether certain business assets used in the activity meet the threshold value. | Can matter for substantial trading setups, though many share traders will not satisfy it.[3][8] |
| **Commissioner’s discretion — special circumstances** | Discretion may apply where unusual external events caused the failure to meet the tests. | Relevant for events like illness, natural disasters, or market disruption, but not ordinary trading losses.[2][5] |
| **Commissioner’s discretion — lead-time / objective expectation** | Discretion may apply where the business is expected to be profitable later because of the nature of the activity. | Important for structured strategies that have an initial loss period, though share trading often struggles to fit this rationale.[5] |
| **Capital loss vs revenue loss distinction** | Losses from non-trader share holdings are generally capital losses, not ordinary deductions. | Traders need this distinction because it changes whether losses can offset salary/other income or only capital gains.[1] |

## 4) Recent developments to document, especially 2024–2026

| Name | One-line description | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **PCG 2021/2 as the current compliance lens** | The ATO’s compliance approach to the s 35-55 discretion remains the practical framework many advisers use. | Even if not law, it materially shapes what support a share trader needs when seeking discretion.[5] |
| **Continued ATO reliance on TR 97/11 indicators** | Recent ATO private advice continues to apply the same business-indicator framework to share traders. | Shows the classification test remains highly factual and record-driven rather than formulaic.[1][2] |
| **Persisting ATO refusal where losses are due to ordinary trading outcomes** | ATO private advice continues to deny discretion where the failure to profit is because of the business model or trading outcomes, not external special circumstances. | This is critical for traders who assume market losses alone justify Division 35 relief.[2] |
| **Ongoing split between business trading and passive investing** | Recent ATO materials continue to distinguish genuine share traders from investors. | This remains the central threshold issue for whether Division 35 even enters the analysis.[1][3] |
| **Post-pandemic and volatility-era fact patterns** | Recent private rulings and commentary reflect more cases involving volatile markets, remote work, and retail trading activity. | These facts affect how the indicators in TR 97/11 are evidenced, especially turnover and operating plan.[1][2] |
| **Increasing reliance on digital records and brokerage exports** | Contemporary ATO review practice places heavy weight on objective records rather than self-description. | Traders need clean transaction data to substantiate business status and any Division 35 positions.[1][3] |
