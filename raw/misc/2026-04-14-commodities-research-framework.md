# Commodities Investing & Trading Research Framework

## Executive Summary

A rigorous commodities research process combines fundamental supply/demand analysis, technical price study, macro/geopolitical context, market positioning data, and robust risk management. This document outlines the core components a researcher or trader should build into their workflow, along with the most reliable data sources for each category. Commodities span five major asset classes — energy, agricultural, metals (precious and base), livestock, and soft commodities — each with distinct price drivers that require tailored analytical approaches.[^1][^2]

***

## 1. Market Overview & Classification

Before diving into analysis, clearly define the commodity universe you're covering. Each asset class behaves differently:

| Asset Class | Examples | Key Price Drivers |
|-------------|----------|-------------------|
| Energy | Crude oil (WTI/Brent), Natural gas, Coal | OPEC policy, inventory draws, geopolitics, seasonal demand |
| Precious Metals | Gold, Silver, Platinum | USD strength, real interest rates, safe-haven demand |
| Base/Industrial Metals | Copper, Nickel, Aluminium | Chinese industrial demand, mining supply, EV transition |
| Agricultural (Grains) | Corn, Wheat, Soybeans | Weather, WASDE reports, export demand, planting intentions |
| Soft Commodities | Coffee, Sugar, Cotton, Cocoa | Weather in growing regions, EM currency moves |
| Livestock | Live Cattle, Lean Hogs | Feed costs, disease outbreaks, seasonal slaughter cycles |

## 2. Fundamental Analysis

Fundamental analysis in commodity trading is the process of determining a commodity's value by examining the variables that influence supply and demand. It answers whether a commodity is currently overvalued or undervalued relative to its physical market reality.[^1]

### 2.1 Supply-Side Factors

- **Production levels**: Mine output (metals), drilling/rig counts (energy), planted/harvested acreage (agriculture)[^3]
- **Inventory and stockpiles**: EIA weekly petroleum stocks, LME warehouse levels, USDA ending stocks[^4]
- **Capital expenditure cycles**: Long lead-time projects (e.g., new mines take 5–10 years); capex cuts create future supply gaps[^5]
- **Ramp-up periods and choke points**: Identifying production bottlenecks and critical supply nodes[^5]
- **Trade flows**: Export bans, tariff changes, import quotas, shipping disruptions[^3]

### 2.2 Demand-Side Factors

- **Industrial consumption**: Chinese PMI data, global manufacturing activity, construction starts for base metals[^3]
- **Consumer demand trends**: Fuel consumption data (vehicles, aviation), food consumption per capita
- **Technology-driven demand shifts**: EV adoption (lithium, copper, nickel), renewable energy build-out (silver, rare earths)[^3]
- **Macroeconomic indicators**: GDP growth of major commodity-consuming countries, infrastructure spending cycles, population trends[^6]
- **Currency effects**: Commodities are mostly USD-priced; a weaker USD historically supports commodity prices

### 2.3 Supply-Demand Balance Modeling

The core output of fundamental analysis is a supply-demand balance:

\[ \text{Balance} = \text{Total Supply} - \text{Total Demand} \]

A persistent surplus pushes prices lower; a deficit creates upward price pressure and inventory draw-downs. Research platforms track this via normalized excess supply models, which show a strong inverse correlation to spot and futures prices.[^7][^3]

### 2.4 WASDE & Crop Reports (Agriculture)

For agricultural commodities, the USDA's monthly World Agricultural Supply and Demand Estimates (WASDE) report is the single most market-moving data release — equivalent to earnings reports in equities. Key metrics include:[^8][^9]
- Planted and harvested acreage
- Yield estimates (bushels per acre)
- Ending stocks (critically watched for tightness)
- Export demand and production forecasts by country

## 3. Technical Analysis

While fundamental analysis explains *why* prices move, technical analysis focuses on *when and how* to act on those moves. Commodity markets are especially suited to technical analysis because prices can move sharply on sudden supply shocks (weather, geopolitics, OPEC decisions).[^10]

### 3.1 Key Technical Indicators by Asset Class

| Indicator | Best For | Notes |
|-----------|----------|-------|
| Moving Averages (50/200-day) | All commodities | Trend identification; 200 DMA is key support/resistance |
| RSI (Relative Strength Index) | Overbought/oversold conditions | Useful for mean-reverting commodities |
| MACD | Trend confirmation | Works well across energy and metals |
| Bollinger Bands | Volatility assessment | Identify breakouts from consolidation |
| Stochastic Oscillator | Short-term reversals | Agricultural and soft commodities |
| Fibonacci Retracement | Entry/exit levels | Widely used in gold and crude oil trading |
| Volume Indicators | Market strength confirmation | Confirm whether price moves are backed by participation |

[^11][^12]

### 3.2 Chart Patterns & Price Structure

- Support and resistance levels — where price has repeatedly bounced or reversed
- Seasonal price patterns — e.g., natural gas demand peaks in winter; grain prices typically fall post-harvest[^13][^14]
- Futures curve structure (contango vs. backwardation) — backwardation signals tight near-term supply; contango implies oversupply or high storage costs[^15]

### 3.3 Seasonality

Seasonal fluctuations in commodity prices are driven by systematic intra-year changes in supply and demand. Energy commodities like heating oil and natural gas display demand peaks in winter; agricultural commodities such as corn and soybeans show periodic supply changes during planting and harvest. Research should always overlay a seasonal price tendency chart as a baseline for entry/exit timing.[^14]

## 4. Market Positioning Analysis (COT Reports)

The Commitments of Traders (COT) report, published weekly by the CFTC each Friday (reflecting the prior Tuesday's positions), is one of the most powerful free tools in commodity research.[^16][^17]

### 4.1 What the COT Report Shows

- **Commercial traders** (hedgers): Producers and consumers managing physical price risk. Their positioning often moves against price — fading rallies, buying dips.
- **Non-commercial traders** (speculators/managed money): Trend-following funds and hedge funds. Extremes in their positioning can signal crowded trades and potential reversals.
- **Non-reportable** (small speculators): Minor market participants[^18][^15]

### 4.2 How to Use COT Data

- **Extreme long positioning** by speculators relative to historical norms = potential top/reversal risk
- **Extreme short positioning** by speculators = potential bottom/squeeze risk
- Combine with price, curve structure, inventory, and seasonality for a holistic signal[^15]
- COT positioning data for futures goes back to 1986, with disaggregated data from 2006[^19]

## 5. Macroeconomic & Monetary Context

Commodities do not trade in isolation — macro forces have significant top-down influence.[^20]

- **USD Index (DXY)**: Most commodities are USD-denominated; a rising dollar historically pressures commodity prices
- **Real interest rates**: Rising real rates (especially in the US) compress gold and silver; falling real rates support them
- **Global PMI data**: Particularly China's Caixin PMI for base metals (copper, aluminium, nickel)
- **Inflation data (CPI/PPI)**: Commodities often lead inflation; CPI breakevens signal real-asset demand
- **Central bank policy**: Fed rate cycle impacts commodity financing costs and USD strength
- **GDP growth forecasts**: Commodity-consuming countries' growth drives long-run demand[^6][^3]

***

## 6. Geopolitical & Policy Risk

Geopolitical events create persistent pricing shifts in commodity markets that go beyond temporary volatility. Modern commodity pricing increasingly embeds geopolitical uncertainty as a permanent cost component rather than a transitory anomaly.[^21]

### 6.1 Key Geopolitical Factors to Monitor

- **OPEC+ production decisions**: The single largest discretionary supply variable for crude oil
- **Trade sanctions and export controls**: Russian energy sanctions (post-2022), grain export bans (wheat)
- **Conflict zones and critical supply routes**: Strait of Hormuz (oil), Red Sea shipping, Black Sea grain corridor
- **Government policy and regulation**: Carbon taxes, mining royalties, fertiliser subsidies, strategic reserves releases
- **Geopolitical Risk Index (GPR)**: Research shows GPR negatively affects returns of crude oil, gold, platinum, and silver, though commodities demonstrate resilience against severe shocks[^22][^23]

### 6.2 Safe-Haven Dynamics

Gold in particular functions as a hedge against geopolitical uncertainty and USD debasement. Gold and silver show persistent sensitivity to GPR across market regimes, displaying what researchers classify as defensive/safe-haven characteristics.[^24]

***

## 7. Risk Management Framework

No commodity research is complete without an explicit risk management framework.[^25]

- **Value-at-Risk (VaR)**: Quantify potential loss at a given confidence level
- **Position sizing**: Adjust for the higher volatility of commodities vs equities (leverage use in futures markets requires careful sizing)
- **Basis risk**: Difference between spot and futures price — particularly relevant in physical delivery markets
- **Roll yield**: The return (positive or negative) from rolling expiring futures contracts; critical in contango markets
- **Correlation analysis**: Commodities offer portfolio diversification; track cross-asset correlations during stress periods
- **Scenario analysis**: Model price outcomes under different supply/demand assumptions and geopolitical scenarios[^7]

## 9. Putting It Together: A Research Workflow

A structured commodity research workflow should follow this sequence:

1. **Define the commodity and market context** — classify asset class, identify key exchanges and benchmarks
2. **Build a supply-demand balance model** — use USDA, EIA, or USGS data; model balance = supply minus demand
3. **Review official reports and calendars** — WASDE (USDA), Short-Term Energy Outlook (EIA), OPEC MOMR, IEA OMR
4. **Analyse the futures curve structure** — contango vs. backwardation signals storage and near-term balance
5. **Check COT positioning** — identify whether speculators are at extremes and what commercials are doing
6. **Overlay technical analysis** — identify trend, key support/resistance, seasonality
7. **Assess macro and geopolitical context** — DXY, real rates, PMI, active conflicts/sanctions
8. **Quantify risk** — set VaR, basis risk exposure, position sizing, roll yield assumptions
9. **Stress-test with scenario analysis** — bull/base/bear across different supply/demand outcomes[^25][^7]

***

## 10. AI & Digital Tools in Modern Commodity Research

Machine learning has become an increasingly important edge in commodity research. Leading applications include:[^25]

- **ML-based supply/demand forecasting**: Models trained on weather data, satellite imagery (crop monitoring), and shipping data
- **Sentiment and news NLP**: Scanning OPEC statements, geopolitical headlines, and regulatory filings for early signals
- **Satellite imagery**: Counting oil tankers in transit, measuring crop greenness (NDVI), monitoring mine tailings
- **Alternative data**: Credit card data for fuel consumption, vessel AIS tracking for commodity flows, shipping freight rates (Baltic Dry Index for bulk commodities)
- **AI-assisted research interfaces**: LLM-powered tools that synthesise market reports and analyst commentary[^25]

Given your background in AI and automation, integrating APIs from USDA, EIA, and CFTC into a unified commodity intelligence pipeline — similar in spirit to blockchain data aggregation — is a natural extension. The Commodity Fundamentals API offers all three government sources through a single authenticated endpoint, which could serve as the foundation for an automated commodity data feed.[^19]
