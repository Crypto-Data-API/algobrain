# Ed Seykota

Ed Seykota is a pioneer of computerized [[trend-following]] and one of the most successful traders in history. Between 1972 and 1988, he turned a client account of approximately $5,000 into over $15 million -- a return of roughly 250,000% over 16 years, accounting for withdrawals. This track record, achieved through systematic [[trend-following]] systems running on some of the earliest commercially available computers, makes Seykota one of the progenitors of modern [[algorithmic-trading|algorithmic trading]] and a foundational figure in the [[trend-following]] tradition.

## Early Life and Background

Born in 1946, Seykota showed an early aptitude for engineering and systems thinking. He studied electrical engineering at MIT, where he developed the analytical framework that would later underpin his trading systems. His introduction to trading came through reading [[richard-donchian|Richard Donchian]]'s work on trend-following methods in the late 1960s, which convinced him that markets exhibited persistent trends that could be captured systematically.

After MIT, Seykota joined a major brokerage firm in the early 1970s. There, he developed one of the first commercial computerized trading systems, programming his models on an IBM mainframe -- a radical approach at a time when virtually all trading was done through fundamental analysis or discretionary chart reading. His early systems used exponential [[moving-averages]] and [[channel-breakout|channel breakouts]] to identify and ride trends across [[futures]] markets.

The brokerage firm's management, however, interfered with his system by overriding signals and cherry-picking trades, which diminished the system's returns. Frustrated, Seykota left to trade independently, managing a small number of client accounts from his home. This move allowed him to follow his systems without interference -- and the results were extraordinary.

## Career and Key Trades

### The Legendary Track Record (1972-1988)

Seykota's most famous track record is a model account that grew from approximately $5,000 in 1972 to over $15,000,000 by 1988. This represents one of the highest compound annual growth rates ever documented over a multi-decade period. The account was managed with a purely systematic approach -- mechanical rules dictated entries, exits, and [[position-sizing]], with minimal discretionary override.

The system traded a diversified basket of [[futures]] markets: agricultural commodities (grains, livestock, cotton), energy ([[futures|crude oil]], heating oil), metals (gold, silver, copper), [[forex|currencies]], and [[interest-rates|interest rate futures]]. Seykota's key insight was that [[trend-following]] works best when applied across many uncorrelated markets, allowing the system to capture trends wherever they appear while [[diversification|diversifying]] away the risk of any single market.

### System Design Philosophy

Seykota's systems were deceptively simple in their core logic:

- **Entry signals**: Long-term [[moving-average-crossover|moving average crossovers]] (e.g., when a shorter-period exponential moving average crosses above a longer-period one) and [[donchian-channel-breakout|Donchian channel breakouts]] (buying new highs, selling new lows)
- **Exit signals**: Reverse crossovers, trailing stops based on [[atr|Average True Range]], or channel breakdowns
- **[[position-sizing]]**: Each position was sized based on the current [[volatility]] of the market and the overall portfolio risk budget
- **[[diversification]]**: Trading 20+ [[futures]] markets simultaneously to ensure exposure to trends in any asset class

What made Seykota exceptional was not the complexity of his signals but the rigor of his execution and his deep understanding of the statistical properties of [[trend-following]] systems. He understood that most individual trades would be small losers, that a small minority of trades would be big winners, and that the key to profitability was letting winners run while cutting losers ruthlessly.

### Beyond System Trading: The Trading Tribe

In later years, Seykota shifted his focus from pure system development to the psychological dimensions of trading. He founded the "Trading Tribe," a community-based process for traders to explore and work through the emotional patterns that sabotage their performance. This work reflects his conviction that [[trading-psychology]] is not merely one element of trading success -- it is the foundational element, without which even the best system will fail in the hands of a human trader.

## Trading Philosophy

Seykota's philosophical contributions to trading, captured primarily in his *Market Wizards* interview (Source: [[book-market-wizards]]), are among the most quoted in trading literature:

1. **"The trend is your friend until the end when it bends."** This encapsulates the core of [[trend-following]]: ride trends as long as they persist, but have clear exit rules for when they reverse. Seykota's entire system is built on this principle -- never fight the [[trend]], never predict reversals, simply follow price.

2. **"Everybody gets what they want out of the market."** This provocative statement reflects Seykota's belief that traders' unconscious motivations -- the desire to be right, the need for excitement, the fear of success -- often override their stated desire to make money. It is a direct application of [[trading-psychology]] and [[behavioral-finance]] principles.

3. **"Win or lose, everybody gets what they want out of the market. Some people seem to like to lose, so they win by losing money."** Seykota argues that until traders understand their own psychological relationship to risk, reward, and loss, no system can save them. This connects to [[loss-aversion]], [[overtrading]], and the broader field of [[trading-psychology]].

4. **"The elements of good trading are: (1) cutting losses, (2) cutting losses, and (3) cutting losses."** The deliberate repetition underscores that [[risk-management]] -- specifically, the willingness to take small losses before they become large ones -- is the single most important skill in trading. This is the foundational principle behind [[stop-loss]] discipline.

5. **"Risk no more than you can afford to lose, and also risk enough so that a win is meaningful."** This captures the [[position-sizing]] dilemma perfectly: too small and winners don't matter; too large and losers destroy you. The optimal zone requires careful calibration of risk per trade relative to total capital.

6. **"Systems trading is ultimately discretionary. The trader still has to decide how much risk to accept, which markets to play, and how aggressively to increase and decrease the trading base as equity changes."** Even for a pioneer of systematic trading, Seykota recognized that human judgment is never fully removed from the process. The meta-decisions -- what system to run, how to size it, when to modify it -- remain discretionary.

7. **"There are old traders and there are bold traders, but there are very few old, bold traders."** This connects to the survival principle central to [[risk-management]]: longevity requires humility about the possibility of being wrong.

## Legacy

Seykota's influence on modern trading is difficult to overstate. He demonstrated, decades before the quantitative revolution, that systematic rules could be applied profitably to financial markets. His track record remains one of the most compelling pieces of evidence for the [[trend-following]] approach. And his philosophical insights about [[trading-psychology]] -- the idea that a trader's relationship with their own emotions is the ultimate determinant of success -- anticipated much of what modern [[behavioral-finance]] has since confirmed.

Jack Schwager, author of *Market Wizards*, has called Seykota's interview "one of the most insightful I've ever done" and noted that Seykota's combination of mathematical rigor and psychological depth is unique among traders. Seykota's work directly influenced [[richard-dennis]] and the [[turtle-trading]] experiment, as well as generations of [[trend-following]] CTAs (Source: [[book-market-wizards]]).
