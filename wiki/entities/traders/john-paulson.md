# John Paulson

John Paulson is an American hedge fund manager who executed what is widely considered the single greatest trade in financial history: a $15 billion profit from betting against the U.S. [[subprime-mortgage]] market during the [[2008-global-financial-crisis]]. His firm, Paulson & Co., grew from $600 million in assets under management to $36 billion on the back of this trade. Paulson's story is a masterclass in [[risk-management]], asymmetric payoffs, and the courage required to hold a deeply contrarian position when the entire market disagrees with you.

## Early Life and Background

Born in 1955 in Queens, New York, Paulson grew up in a middle-class family. He graduated first in his class from New York University's business school and later earned an MBA from Harvard Business School, where he was a Baker Scholar (top 5% of the class). After Harvard, he worked at Boston Consulting Group and then the investment bank Bear Stearns, before moving to the merger [[arbitrage-overview|arbitrage]] desk at Gruss Partners.

Paulson founded Paulson & Co. in 1994 with $2 million and a strategy focused on [[merger-arbitrage|merger arbitrage]] -- betting on the completion of announced mergers and acquisitions. For the first decade, the fund was modestly successful but unremarkable, managing around $600 million by 2005. Nothing in Paulson's track record suggested what was about to come.

## Career and Key Trades

### The Greatest Trade Ever (2005-2008)

The trade that made Paulson a legend began in 2005 when he and his analyst Paolo Pellegrini started investigating the U.S. housing market. Pellegrini conducted research that revealed a critical insight: national housing prices had never declined year-over-year in the available data set -- but the data set only went back to 1945. When they extended the analysis further back and adjusted for inflation, they found that housing prices were 2 standard deviations above the historical trend -- a bubble of unprecedented proportions (Source: [[book-more-money-than-god]]).

The mechanics of the trade were elegantly simple:

1. **The thesis**: [[subprime-mortgage|Subprime mortgages]] with adjustable "teaser" rates would default en masse when the introductory rates reset to much higher levels. This would happen regardless of whether housing prices rose, fell, or stayed flat -- the borrowers simply could not afford the higher payments.

2. **The instrument**: Paulson bought [[credit-default-swaps|credit default swaps]] (CDS) on [[subprime-mortgage]]-backed securities (MBS). These CDS were essentially insurance policies that would pay out if the underlying mortgage bonds defaulted.

3. **The asymmetry**: The annual premium on CDS was only 1-2% of the notional value. If subprime collapsed, the payoff could be 10x to 100x the premium paid. This was a massively asymmetric bet: limited downside (the annual premium) versus potentially unlimited upside.

4. **The [[risk-management]]**: Even if Paulson was wrong and housing continued to appreciate, his losses were capped at the annual CDS premiums. The worst case was manageable; the best case was historic.

Paulson's Credit Opportunities fund returned **590% in 2007**, and his firm made approximately **$15 billion** for its investors in 2007-2008 combined. Paulson personally earned an estimated $3.7 billion in 2007 alone -- the largest single-year payday in hedge fund history at the time. Paulson & Co.'s AUM surged from $600 million to $36 billion (Source: [[book-the-quants]]).

### The Role of Paolo Pellegrini

The trade's intellectual architect was Paolo Pellegrini, a former classmate of Paulson's who had joined the fund after a string of career setbacks. Pellegrini's statistical analysis of housing price data was the foundation of the thesis. He identified that [[credit-rating-agencies]] were using models that assumed housing prices could not decline nationally -- an assumption based on an insufficiently long historical data set. This single insight -- that the models were built on flawed assumptions -- was worth billions (Source: [[book-more-money-than-god]]).

### After the Greatest Trade (2009-Present)

Paulson's post-crisis track record has been decidedly mixed, illustrating one of the most important lessons in trading: **a single great trade does not guarantee future success**.

- **Gold (2009-2013)**: Paulson made a massive bet on [[gold]], arguing that [[quantitative-easing]] by the [[federal-reserve]] would cause inflation. Gold rose to $1,900/oz in 2011, generating significant profits. But Paulson held too long, and as gold collapsed to $1,200/oz by 2013, his gold fund lost approximately 63% in 2013 alone.
- **Sino-Forest (2011)**: Paulson was the largest shareholder of Sino-Forest, a Chinese forestry company later exposed as a fraud. The position lost approximately $720 million.
- **Greek banks (2014-2015)**: Paulson bet on a recovery in Greek banking stocks. Greece's economy continued to deteriorate, and the positions generated significant losses.
- **Pharmaceutical mergers (2015-2016)**: Bet on completion of pharma mergers that were blocked by regulators, leading to further losses.

By 2020, Paulson & Co. had converted from a hedge fund to a family office, managing only Paulson's personal fortune, after AUM had declined from its peak of $36 billion to under $10 billion.

## Trading and Investment Philosophy

Paulson's career offers several critical lessons:

1. **Asymmetric risk/reward is everything.** The greatest trades are those where you risk a little to make a lot. Paulson's CDS position had limited downside (annual premiums) and potentially enormous upside. Traders should constantly search for these [[risk-management|asymmetric setups]] where the payoff structure is heavily skewed in their favor.

2. **Do the primary research.** Paulson and Pellegrini did not rely on Wall Street consensus or [[credit-rating-agencies|rating agency]] models. They went back to the raw data, extended the historical analysis, and discovered that the emperor had no clothes. Original research -- especially when it challenges consensus -- can be worth billions.

3. **Consensus can be catastrophically wrong.** In 2005-2006, the near-universal view was that U.S. housing prices could not decline nationally. Banks, regulators, and rating agencies all shared this belief. Paulson had the intellectual independence to challenge it and the conviction to bet against it. This connects directly to [[behavioral-finance]] -- the dangers of herding and anchoring.

4. **Position sizing requires conviction.** Once Paulson confirmed his thesis, he sized the position with enormous conviction, ultimately risking hundreds of millions in annual CDS premiums. Half-hearted position sizing on a correct thesis generates mediocre returns. This echoes [[george-soros]]'s principle: "Go for the jugular."

5. **One great trade is not a strategy.** Paulson's post-crisis record demonstrates that a single brilliant macro call does not translate into a repeatable edge. [[macro-trading]] is inherently episodic -- the biggest opportunities arise during periods of extreme dislocation, which are by definition rare. Traders who conflate a single win with systematic skill often give back their gains.

6. **Survival bias is real.** For every Paulson who held CDS through 2007 and was vindicated, there were others who put on similar trades too early and were forced to liquidate before the thesis played out. Timing and [[risk-management]] matter as much as being right.

## Strategy Connections

- **[[risk-management]]** — The CDS trade exemplifies the principle of asymmetric risk: small, defined losses for potentially massive gains
- **[[macro-trading]]** — Paulson's approach was fundamentally top-down, identifying systemic vulnerabilities in the housing market
- **[[short-selling]]** — While technically not a short sale, the CDS trade was economically equivalent to shorting [[subprime-mortgage]]-backed securities
- **[[derivatives]]** — The trade was executed entirely through [[credit-default-swaps|credit default swaps]], a form of OTC derivative
- **[[behavioral-finance]]** — The thesis depended on recognizing that consensus was wrong and that institutions were anchored to flawed models
- **[[merger-arbitrage]]** — Paulson's original strategy before the housing trade, and the foundation of Paulson & Co.
