Daniel Kahneman (1934-2024) was an Israeli-American psychologist who received the Nobel Prize in Economic Sciences in 2002 for his work integrating psychological research into economic science, particularly regarding decision-making under uncertainty. His collaboration with Amos Tversky produced [[prospect-theory]], the foundational model of [[behavioral-finance]], and his book *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011) became the definitive popular introduction to cognitive biases and decision-making. Kahneman died on March 27, 2024, at age 90.

## Death and Final Decision (2024–2025)

Kahneman died on March 27, 2024. In March 2025, Wall Street Journal columnist Jason Zweig — a personal friend and former collaborator of Kahneman's — revealed that Kahneman had died by assisted suicide at a clinic in Switzerland. In a farewell email sent to close friends en route to Europe, Kahneman wrote: "I have believed since I was a teenager that the miseries and indignities of the last years of life are superfluous, and I am acting on that belief... my kidneys are on their last legs, the frequency of mental lapses is increasing, and I am ninety years old." He spent his final days in Paris visiting museums and the ballet before traveling to Switzerland. Zweig noted that the decision was characteristic: the world's leading thinker on decision-making applied his own framework — weighing diminishing quality of life against the [[sunk-cost-fallacy|sunk-cost]] instinct to persist — to his last choice. The revelation prompted wide commentary in 2025 on end-of-life decision theory.

## Key Contributions

### Prospect Theory (1979)

Kahneman and Tversky's [[prospect-theory]] replaced the classical expected-utility model with a descriptive framework that matches how humans actually make decisions under risk. The three core findings:

- **[[loss-aversion]]**: Losses feel approximately twice as painful as equivalent gains feel good (the ~2:1 loss/gain asymmetry)
- **Reference dependence**: People evaluate outcomes as gains or losses relative to a reference point (e.g., purchase price), not in terms of absolute wealth
- **Probability weighting**: People overweight rare events and underweight common ones, explaining the simultaneous demand for lottery tickets and insurance

### System 1 and System 2 Thinking

In *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Source: [[book-thinking-fast-and-slow]]), Kahneman popularized the dual-process model of cognition:

- **System 1** — fast, automatic, intuitive, emotional. Operates without conscious effort. Prone to systematic biases
- **System 2** — slow, deliberate, analytical, effortful. Capable of logical reasoning but lazy and easily depleted

For traders, this framework explains why emotional, impulsive trading decisions (System 1) override careful analysis (System 2), particularly under stress, time pressure, or after losses. Most trading biases — [[anchoring-bias|anchoring]], [[overconfidence-bias|overconfidence]], [[confirmation-bias]], the [[disposition-effect]] — are System 1 phenomena.

### Heuristics and Biases Program

Kahneman and Tversky's research program (1970s-1990s) identified the major cognitive heuristics and biases that affect judgment:

- **[[anchoring-bias|Anchoring]]** — over-reliance on initial information when estimating
- **Availability heuristic** — judging probability by how easily examples come to mind
- **Representativeness** — judging probability by similarity to a prototype rather than base rates
- **[[overconfidence-bias|Overconfidence]]** — systematic overestimation of one's knowledge and predictive ability
- **[[framing-effects]]** — decisions change depending on how options are presented

## Impact on Trading and Finance

Kahneman's work is the intellectual foundation of [[behavioral-finance]]. Every major behavioral bias documented in trading — the [[disposition-effect]], [[overconfidence-bias]], [[herding]], [[anchoring-bias]], [[confirmation-bias]] — traces back to the heuristics-and-biases program or to [[prospect-theory]].

Practical applications include:
- Understanding why traders hold losers and sell winners ([[disposition-effect]])
- Designing rule-based systems to counteract System 1 biases
- Recognizing that experience does not eliminate biases — it often reinforces them through [[overconfidence-bias|overconfidence]]
- Structuring portfolio review processes that account for [[framing-effects]]
