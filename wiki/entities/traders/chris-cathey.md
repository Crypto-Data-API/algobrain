# Chris Cathey

Dr. Christopher Cathey is a former goldman-sachs trader and the longest-serving senior mentor at the Institute of Trading and Portfolio Management. He traded European equities at Goldman Sachs from 1993 to 2006, spanning the 1990s bull market, the dot-com bust, and the subsequent recovery — making money in all environments. His career provides a masterclass in [[risk-management]], emotional detachment, and institutional-grade long-short-equity trading.

## Career

### Goldman Sachs (1993–2006)

Cathey joined goldman-sachs in New York in 1993 and moved to the European equities trading desk in London shortly after. Over 13 years at Goldman he held roles across multiple desks and geographies:

- **London** (1993–1998): Pan-European equities trading, combining [[proprietary-trading|proprietary risk-taking]] with customer market-making and agency business
- **New York**: Traded US and international markets
- **Frankfurt** (~1.5 years): Ran German equity risk
- **London** (1999–2006): Returned for the final stretch of his Goldman career

The trading floor had 24–25 traders on the desk and 200–250 people on the floor, all with full visibility of every trader's positions and P&L — a high-pressure "goldfish bowl" environment that forced rapid development of emotional discipline (Source: [[itpm-trading-legends-chris-cathey]]).

### Hedge Fund (2006–2009)

Left Goldman in 2006 to join a hedge fund, where he stayed for three years before departing just as the [[2008-global-financial-crisis]] began.

### Private Equity and ITPM (2009–present)

Now invests in private-equity — specifically established, cash-generative SMEs needing growth capital. Sectors include automotive supply chain, industrial manufacturing, and construction. Applies the same trading disciplines to private business investing.

Simultaneously serves as ITPM's longest-running mentor, having completed 75–80+ mentoring programs. He estimates approximately two years for a mentee to become an "accomplished, all-around trader" (Source: [[itpm-trading-legends-chris-cathey]]).

## The Goldman Sachs Crisis Trade

Cathey's most detailed case study involves a day during the NASDAQ bear market (~2001). The desk was heavily short (~$500 million) in telecoms and technology stocks when a vicious bear market rally squeezed their positions. By lunchtime they were down significantly. Three possible responses:

1. **Panic and cover** — locking in massive losses
2. **Freeze** — "rabbit in headlights," equally destructive
3. **Hedge and wait** — what they actually did

They bought euro-stoxx futures to reduce net short exposure while maintaining their core thesis that the rally was temporary. This kept them functional as market makers. They printed the exact high tick of the day on their futures purchase. When the market turned in the afternoon, they re-increased short positions. They ended the day slightly positive and made a "very big amount of money" over the following two days (Source: [[itpm-trading-legends-chris-cathey]]).

**Key principle:** Never increase losing positions. Only add to positions that are working. Cut losers at predetermined [[stop-loss]] levels without hesitation, then re-enter with confirmation.

## Trading Philosophy

1. **Patience above all.** Wait for opportunities to present themselves. Do not force "boredom trades" when nothing is happening — they consistently lose money (Source: [[itpm-trading-legends-chris-cathey]]).

2. **Generate your own ideas.** Do not rely on broker research or others' views. Develop your own methodology and thought process. Be humble.

3. **Never set financial return targets.** Hard targets force bad trades. Use soft profit targets and be realistic about risk.

4. **Every trade needs a predetermined stop loss.** If triggered, execute without hesitation. This discipline is "absolutely key."

5. **Add to winners, cut losers.** This is the same process — managing risk-reward in your favor.

6. **Technical analysis is for timing only.** Use [[technical-analysis-overview|technical analysis]] to time entries into fundamentally-driven positions — never to generate trade ideas.

## Idea Generation Process

Cathey's process maps directly to the ITPM top-down methodology:

1. Start with **US leading economic indicators** — the US has the highest quality data and broadest equity market
2. The equity market leads the economy by 1–6 months — position to be ahead of where it's heading
3. Identify **sector trends** — where growth is accelerating, where slowdown is emerging
4. Determine **long and short sectors**, then do bottom-up **stock selection** within those sectors
5. Maintain a watchlist of 20+ ideas for an 8–12 stock portfolio — the "subs bench" of potential trades
6. Refresh the watchlist monthly with new economic data
7. Target 1–3 month or 3–6 month holding periods — beyond that "slowly becomes investing"

This is the [[eighty-twenty-analysis|80/20 framework]] in practice: 80% fundamental analysis for idea generation, 20% [[technical-analysis-overview|technical analysis]] for entry timing.
