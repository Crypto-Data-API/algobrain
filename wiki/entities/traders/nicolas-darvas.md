Nicolas Darvas (1920–1977) was a Hungarian-born professional ballroom dancer and self-taught stock speculator who reportedly turned roughly $36,000 into over $2 million in about 18 months during 1957–1959 while touring the world dancing. His book *How I Made $2,000,000 in the Stock Market* (1960) introduced the **[[darvas-box|Darvas Box]]** method — a volume-confirmed breakout system that remains a foundational template for [[momentum]] and [[breakout-trading|breakout]] traders.

## Background

Darvas fled Hungary in 1943 and built an international career as one half of the dance team Darvas & Julia, at one point among the highest-paid acts in show business (Time reported his weekly income at $3,500 in 1959). He stumbled into stocks in 1952 when a Toronto nightclub paid him partly in shares of a mining company called Brilund, which tripled in two months. Years of losses and experimentation followed before he distilled his "techno-fundamentalist" approach.

Because he traded while touring (Hong Kong, Calcutta, Paris), Darvas worked only from week-old copies of *Barron's* and daily broker telegrams quoting high/low/close — making him an early proof-of-concept for **end-of-day, price-action-only trading** with no news flow and no tape watching. He credited this distance from the market as an advantage, not a handicap.

## The Darvas Box Method

Core rules from the 1960 book:

- **Stock selection**: focus on stocks making new 52-week highs in rising industries, with expanding volume ("techno-fundamentalist": earnings power selects the candidates, price/volume action times the trade)
- **The box**: when a stock repeatedly fails to exceed a high and fails to break a low, those bounds define a box (e.g., 35–40). Stacked rising boxes define an uptrend
- **Entry**: buy the [[breakout]] above the box top **on increased volume**, using buy-stop orders so execution is automatic
- **Exit**: a [[stop-loss]] placed just below the box top after entry (later trailed below each new box bottom). No profit targets — let winners run, a pure [[trend-following]] exit
- **Concentration**: he pyramided into a handful of leaders — notable wins included Lorillard, E.L. Bruce (which short-squeezed from ~50 to 100+ in 1958), Universal Controls, and Thiokol, which made the bulk of the $2 million

### How a box is built (mechanics)

The box is a self-defining consolidation range, identified after the fact from successive highs and lows rather than drawn by judgment:

1. A stock prints a new high, then pulls back. The high becomes the **provisional ceiling**.
2. If the stock fails to exceed that ceiling on the next attempt, the ceiling is confirmed; the subsequent low (that the stock holds above) becomes the **floor**.
3. Ceiling and floor together define the box. While price oscillates inside, Darvas does nothing.
4. A close/print **above the ceiling on rising [[volume]]** triggers the buy-stop. Volume confirmation is essential — a breakout on thin volume is treated as a likely false move.
5. After entry, the protective [[stop-loss]] sits just under the box top, and is trailed up under each new higher box as the stock climbs the "ladder." A break back into the prior box exits the trade.

This makes the method almost entirely **mechanical and pre-committed**: the orders are resting in the market, removing the moment-to-moment discretion that Darvas believed had cost him money in his discretionary years. It is an early, hand-run version of a systematic [[momentum]] / [[breakout-trading|breakout]] strategy with built-in [[position-sizing]] and risk control.

## The Time Article and the Investigation

- **May 25, 1959**: Time magazine profiled him in "Pas de Dough," reporting a fortune of more than $2,000,000 and making him a national sensation; the 1960 book sold around 400,000 copies in its first weeks.
- **1960**: New York Attorney General Louis Lefkowitz charged that the book's story was "unqualifiedly false," claiming his office could verify only about $216,000 of profits. Darvas denied the allegations and maintained brokerage records supported his account; no fraud was ever proven in court and the affair faded without conviction. The episode remains a standard cautionary tale about **unaudited track records** in trading publishing.

Darvas wrote several follow-ups (*Wall Street: The Other Las Vegas*, 1964; *The Anatomy of Success*, 1966; *You Can Still Make It in the Market*, 1977) and died in Paris in 1977.

## Trading Relevance

- The [[darvas-box|Darvas Box]] is a direct ancestor of William O'Neil's CAN SLIM (O'Neil cited Darvas) and of modern momentum/breakout systems; "boxes" are functionally consolidation ranges with volume-confirmed breakouts.
- His regime of **mechanical stops, position concentration in leaders, and indifference to news** anticipates systematic [[trend-following]] practice and parallels the price-action discipline of [[jesse-livermore]] a generation earlier.
- The strategy is long-bull-market dependent: Darvas's run coincided with the strong 1957–1959 tape, and he openly admitted earlier discretionary years were net losers — a clean illustration of distinguishing edge from [[market-regime|regime]] tailwind. A breakout system bleeds on whipsaws in a choppy or range-bound market.
- Darvas Box indicators ship in most modern platforms (TradingView, MetaStock, Amibroker), keeping the method in active retail use.

### What kills this style

- **Choppy/sideways regimes** generate repeated false breakouts; the trailing-stop logic produces a death-by-a-thousand-cuts string of small losses with no trend to pay for them.
- **Survivorship and unaudited-record bias**: the Lefkowitz inquiry (below) is the classic warning that a dramatic published track record may not survive audit — the same caution applies to any momentum guru's self-reported results.
- **Crowding / liquidity**: concentrating in a few breaking-out leaders works at retail size but suffers slippage and impact at scale; it is not a high-capacity approach.
