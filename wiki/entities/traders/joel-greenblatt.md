# Joel Greenblatt

Joel Greenblatt (born December 13, 1957) is an American hedge fund manager, author, and adjunct professor at Columbia Business School, best known as the founder of Gotham Capital and the inventor of the "[[magic-formula|Magic Formula]]" — a systematic [[value-investing]] screen popularized in *The Little Book That Beats the Market* (2005). His Gotham Capital partnership produced roughly 40% annualized returns over two decades, one of the best documented value-investing records of the modern era.

## Overview

Greenblatt earned his BS (1979) and MBA (1980) from the Wharton School. In 1985 he founded Gotham Capital with about $7 million in startup capital (a substantial portion reportedly raised through junk-bond financier Michael Milken). Gotham compounded at approximately 50% annualized (gross) over its first ten years (1985–1994), after which Greenblatt returned all outside capital and continued managing partner money; profile sources cite roughly 40% annualized after fees from 1985 to 2005. His strategy centered on concentrated [[special-situations]] investing: spinoffs, restructurings, merger securities, recapitalizations, and rights offerings.

In 2008 he co-founded **Gotham Asset Management** with longtime partner Robert Goldstein, where the two serve as co-chief investment officers. The firm runs long/short and "Index Plus" value-weighted strategies through the Gotham Funds mutual fund platform and remains active as of 2026, with assets in the billions.

## The Magic Formula

Introduced in *The Little Book That Beats the Market*, the [[magic-formula|Magic Formula]] ranks the investable universe on two factors and buys the combined top names. The genius is its simplicity: it operationalizes [[warren-buffett|Buffett's]] idea of "a good company at a bargain price" into two numbers.

$$\text{Earnings Yield} = \frac{\text{EBIT}}{\text{Enterprise Value}} \qquad \text{Return on Capital} = \frac{\text{EBIT}}{\text{Net Working Capital} + \text{Net Fixed Assets}}$$

| Factor | Formula | What it captures | Why this construction |
|--------|---------|------------------|----------------------|
| [[earnings-yield]] (cheapness) | EBIT / Enterprise Value | How much pre-tax operating profit you buy per dollar of total firm cost | EV (equity + net debt) and EBIT are both capital-structure-neutral, so leveraged and unleveraged firms compare fairly — better than P/E |
| [[return-on-capital]] (quality) | EBIT / (net working capital + net fixed assets) | How efficiently the business turns *tangible operating capital* into profit | Uses the capital actually required to run the business; ignores goodwill and excess cash — better than ROE |

**Mechanics:** rank every stock 1..N on earnings yield, separately rank 1..N on return on capital, sum the two ranks, and buy the ~20–30 stocks with the best combined (lowest) rank. Hold for one year (rebalancing tax-efficiently — sell losers just before the one-year mark and winners just after), then repeat.

**Worked example (illustrative round numbers).** Two candidates:

| | Company A | Company B |
|---|---|---|
| EBIT | $200M | $200M |
| Enterprise Value | $1,000M | $2,000M |
| Net working capital + net fixed assets | $500M | $1,600M |
| Earnings yield (EBIT/EV) | 20% | 10% |
| Return on capital (EBIT/capital) | 40% | 12.5% |

Company A is both cheaper (20% vs 10% yield) and higher-quality (40% vs 12.5% return on capital), so it ranks far better on both axes and the Magic Formula prefers it. The formula rewards firms that are simultaneously cheap *and* good — penalizing cheap-but-low-quality "value traps" and good-but-expensive growth names.

The book's backtest claimed ~30.8% annualized returns for 1988–2004 versus ~12.4% for the S&P 500. Greenblatt maintains the free screener at magicformulainvesting.com. The formula is effectively a two-factor quality-plus-value model and a frequent subject of [[backtesting]] replication studies — independent replications generally confirm outperformance but at lower magnitudes, with multi-year stretches of underperformance that Greenblatt argues are precisely why the edge persists (most investors cannot tolerate the tracking error).

**Pitfalls and caveats:** the formula excludes financials and utilities (where EBIT and the capital base are not comparable); it can surface companies with one-off non-recurring EBIT; it endures painful multi-year drawdowns versus the index; and its publication has likely compressed the edge through crowding. It is a disciplined *starting screen*, not a substitute for judgment.

## Books

- *You Can Be a Stock Market Genius* (1997) — cult classic on [[spinoff-investing]] and special situations; widely cited by hedge fund managers as a foundational text
- *The Little Book That Beats the Market* (2005; updated 2010) — the Magic Formula; sold over 300,000 copies
- *The Big Secret for the Small Investor* (2011) — value-weighted indexing
- *Common Sense: The Investor's Guide to Equality, Opportunity, and Growth* (2020)

## Teaching and Other Activities

Greenblatt has taught "Value and Special Situation Investing" at Columbia Business School since 1996. In 1999 he co-founded the **Value Investors Club** (valueinvestorsclub.com) with John Petry, an invitation-only idea-sharing community whose write-ups have been studied academically as a source of alpha. He was also a founding backer and board chair of Success Academy Charter Schools in New York.

## Trading Relevance

Greenblatt matters to traders on three fronts: (1) the [[magic-formula|Magic Formula]] is one of the most widely replicated systematic value screens and a standard benchmark in factor research, combining a [[value-investing|value]] factor ([[earnings-yield]]) with a quality factor ([[return-on-capital]]); (2) *You Can Be a Stock Market Genius* remains the canonical playbook for [[event-driven]] edges — [[spinoff-investing|spinoffs]], post-bankruptcy equities, and merger securities — where forced selling by index funds and institutional constraints creates structural mispricing; (3) his Gotham long/short funds illustrate the capacity and tracking-error tradeoffs of scaling a concentrated edge into a diversified institutional product.

## Influence and Legacy

Greenblatt sits in the value-investing lineage that runs from [[benjamin-graham]] through [[warren-buffett]]: he translated their qualitative principles into a mechanical, repeatable [[magic-formula|formula]] a retail investor could follow. His contributions:

- Made the case that a **two-factor cheap-and-good screen** can beat the market without forecasting, popularizing the modern quality-plus-value factor combination later formalized in academic factor models.
- *You Can Be a Stock Market Genius* seeded a generation of activist and special-situations hedge-fund managers; spinoffs as a structural-mispricing playbook is now standard fare.
- Through Columbia teaching and the Value Investors Club he became one of the most cited educators in [[value-investing]].
