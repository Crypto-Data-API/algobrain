Sheldon Natenberg is the author of *[[option-volatility-and-pricing|Option Volatility and Pricing]]* (1988, updated 2014) -- universally regarded as the definitive textbook on options theory and practice. A former options market maker on the [[cboe|Chicago Board Options Exchange (CBOE)]] floor, Natenberg translated the complex mathematics of options pricing into a practical framework used by professional traders worldwide.

## Overview

Natenberg's career bridged the gap between academic options theory (the [[black-scholes|Black-Scholes model]], the Greeks, volatility surfaces) and the practical realities of trading options on an exchange floor. His book distills this experience into a comprehensive guide that has trained generations of options professionals. It is standard reading at proprietary trading firms, hedge funds, and options market-making desks, and is the foundational text recommended by ITPM and many other options education programs.

## Key Contributions

### Option Volatility and Pricing

First published in 1988 as *Option Volatility and Pricing Strategies*, the book covers:

- **Theoretical pricing models**: [[black-scholes|Black-Scholes]], [[binomial-model|binomial models]], and their assumptions
- **[[options-greeks|The Greeks]]**: [[delta|Delta]], [[gamma|Gamma]], [[theta|Theta]], [[vega|Vega]], and [[rho|Rho]] -- how they interact and how traders use them to manage risk
- **[[implied-volatility|Volatility analysis]]**: The distinction between historical and implied volatility, [[volatility-skew|volatility skew]], term structure, and how to identify mispricings
- **Spread strategies**: Verticals, butterflies, condors, calendars, and how to construct delta-neutral positions
- **[[risk-management|Risk management]]**: Position Greeks, portfolio-level risk, and dynamic hedging
- **Volatility trading**: How to trade volatility as an asset class rather than directional price movement

The 2014 second edition added coverage of [[volatility-skew|volatility skew]] dynamics, more advanced spread strategies, and updated examples reflecting modern electronic markets.

### Practical Framework

What sets Natenberg apart from purely academic treatments is his emphasis on practical decision-making:

- How to evaluate whether an option is "cheap" or "expensive" relative to expected volatility
- How to structure trades that profit from volatility mispricings regardless of market direction
- How market makers manage inventory risk and adjust positions dynamically
- The importance of understanding what the model does NOT capture

## Career

Natenberg worked as an independent options market maker on the CBOE floor during the 1980s and 1990s -- a period when options markets were transitioning from open outcry to electronic trading. He experienced firsthand the practical challenges of managing large options portfolios in real time, including the [[1987-crash|1987 stock market crash]] that exposed the limitations of theoretical models.

After his floor trading career, Natenberg became a sought-after educator and consultant, teaching options theory at trading firms and financial institutions globally. He has been affiliated with Chicago Trading Company and has lectured at numerous industry conferences.

## Legacy

Natenberg's book occupies the same position in options education that [[john-murphy|John Murphy's]] textbook holds in technical analysis -- it is the single most recommended starting point for serious study. Professional options traders frequently cite it as the book that shaped their understanding of volatility and risk. The frameworks he established (thinking in terms of volatility rather than price, managing portfolio Greeks, understanding edge in options) remain the foundation of modern options trading.
