# Warren Buffett

Warren Buffett is the chairman of [[berkshire-hathaway]] and widely regarded as the greatest [[value-investing]] practitioner in history. Known as the "Oracle of Omaha," he compounded book value per share at an extraordinary rate over six decades — roughly 19–20% annualized from 1965 to the mid-2020s, far ahead of the [[s-and-p-500]]'s ~10% — transforming a failing textile mill into a $1 trillion-class conglomerate. He served as CEO from 1965 until January 1, 2026, when [[greg-abel|Greg Abel]] succeeded him; Buffett remains chairman of the board.

## Background

Born in 1930 in Omaha, Nebraska, Buffett showed an early entrepreneurial streak (paper routes, pinball machines, his first stock purchase at age 11). He studied under Benjamin Graham at Columbia Business School and adopted Graham's framework of buying securities trading below their intrinsic value with a [[margin-of-safety]] (Source: [[book-the-intelligent-investor]]). After working for Graham's firm Graham-Newman, he launched the Buffett Partnership in 1956, generating returns of roughly 30% annually (gross) before winding it down and taking control of [[berkshire-hathaway]] in 1965 — a decision he has called one of his worst, since the textile business was a poor "cigar butt" he then had to redeploy capital out of.

## Evolution of Method: Graham → Fisher → Munger

Buffett's philosophy evolved through three intellectual phases, which is essential to understanding his investing:

| Phase | Influence | Core idea |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| Early ("cigar butts") | [[benjamin-graham]] | Buy statistically cheap, beaten-down stocks below net working capital — quantitative, deep-value, "one last puff." |
| Quality turn | [[philip-fisher]] (Source: [[book-common-stocks-and-uncommon-profits]]) | Focus on the *quality and durability* of the business — growth, management, scuttlebutt research. |
| Synthesis | [[charlie-munger]] | "It's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price." Munger pushed Buffett toward paying up for franchises with durable [[economic-moat|moats]]. |

The mature Buffett combines all three: Graham's discipline and [[margin-of-safety]], Fisher's qualitative business analysis, and Munger's insistence on quality and [[economic-moat|competitive moats]].

## Investment Philosophy

Buffett's mature approach centers on buying **wonderful businesses at fair prices** rather than fair businesses at wonderful prices. Core tenets:

- **[[economic-moat|Economic moats]]** — durable competitive advantages (brands, network effects, low-cost production, switching costs, regulatory barriers) that protect long-run returns on capital. Buffett popularized the "moat" metaphor.
- **[[owner-earnings]]** — Buffett's preferred profitability measure, defined in his 1986 letter as reported earnings + depreciation/amortization − the maintenance capex truly required to sustain competitive position and unit volume. It is a more honest proxy for distributable cash than GAAP net income.
- **[[float]]** — Berkshire's insurance operations (GEICO, General Re, National Indemnity) collect premiums before paying claims, generating tens of billions of dollars of "float" Buffett invests at essentially negative cost. Float is the structural engine behind Berkshire's compounding.
- **Circle of competence** — he famously avoids investments he does not understand, which led him to largely sidestep the [[dot-com-bubble]].
- **Temperament over IQ** — long holding periods ("our favorite holding period is forever"), patience, and emotional discipline ("be greedy when others are fearful").
- **[[margin-of-safety]]** — never pay so much that a normal disappointment causes permanent loss.

## Succession and Retirement as CEO (2025-2026)

At the Berkshire Hathaway annual meeting on May 3, 2025, Buffett — then 94 — announced he would step down as CEO at the end of 2025, recommending vice chairman [[greg-abel|Greg Abel]] as his successor. The board voted unanimously to confirm Abel as CEO effective January 1, 2026, with Buffett retained as chairman — the first change of chief executive since Buffett took control in 1965. Buffett said he would keep coming to the office daily and publicly endorsed Abel after the handover (CNBC interview, January 2026).

Key numbers around the transition:

- Berkshire's cash and Treasury bill pile stood at a record $373.3 billion at year-end 2025 and rose to more than $397 billion in Q1 2026 — Abel's first quarter as CEO
- Q1 2026 operating earnings were $11.35 billion; net income attributable to shareholders roughly $10.1 billion (more than double the prior-year quarter)
- Abel wrote his first annual letter to shareholders in February 2026

## Trading Relevance

Buffett's annual shareholder letters are essential reading for understanding [[value-investing]], [[risk-management]], and market psychology. His public portfolio moves are tracked by millions. His famous wager that the [[s-and-p-500]] would outperform a basket of [[hedge-funds]] over ten years (2007-2017) demonstrated the difficulty of beating passive indexing after fees. His approach contrasts sharply with the quantitative methods of [[jim-simons]] and the macro style of [[george-soros]].

For current traders, Berkshire's 13F filings and quarterly cash balance remain widely watched signals: the record ~$397 billion cash position in early 2026 is routinely cited as a gauge of how expensive Buffett and Abel judge equity markets to be. The 2025-2026 succession itself was a market event, with Berkshire shares selling off in the session after the May 2025 announcement as investors began pricing key-man transition risk.

## Influence and Legacy

Buffett's influence on investing is hard to overstate:

- **Defined modern value investing** — his shareholder letters are taught in business schools and treated as canonical texts on [[value-investing]], capital allocation, and [[risk-management]].
- **Popularized the [[economic-moat|moat]] concept**, which became the organizing idea behind Morningstar's equity research and a generation of quality-factor strategies.
- **Annual meeting ("Woodstock for Capitalists")** draws tens of thousands to Omaha; his Q&A sessions shape investor sentiment.
- **The S&P 500 bet (2007–2017)** — Buffett won a $1M wager that a low-cost [[s-and-p-500]] index fund would beat a basket of [[hedge-funds]] over ten years, a landmark demonstration that fees erode active returns.
- **Philanthropy** — co-founder with Bill Gates of the Giving Pledge; has pledged to give away nearly all his fortune.

His style contrasts sharply with the quantitative methods of [[jim-simons]] and the macro style of [[george-soros]], and stands alongside fellow value disciple [[joel-greenblatt]] and his teacher [[benjamin-graham]] in the value-investing canon.
