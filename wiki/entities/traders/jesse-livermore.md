# Jesse Livermore

Jesse Lauriston Livermore (1877-1940) is arguably the most legendary speculator in the history of financial markets. Known as "The Boy Plunger" for his youthful start and "The Great Bear of Wall Street" for his mastery of [[short-selling]], Livermore made and lost several multimillion-dollar fortunes during an era when markets were largely unregulated and trading was a raw contest of skill, nerve, and timing. His most famous trade — shorting the market before the [[1929-crash]] — netted him approximately $100 million (equivalent to roughly $1.5 billion today), making him one of the wealthiest individuals in America at the time.

His life, immortalized in Edwin Lefevre's classic "Reminiscences of a Stock Operator" (1923), provides timeless lessons in [[trend-following]], [[risk-management]], [[position-sizing]], and the psychology of trading that remain as relevant today as they were a century ago (Source: [[book-reminiscences-of-a-stock-operator]]).

## Early Life and the Bucket Shops

Born on July 26, 1877, in Shrewsbury, Massachusetts, Livermore grew up in poverty. He ran away from home at age 14 to escape a future of farming and found work as a quotation board boy at Paine Webber's Boston office, where his job was to chalk up stock prices on a large board as they came in over the ticker tape.

Livermore had an extraordinary natural ability to recognize patterns in price movements. He began keeping notebooks of price data and predictions, and soon started trading in "bucket shops" — informal gambling establishments where participants bet on stock price movements without actually buying or selling shares. His [[tape-reading]] skills proved so profitable that most Boston bucket shops eventually banned him. By his late teens, he had accumulated several thousand dollars — a significant sum at the time.

## Wall Street: Fortunes Made and Lost

Livermore moved to New York City and began trading on the actual stock exchanges, where he discovered that the skills required differed from bucket shop speculation. The real market required patience, understanding of [[liquidity]], and the ability to enter and exit large positions without moving prices against himself.

His career was characterized by dramatic cycles of wealth and ruin:

- **First fortune (early 1900s)**: Livermore built a significant fortune through speculative trading, only to lose it all.
- **1907 Panic**: During the financial panic of 1907, Livermore made approximately $3 million by [[short-selling]] stocks as markets crashed. Legend has it that J.P. Morgan personally asked him to stop shorting to prevent further market collapse.
- **Post-1907**: Livermore shifted to commodities, particularly cotton, and lost his entire fortune through overleveraged positions and a failure to follow his own rules.
- **Recovery and the 1920s**: Livermore rebuilt his wealth through the roaring bull market of the 1920s while simultaneously preparing for what he saw as an inevitable collapse.
- **The [[1929-crash]]**: This was Livermore's masterpiece. Recognizing the signs of an overextended, speculative [[bubble]], he methodically built a massive short position throughout 1929. When the crash came in October, he made approximately $100 million — his greatest triumph.
- **1930s decline**: Tragically, Livermore could not hold onto his final fortune. Through a combination of overtrading, personal difficulties, and depression, he gradually lost his wealth during the 1930s.

## The 1929 Trade: His Greatest Triumph

Livermore's shorting of the [[1929-crash]] stands as one of the most profitable trades in market history. Unlike many speculators of the era who were caught on the wrong side of the crash, Livermore had been studying market conditions for months and recognized multiple warning signs:

- Excessive [[leverage]] among retail speculators buying on [[margin]]
- Narrowing [[market-breadth]] — fewer stocks participating in the advance
- Distribution patterns in leading stocks suggesting institutional selling
- Speculative fervor and unrealistic public optimism

He did not simply go short in one trade. Livermore used a systematic approach, testing his thesis with small initial positions (what he called "probing" the market) and adding to winning positions as the market confirmed his analysis. This approach to [[position-sizing]] — starting small, adding to winners, and cutting losers — remains a cornerstone of professional [[trend-following]] and [[momentum-trading]].

## Livermore's Trading Principles

Livermore's rules, developed through decades of painful experience, form the foundation of much of modern [[technical-analysis]] and [[trading-psychology]]. His key principles include:

1. **Follow the [[trend]]**: "The big money is not in the buying or selling, but in the waiting." Livermore believed in identifying the major direction of the market and trading with it, not against it. This principle underpins all [[trend-following]] strategies.

2. **Let profits run, cut losses quickly**: Livermore understood that the natural human tendency — holding losers and selling winners — is the opposite of what successful trading requires. This insight predates behavioral economics research on [[loss-aversion]] by decades.

3. **Markets are never wrong; opinions are**: Livermore insisted that the price action itself is the ultimate truth. A trader who argues with the market is a trader who will lose money. This principle is foundational to [[technical-analysis]] and [[price-action-trading]].

4. **Trade only when conditions are favorable**: "There is time to go long, time to go short, and time to go fishing." Livermore recognized that not every market environment is conducive to trading and that patience — doing nothing — is often the most profitable strategy.

5. **Beware of tips and opinions**: Livermore learned through costly experience that acting on other people's advice was a reliable path to losses. He insisted on doing his own analysis and trusting his own [[tape-reading]].

6. **Probe before committing**: Rather than entering a full position at once, Livermore advocated testing a thesis with a small initial position and only adding size after the market confirmed the direction. This approach to [[position-sizing]] limits risk while allowing conviction to build.

7. **Study the leading stocks**: Livermore focused on the strongest stocks in the strongest sectors during bull markets and the weakest stocks in the weakest sectors during bear markets — a principle that anticipates modern [[relative-strength]] analysis.

8. **Never average down on a losing position**: Adding to a losing trade was, in Livermore's view, compounding an error. This directly contradicts the [[dollar-cost-averaging]] approach common in long-term investing but is a critical rule for active [[speculation]].

## "Reminiscences of a Stock Operator"

Edwin Lefevre's "Reminiscences of a Stock Operator," published in 1923 and based on extensive interviews with Livermore (fictionalized as "Larry Livingston"), is widely considered the single most important book ever written about trading (Source: [[book-reminiscences-of-a-stock-operator]]). It has influenced generations of traders including [[paul-tudor-jones]], [[william-o-neil]], and countless others.

The book is not a how-to manual but a narrative of a trader's psychological and intellectual development. Its enduring power lies in its honest depiction of the emotional challenges of trading — greed, fear, hope, and the constant temptation to overtrade — and its insight that mastering these psychological forces is more important than any technical system. Key passages on the nature of [[speculation]], the behavior of crowds, and the importance of independent thinking remain as relevant in the age of [[algorithmic-trading]] and [[social-media]] as they were in the bucket shop era.

## Tragic End

On November 28, 1940, Jesse Livermore died by suicide in the cloakroom of the Sherry-Netherland Hotel in Manhattan. He was 63 years old and had lost his last fortune. He left behind a note to his wife that read, in part: "My life has been a failure."

The tragedy of Livermore's end underscores perhaps his most important lesson: that [[risk-management]] extends beyond individual trades to encompass one's entire financial and personal life. His inability to permanently retain the vast fortunes he repeatedly made highlights the dangers of [[overtrading]], the psychological toll of [[speculation]], and the importance of diversifying wealth beyond trading capital.

## Why He Matters

Jesse Livermore's principles are the intellectual bedrock upon which modern [[technical-analysis]], [[trend-following]], and [[trading-psychology]] are built. His emphasis on following the trend, cutting losses, letting profits run, and maintaining psychological discipline anticipated by decades the academic research in [[behavioral-finance]] that would eventually explain why these principles work. Every trader who uses a [[stop-loss]], follows a [[moving-average]], or practices [[position-sizing]] discipline is, whether they know it or not, building on the foundation that Livermore laid. His life is both an inspiration — proving that extraordinary profits are possible through skill and discipline — and a cautionary tale about the ultimate risks of a life devoted entirely to [[speculation]].
