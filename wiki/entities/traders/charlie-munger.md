# Charlie Munger

**Charlie Munger** (1924-2023) was Vice Chairman of [[berkshire-hathaway]] and [[warren-buffett]]'s longtime business partner. Known for his sharp wit and advocacy of mental models, Munger pushed Buffett toward buying wonderful businesses at fair prices rather than mediocre businesses at bargain prices.

His multidisciplinary "latticework of mental models" approach influenced a generation of investors. Munger died on **November 28, 2023**, at the age of 99, just weeks before his 100th birthday.

## Overview

- Born **January 1, 1924** in Omaha, Nebraska — the same hometown as Buffett; as a teenager he worked at the Buffett family grocery store, though the two only met in 1959.
- Served in the U.S. Army Air Corps in WWII; graduated **Harvard Law School (1948)** magna cum laude without a bachelor's degree.
- Co-founded the Los Angeles law firm **Munger, Tolles & Olson (1962)**, still one of the most prestigious litigation firms in the US.
- Ran the investment partnership **Wheeler, Munger & Co. (1962-1975)**, compounding at roughly **19.8% annually versus about 5% for the Dow** over the period — the track record Buffett cited in "The Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville."
- **Vice Chairman of [[berkshire-hathaway]] from 1978** until his death in 2023; **Chairman of Daily Journal Corporation from 1977**, stepping down as chairman in 2022 while remaining a director.
- Notable independent calls: early investor and long-time board member of **Costco** (director from 1997), and the 2008 Daily Journal/Berkshire-linked investment in **BYD**, the Chinese EV maker — Berkshire's ~$232M stake (about 10%) grew more than 30x at its 2022 peak.
- Net worth at death was estimated around **$2.6 billion** (Forbes), deliberately reduced over decades by large charitable gifts (notably to the University of Michigan and UC Santa Barbara).

## Mental Models and Philosophy

Munger's greatest intellectual contribution was his framework of **mental models** -- a "latticework" of ideas drawn from multiple disciplines (psychology, physics, biology, economics, history) that can be applied to investment decisions and business analysis. He argued that relying on a single discipline produces narrow thinking, and that the best decisions come from synthesizing insights across fields.

Key Munger concepts include:

- **Inversion** -- Rather than asking "how do I succeed?", ask "what would guarantee failure?" and avoid those things. Munger famously said, "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there."
- **Circle of competence** -- Only invest in businesses you truly understand. Knowing the boundaries of your knowledge is more important than the size of what you know.
- **Psychology of misjudgment** -- Munger catalogued 25 cognitive biases that lead to poor decisions, detailed in his talk "The Psychology of Human Misjudgment" and collected in *Poor Charlie's Almanack*.

## Partnership with Buffett

As Vice Chairman of [[berkshire-hathaway]] since 1978, Munger played a pivotal role in evolving [[warren-buffett]]'s investment approach. While Buffett began as a strict [[benjamin-graham]]-style deep-value investor, Munger convinced him that it was better to buy "a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price." This philosophical shift led Berkshire toward high-quality businesses with durable competitive advantages -- companies like Coca-Cola, See's Candies, and Apple.

Munger also served as Chairman of the Daily Journal Corporation and was an early investor in Costco and BYD (the Chinese electric vehicle maker). His annual meeting Q&A sessions and shareholder letters were prized for their blunt wisdom and dark humor.

## Trading Relevance

Munger is required reading for traders despite being a buy-and-hold investor, because his **"Psychology of Human Misjudgment"** (25 cognitive biases) is one of the foundational practitioner texts of [[behavioral-finance]] — covering loss aversion, commitment/consistency bias, social proof, and incentive-caused bias, all of which map directly onto trading failure modes (averaging down, chasing crowds, refusing to take stops). His **inversion** heuristic ("figure out what guarantees failure and avoid it") is effectively the origin of modern kill-criteria and pre-mortem thinking in [[risk-management]], and his "circle of competence" maps onto edge definition in [[edge-taxonomy|edge taxonomy]] terms: if you cannot state why the counterparty loses, you have no trade.
