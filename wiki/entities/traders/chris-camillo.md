# Chris Camillo

Chris Camillo is a Dallas-based retail trader and author known for coining the term [[social-arbitrage|"social arbitrage"]] -- a methodology of trading equities and options based on early detection of consumer and cultural trends on social media and in everyday observation. He grew a personal trading account from roughly $84,000 in August 2006 to approximately $42 million by May 2021, a track record independently audited at ~77% compounded annual returns over ~15 years and documented in Jack Schwager's [[unknown-market-wizards]] (2020) in a chapter titled "Neither" (neither fundamental nor technical). He is the author of [[laughing-at-wall-street]] (2011) and co-host of the [[dumb-money-podcast|Dumb Money]] YouTube channel and podcast.

## Background

Camillo is based in the Park Cities area of Dallas, Texas. He attended [[southern-methodist-university|Southern Methodist University]] (SMU) in the mid-1990s. His birth year is widely cited as approximately 1974, though no primary trading-focused profile confirms it -- the figure originates from a non-trading biographical source and should be treated as approximate.

His pre-trading career was in consumer market research, which directly informs his trading edge. He served as VP, Partnership and Panel Development at **e-Rewards Market Research / Research Now / SSI** (now [[dynata]]) from March 2001 to January 2010. He later served as COO and Managing Partner at **eCarList, LLC**, an automotive-dealer SaaS company, from August 2010 to October 2012. He is certified at the Expert Level in Consumer Research by the Marketing Research Association.

## Trading Career

Camillo began trading at age 14 but took it seriously only after 2006. The key milestones in his audited track record:

- **August 2006**: starting account balance of ~$84,000
- **Early 2007**: deployed $20,000 into equities
- **2007-2010**: grew the $20,000 equity allocation to ~$2 million
- **December 2006 - November 2013**: ~84% average annualized return (independently audited)
- **August 2006 - May 2021**: $84,000 → ~$42 million, ~77% compounded annual return (audited and reported in [[unknown-market-wizards]])
- **Post-2021**: self-reported account size of approximately $60-70 million+ as of 2025 (per Lewis Howes Ep. 1789 and Jack Schwager's public statements). These post-audit figures are **unaudited and self-reported** and should be treated as such.
- **AUM**: zero -- he trades his own capital only and has publicly stated he will not accept outside money.

Academic work on his returns (cited in Sahm Capital) notes that his portfolio volatility has run approximately 5× the S&P 500, implying heavy use of options leverage -- a critical caveat when comparing his CAGR to conventional benchmarks.

## Social Arbitrage Methodology

Camillo's signature approach is [[social-arbitrage]] -- "buying a company's stock upon discovery of an information imbalance in social media or real-world observations, and selling when that information becomes widely accepted as fact." The Schwager chapter is titled "Neither" because the methodology is neither fundamental nor technical analysis. His four-step decision process, synthesized from interviews:

1. **Observe** emerging consumer, product, or cultural trends in everyday life and on social platforms
2. **Verify** momentum using [[google-trends]] (5-year view to strip seasonality), social mention frequency, and web traffic
3. **Map** the trend to publicly-traded companies with direct revenue exposure
4. **Concentrate** -- he takes high-conviction positions, typically 5-30% of the portfolio, often expressed through call options for leverage

He holds trades from weeks to months, usually exiting around or shortly after the earnings print that confirms his thesis. He does **not** use stop losses and will add to losing positions if the underlying thesis remains intact. He has said genuine high-conviction trades come along "once every couple of months or less" -- patience, not frequency, is the constraint.

## TickerTags

In 2015 Camillo co-founded tickertags, a social-listening alt-data platform that mapped roughly 350,000 tags (product names, brands, competitors) to approximately 8,000 tickers. Its best-known public output was a correct forecast of the 2016 Brexit referendum Leave outcome. TickerTags was acquired by **M Science** (the alt-data subsidiary of [[jefferies-financial-group|Jefferies]]) around June 2018. The standalone consumer product was wound down around 2020; the underlying technology lives on inside M Science's institutional research.

## Dumb Money (YouTube and Podcast)

In 2018 Camillo and two colleagues -- **Dave Hanson** and **Jordan McLain** -- quit their day jobs to trade full-time and began filming their research and real-money trades. This became the **Dumb Money** YouTube channel and, from early 2020, the *Dumb Money Live* podcast (available on Apple, Spotify, and Audible). The channel reached 50,000 YouTube subscribers by October 2020.

**Clarification**: the "Jordan" in Dumb Money is **Jordan McLain**, not [[jordan-belfort|Jordan Belfort]] -- a conflation that has spread via content-farm articles. Dumb Money is also unrelated to the 2023 Sony/Columbia film *Dumb Money* about [[gamestop-short-squeeze|GameStop]] and Keith Gill.

Site: **dumbmoney.tv**. Content is a mix of live trade discussions, trend-research walkthroughs, and a members-only Discord community.

## Criticisms and Caveats

The track record is extraordinary but deserves careful framing:

1. **Sample of one.** A single 15-year record at ~77% CAGR proves nothing about the replicability of [[social-arbitrage]] for other traders. This is the classic problem with inference from rare outliers.
2. **Extreme volatility.** Portfolio volatility at ~5× the S&P 500 implies heavy and persistent use of call options. A return distribution of this shape has fat tails; one bad year could erase a material portion of the book.
3. **No stop losses.** Willingness to add to losers on a maintained thesis is a powerful tool for a trader with genuine edge and disastrous for a trader who only believes they have edge.
4. **Non-replicable skill.** The methodology rewards obsessive daily observation ("four hours a day" per his interviews) and consumer-trend intuition. It is hard to systematize and harder to teach.
5. **Post-audit figures are self-reported.** The $60-70M+ figures cited in 2025 interviews have not been independently audited; only the $84K → $42M figure through May 2021 has been.
6. **Strategy decay.** The 2018 acquisition of tickertags by M Science commoditized a component of his edge for institutions. Twitter/X API pricing changes in 2023 and the decline of open Reddit data have raised the cost of the retail version of his approach.
