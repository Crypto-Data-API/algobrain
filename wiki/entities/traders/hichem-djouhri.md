# Hichem Djouhri

Hichem Djouhri is a senior trading mentor at [[itpm|the Institute of Trading and Portfolio Management]] based in Dubai, and one of the best-performing portfolio managers in the Middle East / North Africa (MENA) region. He won best-performing asset manager of the MENA region for seven consecutive years, a track record built on deep [[fundamental-analysis]], understanding commodity [[correlation|correlations]], and exploiting the behavioral inefficiencies of retail-dominated markets (Source: [[itpm-trading-legends-hichem-djouhri]]).

## Career

### Education and Early Career

Educated at the University of New Brunswick in Canada. Grew up in Dubai and returned there to begin his career. Started as an equity derivatives trader for a couple of years before moving to the buy side at an investment bank, where he began managing approximately $500 million — a substantial amount for someone early in their career (Source: [[itpm-trading-legends-hichem-djouhri]]).

### MENA Portfolio Management

Career focused on the MENA region, covering approximately 14 different stock markets. Mandates were mainly long-only, with funds covering the whole MENA region plus country-specific funds (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, etc.). Also managed discretionary mandates for institutions and ultra-high-net-worth individuals, which could include shorter-term tactical trading (Source: [[itpm-trading-legends-hichem-djouhri]]).

Was in his early thirties at the time of the ITPM interview — meaning his seven consecutive years of top performance came at a remarkably young age.

## MENA Market Insights

Djouhri provides unique expertise on trading in emerging/frontier markets:

- **Extreme volatility.** MENA market [[volatility]] is approximately three times the [[vix|VIX]]. "Volatility is a trader's best friend or worst enemy if you don't know how to use it" (Source: [[itpm-trading-legends-hichem-djouhri]]).
- **70% retail participation.** Unlike developed markets dominated by institutions, MENA markets are ~70% retail money. These retail investors are driven by news, highly speculative, frequently misuse [[leverage|margin]], and exhibit strong herd mentality following blogs and tips.
- **Institutional edge over retail.** Retail investors drive fundamentally strong companies lower during sell-offs, providing institutions with liquidity to buy at discounts. Retail then buys when expensive, and institutions sell. "It's a cycle that has happened time and time again" (Source: [[itpm-trading-legends-hichem-djouhri]]).

## Key Trading Strategy: Oil Correlation Mispricing

Djouhri's most distinctive insight involves counter-intuitive oil [[correlation]] trades in MENA markets:

When [[oil]] prices drop, market participants reflexively short all oil-related entities (especially in Saudi Arabia). However, many of these companies actually **benefit** from lower oil prices because oil is their **raw material input** — their margins expand. The market misses this because it focuses solely on the sector-level correlation rather than company-specific fundamentals (Source: [[itpm-trading-legends-hichem-djouhri]]).

Similarly, domestic-demand-driven companies (e.g., telecoms that grow as population grows and data becomes more expensive) outperform regardless of oil price movements. Identifying these stocks was a key driver of Djouhri's track record — his portfolio showed a "beautiful negative correlation" to the benchmark.

## Hedging Approaches

Djouhri uses multiple hedging strategies adapted to MENA market conditions:

1. **Short individual stocks** — direct hedges against long positions
2. **[[covered-call|Covered calls]]** — sell calls against long positions to generate income and reduce cost basis
3. **High-dividend stocks** — allocate to 5–6% yielding stocks with quarterly payments that compound and add alpha while providing downside cushion
4. **Short [[oil]] futures** — hedge long equity exposure to oil-sensitive markets
5. **Cross-market correlation trades** — long one MENA market while short another, exploiting money flows between regional markets

## Worst Trade: The Mobily Fraud

Djouhri's most painful loss was Mobily (Etihad Etisalat) in Saudi Arabia — a telecom that was the "crown jewel" across all portfolios with ~7% annual dividend yield, strong cash flows, and predictable earnings. The company then disclosed it had **falsely reported earnings for the past year and a half** (Source: [[itpm-trading-legends-hichem-djouhri]]).

Due to Saudi Arabia's 10% daily circuit breaker limits, if you didn't sell in the first seconds the market opened, the stock was limit-down with no bids for multiple consecutive days. Djouhri exited with a 30% loss. It was a core position across all portfolios — "nobody was spared."

**Lesson:** "Audit your numbers, look at the fundamentals, understand the company, and there is no such thing as a stock that is going to remain great forever. Stories change and you need to adapt very quickly."

## Trading Philosophy

1. **Investment process is non-negotiable.** Without discipline in your process, "you're just a cowboy, shooting everywhere, hoping one of them is going to be a bull's-eye."
2. **Fundamentals first.** Not what a friend said, not a blog post, not that a stock "just broke a certain line." Dig into the company and understand the business.
3. **Global macro awareness.** Even to trade MENA effectively, you must understand US, China, and Europe GDP and macro drivers. "No country is an island."
4. **[[diversification|Diversify]] across geographies.** Investors who only focus on their home market are "completely lost."
5. **Adapt quickly.** Stories change — companies that were crown jewels can become liabilities overnight. Never fall in love with a position.
