# Michael Burry

Michael Burry is an American physician-turned-investor who founded Scion Capital in 2000 and made over $700 million for his fund by shorting [[subprime-mortgage]]-backed securities during the [[2008-global-financial-crisis]]. Featured as the central character in Michael Lewis's *The Big Short* (2010) and the 2015 film adaptation, Burry is renowned for his obsessive deep-dive [[fundamental-analysis]], his willingness to stand alone against market consensus, and his unusual cognitive profile -- he has described his Asperger's syndrome as a key source of his analytical edge.

## Early Life and Background

Born on June 19, 1971, in San Jose, California, Burry lost his left eye to retinoblastoma (a rare cancer) at age two and wore a glass eye throughout his life. He has said that growing up visibly different made him comfortable with being an outsider -- a trait that would define his investment career.

Burry studied economics at UCLA and then earned his MD from Vanderbilt University School of Medicine. He began his residency in neurology at Stanford Hospital but quickly realized that medicine was not his calling. During his residency night shifts, instead of sleeping, Burry wrote about [[value-investing]] on internet message boards -- particularly the old MSN Money boards and a site called Silicon Investor. His posts attracted a following because of their rigor: Burry would methodically analyze individual stocks using the [[benjamin-graham]] framework of buying below intrinsic value, citing specific balance sheet metrics and margin-of-safety calculations.

His online writing was so compelling that Joel Greenblatt of Gotham Capital and other professional investors began following his posts and eventually offered to invest with him. In 2000, Burry left medicine to manage money full-time.

## Career and Key Trades

### Scion Capital: Graham-Style Deep Value (2000-2004)

Burry founded Scion Capital in 2000 with approximately $600,000 of his own money plus $1 million from investors. His early strategy was pure [[benjamin-graham|Graham]]-style deep [[value-investing]]: buying stocks trading below [[net-current-asset-value]] (NCAV) -- companies whose current assets minus all liabilities exceeded their market capitalization. This meant buying businesses for less than their liquidation value, a classic margin-of-safety approach (Source: [[book-the-intelligent-investor]]).

Scion Capital returned **55%** in 2001 (vs. -11.9% for the S&P 500), **16%** in 2002 (vs. -22.1%), and **50%** in 2003 (vs. +28.7%). These returns -- generated during the brutal [[dot-com-bubble|dot-com bust]] -- demonstrated that [[value-investing]] could deliver extraordinary returns precisely when growth-oriented strategies were failing.

### The Big Short: Subprime Mortgage CDS (2005-2008)

> **Full case study:** [[2007-2008-burry-subprime-cds-trade]] documents the trade mechanics, timeline, carry-cost agony, and repeatable framework. Strategy generalization at [[convex-tail-hedge-arbitrage]]; broader pattern context at [[fastest-profitable-trades]].

Beginning in 2003-2004, Burry began reading individual [[subprime-mortgage]] bond prospectuses -- a task so tedious that virtually no one else on Wall Street was doing it. What he found was alarming: the underlying loans were made to borrowers with little income verification, adjustable "teaser" rates that would reset dramatically higher, and underwriting standards that assumed perpetually rising home prices.

Burry's critical insight was granular: he did not just conclude that "housing is a bubble." He read the actual loan-level data and understood that specific tranches of specific mortgage-backed securities were virtually certain to default when the teaser rates reset in 2007. This was not a macro call -- it was a [[fundamental-analysis|fundamental analysis]] of individual securities, applied at a scale that no one else was attempting.

In 2005, Burry convinced Goldman Sachs, Deutsche Bank, and other Wall Street firms to sell him [[credit-default-swaps|credit default swaps]] on [[subprime-mortgage]]-backed securities. The banks were happy to sell -- they believed housing was safe and viewed the CDS premiums as free money.

The next two years were brutal. Burry's CDS positions bled premium -- approximately 2% per year of the notional value -- while the underlying mortgage bonds continued to perform (because the teaser rates had not yet reset). Scion Capital's investors, seeing losses mount with no apparent catalyst, demanded their money back. Burry refused, invoking a contractual lock-up provision that prevented withdrawals. This decision -- locking in his investors against their will -- nearly destroyed his reputation and his relationships, but it saved the trade.

When [[subprime-mortgage|subprime mortgages]] began defaulting en masse in 2007, the CDS positions exploded in value. Scion Capital earned approximately **$700 million** for its investors and Burry personally earned approximately **$100 million**. The trade vindicated his analysis but left him emotionally drained and at odds with his investor base (Source: [[book-when-genius-failed]] provides relevant context on the psychological toll of contrarian bets).

### Post-Crisis: Scion Asset Management (2013-Present)

Burry closed Scion Capital in 2008, citing the personal toll of the subprime trade. He re-emerged in 2013 with Scion Asset Management, a smaller fund that has since become known for occasional dramatic macro and value calls:

- **Water scarcity (2010s)**: Burry has repeatedly warned that water shortage will be one of the defining crises of the 21st century, investing in farmland with water rights. This thesis has yet to fully play out.
- **GameStop (2019)**: Burry built a significant position in [[gamestop-short-squeeze|GameStop]] well before the 2021 short squeeze, arguing that the stock was undervalued on a fundamental basis. He sold before the squeeze itself.
- **Tech bubble warnings (2021)**: Warned publicly about speculation in growth stocks and [[crypto-markets|crypto]], drawing comparisons to 1999-2000.
- **Japan macro trade (2023-2024)**: Bet on Japanese equities and the yen, based on valuation and macro analysis.
- **Large-cap value bets**: Positions in undervalued mega-caps like Meta and Alphabet, consistent with his [[value-investing]] roots.

Burry's post-crisis record is mixed -- his timing on macro calls has been inconsistent, and he has frequently been early (or wrong) on warnings. However, his core approach -- deep [[fundamental-analysis]], contrarian positioning, and willingness to accept short-term pain for long-term conviction -- remains consistent.

## Trading and Investment Philosophy

Burry's philosophy is rooted in [[benjamin-graham]]'s principles but extended through obsessive primary research:

1. **Read the primary documents.** Burry's edge in the subprime trade came from reading individual mortgage bond prospectuses -- documents that were publicly available but that almost no one else bothered to analyze. In any market, the greatest edges come from doing the work that others consider too tedious or too time-consuming.

2. **Margin of safety is paramount.** From [[book-the-intelligent-investor|The Intelligent Investor]], Burry internalized Graham's central principle: buy assets at a sufficient discount to intrinsic value that even if your analysis is partially wrong, you still make money. This principle applies whether you are buying stocks below [[net-current-asset-value]] or shorting mortgage bonds with fraudulent underwriting.

3. **Be willing to stand alone.** Burry's most profitable trades were those where he was one of the very few people on his side of the trade. This requires extraordinary psychological resilience -- the ability to endure losses, criticism, and isolation while waiting for the thesis to play out. This connects directly to [[behavioral-finance]]: the crowd is often wrong at extremes, but going against the crowd is psychologically punishing.

4. **Hyperfocus is an edge.** Burry has openly discussed how his Asperger's syndrome gives him the ability to focus intensely on financial statements and data for extended periods, blocking out social pressure and conventional wisdom. In a market where [[sentiment]] and narrative drive prices in the short term, the ability to ignore noise and focus on fundamentals is genuinely valuable.

5. **Conviction requires lock-up.** Burry's decision to lock in his investors -- preventing them from withdrawing during the most painful phase of the CDS trade -- was legally aggressive but strategically essential. Many correct trades fail because the investor (or the fund's investors) lacks the psychological or structural ability to hold through the drawdown. [[position-sizing]] and liquidity management are as important as the thesis itself.

6. **You do not need to always be in the market.** After closing Scion Capital in 2008, Burry spent five years away from active fund management. His subsequent return with Scion Asset Management involved concentrated, high-conviction bets rather than constant trading. There is no obligation to trade when the opportunities are not compelling.
