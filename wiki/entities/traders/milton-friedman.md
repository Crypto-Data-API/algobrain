Milton Friedman (July 31, 1912 – November 16, 2006) was an American economist, leader of the Chicago school, and the central figure of **[[monetarism]]** — the doctrine that inflation is driven primarily by money-supply growth. He won the 1976 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences for work on consumption analysis, monetary history and theory, and stabilization policy. For traders, Friedman matters twice over: his framework underpins how markets read central-bank policy, and his 1971 advocacy paper directly enabled the launch of **[[currency-futures]]** at the Chicago Mercantile Exchange.

## Overview

Friedman spent most of his academic career at the University of Chicago (1946–1977), later moving to the Hoover Institution at Stanford. Key contributions:

- ***A Monetary History of the United States, 1867–1960*** (1963, with Anna J. Schwartz) — argued the [[federal-reserve|Federal Reserve]]'s monetary contraction turned the 1929 downturn into the Great Depression, cutting the money supply by roughly a third between 1929 and 1933. This reading of history is the intellectual backdrop for the aggressive Fed liquidity responses of 2008 and 2020 (Ben Bernanke famously told Friedman in 2002: "You're right, we did it. We're very sorry").
- **Permanent income hypothesis** (1957) — consumption depends on expected lifetime income, not current income.
- **Natural rate of unemployment** (1968 AEA presidential address) — there is no permanent inflation–unemployment trade-off; the Phillips curve is vertical in the long run. This prediction was vindicated by 1970s stagflation and reshaped central banking.
- **The k-percent rule** — his prescription that the money supply grow at a fixed annual rate rather than at policymakers' discretion.
- *Capitalism and Freedom* (1962) and *Free to Choose* (1980) — popular works advocating free markets, floating exchange rates, and school vouchers.

His most quoted line — **"Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon"** — remains the shorthand thesis behind hard-asset and [[bitcoin]] inflation-hedge trades whenever money-supply growth accelerates, as in 2020–2022.

### The mechanics of monetarism

Friedman rebuilt the classical **quantity theory of money** into a falsifiable framework. Its engine is the equation of exchange, **MV = PY**, where M is the money stock, V its velocity (how often each unit is spent), P the price level, and Y real output. Friedman's empirical claim was that velocity (V) is *stable and predictable* over the medium term, and real output (Y) is set by real factors (technology, labor, capital) that money cannot durably move. Therefore a sustained rise in M, beyond the trend growth of Y, must show up almost one-for-one in P — i.e. [[inflation]]. The corollary that traders use directly: persistent above-trend growth in broad money (M2) is a leading signal for future inflation, but with **"long and variable lags"** (typically 12–24 months), so the policy and price effects of a [[monetary-policy|monetary]] impulse arrive well after the impulse itself.

This is why monetarist accounting briefly returned to the trading mainstream in 2020–2022: US M2 expanded roughly 40% cumulatively, and the framework predicted (correctly, against the consensus "transitory" call) a delayed inflation surge. The same logic explains Friedman's [[federal-reserve|Fed]] critique — discretionary central banks tend to over-stimulate and then over-tighten because they react to data that already reflects past money growth, which is why he favored the rules-based **k-percent rule** over discretion.

## Role in Creating Currency Futures

Friedman has a direct, practical legacy in market structure:

- In **1967**, Friedman tried to short the overvalued British pound ahead of its devaluation, but Chicago banks refused to take the other side for a private speculator — there was no retail-accessible venue for currency speculation.
- In **1971**, as the [[bretton-woods|Bretton Woods]] system collapsed, CME chairman [[leo-melamed|Leo Melamed]] commissioned Friedman (for a fee of $7,500) to write "The Need for Futures Markets in Currencies." The paper gave academic legitimacy to the idea of exchange-traded FX speculation.
- In **May 1972**, the CME launched the **International Monetary Market (IMM)**, the first financial futures exchange — the ancestor of today's entire financial-futures complex at [[cme-group|CME Group]] (FX, eurodollars/SOFR, equity index, Treasury futures).

Friedman's 1953 essay "The Case for Flexible Exchange Rates" had already laid out the argument that floating currencies plus speculators would stabilize, not destabilize, FX markets — the intellectual foundation of the post-1973 floating-rate regime in which all modern [[macro-trading|macro trading]] occurs.

## Trading Relevance

- **Central-bank watching**: Friedman's "long and variable lags" of monetary policy is the standard frame for trading rate cycles — markets price policy effects 12–24 months forward.
- **Inflation trades**: The monetarist M2 lens resurfaced prominently in 2020–2021, when ~40% cumulative M2 growth preceded the 2021–2022 inflation spike — a validation traders such as [[stanley-druckenmiller]] and [[paul-tudor-jones]] cited in positioning long gold, commodities, and bitcoin.
- **Market structure**: Every FX futures contract, and by lineage every financial futures contract, traces to the Friedman–Melamed collaboration of 1971–72.
- Friedman also argued (1953) that profitable speculation is stabilizing — an early formal defense of speculators that prefigures debates around [[efficient-market-hypothesis|market efficiency]].
