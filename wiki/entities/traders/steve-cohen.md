# Steve Cohen

Steve Cohen is an American hedge fund manager who founded SAC Capital Advisors in 1992 and generated approximately 30% annual returns over two decades -- one of the best risk-adjusted track records in the history of [[hedge-funds]]. Known for ultra-short-term holding periods, aggressive information gathering, and an instinctive feel for [[market-microstructure]], Cohen is often cited as the greatest pure trader (as distinct from investor) of his generation. His career, however, is inseparable from the insider trading scandal that resulted in SAC paying $1.8 billion in fines -- the largest insider trading penalty in history.

## Early Life and Background

Born on June 11, 1956, in Great Neck, Long Island, New York, Cohen grew up in a middle-class family. He developed an early interest in the stock market, reportedly reading the financial pages as a teenager and playing poker to develop his feel for risk and probability. He attended the Wharton School at the University of Pennsylvania, and on his very first day as a trader at Gruntal & Co. in 1978, he reportedly made $8,000. Within a short time, he was running a group of traders and generating $100,000 per day for the firm.

Cohen's edge was apparent from the start: he had an exceptional ability to read [[tape-reading|the tape]] -- to interpret price action, [[volume]], and order flow in real time and translate that reading into rapid trading decisions. This is a skill that combines [[market-microstructure]] knowledge with [[trading-psychology]] -- understanding not just what prices are doing, but what other market participants are thinking and doing.

## Career and Key Trades

### SAC Capital Advisors (1992-2013)

Cohen founded SAC Capital Advisors in 1992 with $25 million of his own money. The fund's performance over the next two decades was extraordinary:

- **~30% annualized returns** before fees from 1992 to 2013
- Only **one losing year** in 21 years of operation
- Peak AUM of approximately **$14 billion**
- Typical fees: **3% management fee and 50% performance fee** (far above industry standard 2/20)

Despite the punitive fee structure, investors stayed with SAC because the net returns were still exceptional. The fund's consistency -- high returns with minimal drawdowns year after year -- was unmatched outside of [[renaissance-technologies|Renaissance Technologies]]' [[medallion-fund]] (Source: [[book-more-money-than-god]]).

### Trading Style: Speed and Variant Perception

Cohen's trading approach was fundamentally different from long-term investors like [[warren-buffett]] or macro traders like [[george-soros]]:

- **Ultra-short holding periods**: SAC's portfolio turnover exceeded **300% per year**, meaning the average position was held for less than four months. Many of Cohen's personal trades lasted days or hours.
- **Massive diversification**: SAC ran hundreds of positions simultaneously, managed by dozens of portfolio managers, each operating semi-independently. This structure resembled a trading floor more than a traditional hedge fund.
- **"Variant perception"**: Cohen's core concept -- find situations where the market's prevailing view differs from reality and bet on the gap closing. This requires both superior information and the confidence to act on it before the rest of the market catches up.
- **Conviction scoring**: Cohen reportedly asked his portfolio managers to rate their conviction on each idea on a 1-10 scale. Ideas scoring below 7 were not worth trading. This framework forced explicit thinking about [[position-sizing]] and edge quality.

### Information Edge and the SAC Machine

SAC was legendary for its information-gathering infrastructure. The fund employed hundreds of analysts and portfolio managers, maintained extensive networks of industry contacts and expert consultants, and invested heavily in technology to aggregate and process information. Cohen expected his PMs to have an "edge" on every trade -- a piece of analysis or information that the rest of the market did not yet have (Source: [[book-the-quants]]).

This culture of aggressive information-seeking ultimately led to SAC's downfall.

### The Insider Trading Scandal (2010-2014)

Beginning in 2010, federal prosecutors and the SEC launched a sweeping investigation into insider trading at SAC Capital. Over the next several years:

- **Eight** SAC employees were convicted of or pleaded guilty to insider trading
- SAC Capital pleaded guilty to securities fraud in 2013 and paid **$1.8 billion** in criminal and civil fines -- the largest insider trading penalty in history
- Cohen was **not personally charged** with insider trading, but the SEC filed a civil action alleging he failed to supervise portfolio managers. He agreed to a settlement that barred him from managing outside money until 2018.
- The investigation revealed a pattern of SAC traders obtaining material non-public information (MNPI) from corporate insiders, expert network consultants, and industry contacts

The SAC scandal raised fundamental questions about the line between aggressive research and illegal insider trading, and about whether the culture at top [[hedge-funds]] inherently incentivizes crossing that line.

### Point72 Asset Management (2014-Present)

After SAC closed to outside investors, Cohen converted the fund to a family office called Point72 Asset Management, managing his personal fortune of approximately $9 billion. In 2018, when the ban on managing outside money was lifted, Point72 reopened to external investors and now manages approximately **$25-30 billion** in assets.

Point72 has invested heavily in [[quantitative-trading]], data science, and technology, reflecting the broader industry shift toward systematic strategies. Cohen has also been active in venture capital and direct investments, including his 2020 purchase of the New York Mets baseball team for $2.4 billion.

## Trading and Investment Philosophy

Cohen's philosophy centers on speed, information, and [[risk-management]]:

1. **Cut losers immediately.** Cohen's "10% rule" is well known: any position that declines 10% from its entry price gets cut, no questions asked. This strict [[stop-loss]] discipline prevents small losses from becoming large ones and frees up capital for better opportunities. "The best way to manage risk is to not lose money."

2. **Speed of decision-making matters.** In Cohen's world, the ability to process information and act on it quickly is a competitive advantage. Markets are not efficient instantaneously -- there is a window between when new information becomes available and when it is fully reflected in prices. The fastest, most decisive traders capture the edge in that window. This connects directly to [[market-microstructure]] -- understanding how information flows through markets.

3. **Variant perception drives alpha.** The most profitable trades come from identifying where the market's consensus view is wrong. This requires both better information and the analytical framework to interpret it differently from the crowd. Cohen's approach echoes [[george-soros]]'s emphasis on finding situations where market perception diverges from reality.

4. **Diversify across many uncorrelated bets.** SAC's structure -- dozens of semi-independent portfolio managers running hundreds of positions -- was designed to generate returns from many small edges rather than a few concentrated bets. This is the opposite of [[warren-buffett]]'s concentrated approach but shares the mathematical logic of [[statistical-arbitrage]]: many small, uncorrelated bets produce smoother returns than a few large ones.

5. **Invest in information infrastructure.** Cohen spent enormous sums on analysts, technology, expert networks, and data. In his view, information is the raw material of trading, and the firm that processes information fastest and most thoroughly wins. This philosophy anticipates the modern arms race in [[alternative-data|alternative data]] and [[machine-learning]].

6. **Emotions are the enemy.** Cohen has spoken extensively about the importance of emotional discipline in trading. Losses should not cause despair; wins should not cause euphoria. The goal is to execute the process consistently, cutting losers and letting winners run, without emotional interference. This connects to [[trading-psychology]] and the [[behavioral-finance]] literature on [[loss-aversion]] and [[overtrading]].
