Larry R. Williams (b. 6 October 1942) is a legendary American futures trader, author, and indicator creator who won the 1987 Robbins World Cup Championship of Futures Trading by turning **$10,000 into $1,137,600 — an 11,376% return in twelve months**, still the most famous trading-competition result in history. He created [[williams-percent-r|Williams %R]] in 1966, a mirror-image momentum oscillator closely related to the [[stochastic|Stochastic Oscillator]], and pioneered trading off the CFTC's Commitments of Traders report (Source: [[2026-04-20-comprehensive-guide-technical-trading-indicators]]).

## Trading Record

Williams' 1987 Robbins World Cup performance — turning approximately $10,000 into over $1.1 million in a single year of real-money futures trading — remains the all-time competition record. He used momentum and %R methods, aggressive position sizing, and seasonal/COT inputs as core components of his approach (Source: [[2026-04-20-comprehensive-guide-technical-trading-indicators]]). Critics note the drawdowns en route were extreme (the account reportedly swung down well over 50% intra-contest), making it a lesson in both edge and risk appetite.

A famous coda: his daughter, actress **Michelle Williams**, won the same contest in 1997 at age 16 with roughly a 1,000% return ($10,000 to about $110,000) trading her father's methods.

## Indicators and Concepts

### [[williams-percent-r|Williams %R]] (1966)

A bounded momentum oscillator that measures where the current close sits within the N-period high–low range. The formula is:

**%R = (Highest High − Close) / (Highest High − Lowest Low) × −100**

over a default 14-period lookback. The output runs from 0 to −100: readings between 0 and −20 are overbought, −80 to −100 oversold. %R is effectively the **mirror image of the [[stochastic|Stochastic %K]]** — Stochastic measures the close relative to the *low* of the range, while %R measures it relative to the *high*, so the two are inverted versions of the same momentum idea. It was published daily in financial media for decades.

### Ultimate Oscillator (1985)

A multi-timeframe momentum blend (typically 7-, 14-, and 28-period windows) built on Williams's "buying pressure" concept, weighted so the shortest window has the largest influence. By combining three lookbacks it is designed to reduce the false divergence signals that single-period oscillators throw off.

### COT analysis

Williams was the first to systematically research and write about the CFTC's Commitments of Traders report (starting in the early 1970s) and authored an early book on it. His COT indices track [[commercial-hedger-positioning|commercial hedger positioning]] — the idea being that commercial hedgers (the "smart money" close to the physical commodity) are net-right at extremes, while large speculators are net-wrong. Fading speculative extremes and following commercials underpins modern [[cot-report-analysis]].

### Other tools

Accumulation/distribution measures, seasonal patterns, and cycle forecasts for commodities.

## Current Status (2026)

Williams, in his early 80s, remains active: he publishes research, COT data tools, and annual market forecasts through his site I Really Trade (ireallytrade.com) and continues to present market outlooks (e.g., his 2025 forecast cycle work). Outside trading he was a two-time Montana US Senate candidate in the 1970s–80s.

## Influence and Legacy

- **%R and the Ultimate Oscillator** are standard on every charting platform — knowing their construction matters when testing momentum signals, and %R remains a textbook example of how two "different" indicators (it and the [[stochastic|Stochastic]]) are mathematically the same idea inverted.
- **COT framework** — fade the speculators, follow the commercials at extremes — remains the backbone of positioning-based [[futures]] strategies and is now formalised in [[cot-report-analysis]] and [[commercial-hedger-positioning]].
- **The 1987 record** is a canonical case study in the leverage/drawdown trade-off: the documented ~11,000% contest return required near-ruin volatility (drawdowns reportedly exceeding 50% intra-contest), making it a perennial lesson that headline returns and survivability are different things. It is widely cited as an argument *for* prudent [[position-sizing|position sizing]], not against it.
- **Education and longevity** — through dozens of books and his I Really Trade platform, Williams has been one of the most prolific popularisers of retail futures trading for over five decades.
