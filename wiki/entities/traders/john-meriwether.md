John Meriwether (born August 10, 1947, Chicago) is the bond trader best known as the founder of [[ltcm|Long-Term Capital Management]] ([[long-term-capital-management|LTCM]]) and, before that, as the head of [[salomon-brothers|Salomon Brothers']] legendary fixed-income arbitrage desk. He is one of the central figures in modern Wall Street history — credited with building the prototype of the quantitative bond [[arbitrage]] business that dominated the 1990s, and infamous for presiding over its most spectacular failure.

## Career

### Salomon Brothers (1974–1991)

Meriwether (Northwestern undergraduate, University of Chicago MBA 1973) joined [[salomon-brothers|Salomon Brothers]] in 1974 and rose to run its fixed-income arbitrage group, where he assembled a team of mathematically-trained traders — many recruited directly from academia — to trade tiny pricing inefficiencies between related bonds. The group became Salomon's most profitable unit and effectively invented the template for [[convergence-arbitrage]].

The mechanics: identify two securities whose price spread *should* be stable because their cash flows or hedging relationships pin them together — the classic example is the **on-the-run vs. off-the-run** Treasury trade, where the most recently auctioned ("on-the-run") bond trades at a small premium for its superior liquidity. Short the rich (on-the-run) bond, buy the cheap (off-the-run) bond of nearly identical maturity, and the position is roughly duration- and rate-neutral: it makes money only when the **liquidity spread converges** as the newer bond ages and loses its premium. Because the spread is tiny (often a few basis points), the trade is run in enormous size with heavy [[leverage]] to produce a meaningful return — turning a near-riskless-looking spread into a respectable yield. The hidden danger, which would later destroy [[long-term-capital-management|LTCM]], is that during a liquidity crisis the spread *widens* instead of converging (everyone wants the liquid bond at once), and the leverage that magnified the small profit now magnifies the loss and triggers margin calls precisely when positions cannot be unwound.

Meriwether is also immortalized in Michael Lewis's *Liar's Poker* as the trader who allegedly answered CEO John Gutfreund's "one hand, one million dollars, no tears" challenge with a counter-offer of $10 million.

Meriwether was made vice chairman of Salomon in 1988 but resigned in **August 1991** in the aftermath of the Treasury auction bidding scandal involving his subordinate Paul Mozer. Though Meriwether was not personally accused of submitting false bids, he had been informed of Mozer's misconduct and his delayed escalation cost him his job and a $50,000 SEC fine.

### Long-Term Capital Management (1994–1998)

In 1994, Meriwether founded [[ltcm|Long-Term Capital Management]] in Greenwich, Connecticut, recruiting much of his old Salomon team along with Nobel-laureates-to-be [[myron-scholes]] and [[robert-merton]]. LTCM raised $1.25 billion in its first year — the largest hedge fund launch in history at the time — and produced exceptional returns from 1994 through 1997 (roughly +43% in 1995 and +41% in 1996, after fees).

The fund's strategy was a scaled-up, more academically rigorous version of the Salomon arb desk's playbook: convergence trades across government bonds, swap spreads, mortgage-backed securities, and equity volatility, levered roughly 25:1 on a balance-sheet basis and far higher through derivatives.

In **August 1998**, Russia's sovereign default triggered a global flight to quality. LTCM's spread trades — diversified on paper but in fact all short liquidity and short volatility — moved against the fund *simultaneously* as historically uncorrelated relationships snapped to correlation 1 under stress. The fund lost $4.6 billion within weeks, and its $125 billion balance sheet (with derivatives notional of more than $1 trillion) threatened systemic damage. The Federal Reserve Bank of New York organized a $3.6 billion rescue by 14 major banks in September 1998 (a coordinated recapitalization, not public money). Roger Lowenstein's *[[when-genius-failed|When Genius Failed]]* (Source: [[book-when-genius-failed]]) is the definitive narrative account; see also [[long-term-capital-management]] for the full chronology.

### JWM Associates (1999–2009) and JM Advisors (2010–)

Meriwether returned to the market with **JWM Associates** in 1999, running similar relative-value strategies on lower leverage; the firm managed roughly $3 billion at its 2007 peak. JWM survived until the 2008–2009 crisis, when its main fund lost roughly 44% between September 2007 and February 2009 and was wound down in July 2009. He launched **JM Advisors Management** in 2010 — once again pursuing fixed-income relative-value trades — proving that, in his own words, the strategies still worked, only the leverage required discipline.

### Current status (as of June 2026)

JM Advisors never regained institutional scale and has faded from public prominence. Meriwether, now in his late 70s, has effectively retired from public fund management; he has made no significant public appearances or launches in the 2024–2026 period and is regarded as a legacy figure whose relevance is historical and instructional.

## Significance

Meriwether's career is a study in how a brilliant trading franchise can be exported, scaled, and ultimately undone by the same leverage that made it profitable. His lasting contribution is the institutionalization of quantitative bond arbitrage as a discipline; his cautionary legacy is the demonstration that historical correlations and Gaussian risk models cannot constrain the size of crisis-driven losses.

## Trading Relevance

For traders, Meriwether's three-act career (Salomon → LTCM → JWM) is the canonical dataset on [[leverage]] and strategy decay: the same [[convergence-arbitrage]] trades produced fortunes at 5:1, systemic collapse at 25:1+, and a second 44% drawdown at "conservative" leverage in 2008 — evidence that crisis correlations, not average-regime spreads, determine survivable position size. LTCM's failure directly shaped modern prime-brokerage margin practice, counterparty risk management, and stress-testing norms.
