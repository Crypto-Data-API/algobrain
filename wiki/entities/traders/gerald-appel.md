Gerald Appel (June 2, 1933 – 2020) was an American investment advisor, money manager, and author best known for inventing the Moving Average Convergence/Divergence ([[macd|MACD]]) indicator in the late 1970s. One of the most widely used technical indicators ever created, MACD subtracts a 26-period EMA from a 12-period EMA, with a 9-period EMA signal line and a histogram, and is now a staple across stocks, forex, futures, and crypto on virtually every charting platform (Source: [[2026-04-20-comprehensive-guide-technical-trading-indicators]]).

## Overview

- Born in New York City; BA from Brooklyn College (1954) and a Master of Social Work from NYU (1956). Unusually for a famous technician, Appel practiced as a **psychoanalyst** in Great Neck, New York (1963–1995) while building his markets career.
- In **1973** he founded both the newsletter ***Systems and Forecasts*** and **Signalert Corporation**, an investment advisory/asset-management firm; Signalert managed over **$550 million** in client assets by his retirement in 2012. The firm continued under his son, Dr. Marvin Appel.
- He invented MACD circa **1977–1979** as a trend-following momentum tool built entirely from exponential moving averages; the 9-period signal-line histogram was added by Thomas Aspray in 1986.
- Author of more than 15 books on markets, including *Technical Analysis: Power Tools for Active Investors* (2005) and *Opportunity Investing* (2006).
- Appel died in 2020 (obituary published in *The New York Times*, Great Neck, NY).

## The MACD: His Key Contribution

- **Construction.** MACD = (12-period [[exponential-moving-average|EMA]] − 26-period EMA). A 9-period EMA of that line is the "signal line," and the difference between the MACD line and signal line is plotted as a histogram. Appel chose 12/26/9 as defaults, and they remain the worldwide standard.
- **What it measures.** It combines [[trend-following]] and [[momentum]] in one tool: the relationship between two moving averages captures trend direction and acceleration, while crossovers and the histogram flag shifts in momentum.
- **Three classic signals.** (1) Signal-line crossovers (MACD crossing above/below its signal line); (2) zero-line crossovers (MACD crossing the centerline, marking the faster EMA crossing the slower); (3) [[divergence]] between MACD and price, often warning of weakening trends.
- **The histogram.** The histogram visualizing the MACD-minus-signal gap was popularized/added by Thomas Aspray in 1986 to give earlier reads on momentum than crossovers alone.
- **Design philosophy.** Appel insisted a useful indicator be simple, mechanical, and derived from price alone — a template most later indicator designers followed.

## Why Appel Matters to Traders

- **MACD is everywhere** — the 12/26/9 defaults Appel chose remain the global standard for trend/momentum confirmation, crossover entries, and histogram [[divergence]] signals in every asset class.
- His core philosophy — that a useful indicator must be simple, mechanical, and derived from price alone — is the design template followed by most modern indicator builders.
- Appel was also an active [[market-timing|market-timing]] practitioner: his published timing models (e.g., bond/equity relative-strength switches in *Power Tools*) are early, fully disclosed examples of systematic tactical asset allocation.

## Influence and Legacy

- **A universal indicator.** MACD ranks among the few technical tools (alongside [[rsi]] and moving averages) that appear by default on essentially every charting platform, and Appel's 12/26/9 parameters are the unquestioned global default.
- **Bridge between trend and momentum.** By packaging trend and momentum into one readable oscillator, MACD shaped how a generation of traders thinks about confirmation and divergence.
- **A disclosure-first newsletter writer.** Through *Systems and Forecasts*, Appel published fully specified, rule-based timing systems decades before "quant" was a common term — an early model of transparent, testable technical research.
- **Limitations.** Like all moving-average-based tools, MACD lags price and can whipsaw in sideways markets; modern practitioners pair it with volatility or trend filters and treat divergence signals as probabilistic, not deterministic.
