Louis Moore Bacon (b. 25 July 1956, Raleigh, North Carolina) is an American [[hedge-fund|hedge fund]] manager and founder of **Moore Capital Management**, one of the defining [[global-macro]] funds of the 1990s–2010s alongside [[george-soros]]'s Quantum, [[paul-tudor-jones]]'s Tudor, and [[bruce-kovner]]'s Caxton. For traders he is a canonical study in [[risk-management]]: he was famous for cutting risk fast, sizing around volatility, and treating capital preservation as the precondition for compounding.

## Career

- **Education**: Middlebury College; MBA, Columbia Business School. Early career trading futures at Shearson/American Express and as a floor trader on the New York Cotton Exchange.
- **1989**: Founded Moore Capital Management in New York (named for his mother's family name, Moore).
- **1990**: Flagship Moore Global Investments returned roughly **86% in its first full year**, driven by shorting the Japanese equity bubble as the Nikkei collapsed and positioning long oil around Iraq's invasion of Kuwait — one of the most famous debut years in macro history.
- **1990s–2000s**: Compounded at roughly 17–21% annualized over three decades (reported figures vary by share class and period), with notably low drawdowns for the volatility of the markets traded — FX, rates, commodities, and equity indices.
- **Peak AUM**: Moore managed in the region of $15B+ at its mid-2000s peak; roughly $8–9B by 2019.

## 2019: Returning Outside Capital

In November 2019, after several years of subdued returns and a fee environment he described as challenging, Bacon announced he would **return outside investor capital** from the three flagship funds — Moore Global Investments, Remington Investment Strategies, and Moore Macro Advisors — ending a 30-year run managing client money. The three funds were consolidated into a single proprietary vehicle running partner and employee capital, effectively converting Moore into a family-office-style operation with internal trading teams. Moore continues to operate in this form as of 2026.

## The Method: Discretionary Global Macro

Bacon traded **[[global-macro]]** — taking directional and relative-value positions across currencies ([[forex]]), interest rates, commodities, and equity indices based on a top-down read of economic and geopolitical regimes. Unlike quant-driven macro shops, his process was *discretionary*: a thesis (a central-bank policy shift, a geopolitical shock, an asset bubble) expressed through whichever instrument offered the best risk/reward.

What distinguished him was not the macro view but the **risk architecture** wrapped around it:

- **Asymmetry first** — he sought trades where the downside was tightly bounded by a stop and the upside was large, accepting many small losses to capture a few large wins (a convex, trend-following payoff profile shared with peers like [[paul-tudor-jones]]).
- **Volatility-based sizing** — position size was scaled to the instrument's volatility so that each bet contributed a comparable amount of risk to the book; a more volatile market got a smaller notional position.
- **Fast, unsentimental risk-cutting** — when a position moved against the thesis or correlations broke, he reduced exposure aggressively rather than "averaging down," treating drawdown control as the precondition for the compounding that follows.
- **Capital preservation as the edge** — by keeping drawdowns shallow relative to the volatility of the markets traded, the fund could stay solvent and psychologically intact through bad stretches, then size up when conviction returned.

His two most-cited trades illustrate the style: shorting the late-1980s **Japanese equity bubble** as the Nikkei rolled over, and going **long oil around Iraq's 1990 invasion of Kuwait** — both large macro dislocations expressed with controlled downside, together producing the ~86% debut year.

## Trading Style and Lessons

- **Global macro with a trader's risk discipline** — Bacon came from the futures pits, not research; his edge was execution and risk control layered on macro themes (see [[macro-relative-value]]).
- **Volatility-based sizing and hard stops** — Moore was known internally for tight drawdown limits per portfolio manager, an approach since institutionalized by multi-manager platforms.
- **The multi-PM precursor** — Moore's structure of allocating to many internal risk-takers under a central risk umbrella prefigured the modern pod-shop model (Millennium, Citadel, Point72).
- **Knowing when to stop** — the 2019 decision to hand back capital rather than grind out fees on diminished edge is frequently cited as a model exit for a discretionary manager.

## Influence and Legacy

- **A pillar of the golden age of macro**: Bacon is grouped with [[george-soros]], [[stanley-druckenmiller]], [[paul-tudor-jones]], and [[bruce-kovner]] as one of the discretionary [[global-macro]] managers who defined the 1990s–2000s, when nimble traders could move large sums across global markets ahead of slower institutions.
- **Risk management as the lesson**: among traders he is studied less for any single call than for *how he sized and cut risk* — a living example of why capital preservation and volatility-aware sizing matter more than being right (a recurring theme in [[risk-management]]).
- **Forerunner of the multi-PM pod shop**: Moore's structure of many internal risk-takers under a central risk-and-capital umbrella prefigured the platform/"pod-shop" model later industrialized by Millennium, Citadel (see [[ken-griffin]]), and Point72.
- **A model exit**: his 2019 decision to return outside capital rather than collect fees on a diminished edge is cited as a disciplined way for a discretionary [[hedge-fund|hedge fund]] manager to wind down client money on his own terms.
