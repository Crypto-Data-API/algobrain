# Richard Dennis

Richard Dennis, known as the "Prince of the Pit," is a legendary [[futures]] trader who turned a borrowed $400 into over $200 million by his early 30s through [[trend-following]] in the Chicago commodity pits. He is best known for the [[turtle-trading|Turtle Trading]] experiment (1983-1988), in which he and partner William Eckhardt recruited and trained 23 novice traders to prove that trading could be taught -- a bet that produced over $100 million in profits and permanently changed the debate about nature versus nurture in trading.

## Early Life and Background

Born in 1949 in Chicago, Illinois, Dennis grew up in a working-class South Side neighborhood. His father worked for the city. Dennis became interested in trading as a teenager, hanging around the commodity exchange floors. At 17, he got a job as a runner at the Chicago Mercantile Exchange (CME), earning $40 per week and absorbing the mechanics of [[futures]] trading by observing floor traders.

After graduating from DePaul University, Dennis scraped together $400 (some accounts say $1,600, including family loans) and began trading mini-contracts at the MidAmerica Commodity Exchange in 1970, at age 21. The floor minimum was higher at the Chicago Board of Trade (CBOT), so he started at the smaller exchange where minimums were lower.

## Career and Key Trades

### From $400 to $200 Million (1970-1983)

Dennis's rise was meteoric. Trading floor commodities -- initially grains like soybeans, wheat, and corn, later expanding to all major [[futures]] markets -- he turned his initial $400 stake into approximately $200 million by 1983. He earned the nickname "Prince of the Pit" for his dominance on the trading floor.

His approach was systematic [[trend-following]] applied with aggressive [[position-sizing]]. He identified major trends in commodity markets and pyramided positions as the trend confirmed, adding to winning trades while cutting losers quickly. His timing coincided with several major commodity bull markets in the 1970s, driven by inflation, the oil crisis, and geopolitical instability.

By his late 20s, Dennis was trading positions so large that he could move markets. He reportedly made $100 million in 1979 alone. His success was driven by a small number of large, sustained trends rather than a high win rate -- consistent with the statistical properties of [[trend-following]] systems.

### The Turtle Trading Experiment (1983-1988)

The trade that cemented Dennis's place in trading history was not a market position but a bet about human nature. Dennis and his partner William Eckhardt had a long-running argument: Dennis believed that trading could be taught to anyone, while Eckhardt believed that successful traders were born with innate abilities. To settle the debate, they designed an experiment.

In 1983 and 1984, Dennis recruited two groups of aspiring traders through newspaper advertisements. From over 1,000 applicants, he selected 23 "Turtles" (named after turtle farms Dennis had visited in Singapore, where he quipped: "We are going to grow traders just like they grow turtles in Singapore"). The trainees came from diverse backgrounds -- a professional blackjack player, a Dungeons & Dragons game designer, an accountant, a pianist, and others with no trading experience.

Dennis gave each Turtle a set of explicit, mechanical trading rules and funded them with real capital (ranging from $500,000 to $2 million per Turtle). The rules were straightforward:

#### The Turtle System Rules

**Entry signals (two systems used in parallel):**
- **System 1**: Buy when price breaks above the 20-day high (or sell short below the 20-day low). This is a classic [[donchian-channel-breakout]] signal. A breakout was skipped if the prior breakout was profitable (to avoid false signals in trending markets)
- **System 2**: Buy when price breaks above the 55-day high (or sell short below the 55-day low). This longer-term system was never skipped

**Exit signals:**
- System 1: Exit longs on a 10-day low, exit shorts on a 10-day high
- System 2: Exit longs on a 20-day low, exit shorts on a 20-day high
- [[stop-loss]]: 2 ATR from the entry point (using [[atr|Average True Range]] as the volatility measure)

**[[position-sizing]] (the ATR-based "Unit" system):**
- Position size = 1% of account equity / ([[atr|ATR]] x Dollar value per point)
- This formula ensured that each position represented approximately 1% risk of total capital
- Maximum units per market: 4 (pyramiding up to 4 entries as the trend confirms)
- Maximum total portfolio risk: capped across all positions and correlated markets

**Markets traded:**
- Diversified across 20+ [[futures]] markets: bonds, [[forex|currencies]], commodities (grains, metals, energy), and stock index futures
- The [[diversification]] ensured exposure to trends in any asset class

#### Turtle Results

The experiment was a resounding success. Over the approximately five years of the program, the Turtles collectively earned over $100 million in profits. Some individual Turtles compiled extraordinary track records -- Curtis Faith, the youngest Turtle (age 19 at selection), reportedly earned over $30 million for Dennis's account. Jerry Parker, another Turtle, went on to found Chesapeake Capital, a successful [[trend-following]] CTA firm.

The experiment proved Dennis's thesis: given a clear, mechanical system with defined rules for entries, exits, and [[position-sizing]], ordinary people could become successful traders. The critical ingredients were the system's edge (capturing major trends), proper [[risk-management]] (the ATR-based position sizing), and the discipline to follow the rules without deviation.

### Later Career and Losses

Despite his extraordinary early success, Dennis's later career was turbulent. In the late 1980s, he suffered significant losses -- reportedly losing approximately $10 million of his personal capital and over $50 million of investor funds in 1988. He partially attributed these losses to deviating from his own system and overleveraging.

Dennis closed his fund to outside investors in 1988 but continued to trade his own money. He later returned to the markets and reportedly made significant profits in the 1990s and 2000s, though with less public visibility than in his earlier career.

His experience illustrates a critical lesson: even the creator of a proven system can fail by not following it. The Turtles who succeeded were those who followed the rules mechanically; Dennis's own later losses came, at least in part, from the very discretionary overrides he had designed the system to avoid.

## Trading Philosophy

Dennis's philosophy, informed by his own experience and the Turtle experiment, centers on several core principles:

1. **Trading can be taught.** This is Dennis's most famous conviction. Given a system with a statistical edge, clear rules, and proper [[risk-management]], the specific individual executing the system is largely irrelevant. "I always say that you could publish trading rules in the newspaper and no one would follow them. The key is consistency and discipline" (Source: [[book-market-wizards]]).

2. **Follow the [[trend]].** Dennis was a lifelong [[trend-following|trend follower]]. "I learned that you have to try to trade with the trend. You can't predict it, but you can follow it." He never tried to pick tops or bottoms -- the system waited for a breakout to confirm the trend before entering.

3. **Cut losses, ride winners.** The asymmetric payoff structure of [[trend-following]] means that most trades are small losers. Profitability comes from the small percentage of trades that become large winners. The system must be designed to protect the portfolio during the frequent losing trades while maximizing the capture of the rare winning trends.

4. **[[position-sizing]] is everything.** Dennis considered the ATR-based [[position-sizing]] system to be the most important component of the Turtle rules -- more important than the entry signals. Proper position sizing transforms a mediocre system into a robust one and transforms a good system into a great one.

5. **[[diversification]] across markets.** Trends can emerge in any market. By trading 20+ [[futures]] markets simultaneously, the system maximized the probability of catching at least some significant trends at any given time.

6. **Mechanical discipline defeats human psychology.** The Turtle experiment showed that the greatest threat to a trading system's performance is the human tendency to override it. Fear, greed, boredom, and the desire to be "right" all conspire against systematic execution. Dennis designed the rules to be as unambiguous as possible to minimize room for [[trading-psychology|psychological]] interference.

## Legacy

Richard Dennis's legacy rests on two achievements. First, his personal track record -- turning $400 into $200 million through [[trend-following]] -- demonstrated that systematic approaches to trading could generate enormous returns. Second, and more importantly, the Turtle experiment proved that trading success could be taught and codified into rules, challenging the prevailing belief that successful traders were born, not made.

The Turtle system and its variants continue to be studied, backtested, and traded decades later. Many successful [[trend-following]] CTA firms trace their intellectual lineage directly to Dennis and the Turtles. Curtis Faith's book *Way of the Turtle* and Michael Covel's *The Complete TurtleTrader* have brought the system to a broader audience. The strategy page [[turtle-trading]] provides full details on the system rules and their modern adaptations.

Dennis's later losses serve as an equally important lesson: a system only works if you follow it. The discipline to execute mechanically -- to take every signal, accept every loss, and not override the rules during drawdowns -- is the hardest part of systematic trading, and it is the part that no rule book can guarantee.
