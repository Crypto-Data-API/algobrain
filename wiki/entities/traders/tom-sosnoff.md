Tom Sosnoff is an American entrepreneur, options trader, and financial media personality who has had an outsized influence on how retail traders approach [[options]] selling strategies. He co-founded the thinkorswim trading platform (sold to TD Ameritrade in 2009 in a deal announced at roughly $606 million, often cited at up to $750 million including stock appreciation at closing), created the tastytrade media network (now tastylive), and launched the tastyworks brokerage (now tastytrade brokerage). His core philosophy -- that selling [[options]] premium systematically with defined risk is the highest-probability approach for retail traders -- has shaped an entire generation of options traders. In September 2025 he stepped down after 15 years leading tastylive, remaining a shareholder and consultant while launching a new AI venture, Lossdog.

## Background and Career

Sosnoff began his career as a floor trader at the [[cboe|Chicago Board Options Exchange (CBOE)]] in the 1980s, where he traded equity options and developed an intuitive understanding of [[implied-volatility]], probability, and [[theta]] decay. This floor experience -- seeing firsthand how options are priced and how [[greeks]] behave in real time -- underpins his teaching methodology.

### Thinkorswim (1999-2009)
Sosnoff co-founded thinkorswim in 1999 with Scott Sheridan. The platform became known for its advanced options analytics, probability-based tools, and multi-leg order execution -- features that were previously available only to professional traders. Thinkorswim democratized sophisticated options trading for retail investors. TD Ameritrade acquired the platform in 2009 for approximately $750 million, and it remains one of the most widely used options trading platforms (now integrated into Charles Schwab).

### Tastytrade / Tastylive (2011-present)
After the thinkorswim sale, Sosnoff launched tastytrade in 2011 as a financial media network focused on options trading education. The network broadcasts live programming daily, covering trade ideas, market analysis, [[greeks]] management, and backtested strategy research. The brand rebranded to tastylive in 2023. Key shows include "Market Measures" (strategy research with backtested data) and "Closing the Gap" (educational series).

### Tastyworks / Tastytrade Brokerage (2017-present)
Sosnoff launched tastyworks in 2017 as a brokerage built specifically for options and futures traders, with low commissions ($1/contract, capped at $10/leg) and a platform optimized for multi-leg options strategies. IG Group acquired tastytrade in a deal announced as a $1 billion partnership ($300 million cash plus 61 million new IG ordinary shares), completed in June 2021. In 2023 the brokerage rebranded from tastyworks to tastytrade and the media network rebranded from tastytrade to tastylive. Sosnoff continued to lead the tasty businesses post-acquisition.

### Departure from tastylive and Lossdog (2025-present)
In September 2025, Sosnoff stepped down after 15 years as founder and CEO of tastylive, saying he would remain a shareholder and consultant. Daily tastylive programming continued with the existing on-air talent (including long-time co-host Tony Battista). After leaving, Sosnoff announced Lossdog, an AI-driven platform for negotiating compensation, where he serves as founder and CEO.

## Trading Philosophy

Sosnoff's approach to options trading is distinctive and internally consistent:

### Core Principles
1. **Sell premium, do not buy it:** Options decay over time ([[theta]]). Sellers collect this decay as income. Over large samples, selling options has a statistical edge because [[implied-volatility]] historically overstates realized volatility by ~2-4 vol points on average
2. **High probability of profit:** Enter trades with 65-85% probability of profit (selling options at 1-2 standard deviations OTM). Accept smaller per-trade profits in exchange for high win rates
3. **Defined risk:** Use spreads ([[credit-spread|credit spreads]], [[iron-condor|iron condors]], [[iron-butterfly|iron butterflies]]) rather than naked options to cap maximum loss. This allows consistent position sizing and portfolio management
4. **Mechanical rules:** Enter at 45 DTE (days to expiration), manage at 21 DTE or 50% of max profit (whichever comes first), take losses at 2x credit received. Removes emotional decision-making
5. **Small position size:** No single trade should represent more than 1-5% of portfolio. Spread risk across 10-20+ positions at any time
6. **Trade often:** More occurrences = more reversion to expected probabilities. A single [[iron-condor]] might lose; 100 iron condors over a year will approximate the theoretical win rate
7. **Sell when IV is high:** Enter premium-selling trades when [[implied-volatility]] is elevated (IV rank > 50%). This increases credit received, widens breakevens, and captures [[vega]] contraction as IV reverts to mean

### Preferred Strategies
- [[credit-spread|Credit spreads]] (bull put, bear call) -- the building block
- [[iron-condor|Iron condors]] -- the flagship neutral strategy
- [[iron-butterfly|Iron butterflies]] -- higher credit, tighter range alternative
- Strangles (undefined risk) -- for experienced traders in high-IV environments
- [[covered-call|Covered calls]] -- for equity holders seeking income

## Influence on Retail Options Trading

Sosnoff's impact on retail options trading is difficult to overstate:

- **Probability-based framework:** Before tastytrade, most retail options education focused on directional bets. Sosnoff popularized the idea that options are probability instruments and should be traded as such
- **Backtested research:** The tastytrade team publishes extensive backtested studies on strategy performance across different market conditions, strike selections, and management techniques -- bringing institutional-quality research to retail
- **Community building:** The tastytrade community (hundreds of thousands of active traders) shares trade ideas, management techniques, and real-time performance, creating a collaborative learning environment
- **Platform innovation:** Both thinkorswim and tastyworks lowered the technical and cost barriers to multi-leg options trading

## Criticism

- **Survivorship bias in backtests:** Some critics argue that tastytrade's backtests focus on favorable time periods or do not fully account for transaction costs and slippage
- **Tail risk underestimation:** Systematic premium selling strategies are vulnerable to large, sudden moves (e.g., COVID crash of March 2020). Defined-risk strategies mitigate but do not eliminate this risk
- **Overemphasis on probability:** Win rate alone does not determine profitability -- expected value (win rate x average win - loss rate x average loss) matters more, and some tastytrade strategies have asymmetric payoffs that favor the long side in certain regimes
