# Bill Ackman

William Albert Ackman (born May 11, 1966) is an American billionaire investor, hedge fund manager, and the founder and CEO of Pershing Square Capital Management, a concentrated activist hedge fund based in New York City. Ackman is known for taking enormous, publicly declared positions -- both long and short -- and then aggressively advocating for his thesis through media appearances, investor presentations, and direct corporate engagement. His career is defined by spectacular wins and equally spectacular losses, including a $2.6 billion profit on a $27 million [[options|credit hedge]] in 30 days during the COVID-19 crash and a $1 billion loss on a six-year [[short-selling|short]] battle against Herbalife. With assets under management peaking above $18 billion, Ackman represents the modern archetype of the celebrity activist investor -- part [[fundamental-analysis|fundamental analyst]], part media strategist, part corporate activist.

## Early Life and Background

Bill Ackman was born on May 11, 1966, in Chappaqua, New York, an affluent suburb in Westchester County. His father, Lawrence Ackman, was the chairman of a New York real estate financing firm, Ackman-Ziff Real Estate Group. Growing up in a family steeped in real estate and finance, Ackman developed an early interest in investing and business.

Ackman attended Harvard College, graduating magna cum laude in 1988, and then Harvard Business School, earning his MBA in 1992. His academic pedigree and family connections gave him access to the world of institutional investing, but it was his intensity, public charisma, and willingness to make bold, concentrated bets that would define his career.

After business school, Ackman co-founded his first hedge fund, Gotham Partners, in 1992 with fellow Harvard Business School graduate David Berkowitz. Gotham focused on [[value-investing]]-style investments in publicly traded companies. The fund performed well initially, but ran into trouble in the early 2000s when illiquid investments and investor redemptions forced a painful unwinding. Gotham Partners was dissolved in 2003 -- an experience that profoundly shaped Ackman's later emphasis on [[liquidity]] and transparency.

## Career and Key Trades

### Pershing Square Capital Management (2004-Present)

After the Gotham Partners dissolution, Ackman founded Pershing Square Capital Management in January 2004 with approximately $54 million in initial capital. He built the new fund around a concentrated portfolio philosophy: typically holding 6-12 large positions, each representing a significant percentage of the portfolio. This concentration was deliberate -- Ackman believed that true conviction investing required large positions, and that [[diversification]] beyond a handful of best ideas diluted returns.

Pershing Square's AUM grew to over $18 billion at its peak, driven by several spectacularly successful trades and Ackman's high public profile.

### MBIA Short (2002-2008)

Ackman's first major public campaign began while he was still at Gotham Partners. Starting in 2002, he built a substantial [[short-selling|short position]] against MBIA, one of the largest bond insurance companies in the United States. Ackman's thesis was that MBIA was massively undercapitalized relative to its potential liabilities -- that the company was insuring structured finance products (including [[subprime-mortgage]]-backed securities) without adequate reserves to cover potential losses.

For six years, Ackman publicly presented his case, publishing detailed research reports and engaging with regulators. He was ridiculed by much of Wall Street and fought publicly with MBIA's management and with [[credit-rating-agencies]] (Moody's and S&P) that maintained MBIA's AAA rating. When the [[2008-global-financial-crisis]] struck and MBIA's insured products defaulted en masse, the company's stock collapsed from ~$70 to under $5. Ackman's short position generated hundreds of millions in profits, and his long-running thesis was vindicated.

The MBIA trade established Ackman's reputation as a relentless, forensically detailed short seller willing to fight for years against consensus.

### General Growth Properties (2008-2010)

During the 2008 financial crisis, Ackman identified General Growth Properties (GGP), the second-largest mall operator in the US, as massively undervalued. GGP's stock had fallen from $60 to under $1 as the company faced bankruptcy due to its inability to refinance its debt in the frozen credit markets. Ackman's [[fundamental-analysis]] concluded that GGP's underlying real estate assets were worth far more than the market was pricing -- the problem was purely a liquidity/refinancing issue, not an insolvency issue.

Pershing Square bought approximately 25% of GGP's outstanding shares at an average price around $1. When GGP successfully reorganized in bankruptcy and relisted, the stock recovered dramatically. Ackman's investment of roughly $60 million grew to approximately $1.6 billion -- a return of over 2,500%.

### Herbalife Short (2012-2018)

In December 2012, Ackman publicly announced a $1 billion short position in Herbalife, declaring the multi-level marketing company a "pyramid scheme." He presented a 342-slide presentation laying out his thesis and pledged to "take it to the end of the earth."

The Herbalife short became the most public and personal hedge fund battle of the decade:

- **[[carl-icahn]] goes long**: Carl Icahn took a massive long position in Herbalife directly to oppose Ackman. In a legendary January 2013 CNBC confrontation, the two billionaires argued on live television for nearly 30 minutes, with Icahn calling Ackman "a crybaby" and Ackman accusing Icahn of market manipulation
- **The stock refused to collapse**: Despite Ackman's campaign -- which included lobbying the [[sec|SEC]] and FTC to investigate Herbalife -- the stock remained resilient. The FTC did fine Herbalife $200 million in 2016 but stopped short of calling it a pyramid scheme
- **Ackman exits at a loss**: In February 2018, Ackman finally closed his Herbalife position at a loss of approximately **$1 billion**. Icahn's long position, by contrast, was significantly profitable

The Herbalife saga illustrated both the risks of [[short-selling]] (unlimited downside, the need for the market to agree with your timeline) and the dangers of making a trade into a public crusade where ego becomes entangled with the investment thesis (Source: [[book-more-money-than-god]]).

### Valeant Pharmaceuticals (2015-2017)

Ackman built a position in Valeant Pharmaceuticals that grew to approximately $4 billion. Valeant's business model -- acquiring pharmaceutical companies and dramatically raising drug prices -- attracted controversy and eventually regulatory scrutiny. When Valeant's accounting practices were questioned and its drug pricing strategy became a political target, the stock collapsed from $250 to under $15. Ackman sold in March 2017, crystallizing losses estimated at approximately **$4 billion** -- one of the largest single investment losses in hedge fund history.

The Valeant loss nearly destroyed Pershing Square's reputation and caused AUM to drop from $18 billion to roughly $8 billion as investors withdrew.

### COVID-19 Hedge and Recovery Trade (February-March 2020)

> **Full case study:** [[2020-03-ackman-pandemic-cds-trade]] documents the trade mechanics, timeline, and repeatable framework. The strategy generalization is at [[convex-tail-hedge-arbitrage]]; the broader pattern context is at [[fastest-profitable-trades]].

Ackman's single greatest trade came during the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic. In February 2020, as the virus spread beyond China but before markets crashed, Ackman spent approximately **$27 million** purchasing credit protection (credit default swaps on investment-grade and high-yield corporate bond indices). He essentially bought insurance against a market crash.

When markets plunged in late February and March, Ackman's credit hedge exploded in value. He closed the position in mid-March for approximately **$2.6 billion** -- a roughly **100x return** in approximately 30 days. This trade has been called one of the greatest single trades in the history of financial markets.

But Ackman did not stop there. He immediately reinvested the proceeds -- and then some -- into discounted equities during the March 2020 crash, buying shares of Hilton, Lowe's, Starbucks, and other high-quality companies at crisis prices. These positions then appreciated dramatically during the subsequent market recovery, compounding his gains.

The COVID trade demonstrated the full Ackman playbook: identify a macro asymmetry, express it with limited downside (the $27 million premium), and then redeploy the winnings into deep [[value-investing|value]] opportunities.

## Trading and Investment Philosophy

Ackman's philosophy combines several distinctive elements:

1. **Concentrated conviction**: Ackman typically holds 6-12 positions, with each position representing 5-25% of the portfolio. He believes that [[diversification]] beyond a small number of best ideas is "de-worsification" (borrowing [[peter-lynch]]'s term). This approach amplifies both gains and losses.

2. **Buy a fraction of the business, think like you're buying the whole thing**: Ackman's central maxim is: "Investing is buying a fraction of a business, and you should think about it as buying the whole thing." This means conducting [[fundamental-analysis]] deep enough to understand the business as if you were acquiring 100% of it.

3. **Activist engagement**: Unlike passive investors, Ackman frequently engages with management, pushes for board seats, and publicly campaigns for strategic changes (spin-offs, management replacements, capital allocation changes). This [[value-investing|activist value investing]] approach is similar to [[carl-icahn]]'s but with a more public, media-savvy execution.

4. **Asymmetric bets**: The COVID trade exemplified Ackman's search for asymmetric risk/reward -- situations where the downside is limited and defined (the cost of the hedge) but the upside is potentially enormous. This connects directly to [[risk-management]] and the [[options|optionality]] concept.

5. **Public transparency**: Unlike most hedge fund managers who operate in secrecy, Ackman frequently presents his thesis publicly -- through conference presentations, media appearances, and social media (especially X/Twitter). This transparency is both a strategic tool (it can move stock prices and attract allies) and a risk (it invites opponents and creates accountability).

6. **Resilience after catastrophic losses**: Ackman's career demonstrates extraordinary psychological resilience. After the ~$4 billion Valeant loss and the $1 billion Herbalife loss, he rebuilt Pershing Square through the COVID trade and subsequent investments. This resilience connects to [[trading-psychology]] and the importance of surviving drawdowns.

## Strategy Connections

Ackman's approach connects to several core wiki concepts and strategies:

- **[[fundamental-analysis]]** -- Every Ackman position begins with exhaustive [[fundamental-analysis]], often including proprietary models of the target company's intrinsic value, forensic accounting review, and detailed competitive analysis
- **[[short-selling]]** -- Ackman is one of the most prominent public [[short-selling|short sellers]] of his generation, with the MBIA short being his most successful and the Herbalife short his most painful
- **[[value-investing]]** -- At his core, Ackman is a [[value-investing|value investor]] who buys businesses at a discount to intrinsic value, but with an activist twist -- he creates catalysts rather than waiting for them. See: [[value-investing-strategy]]
- **[[risk-management]]** -- The COVID hedge ($27 million premium for $2.6 billion return) is a masterclass in [[risk-management]] through [[options|asymmetric payoffs]]. Conversely, the Valeant and Herbalife losses demonstrate the [[risk-management]] failures of excessive concentration and ego-driven holding
- **[[behavioral-finance]]** -- Ackman's public campaigns create self-reinforcing narratives (both positive and negative), and his Herbalife battle showed how [[behavioral-finance|cognitive biases]] (commitment bias, sunk-cost fallacy) can trap even sophisticated investors
- **[[trading-psychology]]** -- Ackman's ability to recover from catastrophic losses and immediately deploy capital into the March 2020 crash is a study in psychological resilience and emotional discipline
