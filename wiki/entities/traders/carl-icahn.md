# Carl Icahn

Carl Celian Icahn (born February 16, 1936) is an American financier, activist investor, and the founder of Icahn Enterprises, a diversified conglomerate and holding company. He is widely regarded as the pioneer of activist investing -- the practice of acquiring significant stakes in publicly traded companies and then agitating for changes (board seats, management replacements, share buybacks, spin-offs, mergers) designed to unlock shareholder value. His net worth peaked above $20 billion, built almost entirely through hostile takeovers, activist campaigns, and opportunistic trading over a career spanning more than five decades. Often called "The Corporate Raider," Icahn's approach blurs the line between [[value-investing]] and corporate governance activism.

## Early Life and Background

Carl Icahn was born on February 16, 1936, in Queens, New York City, to a middle-class Jewish family. His father was a cantor and his mother was a schoolteacher. Despite modest family means, Icahn excelled academically. He graduated from Far Rockaway High School (which also produced physicist Richard Feynman) and went on to Princeton University, where he studied philosophy and graduated in 1957. He briefly attended New York University School of Medicine before dropping out, recognizing that his interests lay in finance rather than medicine.

Icahn entered Wall Street in 1961 as a stockbroker at Dreyfus & Company, later moving to several other firms. In 1968, he formed Icahn & Co., a securities firm focused on [[short-selling|options arbitrage]] and risk arbitrage. This early training in derivatives and arbitrage gave him a sophisticated understanding of how to structure complex financial transactions -- skills he would deploy in his later career as a corporate raider.

## Career and Key Campaigns

### Early Takeover Career (1978-1985)

Icahn's transition from options trader to corporate activist began in the late 1970s when he recognized that many public companies were trading well below the liquidation value of their assets. Rather than simply buying undervalued stocks and waiting (the [[benjamin-graham]] approach), Icahn developed a more aggressive strategy: buy a controlling or influential stake, then force changes that would unlock the hidden value.

His early targets included smaller companies where he could acquire enough shares to win board seats or force mergers. These early campaigns established his reputation as a formidable opponent of entrenched management teams.

### Trans World Airlines / TWA (1985-1993)

Icahn's most famous (and most controversial) early campaign was his hostile takeover of Trans World Airlines (TWA) in 1985. He acquired TWA in a leveraged buyout, took the airline private, and proceeded to strip its assets to pay down acquisition debt:

- Purchased TWA in 1985 through a hostile takeover valued at approximately $469 million
- Sold TWA's prized London routes to American Airlines for $445 million in 1991 to service debt
- The airline eventually filed for bankruptcy in 1992, with Icahn personally profiting an estimated $469 million from the deal while the airline was left financially gutted
- Critics argue the TWA deal exemplified the worst excesses of 1980s corporate raiding; defenders argue Icahn unlocked value from a poorly managed company that was heading for bankruptcy regardless

### Major Activist Campaigns

Icahn's career is defined by dozens of high-profile activist campaigns against some of America's largest companies:

- **RJR Nabisco (1988-1989)**: Involved in the bidding war for RJR Nabisco alongside KKR and other leveraged buyout firms during what became the largest takeover in history at the time
- **Texaco (1988)**: Acquired a significant stake and agitated for management changes after the company filed for bankruptcy due to a legal dispute with Pennzoil
- **Netflix (2012-2015)**: Bought shares at approximately $58 per share in late 2012, accumulated a ~10% stake, and sold his position at an average price of roughly $340 per share -- generating approximately $2 billion in profit in roughly three years. One of his most successful trades ever
- **Apple (2013-2016)**: Acquired a multi-billion dollar stake and publicly pressured CEO Tim Cook to return more cash to shareholders through [[share-buybacks]] and dividends. Apple ultimately increased its buyback program by hundreds of billions. Icahn sold his Apple stake in 2016, citing concerns about China risk, earning an estimated $2 billion in profits
- **Herbalife (2013-2018)**: Took a large long position in Herbalife, directly opposing Bill Ackman's $1 billion short position. The feud became one of Wall Street's most public and personal battles. The two billionaires argued on live television (CNBC, January 2013) in what became iconic financial media. Icahn's long bet ultimately proved correct as the stock rose significantly, while Ackman eventually closed his short at a loss of approximately $1 billion
- **eBay (2014)**: Pushed successfully for eBay to spin off PayPal as a separate public company, unlocking significant value for shareholders
- **Xerox (2016-2018)**: Acquired a ~10% stake and pushed for cost cuts, mergers, and management changes

### Icahn Enterprises

Icahn Enterprises L.P. (NASDAQ: IEP) is the publicly traded holding company through which Icahn manages his conglomerate of investments. It holds positions across energy, automotive, food packaging, real estate, home fashion, and metals -- reflecting Icahn's willingness to invest in unglamorous industries where value can be unlocked through operational improvements or financial restructuring.

## Investment Philosophy

Icahn's approach is distinctive in that it combines [[value-investing]] identification of undervalued assets with active intervention to realize that value:

1. **Identify Undervalued Companies with Catalysts You Can Create**: Unlike passive [[value-investing|value investors]] who buy cheap stocks and wait for the market to recognize their worth, Icahn buys cheap stocks and then creates the catalysts himself -- through board fights, management changes, strategic transactions, or public pressure campaigns. This eliminates the "value trap" problem that plagues traditional value investors

2. **The "Icahn Lift"**: Stock prices frequently rise 5-15% simply on news that Icahn has acquired a position in a company. This phenomenon -- the market's anticipation that Icahn will force value-unlocking changes -- provides a built-in margin of safety. If the activist campaign succeeds, further upside follows; if it fails, the initial "Icahn Lift" often cushions the downside

3. **Corporate Governance Is Alpha**: Icahn believes that poor corporate governance -- overpaid executives, entrenched boards, wasteful spending, empire-building acquisitions -- is the single largest source of value destruction in corporate America. By confronting these governance failures, activist investors perform a market function analogous to the predator in an ecosystem: culling the weak

4. **Leverage Is a Tool, Not a Risk**: Throughout his career, Icahn has used leverage (borrowed money) aggressively to acquire companies and fund campaigns. His background in [[short-selling|options]] and arbitrage gave him comfort with leveraged positions that would terrify most investors. However, the TWA experience and subsequent campaigns show that leverage can amplify both gains and losses

5. **Diversify Across Campaigns**: While each individual activist campaign is a concentrated bet, Icahn typically runs multiple campaigns simultaneously across different industries, providing portfolio-level [[diversification]] even as each position is high-conviction

6. **Short Selling as Part of the Toolkit**: Icahn is a prolific [[short-selling|short seller]], willing to bet against companies he views as overvalued or fraudulent. His long Herbalife / Ackman's short Herbalife feud demonstrated that [[short-selling]] disputes can become as much about ego and public positioning as about fundamentals

7. **Patience and Aggression in Equal Measure**: "In this business, if you're good, you're right six times out of ten. You're never going to be right nine times out of ten." Icahn accepts a meaningful failure rate, relying on his winners being large enough to more than compensate for losses -- an asymmetric return philosophy similar to [[peter-lynch]]'s tenbagger concept

## Strategy Connections

Icahn's approach connects to several core wiki concepts and strategies:

- **[[fundamental-analysis]]** -- Every Icahn campaign begins with [[fundamental-analysis]] identifying a company trading below intrinsic value, typically due to poor management, inefficient capital allocation, or underappreciated assets
- **[[value-investing]]** -- Icahn is a [[value-investing|value investor]] at his core, but an activist one -- he creates catalysts rather than waiting for the market to self-correct. See: [[value-investing-strategy]]
- **[[short-selling]]** -- Icahn uses [[short-selling]] both as a standalone strategy (betting against overvalued companies) and as a hedge within his broader portfolio. See also: [[short-interest]]
- **[[risk-management]]** -- While Icahn is often perceived as aggressive, his use of diversified campaigns, leveraged structures, and hedging via [[options]] reflects sophisticated [[risk-management]]. His willingness to exit positions (selling Apple, selling Netflix at 6x his entry) shows discipline in taking profits
- **[[behavioral-finance]]** -- The "Icahn Lift" is itself a behavioral phenomenon -- the market's Pavlovian response to Icahn's involvement demonstrates how [[sentiment]] and reputation influence stock prices independently of fundamentals
