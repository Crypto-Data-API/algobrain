# Peter Lynch

Peter Lynch is one of the most successful mutual fund managers in history, best known for running the Fidelity Magellan Fund from 1977 to 1990. During his 13-year tenure, he grew the fund from $18 million in assets to over $14 billion, delivering an average annual return of 29.2% -- the best track record of any mutual fund manager ever recorded. His philosophy of "buy what you know" democratized [[fundamental-analysis]] for individual investors and made him one of the most widely read voices in personal investing.

## Early Life and Background

Born on January 19, 1944, in Newton, Massachusetts, Lynch grew up in a middle-class family. His father died when Lynch was ten, forcing his mother to work and Lynch to caddy at a local golf course -- an experience that gave him early exposure to the investment thinking of the businessmen he caddied for. He attended Boston College on a caddy scholarship, graduating in 1965, and earned his MBA from the Wharton School at the University of Pennsylvania in 1968.

Lynch joined Fidelity Investments as a summer intern in 1966 while still at Wharton, initially covering the textiles and metals industries. After serving in the Army from 1967 to 1969, he returned to Fidelity full-time as a research analyst, working his way up through coverage of multiple sectors before being named head of the Magellan Fund in May 1977.

## Career and Key Trades

### The Magellan Fund Era (1977-1990)

When Lynch took over [[index-funds|Magellan]] in 1977, it was a small fund with approximately $18 million in assets under management. Over the next 13 years, he transformed it into the largest mutual fund in the world:

- **1977-1983**: Built the fund from $18M to $1.6B through aggressive stock-picking in an environment where many investors feared equities after the brutal 1973-74 bear market
- **1983-1987**: Grew AUM to over $10B, with the fund consistently outperforming the [[s-and-p-500]]. His broad approach -- sometimes holding 1,400+ positions -- was unique among fund managers
- **1987 crash**: The fund lost 11% on Black Monday (October 19, 1987), but Lynch stayed the course and the fund recovered within two years
- **1988-1990**: Continued outperformance, with AUM exceeding $14B by his retirement in May 1990

### Notable Stock Picks

Lynch's most famous picks demonstrated his "buy what you know" philosophy in action:

- **Dunkin' Donuts**: Lynch noticed the coffee was consistently good and the stores were always busy. Bought the stock and rode a multi-year appreciation
- **Taco Bell**: Enjoyed the food while traveling, researched the company, and invested before it was acquired by PepsiCo
- **Hanes (L'eggs)**: His wife's enthusiasm for the L'eggs pantyhose brand led him to investigate the parent company -- a classic example of consumer-driven research
- **Ford Motor Company**: Bought Ford in the early 1980s when the stock was deeply undervalued and the balance sheet showed massive hidden value
- **Fannie Mae**: One of his largest positions, based on analysis of the company's [[earnings-momentum]] trajectory and dominant market position

### Retirement and Legacy

Lynch retired from Magellan on May 31, 1990, at the age of 46. He famously cited work-life balance concerns, stating: "Nobody on their deathbed says, 'I wish I'd spent more time at the office.'" After retirement, he continued as a vice chairman at Fidelity and devoted significant time to philanthropy, particularly in Catholic education and medical research. He also wrote three bestselling books that became essential reading for individual investors.

## Investment Philosophy

Lynch's approach combined rigorous [[fundamental-analysis]] with an intuitive, everyman sensibility that made his methods accessible to non-professionals. His core principles:

1. **"Buy What You Know"**: Lynch believed individual investors have an inherent advantage over Wall Street professionals because they encounter potential investments in their daily lives -- at the mall, at work, in their hobbies. If a consumer product is exceptional, the company behind it may be worth investigating (Source: [[book-one-up-on-wall-street]])

2. **The PEG Ratio**: Lynch popularized the price/earnings-to-growth ratio (PEG = [[fundamental-analysis|P/E ratio]] divided by [[earnings-momentum|earnings growth rate]]). A PEG below 1.0 suggests a stock is undervalued relative to its growth; above 2.0 suggests overvaluation. This became one of the most widely used [[value-investing]] metrics

3. **Six Stock Categories**: Lynch classified every stock into one of six categories, each requiring a different investment approach:
   - **Slow Growers**: Large, mature companies growing at 2-4% annually (utilities). Buy for dividends, not appreciation
   - **Stalwarts**: Solid companies growing at 10-12% (Coca-Cola, Procter & Gamble). Expect 30-50% gains, then rotate
   - **Fast Growers**: Small, aggressive companies growing at 20-25%+ annually. The source of "tenbaggers" -- but require careful monitoring
   - **Cyclicals**: Companies whose earnings rise and fall with the economic cycle (auto, steel, airlines). Timing matters more than with other categories
   - **Turnarounds**: Depressed companies with potential for recovery (Chrysler in 1982). High risk, high reward
   - **Asset Plays**: Companies sitting on valuable assets the market has not recognized (real estate, patents, cash)

4. **The Tenbagger Concept**: Lynch coined the term "tenbagger" -- a stock that returns 10x the original investment. He argued that a single tenbagger in a portfolio of many stocks can make up for numerous small losses, which justified his approach of broad [[diversification]] combined with high-conviction [[growth-investing]] picks

5. **Do Your Homework**: Despite the "buy what you know" mantra, Lynch insisted that consumer familiarity was only the starting point. Investors must then analyze financial statements, understand the competitive landscape, check the debt levels, and calculate whether the stock price makes sense relative to [[earnings-momentum]] and growth prospects

6. **Avoid Market Timing**: Lynch was emphatic that trying to predict [[market-breadth|market direction]] was futile. He stated: "Far more money has been lost by investors preparing for corrections, or trying to anticipate corrections, than has been lost in corrections themselves"

7. **Invest for the Long Term**: Lynch argued that stocks are the best long-term investment vehicle, outperforming [[bond-market|bonds]], gold, and cash over every 20-year period in modern history. He encouraged investors to stay invested through [[volatility]] rather than flee to safety

## Strategy Connections

Lynch's methodology connects to several core wiki concepts and strategies:

- **[[fundamental-analysis]]** -- Lynch was a bottom-up fundamental analyst at his core, relying on earnings, balance sheets, and competitive positioning rather than charts or macroeconomics
- **[[growth-investing]]** -- His focus on fast growers and tenbaggers places him squarely in the [[growth-investing]] tradition, though he blended this with [[value-investing]] through the PEG ratio
- **[[earnings-momentum]]** -- The PEG ratio and his focus on accelerating earnings growth connect directly to [[earnings-momentum]] strategies
- **[[sector-rotation]]** -- Lynch's six-category framework (especially his handling of cyclicals and stalwarts) is a form of [[sector-rotation]], adjusting exposure based on where each company and industry sits in its cycle
- **[[value-investing]]** -- While primarily a growth investor, Lynch insisted on buying at reasonable prices, bridging the gap between [[value-investing]] and [[growth-investing]] philosophies. See also: [[value-vs-growth-investing]]
- **[[diversification]]** -- His willingness to hold 1,400+ stocks was extreme diversification by any standard, ensuring that a few big winners could drive overall returns even with many mediocre picks
- **[[position-sizing]]** -- Despite broad diversification, Lynch concentrated heavily in his highest-conviction ideas, sometimes allocating 5%+ of the fund to a single position
