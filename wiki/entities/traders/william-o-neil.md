# William O'Neil

William J. O'Neil (March 25, 1933 - May 28, 2023) was an American entrepreneur, stockbroker, and author who created the [[canslim|CANSLIM]] method — a systematic approach to growth-stock selection — and founded Investor's Business Daily (IBD). His work fused [[fundamental-analysis|fundamental]] and [[technical-analysis-overview|technical analysis]] into a single hybrid framework: buy fundamentally superior companies, but only when the chart confirms institutional demand. The method, the chart patterns he named (most famously the [[cup-with-handle|cup-with-handle]]), and the proprietary ratings he invented for IBD remain core vocabulary for active growth and [[momentum-trading|momentum]] traders. He died at his home of natural causes at age 90.

## Background

Born in 1933 in Oklahoma City and raised in Texas, O'Neil served in the U.S. Air Force, graduated from Southern Methodist University, and began his career as a stockbroker at Hayden, Stone & Co. in 1958. The formative work of his life was an empirical study of the biggest winning stocks of the past — eventually a database running back to the 1880s — to isolate the *common characteristics* that recurred just before a stock launched its largest advance. That research, rather than any single theory, became the data foundation of [[canslim|CANSLIM]] and of the model book studies IBD still publishes.

In 1963, at age 30, he founded William O'Neil + Co., an institutional equity-research firm, and became the youngest member at the time to buy a seat on the NYSE. The firm built one of the first computerized stock databases, daily-updated and graphically printed — the ancestor of the now-iconic IBD "Daily Graphs" / MarketSmith charts. In 1984 he launched Investor's Business Daily (originally *Investor's Daily*) as a data-rich competitor to *The Wall Street Journal*, the first national paper to print proprietary ratings such as the EPS Rating, the [[relative-strength|Relative Strength]] (RS) Rating, and the Composite Rating directly in its stock tables.

## Later Years and Legacy

In March 2021, News Corp (parent of The Wall Street Journal) agreed to acquire Investor's Business Daily from O'Neil Capital Management for $275 million; the deal closed in mid-2021. IBD continues to publish CANSLIM-based ratings (Composite Rating, Relative Strength Rating) and runs the IBD Live community for growth traders. O'Neil died on May 28, 2023, at age 90; his death was announced by William O'Neil + Co. His firms (William O'Neil + Co., O'Neil Global Advisors) continue to provide institutional research, and *How to Make Money in Stocks* — with over 2 million copies sold across four editions — remains a canonical text for momentum and growth-stock traders. Prominent traders including Mark Minervini and David Ryan (a U.S. Investing Championship winner mentored by O'Neil) trace their methods directly to CANSLIM.

## The CANSLIM Method

[[canslim|CANSLIM]] is a seven-factor screening framework for identifying potential leading growth stocks before their biggest advance, detailed in his book *How to Make Money in Stocks* (Source: [[book-how-to-make-money-in-stocks]]). Each letter is a filter; O'Neil's research found that historical big winners tended to satisfy most or all of them simultaneously:

- **C — Current quarterly earnings.** Big EPS acceleration in the most recent quarter(s), typically up 25%+ year-over-year, ideally accelerating versus prior quarters.
- **A — Annual earnings growth.** Sustained annual EPS growth (commonly 25%+ over the last several years) plus a strong return on equity, evidence of a durable, not one-off, business.
- **N — New.** A new product, service, management team, industry condition, or — critically for the chart side — a *new price high* emerging from a sound base. O'Neil's counterintuitive thesis: stocks making new highs tend to go higher.
- **S — Supply and demand.** Smaller or shrinking float (e.g., from buybacks) plus a sharp *volume* surge on breakout days, signaling real institutional demand against limited supply.
- **L — Leader or laggard.** Buy the leaders, not the laggards — typically a [[relative-strength|Relative Strength]] (RS) Rating of 80+ (the stock outperforming 80%+ of the market). O'Neil favored the top one or two stocks in a leading industry group.
- **I — Institutional sponsorship.** Increasing ownership by mutual funds and other large investors (the "smart money" whose accumulation drives sustained trends), but not so over-owned that demand is exhausted.
- **M — Market direction.** Roughly three of four stocks follow the general market, so trade only in a confirmed uptrend. O'Neil pioneered the "follow-through day" concept (a strong, high-volume up day several days into a rally attempt) to time market re-entry, and going to cash in corrections.

### Chart patterns and rules

CANSLIM's technical side rests on a small set of base patterns that precede valid breakouts. The signature is the **[[cup-with-handle|cup-with-handle]]** — a rounded "U" correction followed by a short, slight pullback (the handle) before the stock breaks out above the prior pivot on heavy volume. O'Neil also catalogued the **[[flat-base]]**, the double-bottom, and the saucer base, and stressed buying within ~5% of the precise pivot point. His most-quoted risk rule is the **7–8% stop**: sell any new purchase that falls 7–8% below the buy point, no exceptions, since a series of small losses is survivable while one large loss is not. Profits, by contrast, are let to run, often with a "sell into strength" rule near 20–25% gains for most positions.

## Trading Relevance

O'Neil's approach is particularly relevant for [[swing-trading|swing]] and [[position-trading|position]] traders in [[stocks]]. His emphasis on buying stocks making new highs (rather than bargain hunting), cutting losses at 7-8%, and respecting [[market-regime|market direction]] provides a disciplined [[breakout]] framework that contrasts sharply with the [[value-investing]] approach favored by [[warren-buffett]] — O'Neil deliberately bought what looked "expensive" and sold what looked "cheap" (Source: [[book-how-to-make-money-in-stocks]]). His book *How to Make Money in Stocks* remains one of the best-selling trading books of all time.

## Influence and Legacy

CANSLIM's vocabulary — base, pivot, breakout, [[relative-strength|RS]] line, follow-through day, distribution day — has become part of the standard lexicon of growth trading, independent of whether a trader follows IBD. Practitioners who explicitly trace their methods to O'Neil include David Ryan (a multiple U.S. Investing Championship winner whom O'Neil mentored) and [[mark-minervini|Mark Minervini]], whose SEPA / "Volatility Contraction Pattern" approach is a direct descendant of CANSLIM base analysis. The American Association of Individual Investors has, over multi-decade screening studies, repeatedly found the CANSLIM screen among the better-performing mechanical strategies it tracks, lending some out-of-sample support to O'Neil's empirical claims. IBD's MarketSmith platform, the IBD 50 list, and the IBD Live community continue to operationalize the method for retail traders, and the cup-with-handle and flat-base patterns are now built into virtually every charting package.
