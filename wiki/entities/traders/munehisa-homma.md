Munehisa Homma (commonly dated 1724–1803, also romanized Honma) was a Japanese rice merchant from Sakata who traded rice futures at the [[dojima-rice-exchange|Dojima Rice Exchange]] in Osaka — the world's first organized futures market — and is traditionally credited as the father of [[candlestick-patterns|candlestick charting]]. He embedded crowd psychology into price visualisation, producing patterns — doji, [[hammer]], engulfing — that remain in use more than 300 years later. Homma's work represents the earliest known form of technical analysis, predating Western methods by over a century (Source: [[2026-04-20-comprehensive-guide-technical-trading-indicators]]).

## Overview

Homma was born into a wealthy merchant family in Sakata, a port city in Dewa Province (modern Yamagata Prefecture) that served as a rice-shipping hub. Taking over the family business around 1750, he traded first at the local Sakata exchange and then at Dojima in Osaka, where by the 1710s–1730s standardized rice-coupon (futures) contracts were already trading — complete with contract months, clearing, and cash settlement, some 130 years before the Chicago Board of Trade.

Key elements of the traditional account (largely transmitted through later Japanese sources and popularized in the West by [[steve-nison|Steve Nison]]; primary documentation is thin and details should be treated as merchant lore rather than verified history):

- He is said to have written ***San-en Kinsen Hiroku*** ("The Fountain of Gold — The Three Monkey Record of Money," traditionally dated 1755), an early text on market psychology arguing that the mental state of the market — fear and greed — drives prices, and that markets alternate between **Yang** (bull) and **Yin** (bear) phases containing the seeds of their reversal.
- Legend credits him with **100 consecutive winning trades** and rice-market profits so large that he became known as the "god of the markets" (*soba no kamisama*), was reportedly granted honorary samurai status, and served as a financial adviser to the shogunate.
- He built one of the first **information networks** in trading history — runners (in some tellings, rooftop signalers) relaying Osaka prices from Sakata, roughly 600 km away — an early example of paying for an informational edge in latency.

## The Sakata Rules and Candlestick Legacy

The body of pattern heuristics attributed to Homma's tradition survives as the **Sakata Rules / Sakata's Five Methods (Sakata Goho)**, each built on the number three:

| Rule | Japanese | Modern equivalent |
|------|----------|-------------------|
| San-zan | three mountains | triple top reversal |
| San-sen | three rivers | triple bottom reversal |
| San-ku | three gaps | gap exhaustion (often a reversal cue) |
| San-pei | three soldiers | three white soldiers (strong advancing trend) |
| San-poh | three methods | rising/falling three methods (continuation pause) |

These map directly onto patterns modern traders still trade: triple tops/bottoms, gap exhaustion, three white soldiers, and rising/falling three methods.

### How a candlestick encodes the four prices

The enduring contribution of Homma's tradition is the **candlestick** itself — a single graphic that shows the open, high, low, and close (OHLC) of a period and, crucially, the *relationship* between them:

```
        │   ← upper wick / shadow (the period's HIGH)
      ┌─┴─┐
      │   │ ← real body: span between OPEN and CLOSE
      └─┬─┘   (filled/black or red = close below open = bearish;
        │      hollow/white or green = close above open = bullish)
        │   ← lower wick / shadow (the period's LOW)
```

The *body* shows conviction (large body = decisive move), the *wicks* show rejection (long upper wick = sellers repelled a rally; long lower wick = buyers absorbed a sell-off). Reversal patterns Homma's tradition anticipates — the **doji** (open ≈ close, indecision), the [[hammer]] (long lower wick after a decline, bullish rejection), and the **engulfing** pattern (a body that fully covers the prior body) — are direct readings of this open/close/high/low geometry as a snapshot of crowd emotion.

Candlestick charts in their modern OHLC graphical form were systematized in Japan after Homma's era (Meiji period) and only reached Western traders in 1989–1991 through Steve Nison's research and his book *Japanese Candlestick Charting Techniques*, after which candlesticks displaced bar charts as the default chart type on virtually every trading platform.

## Trading Relevance

- Homma is the historical anchor for the claim that **price action reflects crowd psychology** — the founding premise of [[technical-analysis-overview|technical analysis]] and a precursor to [[behavioral-finance-overview|behavioral finance]].
- Dojima demonstrates that futures, margin, and speculative excess are not modern inventions; the exchange suffered manipulation episodes and government interventions familiar to any modern futures trader.
- The default chart on every modern platform is his tradition's descendant; candlestick reversal patterns (doji, engulfing, hammer) remain among the most-tested signals in the pattern-recognition literature.

## Influence and Legacy

- **Father of charting psychology**: Homma's tradition is the historical root of reading *price action as emotion* — the idea that fear, greed, and indecision are legible in the open/high/low/close geometry of a bar. This premise underlies all later [[technical-analysis-overview|technical analysis]] and prefigures [[behavioral-finance-overview|behavioral finance]].
- **Western transmission via [[steve-nison|Steve Nison]]**: candlesticks were essentially unknown outside Japan until Nison's research and 1991 book; within a decade they became the global default chart type, retroactively making Homma one of the most influential figures in modern charting despite living three centuries earlier.
- **Lineage of pattern traders**: the Sakata heuristics seeded an entire vocabulary — doji, [[hammer]], engulfing, morning/evening star, three white soldiers — now embedded in pattern-scanner software and taught to every new technical trader.
- **Futures-market prehistory**: by trading standardized rice coupons at [[dojima-rice-exchange|Dojima]], Homma operated in a recognizably modern [[futures]] market — with contract months, clearing, and speculation — roughly 130 years before the Chicago Board of Trade, a reminder that derivatives and speculative excess long predate Wall Street.
