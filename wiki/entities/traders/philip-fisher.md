# Philip Fisher

Philip Arthur Fisher (1907-2004) was a pioneering American investor and author who is widely regarded as the father of [[growth-investing]]. His 1958 book *Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits* introduced qualitative research methods -- particularly the "scuttlebutt" technique -- that transformed how investors evaluate companies. [[warren-buffett]] has described his own investment approach as "85% [[benjamin-graham|Graham]] and 15% Fisher," though over time Buffett's emphasis shifted increasingly toward Fisher's quality-focused principles. Fisher founded the investment counseling firm Fisher & Company in 1931 and ran it for nearly 70 years.

## Early Life and Background

Philip Fisher was born on September 8, 1907, in San Francisco, California. He showed early aptitude for finance and business, studying at Stanford University's Graduate School of Business Administration, which he entered at the age of 19. After briefly working as a securities analyst at a San Francisco bank, he was deeply affected by the [[1929-crash]] and the [[great-depression]], experiences that shaped his understanding of how market panics can create extraordinary long-term buying opportunities.

In 1931, at just 24 years old and in the depths of the Depression, Fisher founded Fisher & Company, an investment counseling firm based in San Francisco. This was an unusually bold move -- most investment professionals were leaving the industry, not entering it. His willingness to start a firm during the worst economic crisis in American history demonstrated the contrarian conviction that would define his career.

## Career and Key Investments

### Fisher & Company (1931-1999)

Fisher ran Fisher & Company for nearly seven decades, making it one of the longest-tenured investment firms operated by its founder. He was famously selective, typically managing money for only a handful of clients and focusing on a concentrated portfolio of perhaps 10-30 stocks at most. He deliberately kept the firm small to maintain research quality and personal attention.

### The Motorola Position (1955-2004)

Fisher's most legendary investment was his purchase of Motorola stock in 1955. At the time, Motorola was primarily a radio and television manufacturer, but Fisher's research identified the company's exceptional management, strong R&D culture, and potential for growth into new electronic technologies. He bought the stock and held it for the rest of his life -- nearly 50 years -- making it one of the longest continuously held positions in investment history.

During Fisher's holding period, Motorola evolved from a consumer electronics company into a semiconductor and cellular communications pioneer, growing revenue from approximately $227 million (1955) to over $27 billion by the late 1990s. The stock appreciated thousands of percent, validating Fisher's conviction in buying outstanding companies and holding them indefinitely.

### Other Notable Investments

Fisher was known to have invested in other technology and innovation-driven companies, including Texas Instruments and Dow Chemical, though he was characteristically private about most of his portfolio. He focused consistently on companies with:
- Superior management teams
- Above-average growth potential
- Significant investment in research and development
- Strong competitive advantages (what [[warren-buffett]] would later call "moats")

## Investment Philosophy

Fisher's approach was revolutionary because it shifted the focus from purely quantitative measures (the domain of [[benjamin-graham]]) to qualitative evaluation of businesses and management. His key principles:

1. **The Scuttlebutt Method**: Fisher's most original contribution to [[fundamental-analysis]]. Before investing, he would systematically interview a company's customers, suppliers, competitors, former employees, and industry experts to build a comprehensive picture of the business from multiple independent perspectives. This was qualitative "channel checking" decades before it became standard practice on Wall Street (Source: [[book-common-stocks-and-uncommon-profits]])

2. **The 15-Point Checklist**: Fisher developed a rigorous 15-point framework for evaluating whether a company was worth investing in. Key questions included:
   - Does the company have products or services with sufficient market potential to make possible a sizable increase in sales for at least several years?
   - Does management have a determination to continue developing new products that will further increase sales?
   - How effective is the company's research and development effort relative to its size?
   - Does the company have a worthwhile profit margin?
   - What is the company doing to maintain or improve profit margins?
   - Does the company have outstanding labor and personnel relations?
   - Does the company have outstanding executive relations?
   - Does the company have depth to its management?
   - How good is the company's cost analysis and accounting?
   - Does the company have outstanding long-range outlook?

3. **Concentrated Investing**: Unlike [[peter-lynch]], who held up to 1,400 stocks, Fisher argued fiercely against excessive [[diversification]]. He believed it was far better to own a few outstanding companies that you understood deeply than to spread investments across dozens of mediocre ones. He wrote: "Owning stocks of a large number of companies is not diversification. It is a confession that you cannot identify the outstanding ones" (Source: [[book-common-stocks-and-uncommon-profits]])

4. **Buy and Hold Indefinitely**: Fisher believed that if you selected the right companies, the optimal holding period was essentially forever. His Motorola position -- held for nearly 50 years -- demonstrated this commitment. He argued that the greatest investment returns come from buying outstanding companies and holding through all market conditions, rather than trading in and out

5. **Management Quality Is Paramount**: Fisher placed extraordinary emphasis on evaluating management integrity, capability, and vision. He argued that even a company with excellent products and market position would underperform if led by mediocre management, while outstanding leaders could transform average companies into exceptional ones

6. **Invest in Innovation**: Fisher consistently favored companies that invested heavily in research and development. He believed that companies which continuously innovated would compound their competitive advantages over time, while those that coasted on existing products would inevitably decline

7. **Ignore Short-Term Market Noise**: Fisher argued that investors should focus on the long-term business trajectory rather than quarterly earnings fluctuations or stock price [[volatility]]. He was contemptuous of attempts at [[technical-analysis]] and market timing: "The stock market is filled with individuals who know the price of everything, but the value of nothing"

## Strategy Connections

Fisher's work connects to several important wiki concepts and strategies:

- **[[fundamental-analysis]]** -- Fisher's scuttlebutt method and 15-point checklist represent a qualitative branch of [[fundamental-analysis]] that complements [[benjamin-graham]]'s quantitative approach. Together, they form the two pillars of modern fundamental investing
- **[[growth-investing]]** -- Fisher is the intellectual father of [[growth-investing]], emphasizing revenue growth, R&D investment, and expanding market opportunities over [[value-investing]] metrics like book value and P/E ratios. See also: [[growth-investing-strategy]]
- **[[value-investing]]** -- While Fisher is often contrasted with [[benjamin-graham]]'s [[value-investing]], his insistence on buying at reasonable prices and his focus on intrinsic business quality influenced [[warren-buffett]]'s evolution from pure value to "quality at a fair price." See: [[value-vs-growth-investing]]
- **[[earnings-momentum]]** -- Fisher's focus on companies with accelerating growth trajectories connects to modern [[earnings-momentum]] strategies
- **[[diversification]]** -- Fisher's arguments against excessive diversification remain influential and stand in direct contrast to Modern Portfolio Theory and [[index-funds]] approaches

## Influence on Other Investors

Fisher's impact on subsequent generations of investors is difficult to overstate:

- **[[warren-buffett]]**: Buffett has publicly credited Fisher with shifting his approach from pure Graham-style deep value toward buying wonderful businesses at fair prices. Buffett's emphasis on competitive moats, management quality, and long holding periods are all Fisher concepts (Source: [[book-common-stocks-and-uncommon-profits]])
- **[[charlie-munger]]**: Munger was arguably even more influenced by Fisher than Buffett, and it was Munger who most strongly pushed Buffett toward Fisher's quality-focused philosophy
- **Kenneth Fisher**: Philip's son, Kenneth Fisher, became a prominent investor and Forbes columnist in his own right, founding Fisher Investments (now managing over $200 billion)
