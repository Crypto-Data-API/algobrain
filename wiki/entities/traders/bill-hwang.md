# Bill Hwang

Sung Kook "Bill" Hwang (b. 1964, South Korea) was the founder of Tiger Asia Management (2001-2012) and Archegos Capital Management (2013-2021). His March 2021 collapse triggered the largest single prime-brokerage loss in modern banking history (~$10-12B aggregate across Credit Suisse, Nomura, Morgan Stanley, UBS, MUFG, Mizuho), and accelerated [[credit-suisse-collapse-2023|Credit Suisse's eventual collapse]]. Convicted on 10 of 11 counts of securities fraud, wire fraud, and market manipulation in July 2024; sentenced to **18 years** federal prison in November 2024 — one of the longest white-collar sentences of the decade. As of mid-2026 he remains **free on bail pending appeal** to the Second Circuit, was ordered to pay **more than $9 billion in restitution**, and has filed a pardon application with the U.S. Justice Department (reported January 2026).

## Background

- Born 1964 in South Korea; emigrated to US 1982.
- UCLA undergrad; Carnegie Mellon MBA (1988).
- Hyundai Securities US sales role early 1990s.
- Joined [[tiger-management]] in 1996 as analyst under julian-robertson — making him a charter "[[tiger-cubs|Tiger Cub]]."

## Tiger Asia Management (2001-2012)

After Tiger Management closed its public funds in 2000, Robertson seeded ~30 Tiger Cubs with capital. Hwang launched **Tiger Asia Management** in 2001 with $25M, focused on East Asian equities (Korea, Japan, China, Hong Kong).

Peak AUM ~$8B (2007). Beat the MSCI Asia ex-Japan index meaningfully through the 2000s.

**Insider trading conviction (2012):**

In December 2012, Tiger Asia pled guilty to U.S. wire fraud charges. The SEC also brought parallel civil charges. The case involved trading ahead of two 2008-2009 Chinese bank private placements (Bank of China, China Construction Bank) — Tiger Asia received material non-public information through pre-deal soundings, then traded short ahead of the announcements.

- Criminal fine: $16M.
- Civil disgorgement: $44M.
- Hwang personally barred from registered investment advice.
- Hong Kong SFC banned him from Hong Kong markets for 4 years (2014-2018).

The conviction made Hwang ineligible to manage outside money — but the Dodd-Frank "family office rule" (2011) provided an exemption for funds managing only the principal's personal capital.

## Archegos Capital Management (2013-2021)

Hwang reorganized his remaining personal wealth (~$200M) into [[archegos|Archegos Capital Management]] in 2013, structured as a single-family office.

**Strategy:** long-biased, highly concentrated US-listed equity, with heavy China-ADR exposure. Built positions almost entirely via [[total-return-swap|total-return swaps]] and [[contract-for-difference|CFDs]] across multiple prime brokers — Credit Suisse, Nomura, Morgan Stanley, Goldman Sachs, UBS, Wells Fargo, MUFG, Mizuho.

**Growth:** $200M (2013) → ~$10-15B equity / $35B+ gross exposure (March 2021). Compounded ~60-80% annually through bull-market 2013-2021.

**Religious framing:** Hwang publicly described his trading as a religious calling — "I love God; He likes me." Founded Grace and Mercy Foundation 2006 (charitable arm). Some commentators interpreted his conviction in his own picks as the religious cover for an extreme-leverage strategy.

## March 2021 Collapse

See [[archegos-blowup-2021]] for the full timeline. Briefly:

- ViacomCBS secondary offering Mar 22 triggered VIAC sell-off.
- Mark-to-market losses on Hwang's concentrated TRS book exceeded posted margin by Mar 25.
- Prime brokers met Mar 25; failed to agree on coordinated unwind.
- Mar 26 firesale: GS/MS unloaded ~$20B at -10-30% discounts; CS/Nomura/UBS/MUFG/Mizuho stuck holding the rest.
- Aggregate bank losses ~$10-12B; Archegos itself wiped out.

## Trial and Conviction (2024)

- **April 2022:** SEC civil + DOJ criminal indictment. Charges: racketeering conspiracy, securities fraud, wire fraud, market manipulation.
- **CFO Patrick Halligan** indicted alongside Hwang.
- **Trial:** May-July 2024, SDNY, Judge Alvin Hellerstein.
- **Verdict (July 10, 2024):** Hwang convicted on 10 of 11 counts; Halligan on 3 counts.
- **Sentence (November 20, 2024):** Hwang sentenced to **18 years**; Halligan **8 years**.

Prosecution argued Hwang's TRS positions were used to manipulate the prices of his concentrated holdings — by buying via swaps without disclosure, the underlying stocks were pushed up, creating mark-to-market profits that justified more borrowing, in a self-reinforcing loop.

In addition to the prison term, the court imposed three years of supervised release and ordered Hwang to pay **more than $9 billion in restitution** to the victim banks (DOJ, November 20, 2024).

## Appeal and Pardon Bid (2025-2026)

- **Bail pending appeal:** Hwang has remained free on bail while appealing his conviction to the Second Circuit; as of late 2025 the appeal was still in the briefing stage (written arguments being scheduled), with no ruling as of June 2026.
- **Restitution challenge:** Hwang moved to vacate the $9B+ restitution order, arguing a conflict after Judge Hellerstein's law clerk accepted a position at Davis Polk & Wardwell, counsel to victim-bank Morgan Stanley.
- **Pardon application:** In January 2026 Bloomberg reported Hwang had submitted a pardon application to the U.S. Justice Department seeking clemency from President Trump for the ~$10B fraud.
- **Related civil ruling:** On September 16, 2025 the Second Circuit affirmed dismissal of civil insider-trading claims against banks (Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley) that sold Archegos-linked blocks ahead of the collapse, holding plaintiffs failed to plausibly allege insider trading.

## Personal Wealth

Pre-collapse net worth estimated at $20B at peak (March 19, 2021). Post-collapse: zero — Archegos was wholly liquidated, Hwang's personal assets frozen pending civil/criminal forfeiture.

## Legacy / Trader Lessons

1. **Family-office exemption is a regulatory gap.** A single principal managing $35B of gross exposure has the same systemic footprint as a hedge fund.
2. **TRS structure obscures real ownership.** Hwang's effective ~20% economic stake in ViacomCBS never appeared in any 13D filing.
3. **Multi-bank fragmentation hides aggregate concentration.** No single counterparty saw the full book.
4. **Personal narrative ≠ risk control.** Religious or ideological framing can mask extreme leverage.
5. **A prior conviction is a real signal.** Goldman's 2020 decision to onboard Hwang despite the 2012 Tiger Asia plea was overruled by revenue interests; risk lost the internal debate.
