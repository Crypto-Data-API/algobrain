# Richard Arms

Richard W. Arms Jr. (January 12, 1935 – March 10, 2018) was an American technical analyst who created the [[arms-index|Arms Index (TRIN)]], one of the most widely watched [[market-breadth|market breadth]] indicators, and invented Equivolume charting. His career-long thesis — that volume, not just price, reveals the market's true condition — shaped a whole family of volume-based tools still used on trading desks today.

## Overview

Arms developed the Arms Index in 1967 and introduced it publicly through *Barron's*; the indicator measures market internals as the ratio of advancing/declining issues to advancing/declining volume (Source: [[2026-04-20-comprehensive-guide-technical-trading-indicators]]). A reading above 1.0 indicates volume is concentrated in declining stocks (bearish internals); below 1.0, in advancing stocks. Extreme readings (e.g., above ~2.0) are commonly used as short-term capitulation/oversold signals. The index — originally called TRIN ("TRading INdex") — became so standard that it was carried on the NYSE data feed and displayed on the exchange floor and financial television for decades.

### How TRIN is calculated

The [[trin|TRIN]] (Trading Index / Arms Index) is a ratio of ratios:

**TRIN = (Advancing issues / Declining issues) / (Advancing volume / Declining volume)**

- **TRIN ≈ 1.0** — volume is distributed in proportion to the number of advancing vs. declining stocks (neutral).
- **TRIN < 1.0** — volume is flowing disproportionately into advancing stocks (bullish internals); very low readings can flag a buying climax.
- **TRIN > 1.0** — volume is concentrated in declining stocks (bearish internals).
- **TRIN > ~2.0 (intraday spike)** — classic short-term **capitulation/oversold** signal used by index and [[etf]] traders for [[mean-reversion]] entries during selloffs.

Because it is a ratio, TRIN moves *inversely* to the market on a normal day — a strong up day usually prints a low TRIN. This counter-intuitive scaling is why it is interpreted at extremes rather than as a directional trend gauge.

Beyond TRIN, Arms invented:

- **Equivolume charting** — price boxes whose width is proportional to volume, so heavy-volume bars visually dominate the chart (*Profits in Volume: Equivolume Charting*, 1971)
- **Ease of Movement (EMV)** — an indicator relating price change to the volume required to produce it
- **Volume-Adjusted Moving Averages** — weighting averages by volume rather than time

He wrote five books on technical analysis, including *Volume Cycles in the Stock Market* (1983) and *Trading Without Fear* (1996), and received the Market Technicians Association (MTA) Annual Award in 1995. He worked for decades as an analyst and money manager in Albuquerque, New Mexico, where he died on March 10, 2018, aged 83, following heart surgery.

- **Equivolume charting** plots each bar as a box whose height is the high–low range and whose **width is proportional to volume**, so high-volume sessions are visually wider and dominate the chart. A wide, short box ("a square") signals heavy volume with little progress (potential exhaustion), while a narrow tall box signals a move on thin volume. Introduced in *Profits in Volume: Equivolume Charting* (1971).
- **[[ease-of-movement|Ease of Movement (EMV)]]** combines the box midpoint shift with a "box ratio" (volume / range) to measure how easily price moves on a given amount of volume; high positive EMV means price is rising on light volume.

## Trading Relevance

- The Arms Index remains a staple intraday breadth gauge for index and [[etf]] traders; extreme TRIN spikes are classic [[mean-reversion]]/capitulation signals during selloffs.
- His volume-centric framework anticipated modern [[volume-profile|volume-profile]] and liquidity-based analysis used in [[market-microstructure]] studies, and sits in the same Wyckoffian "volume reveals intent" tradition.
- Equivolume and Ease of Movement remain built into major charting platforms (StockCharts, MetaStock, TradingView).

## Influence and Legacy

Arms is remembered as the analyst who made *volume-weighted* internals a mainstream desk tool. TRIN's presence on the NYSE feed and on financial-TV ticker overlays for decades made it one of the few breadth indicators a generation of floor and retail traders watched in real time, and it remains a standard input alongside the [[mcclellan-oscillator|McClellan Oscillator]] and the advance-decline line. StockCharts memorialised him as a "Wyckoffian" — a lineage that runs from Richard Wyckoff's volume-and-effort principles through Arms to today's order-flow and volume-profile schools. His 1995 MTA Annual Award recognised this lasting contribution to the discipline.
