Brian Hunter (born 1974) is a Canadian former natural-gas trader who, as head of energy trading at [[amaranth-advisors|Amaranth Advisors]], lost approximately **$6.6 billion in a single week** in September 2006 — the largest single-trader hedge-fund loss in modern history at the time. The trade — a concentrated bet on widening March-April [[calendar-spread-arbitrage|calendar spreads]] in [[natural-gas]] [[futures]] — combined inadequate position sizing, fundamental misjudgment, and capital-structure pressure into a textbook blowup. Hunter's career trajectory (Calgary → Deutsche Bank → Amaranth → [[cftc|CFTC]] sanctions → comeback attempts → obscurity) is the canonical cautionary tale of how a successful commodities trader transitions to a catastrophic blowup. The counter-trader was [[john-arnold|John Arnold]] of Centaurus Energy, who profited approximately $1B from the unwind. See [[2006-09-amaranth-natural-gas-blowup]] for full incident detail.

## Career

### TransCanada and Deutsche Bank (1999-2004)

Hunter started his career at TransCanada Pipeline as a quantitative analyst, working on fundamental natural-gas-market modeling. The role exposed him to the structural seasonality of gas markets — winter heating demand, summer cooling demand, storage cycle, hurricane disruptions in the Gulf of Mexico, LNG-import dynamics.

He moved to Deutsche Bank in the early 2000s, joining their natural-gas trading desk in New York. Hunter's edge at Deutsche Bank came from quantitative modeling combined with aggressive position-taking. He developed a reputation as a high-conviction trader willing to size large positions in calendar spreads and options structures.

In 2003 Hunter reportedly produced a single losing day of $51M for Deutsche Bank — early evidence of the position-sizing aggression that would later define his career. After internal disputes about risk management, he left Deutsche Bank in 2004.

### Amaranth Advisors (2004-2006)

Hunter joined Amaranth Advisors in 2004. Amaranth had been founded in 2000 as a multi-strategy hedge fund initially focused on convertible arbitrage and statistical arbitrage; by the mid-2000s, energy trading had become an increasingly large share of the firm's P&L.

Hunter's tenure at Amaranth featured rapid rise:

- **2005**: Generated estimated **$1B+ in profit** for Amaranth, primarily through long positions in winter natural-gas contracts that paid off after Hurricane Katrina disrupted Gulf production. Hunter personally received an estimated **~$100M in compensation** for the year.
- **Early 2006**: Hunter promoted to head of energy trading. Energy book grew to dominate Amaranth's P&L.
- **By mid-2006**: Hunter's position size reached approximately **40% of open interest** in some natural-gas contract months. Amaranth was the largest single counterparty in the NYMEX natural-gas futures market.

The fund used both NYMEX and ICE (Intercontinental Exchange) to circumvent NYMEX position limits — a regulatory issue that would later draw CFTC scrutiny.

### The 2006 blowup

By August 2006, Hunter's position had become structurally untenable:

- US natural-gas storage was at multi-year highs
- The Atlantic hurricane season was unexpectedly quiet
- LNG imports were rising
- Demand was soft

The March-April 2007 spread — Hunter's largest single bet — began compressing instead of widening. Hunter reportedly **doubled down** through August into September, viewing the move as a temporary mispricing rather than a fundamental thesis breakdown.

By early September 2006:
- Mark-to-market losses approached $1B and rose daily
- Prime brokers (JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, others) began margining harder
- News of Amaranth's distress leaked, accelerating the price moves against the position

The week of September 14-21, 2006:
- Single-day losses of $560M+ on September 14 alone
- Total weekly losses approaching $6.6B
- Amaranth attempted emergency capital raises (Goldman, Citadel, JPMorgan) over the weekend of September 16-17
- JPMorgan and Citadel jointly assumed the energy book in a "rescue" transaction
- The new counterparties continued unwinding, compressing spreads further

Amaranth officially announced ~$6.6B in losses on September 21, 2006 and the fund unwound. Investors recovered approximately ~30% of pre-blowup NAV.

### Post-Amaranth (2007-present)

Hunter attempted multiple comebacks:

- **Solengo Capital (2007)**: Hunter attempted to launch a new fund, but the CFTC's and FERC's pending market-manipulation cases made institutional LPs unwilling to commit capital. The fund was effectively blocked (its assets were sold to Peak Ridge).
- **Peak Ridge Capital (2008-2010)**: Hunter joined Peak Ridge Capital as an advisor / portfolio consultant. Reports of mixed performance.
- **Regulatory endgame (2007-2014)**: The CFTC and FERC both charged Hunter in July 2007 over alleged manipulation of NYMEX natural-gas settlement prices (February-April 2006 expiries). Amaranth entities settled for **$7.5M in 2009**. FERC assessed a **$30M civil penalty against Hunter in April 2011**, but the D.C. Circuit **vacated it in March 2013**, ruling that jurisdiction over futures manipulation belonged exclusively to the CFTC. Hunter then settled the CFTC case in **2014 for a $750K civil penalty** plus restrictions on his natural-gas trading during settlement periods.
- **Various consulting and proprietary trading roles**: Hunter has reportedly continued to trade in various capacities but has remained outside major institutional fund management.

He maintains a low public profile and has not given significant interviews since the early 2010s. **Status as of June 2026:** still private, with no notable public news since the regulatory settlements (Perplexity check, 2026-06-10).

## Trading approach (and where it failed)

Hunter's trading approach combined elements that worked spectacularly in 2005 and failed catastrophically in 2006:

### What worked in 2005

1. **Fundamental gas-market modeling**: Hunter's quantitative training at TransCanada gave him deep storage and demand modeling capability.
2. **Aggressive position sizing on high-conviction setups**: Hunter's willingness to size large in calendar spreads produced outsized returns when fundamentals aligned.
3. **Hurricane-disruption thesis**: Katrina's 2005 impact validated Hunter's bullish-winter thesis spectacularly.

### What failed in 2006

1. **Position-size beyond market capacity**: 40% of open interest in specific contract months meant any unwind would move the market against the position by amounts proportional to size. The position was self-defeating once stress arrived.
2. **Doubling down on losses**: Hunter increased positions as the spread moved against him in August-September 2006, treating it as a buying opportunity rather than a thesis breakdown.
3. **Fundamental misjudgment**: high storage, mild winter outlook, no hurricane disruption — these signals were public and should have triggered reassessment.
4. **No exit plan for loss scenario**: Amaranth's risk management was structurally inadequate for a position of this size; once losses began, there was no orderly unwind path.
5. **Regulatory circumvention via ICE**: using ICE to evade NYMEX position limits added regulatory risk to the operational risk.

The combination produced the **largest single-trader hedge-fund loss in history at the time** ($6.6B exceeded LTCM's 1998 losses in absolute terms; LTCM remains larger as a percentage of fund AUM).

## Lessons from Hunter's career

Hunter's career is studied as the canonical cautionary tale for several specific risk-management failures:

1. **Position size relative to market liquidity is itself a risk factor.** A position of 40% of open interest is structurally fragile to any unwind catalyst because the unwind itself moves the market.
2. **Doubling down on a thesis is psychologically hard to resist when conviction is high — and frequently catastrophic.** Hunter's August-September 2006 actions follow the textbook "averaging down on a losing position" pattern.
3. **Fundamental signals should override prior conviction.** When public storage and weather data contradicts the thesis, the prior conviction is wrong, not the data.
4. **Regulatory arbitrage compounds risk.** Using ICE to circumvent NYMEX limits made the position both larger and legally exposed.
5. **Capital structure matters as much as thesis.** Amaranth had broad outside-LP capital, prime-broker dependency, and no structural ability to ride out a multi-month drawdown. Hunter was set up to be forced-unwound.
6. **Compensation incentives can drive risk-taking beyond the firm's tolerance.** Hunter's $100M+ 2005 compensation incentivized aggressive 2006 sizing; the firm's risk-management infrastructure didn't catch up.

These lessons apply directly to modern markets — see [[risk-management]] and [[behavioral-finance]] for the broader frameworks. The crypto era has produced multiple Hunter analogs: [[2022-06-three-arrows-blowup|3AC]], [[2022-11-ftx-collapse-arbitrage|FTX/Alameda]], multiple DeFi protocol-treasury blowups.

## Why Hunter is not John Arnold

A key contrast in the Amaranth episode is between Hunter (who blew up) and [[john-arnold|Arnold]] (who profited from Hunter's blowup). Both were natural-gas traders of similar age and similar fundamental sophistication. The difference:

| Factor | Hunter (Amaranth) | Arnold (Centaurus) |
|--------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Capital structure** | Multi-strategy fund with outside LPs, prime-broker dependency | Principal-driven, low outside-LP exposure |
| **Position size relative to capacity** | 40% of OI; structurally fragile | Large but proportional to liquidity |
| **Behavior under losses** | Doubled down | Held independent fundamental view; sized to fund |
| **Exit planning** | None for adverse scenario | Defined exit at structural-clearing prices |
| **Regulatory posture** | ICE circumvention of position limits | Operated within stated limits |
| **Outcome** | $6.6B loss, fund unwind | $1B profit, multi-year continuing returns |

Same market, same period, similar fundamental knowledge, opposite outcomes. The structural and behavioral differences were the entire trade.
