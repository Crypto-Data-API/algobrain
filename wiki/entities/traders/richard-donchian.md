# Richard Donchian

Richard Davoud Donchian (1905–1993) is often called the "father of [[trend-following|trend following]]" and the father of the [[managed-futures|managed futures]] industry. He created [[donchian-channels|Donchian Channels]] — plotting the N-period high and low — establishing one of the earliest systematic approaches to breakout trading (Source: [[2026-04-20-comprehensive-guide-technical-trading-indicators]]), and in 1949 launched Futures, Inc., widely regarded as the first publicly available commodity futures fund.

## Who He Was and His Era

Born in **Hartford, Connecticut** to **Armenian immigrant parents**, Donchian graduated from **Yale (BA, Economics, 1928)** and entered Wall Street just before the **1929 crash** — an experience that pushed him toward systematic, rule-based methods. Watching discretionary speculators wiped out convinced him that durable success required mechanical discipline rather than judgment and tips.

In **1949** he founded **Futures, Inc.**, the first publicly held commodity futures fund, pioneering the idea that futures trading could be packaged as a professionally managed, diversified product — effectively inventing the [[managed-futures|managed futures]] business decades before it became an institutional asset class. He later spent decades at **Hemphill, Noyes & Co.** and then **Shearson/American Express**, where he wrote his long-running newsletter ***Commodity Trend Timing***, distributing his rules to a wide audience.

## What He Is Known For: Systematic Trend Following

Donchian's channel concept is deceptively simple: **buy when price breaks above the highest high of the last N periods; sell (or go short) when it breaks below the lowest low of the last N periods.** This is the canonical **[[breakout]]** rule — the price has, by definition, exceeded everything recent, signaling that a new trend may be underway.

### Donchian Channel Rules

- **Upper channel** = highest high over the last N periods
- **Lower channel** = lowest low over the last N periods
- **Middle line** = the average of the two
- **Long entry** = close/price exceeds the upper channel (a new N-period high)
- **Exit / short entry** = price falls below the lower channel (a new N-period low)

His famous **four-week rule** (buy a new 4-week high, sell a new 4-week low) and his **5/20-day [[moving-average|moving average]] crossover system** (published 1960) were among the first fully **mechanical** trading systems ever publicized. In the 5/20 system, the short (5-day) average crossing above the long (20-day) average generates a buy and the reverse generates a sell — an early, fully specified [[moving-average|moving-average crossover]] rule. This mechanical approach removed discretion from trading and laid the groundwork for the entire trend-following industry. His **"20 Trading Guides"** (first circulated in the 1930s) distilled his risk and trend-following principles — cut losses, let profits run, trade with the trend — and are still widely quoted today.

## Legacy

In the early 1980s, commodities trader [[richard-dennis|Richard Dennis]] and [[bill-eckhardt|Bill Eckhardt]] adapted Donchian's channel system to train 23 novice "[[turtle-traders|Turtles]]," funding them with $200K–$2M each. The [[turtle-trading|Turtle Trading]] experiment — using **20-day and 55-day Donchian breakouts** with **[[atr|ATR]]-normalised position sizing** — reportedly produced ~$150–175M in profits over four to five years, averaging roughly 80% per year. It remains one of the most famous trading experiments in history and a direct testament to Donchian's original insight (Source: [[2026-04-20-comprehensive-guide-technical-trading-indicators]]).

The Market Technicians Association honored him with its **Annual Award in 1975**, and the managed futures industry's lifetime achievement award (the **Richard Donchian Award**) bears his name. He died on **April 24, 1993**.

## Trading Relevance

- **[[donchian-channels|Donchian channels]]** remain a core component of systematic [[trend-following]] programs in futures and, more recently, crypto — N-day breakout entries are still a standard baseline strategy in [[backtesting]] research and a common benchmark against which more complex systems are measured.
- His career is the canonical proof that **simple, robust rules can survive decades**: his systems only became highly profitable at scale late in his life (he reportedly did not become wealthy from trading until his late 60s), a frequently cited lesson in persistence and edge durability.
- His insistence on **mechanical execution, trend alignment, and disciplined loss-cutting** prefigured the entire CTA / managed-futures industry and the quantitative trend-following programs (Winton, AHL, etc.) that run billions today.
