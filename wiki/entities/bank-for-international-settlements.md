The **Bank for International Settlements (BIS)** is an international financial institution owned by 63 central banks and often described as the **"central bank of central banks."** Founded in **1930** and headquartered in **Basel, Switzerland**, the BIS fosters international monetary and financial cooperation: it provides banking services to member [[central-banks|central banks]], hosts the forums where they coordinate, and produces influential research and data on global markets. For traders, the BIS is most important as (1) the home of the **Basel capital accords**, (2) the publisher of the authoritative **Triennial FX survey** and **Quarterly Review**, and (3) a source of early warnings on systemic risk and hidden leverage.

## What the BIS Does

- **Bank for central banks**: Holds reserves, executes gold and FX transactions, and provides short-term credit for member central banks.
- **Standard-setting host**: Houses the **Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS)**, the **Committee on Payments and Market Infrastructures (CPMI)**, and the Markets Committee — the bodies that write global rules for bank capital, payments, and market plumbing.
- **Research and data**: Publishes the most-cited statistics on global banking, debt, derivatives, and FX, plus a research program that frequently flags vulnerabilities (e.g., dollar funding stress, non-bank leverage, "hidden" FX-swap debt) ahead of the official sector.

## Relevance to Trading

### Basel Capital Standards

The [[basel-iii|Basel accords]] set minimum capital and liquidity requirements for internationally active banks:

| Accord | Year | Key features |
|--------|------|--------------|
| **Basel I** | 1988 | Simple risk-weighted capital ratios (8% minimum) |
| **Basel II** | 2004 | Internal ratings-based credit risk, operational risk charge |
| **[[basel-iii|Basel III]]** | 2010 (post-GFC) | Higher and better-quality capital, leverage ratio, Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR), Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR), countercyclical buffers |
| **Basel III "Endgame"** | ongoing | Standardized output floors finalizing capital comparability |

These standards directly affect bank **lending capacity, market-making ability, and the cost of balance sheet** — and therefore market liquidity and volatility. For example, the leverage ratio's treatment of Treasuries and repo is widely cited as a reason dealer balance sheets shrink during stress, contributing to episodes like the March 2020 Treasury-market dislocation.

### FX and Derivatives Data

- **Triennial Central Bank Survey**: The definitive measure of global FX and OTC derivatives market size and structure. The **April 2022** survey put daily global FX turnover at ~$7.5 trillion; the **April 2025** survey reported roughly **$9.6 trillion** per day, up about 28%, with a rising share intermediated by non-bank financial institutions and electronic platforms.
- **Stylized facts from BIS surveys**: the USD is on one side of ~88% of all FX trades; the top five currencies (USD, EUR, JPY, GBP, AUD) account for the bulk of turnover; FX **swaps and forwards** dominate while spot is only ~28% of volume; and trading is concentrated among ~10 global dealer banks.
- **Quarterly Review**: Regular analysis of global market conditions, capital flows, and systemic-risk indicators — a standard macro read for cross-asset desks.

### Notable BIS Analysis

- **[[flash-crash-2016-gbp|Sterling flash-crash report]]** (January 2017): The BIS's Markets Committee produced the definitive post-mortem on the October 2016 GBP flash crash, attributing it to thin Asian-session liquidity, algorithmic order cascades, and options-related hedging flows.
- **"Hidden" FX-swap debt**: BIS research highlighting tens of trillions of dollars of off-balance-sheet FX-swap obligations — a key, under-appreciated source of dollar funding risk in a crisis.

## Trading Relevance

1. **Regulatory plumbing drives liquidity.** Basel rules shape how much balance sheet dealers can deploy, which shows up as repo rates, basis spreads, and how markets behave in stress.
2. **The Triennial survey is the FX ground truth.** It anchors estimates of venue share, currency dominance, and the spot-vs-swap mix that underlie any serious FX market-structure view.
3. **BIS warnings are leading indicators.** The institution's research has repeatedly flagged build-ups of leverage and funding fragility before they surfaced as market events.
