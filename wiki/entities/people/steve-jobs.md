Steve Jobs (February 24, 1955 -- October 5, 2011) was the co-founder, and twice CEO, of [[apple|Apple Inc]], as well as the founder of NeXT and a key figure in Pixar Animation Studios. Jobs is widely regarded as one of the most transformative business leaders in modern history, credited with revolutionising personal computing, animated film, music distribution (iTunes/iPod), smartphones (iPhone, 2007), and tablet computing (iPad, 2010).

Fred McNaught references Steve Jobs in the context of [[company-management|management quality]] as an investment factor. Jobs exemplifies Fred's principle that exceptional management can be the single most important driver of long-term shareholder value — Apple's trajectory under Jobs (from near-bankruptcy in 1997 to the world's most valuable company) versus its stagnation without him (1985-1997) demonstrates the asymmetric impact of visionary leadership. Fred's missed opportunity to buy Apple at $8/share is directly connected to his recognition of Jobs' transformative capabilities, making Jobs a recurring reference point in discussions about identifying and valuing management quality in equity analysis.

## Career and Key Milestones

Jobs co-founded Apple Computer with Steve Wozniak and Ronald Wayne in 1976, launching the personal computing revolution with the Apple II (1977) and the Macintosh (1984). Despite the Macintosh's groundbreaking graphical user interface, internal power struggles led to Jobs' ouster from Apple in 1985. During his 12-year exile, Jobs founded NeXT (whose operating system would eventually become the foundation of macOS) and acquired the computer graphics division of Lucasfilm, building it into Pixar Animation Studios. Pixar's 1995 IPO, following the success of Toy Story, made Jobs a billionaire and demonstrated his ability to build category-defining companies across entirely different industries.

Jobs returned to Apple in 1997 when the company acquired NeXT, and was named interim CEO of a company that was weeks from bankruptcy. The turnaround that followed is one of the most studied cases in business history. Jobs ruthlessly simplified Apple's product line, introduced the iMac (1998), launched the iPod and iTunes ecosystem (2001-2003) that redefined music distribution, opened Apple Retail stores that became the highest-revenue-per-square-foot retail operation in the world, and then created the iPhone (2007) — a device that effectively created the modern smartphone industry and destroyed incumbents like Nokia, BlackBerry, and Motorola's mobile divisions.

## Investment Significance

From an investment perspective, Jobs' Apple represents a compelling case study in how [[company-management|management quality]] can override traditional valuation frameworks. During Jobs' second tenure (1997-2011), Apple's market capitalization grew from roughly $3 billion (mid-1997, near bankruptcy) to about $350 billion at his death in October 2011 — a more than 100-fold increase, with the shares returning on the order of 9,000% over the period (a figure widely cited at his August 2011 resignation as CEO). In August 2011, weeks before his death, Apple briefly surpassed ExxonMobil as the world's most valuable public company. The key insight for equity investors is that Jobs' value creation was not predictable through conventional financial analysis — earnings multiples, discounted cash flow models, and comparable company analysis all systematically underestimated Apple because they could not capture the optionality embedded in Jobs' ability to create entirely new product categories. Fred's missed Apple trade at $8/share illustrates a broader lesson: the most asymmetric investment opportunities often arise when a visionary operator is running a company with a depressed valuation, and traditional analysts dismiss the stock because they cannot model what does not yet exist.

Jobs' death on October 5, 2011 from pancreatic neuroendocrine tumor raised the critical question of "key person risk" in equity investing — the danger that a company's value is disproportionately dependent on a single individual. Under Tim Cook's subsequent leadership, Apple has continued to grow revenue and market capitalisation substantially, though the company has shifted from a product innovation engine to more of an ecosystem and services business. This transition itself provides useful data for evaluating whether a company's competitive advantages are sustainable beyond a founder's tenure.
