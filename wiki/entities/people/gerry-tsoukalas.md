Gerry Tsoukalas is a **Full Professor in the Information Systems Department at Boston University's Questrom School of Business**, a Senior Fellow at the [[wharton|Wharton School]], and co-author, with [[brett-hemenway-falk|Brett Hemenway Falk]] of Penn, of the **AI Layoff Trap** mathematical model. A former Morgan Stanley structured-products trader, his research focuses on digital platforms, AI/ML analytics, and blockchain economics — and the paper's mathematical proof framework rattled markets when financial press picked it up in early 2026 (Source: [[2026-04-22-gap-finder-possible-ai-fueled-global-job-loss-reces]]).

## Overview

Tsoukalas works at the boundary of operations research, economics, and the economic implications of AI deployment. The [[ai-layoff-trap|AI Layoff Trap]] paper's contribution is to take the informal narrative of "AI eats jobs, demand collapses, more AI eats more jobs" and translate it into a formal model with proven equilibrium properties — turning a qualitative anxiety into a quantitative, peer-reviewable claim that traders can size against (Source: [[2026-04-22-gap-finder-possible-ai-fueled-global-job-loss-reces]]).

## The AI Layoff Trap paper

The paper is verified: **"The AI Layoff Trap"**, Brett Hemenway Falk and Gerry Tsoukalas, arXiv:2603.20617 (March 2026; revised version dated 3 June 2026), also on SSRN (abstract 6448898). Beyond the Pigouvian-tax result, the paper shows that more competition and "better" AI *amplify* the excess displacement, and that wage adjustments, free entry, capital income taxes, worker equity, universal basic income, upskilling, and Coasean bargaining all fail to eliminate it (arXiv:2603.20617).

The model, co-authored with [[brett-hemenway-falk|Brett Hemenway Falk]], establishes:

- A multi-firm setting where each firm independently decides how much labour to replace with AI.
- A demand side where displaced workers (and those facing [[wage-compression-vs-job-loss|wage compression]]) reduce aggregate consumption.
- A proof that, under standard assumptions about firm behaviour, the system can converge to a locally stable low-employment equilibrium.
- A finding that no laissez-faire intervention escapes the trap — only a [[pigouvian-automation-tax|Pigouvian automation tax]] internalising the demand-destruction externality is shown to restore the high-employment equilibrium (Source: [[2026-04-22-gap-finder-possible-ai-fueled-global-job-loss-reces]]).

## Why this rattled markets

The paper became one of several institutional reference points — alongside the [[esrb|ESRB]]'s December 2025 [[esrb-ai-systemic-risk-channels|AI systemic-risk report]] and [[citrini-2028-global-intelligence-crisis|Citrini's 2028 Global Intelligence Crisis scenario]] — that took AI-recession tail risk from blogosphere speculation to mainstream financial discourse. Together they re-rated the probability weight on extreme scenarios such as 10%+ unemployment and ~40% equity drawdowns (Source: [[2026-04-22-gap-finder-possible-ai-fueled-global-job-loss-reces]]).

## Why traders care

- Mathematical proof structure makes the AI tail risk *defensible* in institutional risk reports rather than narrative-only
- Identifies the Pigouvian tax as the only effective lever — the political probability of such a policy is a tradeable variable
- Compatible with [[capital-vs-labor-asymmetry]]: capital captures AI productivity gains immediately while labour losses lag, so the firm-level automation incentive remains positive even as aggregate demand erodes
- Connects directly to [[ai-driven-demand-destruction]] and the cascading labour-to-credit-to-municipal-finance chain
