Brett Hemenway Falk is a **research professor in the Department of Computer and Information Science at the University of Pennsylvania** and director of Penn's **Crypto and Society Lab**, and the co-author of the **AI Layoff Trap** mathematical model with [[gerry-tsoukalas|Gerry Tsoukalas]]. The model, picked up by financial press in early 2026, formalises a feedback loop in which competitive automation pressure drives labour reduction, consumer demand collapse, and further automation — converging on a low-output, low-employment equilibrium (Source: [[2026-04-22-gap-finder-possible-ai-fueled-global-job-loss-reces]]).

## Overview

Falk's research sits at the intersection of computer science, applied cryptography, and economic modelling. His work with Tsoukalas applies formal proof techniques to a question normally addressed in narrative or qualitative form — whether the diffusion of general-purpose AI into knowledge work can mechanically destabilise aggregate demand. The paper became a reference point in 2026 institutional discourse around AI tail risk because it provided a peer-reviewable mathematical structure rather than a vibes-based forecast (Source: [[2026-04-22-gap-finder-possible-ai-fueled-global-job-loss-reces]]).

## The AI Layoff Trap paper

The paper is verified: **"The AI Layoff Trap"**, Brett Hemenway Falk and Gerry Tsoukalas, available as arXiv:2603.20617 (March 2026; revised version dated 3 June 2026) and on SSRN (abstract 6448898). In the authors' competitive task-based model, each firm captures the full cost saving from automation but bears only a fraction of the demand loss it creates in the product market; this demand externality traps rational firms in an automation arms race, displacing workers well beyond the collectively optimal level. Notably, the paper shows that wage adjustments, free entry, capital income taxes, worker equity, universal basic income, upskilling, and Coasean bargaining all fail to eliminate the excess — only a Pigouvian automation tax does (arXiv:2603.20617).

Co-authored with [[gerry-tsoukalas|Gerry Tsoukalas]] (Boston University), the [[ai-layoff-trap|AI Layoff Trap]] model argues:

1. Once one firm in a sector adopts AI to cut labour costs, competitors must follow or be priced out (a prisoner's dilemma).
2. Sector-wide labour reduction shrinks aggregate consumer demand.
3. Falling demand pushes firms to cut more labour — the cheapest variable cost — accelerating the cycle.
4. Standard firm-level optimisation can converge to a locally stable low-employment equilibrium where no individual firm has incentive to rehire.

The result is a coordination failure rather than a market efficiency, with implications for [[recession]] modelling that pre-AI cycle data does not capture.

## The Pigouvian intervention finding

The paper's most market-relevant finding is that, within the model, the only effective intervention is a [[pigouvian-automation-tax|Pigouvian automation tax]] — a per-replacement levy that forces firms to internalise the externality of destroyed consumer demand (Source: [[2026-04-22-gap-finder-possible-ai-fueled-global-job-loss-reces]]).

Trader implication: the political probability of such a tax becomes a tradeable variable. If priced low and adopted, it caps the trap's downside; if priced low and rejected, the [[ai-driven-demand-destruction]] tail thickens.

## Why traders care

- Provides peer-reviewed mathematical framework for sizing AI-recession tail risk
- Cited alongside [[citrini-2028-global-intelligence-crisis]] as institutional grounding for extreme scenarios
- Maps directly to [[capital-vs-labor-asymmetry]] — the model assumes capital captures gains immediately while labour losses lag
- Anchors the channel-level reasoning in the [[esrb|ESRB]]'s December 2025 [[esrb-ai-systemic-risk-channels|AI systemic risk]] report
- Connects to [[wage-compression-vs-job-loss]] — the trap can form even with low headline unemployment if displacement shows up as wage compression

## Biography and affiliations

- **Research professor**, Department of Computer and Information Science, University of Pennsylvania
- **Director**, Crypto and Society Lab at Penn (privacy/security in digital environments; transparency and trust)
- Sc.B. in mathematics, Brown University; Ph.D. in mathematics, UCLA
- Research areas: cryptography, coding theory, secure computation, and network analysis; teaches a popular blockchain course in Penn's MCIT program
- Co-author (with Sarah Hammer) of "A Comprehensive Approach to Crypto Regulation", *University of Pennsylvania Journal of Business Law* (2023)
