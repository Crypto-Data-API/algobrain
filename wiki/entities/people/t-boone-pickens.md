T. Boone Pickens (1928-2019) was an American oil trader, corporate raider, and energy entrepreneur who became one of the most prominent figures in the US energy industry over a six-decade career. He was known for deep fundamental conviction, concentrated positions, and a willingness to make and lose fortunes.

## Career

### Mesa Petroleum (1956-1996)

Pickens founded Mesa Petroleum in 1956 in Amarillo, Texas. Starting with just $2,500, he built Mesa into one of the largest independent oil and gas companies in the United States through aggressive exploration and acquisitions. Mesa's growth demonstrated Pickens' core skill: identifying undervalued energy assets and acquiring them at prices below replacement cost.

### Corporate Raider Era (1980s)

Pickens became nationally famous — and controversial — during the 1980s corporate takeover wave. He launched hostile bids for some of the largest oil companies in the world:

- **Gulf Oil** (1984): Pickens' bid ultimately forced Gulf into a defensive merger with Chevron at a substantial premium. Mesa profited enormously.
- **Phillips Petroleum** (1984-1985): Another hostile bid that led to Phillips recapitalizing to fend off the takeover.
- **Unocal** (1985): A bid that prompted Unocal to adopt aggressive anti-takeover defenses.

Pickens argued that Big Oil management was undervaluing assets and wasting shareholder capital. His raids, while not always successful as acquisitions, consistently generated profits for Mesa by forcing target companies to unlock value. He became a symbol of the 1980s shareholder-activist movement.

### BP Capital Management (1996-2018)

After leaving Mesa, Pickens founded BP Capital Management, a commodity-focused hedge fund based in Dallas. BP Capital traded primarily [[crude-oil]] and [[natural-gas]] futures based on Pickens' fundamental analysis of supply and demand.

- **2005-2007 boom**: Pickens publicly called for $100+ oil based on his "peak oil" thesis and global supply constraints, and BP Capital's funds captured the rally. Forbes estimated his net worth doubled in 2005 to **$1.5 billion** and reached roughly **$3 billion by 2007**. His best single year was 2007, when he reportedly earned about **$2.7 billion** personally as the BP Capital Equity Fund returned 24% after fees and the commodity fund gained 40%.
- **2008 reversal**: After riding oil's surge toward $147/barrel in mid-2008, BP Capital's funds lost **more than $1 billion** in the second half of 2008 as oil crashed to the low $30s during the financial crisis. Pickens' concentrated, conviction-based approach meant that being wrong was extremely costly.
- **Later years**: BP Capital's commodity-focused fund was wound down in the early 2010s after sustained losses. Pickens closed the hedge fund entirely in **January 2018**, citing declining health, and converted BP Capital to a family office.

## Investing Philosophy

Pickens' approach to energy markets combined:

1. **Deep fundamental analysis**: Decades of hands-on experience in the oil industry gave Pickens an intimate understanding of supply, demand, and cost curves. He analyzed production data, rig counts, and reserve reports with industry-insider knowledge.
2. **Concentrated positions**: Pickens was not a diversified investor. When he had conviction, he bet big — often putting a majority of his capital into a single directional energy trade.
3. **Conviction and stubbornness**: His willingness to hold through drawdowns was both his greatest strength (holding through volatility to capture large moves) and his greatest weakness (refusing to cut losses when fundamentals changed).
4. **Public advocacy**: Pickens was unusually vocal about his market views, frequently appearing on CNBC and other financial media. His public calls sometimes moved markets.

## The Pickens Plan

In 2008, Pickens launched "The Pickens Plan," advocating for the US to reduce oil imports by:

- Expanding [[natural-gas]] use in transportation (particularly heavy trucks)
- Investing in wind energy (Pickens briefly held the rights to develop the world's largest wind farm in the Texas panhandle)
- Reducing dependence on foreign oil

The wind energy project was ultimately abandoned due to transmission infrastructure challenges and falling natural gas prices.

## Notable Quote

> "I've been in the energy business for 60 years, and I've learned the most important thing is to know when to buy, when to sell, and when to stand aside."

## Legacy

Pickens died on September 11, 2019 at age 91. He reportedly made and lost fortunes multiple times — his net worth peaked at approximately $3 billion (Forbes, 2007; some accounts cite up to $4 billion) and fell to an estimated $500 million to $1 billion at the time of his death, after both the post-2008 oil bust and more than $1 billion in lifetime charitable giving (including roughly $650 million to Oklahoma State University). His career illustrates both the potential and the danger of concentrated, conviction-based commodity trading: the same approach that generated billion-dollar profits could produce devastating losses when the thesis was wrong or timing was off.
