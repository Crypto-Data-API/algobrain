Paul Krugman (born February 28, 1953) is an American economist and Nobel Memorial Prize laureate in Economics (2008), awarded for his work on international trade theory and economic geography. He is Distinguished Professor Emeritus at the City University of New York and remains a prominent public intellectual — formerly through his New York Times column (1999-2024), now through his widely read Substack — commenting on [[macro-economics|macroeconomic]] policy, trade, and inequality. Krugman is broadly identified with the New Keynesian school of thought and has been one of the most influential public economists of the past three decades.

Krugman serves as a useful counterpoint to [[larry-summers|Larry Summers]] on macro views. Where Summers tends toward hawkish positions on inflation and fiscal restraint, Krugman generally advocates for more accommodative monetary policy and government spending during downturns. The Summers-Krugman intellectual divide is a lens for evaluating competing macro frameworks — the tension between their views helps stress-test assumptions about [[interest-rates]], [[inflation]] trajectories, and fiscal policy impacts on equity and [[us-treasury-bonds|bond]] markets.

## Academic Contributions and Nobel Prize

Krugman's Nobel-winning work developed the "New Trade Theory," which explained why countries with similar economies trade similar products with each other (intra-industry trade) — a phenomenon that classical comparative advantage models could not explain. His framework showed how economies of scale and consumer preferences for variety drive trade patterns, and he extended this into "New Economic Geography," modelling why economic activity clusters in specific regions. These contributions were primarily academic, but they have practical implications for understanding global supply chains, trade policy, and the geographic concentration of industries that affect equity valuations in sectors from technology to manufacturing.

## Public Commentary and Forecasting Record

Krugman's public profile rests heavily on his New York Times column (published from 1999 to 2024) and his active presence on social media. His commentary has been prescient on several occasions — he was an early and vocal critic of the Iraq War's economic costs, correctly warned about the housing bubble in the mid-2000s, and accurately predicted that the [[2008-global-financial-crisis|2008 financial crisis]] would lead to a prolonged period of low growth and low [[interest-rates]] rather than rapid inflation (contrary to many inflation hawks who warned that the Federal Reserve's [[quantitative-easing]] programs would trigger hyperinflation).

However, Krugman's forecasting record also includes notable misses that are relevant for traders evaluating his macro calls. His 1998 prediction that "by 2005 or so, it will become clear that the Internet's impact on the economy has been no greater than the fax machine's" is frequently cited as one of the worst technology predictions by a major economist. More consequentially for recent market positioning, Krugman was slow to acknowledge the severity of post-pandemic inflation in 2021-2022, initially characterizing price increases as transitory — a call where [[larry-summers|Summers]] proved substantially more accurate. These episodes illustrate why Fred treats both Summers and Krugman as signal sources to be weighed against each other rather than followed unconditionally.

## After the Times: Substack (2024-present)

Krugman retired as a New York Times columnist in December 2024; his final column, "Finding Hope in an Age of Resentment," ran on **December 9, 2024**, ending a 25-year run that began in 1999. He moved his commentary to **Substack** (paulkrugman.substack.com), where he posts near-daily notes and "wonkish" technical pieces — a higher-frequency, less-edited channel than the Times column.

The move proved well-timed for relevance: with the April 2025 "Liberation Day" tariff announcements and the broader 2025-2026 trade-war repricing, Krugman's core academic specialty — international trade — returned to the center of market narratives. His Substack analyses of tariff incidence, retaliation dynamics, and inflation pass-through became a heavily cited counterweight to administration claims during the 2025-2026 tariff episodes. ## Relevance to Trading and Markets

For portfolio positioning purposes, Krugman's public views serve as a useful indicator of mainstream progressive economic thinking. When Krugman advocates for fiscal expansion, it often signals the direction of Democratic Party economic policy proposals. His views on [[inflation]], [[interest-rates]], and government spending provide a framework for anticipating policy shifts that affect [[us-treasury-bonds|bond yields]], equity multiples, and currency markets. The Summers-Krugman divergence, as Fred uses it, is particularly valuable during regime transitions — periods when the macro consensus is shifting and the spread between dovish and hawkish interpretations of the same data widens.
