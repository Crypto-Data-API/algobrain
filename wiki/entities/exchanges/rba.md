The Reserve Bank of Australia (RBA) is Australia's central bank, responsible for [[monetary-policy]], financial system stability, and the issuance of Australian currency. Established in 1960 when the central banking functions were separated from the Commonwealth Bank of Australia, the RBA operates independently of the government under the Reserve Bank Act 1959. Its cash rate decisions are the single most important scheduled driver of the [[australian-dollar|AUD]], Australian bond yields, and rate-sensitive ASX sectors.

## Monetary Policy

The RBA's primary policy tool is the official cash rate. Historically the Board met monthly (11 meetings per year, none in January) on the first Tuesday of the month; following the 2023 independent review, the schedule was reduced from 2024 onward to **eight meetings per year**, each held over two days with the decision announced at 2:30 PM Sydney time on the second day, followed by a Governor's press conference. The cash rate target directly influences the overnight interbank lending rate and flows through to mortgage rates, business lending, and deposit rates across the economy. The RBA targets an [[inflation]] band of 2-3% over the medium term, a framework that guides its rate decisions. When inflation rises above the target band, the RBA tightens by raising the cash rate; when economic activity weakens and inflation falls below target, it eases. Changes to the cash rate are a primary driver of stock valuations for interest rate-sensitive sectors such as banks, REITs, and utilities, and are closely watched by traders positioning in the [[australian-dollar|AUD]].

## Policy History 2022–2026

- **2022–2023 tightening cycle** — the RBA raised the cash rate from the pandemic-era 0.10% to **4.35%** (November 2023) to fight post-COVID inflation.
- **2024** — extended hold at 4.35% under Governor **Michele Bullock** (appointed September 2023, the first female RBA Governor).
- **2025 easing cycle** — with inflation back inside the band, the RBA cut 25 bp in **February 2025** (to 4.10%, first cut since 2020), again in **May 2025** (to 3.85%) and **August 2025** (to 3.60%), then held into late 2025.
- **2026 re-tightening** — inflation re-accelerated materially in the second half of 2025 amid capacity pressures and sharply higher fuel and commodity prices linked to the Middle East conflict. The Monetary Policy Board responded with its first hikes in more than two years, lifting the cash rate in successive 25 bp steps beginning February 2026 and reaching **4.35% on 5 May 2026** (vote: 8–1, one member preferring to hold at 4.10%).
- **Next decision**: 16 June 2026, 2:30 PM Sydney time.

This round trip — cuts to 3.60% then hikes back to 4.35% within twelve months — is a textbook example of [[interest-rate-risk]] and the danger of extrapolating an easing cycle.

## Market Impact

RBA rate decisions are among the highest-impact scheduled events on the Australian economic calendar. The announcement can trigger sharp moves in AUD/USD, the ASX 200, and Australian government bond yields. The accompanying statement and quarterly Statement on Monetary Policy provide forward guidance that markets parse for signals about future rate direction. The RBA Governor's press conferences, introduced in 2023 as part of governance reforms recommended by an independent review, add an additional channel for communicating policy intent. For international portfolio managers, RBA policy divergence relative to the [[federal-reserve|Federal Reserve]] and other major central banks is a key input into [[currency-hedging]] and [[carry-trade]] strategies — the 2026 RBA hikes against a stable-to-easing Fed materially improved AUD carry. The ASX lists an **RBA Rate Tracker** based on 30-day interbank cash rate futures, giving traders a market-implied probability of each decision.

## Structure and Governance

The Reserve Bank Board historically consisted of nine members: the Governor (chair), Deputy Governor, Secretary to the Treasury, and six non-executive members appointed by the Treasurer. The 2023 Review of the RBA, commissioned by the Australian Government, led to significant governance reforms: from **1 March 2025** the single board was split into a dedicated **Monetary Policy Board** (which sets the cash rate) and a separate **Governance Board**, with greater transparency through post-meeting press conferences and an unattributed published vote count. Michele Bullock serves as Governor (term from September 2023); Andrew Hauser is Deputy Governor.
