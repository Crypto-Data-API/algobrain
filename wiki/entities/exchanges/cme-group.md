# CME Group

**CME Group** is the world's largest financial [[derivatives]] exchange, operating four major exchanges under a single holding company: the **Chicago Mercantile Exchange** (CME), the **Chicago Board of Trade** (CBOT), the **New York Mercantile Exchange** (NYMEX), and **COMEX**. Together these exchanges handle billions of contracts annually across asset classes including equity indices, [[interest-rates]], energy, metals, agriculture, [[forex]], and [[crypto]] [[futures-overview|futures]]. CME Group is the central venue for global futures trading and the primary market for [[order-flow]] analysis practitioners. Referenced in ITPM content as a core venue for professional derivatives trading.

## History

CME Group traces its origins to 1898, when the **Chicago Butter and Egg Board** was founded as a marketplace for agricultural [[commodities]]. It was reorganized as the Chicago Mercantile Exchange in 1919. The exchange pioneered financial futures with the launch of currency futures in 1972 under the leadership of Leo Melamed -- the first time futures contracts were applied to financial instruments rather than physical commodities.

Key milestones:

- **1972**: Launch of currency futures via the International Monetary Market (IMM) -- the first financial futures ever
- **1982**: Introduction of S&P 500 futures (now among the most traded contracts globally) and Eurodollar futures, for decades the world's most active futures contract and the benchmark for short-term [[interest-rates|interest rate]] pricing (since transitioned to SOFR futures)
- **1992**: Launch of **Globex**, one of the first electronic trading platforms for futures
- **1997**: E-mini S&P 500 futures launched -- smaller contract size democratized access for individual traders
- **2002**: CME Holdings became the first US financial exchange to go public via IPO
- **2007**: CME merged with CBOT to form CME Group
- **2008**: Acquired NYMEX and COMEX, consolidating energy and metals futures
- **2017**: Launched [[bitcoin]] futures (the first regulated crypto futures in the US)
- **2019**: Micro E-mini equity index futures launched at 1/10th E-mini size
- **2021**: Launched Micro Bitcoin and Ether futures for retail traders
- **2025**: Fifth consecutive record year -- ADV 28.1 million contracts (+6%), $6.5 billion revenue, $3 trillion crypto notional traded
- **2026**: Record Q1 ADV of 36.2 million contracts (+22% YoY); announced move toward 24/7 trading for crypto futures

## Key Products

### Equity Index Futures

- **E-mini S&P 500 (ES)** -- the most liquid futures contract in the world, with daily volume regularly exceeding 1.5 million contracts. The benchmark for equity index trading
- **E-mini NASDAQ-100 (NQ)** -- heavily traded by technology-focused traders, correlates with nasdaq
- **Micro E-mini contracts** -- 1/10th the size of E-minis, launched in 2019 to serve retail traders and smaller accounts

### Interest Rate Futures

- **Treasury futures** (2Y, 5Y, 10Y, 30Y) traded on CBOT -- critical instruments for macro traders and the primary benchmark for [[interest-rates]] positioning
- **Eurodollar / SOFR futures** -- the foundation of short-term rate trading

### Energy Futures (NYMEX)

- **WTI Crude Oil (CL)** -- the US crude oil benchmark, one of the most traded commodity futures globally. See [[crude-oil]]
- **Natural Gas (NG)** -- see [[natural-gas]]. Known for extreme volatility and the "Widowmaker" March-April spread
- **Heating Oil (HO)** and **RBOB Gasoline (RB)** -- refined product futures, components of the [[crack-spread]]

### Metals Futures (COMEX)

- **Gold (GC)** and **Silver (SI)** traded on COMEX -- see [[gold]], [[silver]]
- **Copper (HG)** -- "Dr. Copper," the economic bellwether. See [[copper]]
- These contracts are the global price benchmarks for their respective [[commodities]]

### Agricultural Futures (CBOT)

- **Corn (ZC)**, **Wheat (ZW)**, **Soybeans (ZS)** -- staple grain futures
- **Soybean Meal (ZM)** and **Soybean Oil (ZL)** -- components of the [[crush-spread]]
- **Live Cattle (LE)** and **Lean Hogs (HE)** -- livestock futures

### FX Futures (CME)

- **Euro FX (6E)**, **Japanese Yen (6J)**, **British Pound (6B)** and other major currency pairs -- the listed, cleared complement to OTC [[forex]] markets, descended from the original 1972 IMM contracts

### Crypto Futures

- **Bitcoin futures (BTC)** and **Micro Bitcoin futures (MBT)** -- launched 2017 and 2021 respectively
- **Ether futures (ETH)** and **Micro Ether futures (MET)** -- launched 2021; Solana and XRP futures followed in 2025
- CME crypto futures are regulated by the [[cftc|CFTC]] and are the preferred venue for institutional crypto hedging and speculation
- CME's crypto complex traded **$3 trillion notional in 2025**; in early 2026 crypto ADV ran 46% above prior-year levels at ~407,200 contracts/day, and CME announced plans to take crypto futures **24/7** -- the first always-on market at the world's largest derivatives exchange

## Electronic Trading: Globex

CME Globex is the electronic trading platform that handles nearly all CME Group volume today. Launched in 1992, it was a landmark in the transition from open-outcry pit trading to electronic markets. It operates nearly 24 hours a day (Sunday evening through Friday afternoon US Central Time, with a daily maintenance break) and handles billions of messages per day at peak. Globex provides the transparent, centralized order book that makes CME the premier venue for [[order-flow]] analysis -- unlike fragmented [[crypto]] markets, all CME trading occurs on a single matching engine with full depth-of-market visibility. A multi-year migration of CME's technology infrastructure to Google Cloud (partnership announced 2021, expanded with a dedicated private cloud region for low-latency trading) underpins the planned move to 24/7 markets.

## Clearing and Risk Management

CME Clearing acts as the central counterparty (CCP) for all trades, guaranteeing performance and managing [[risk-management|counterparty risk]] through margin requirements, daily mark-to-market settlement, and a default waterfall (including a guaranty fund). CME's SPAN margining system, developed in 1988, became the industry standard for calculating futures and options portfolio margin and is being superseded by SPAN 2. CME's settlement prices serve as reference rates across the global financial system -- from WTI and Henry Hub benchmarks to SOFR-based rate fixings.

## Performance and Competition (2025-2026)

- **2025**: the most successful year in CME's history -- fifth straight record year, ADV up 6% to **28.1 million contracts/day**, revenue **$6.5 billion**, with records across interest rates, energy, agriculture, metals, and crypto.
- **Q1 2026**: record quarterly ADV of **36.2 million contracts** (+22% YoY), with all six asset classes setting simultaneous quarterly records for the first time; international ADV hit a record 11.4 million contracts (+30%).
- **Competition**: BGC Group's **FMX Futures Exchange** launched in September 2024 to attack CME's rates-futures franchise (SOFR, then Treasury futures), backed by major dealer banks and using LCH for cross-margining -- the most serious challenge to CME's rates moat in years, though CME's open-interest network effects have so far held.
- Longtime chairman and CEO **Terry Duffy** remains at the helm (CEO since 2016, with CME through the entire electronic transition).

## Importance for Order Flow Traders

CME Group is the most important venue for [[order-flow]] traders for several reasons:

1. **Centralized order book**: All orders flow through Globex, providing a complete and unfragmented view of market depth. There is no hidden venue or dark pool equivalent
2. **Transparent data**: CME provides full depth-of-market data (Level 2) showing all resting limit orders, enabling tools like bookmap, [[sierra-chart]], and jigsaw to display accurate heatmaps and reconstructed tape
3. **Liquid markets**: Instruments like ES, NQ, and CL have deep order books where [[absorption]], [[iceberg-orders]], and [[delta-divergence]] patterns are reliable signals rather than noise
4. **Regulated environment**: As a [[cftc]]-regulated exchange, CME markets have rules against spoofing and manipulation, making order flow data more trustworthy than unregulated venues

The [[smart-money-orderflow-combo|Smart Money + Order Flow Combo]] strategy is most effective on CME instruments precisely because of this data quality and transparency.
