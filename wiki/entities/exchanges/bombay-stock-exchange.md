The Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE) is Asia's oldest [[stocks|stock exchange]], founded in 1875, and one of the oldest in the world. Located on Dalal Street in Mumbai — India's equivalent of Wall Street — the BSE lists approximately 5,500 companies, making it one of the world's largest exchanges by number of listed firms. The exchange operates the S&P BSE Sensex, a free-float market-capitalization-weighted index of 30 well-established and financially sound companies that serves as a primary benchmark for the Indian equity market. Ticker prefix: BOM.

## History

The BSE traces its origins to 1875, when a group of stockbrokers who had been meeting informally under a banyan tree on Dalal Street since the 1850s established the "Native Share & Stock Brokers' Association." It was the first stock exchange in Asia and predates the Tokyo Stock Exchange (1878) and the Hong Kong Stock Exchange (1891). For over a century, the BSE operated as an open-outcry trading floor — the chaotic, hand-signal-driven trading pit became an iconic image of Indian capitalism. The exchange played a central role in financing India's industrialization, listing companies across textiles, steel, chemicals, and banking that built the modern Indian economy.

The BSE's history also includes some of India's most notorious financial scandals. The Harshad Mehta securities scam of 1992 — in which a single broker manipulated bank receipts to pump stock prices before the scheme collapsed — exposed massive regulatory failures and triggered reforms that led to the creation of SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India) as a more empowered regulator. Ironically, the 1992 crisis also catalyzed the establishment of the [[national-stock-exchange-india|National Stock Exchange (NSE)]] as a modern, electronic alternative to the BSE's floor-based system.

## Modern Operations and Competition with NSE

The BSE transitioned to electronic trading in 1995 (the BOLT system), responding to competitive pressure from the [[national-stock-exchange-india|NSE]], which had launched with fully electronic trading in 1994. Despite this modernization, the BSE has progressively lost market share to the NSE in equity trading volume and has been comprehensively overtaken in the derivatives segment, where the NSE commands near-total dominance. The BSE has sought to differentiate through initiatives like the BSE SME platform (for listing small and medium enterprises), its own derivatives offerings, and the BSE StAR MF mutual fund platform, which has become a significant distribution channel for India's rapidly growing mutual fund industry.

The BSE itself became a listed company when it completed its IPO in January 2017, trading on the [[national-stock-exchange-india|NSE]] — a notable irony given the competitive dynamics between the two exchanges. Despite its relatively smaller trading volumes compared to the NSE, the BSE retains significant historical, cultural, and institutional importance in Indian finance. The Sensex remains widely quoted alongside the Nifty 50 in financial media, and Dalal Street continues to serve as shorthand for Indian markets, much as Wall Street does for American markets.

## Derivatives Revival and SEBI Curbs (2023-2026)

The BSE staged an unexpected comeback in derivatives starting in May 2023, when it relaunched Sensex and Bankex futures and options with differentiated weekly expiry days, capturing meaningful index-options flow from the NSE for the first time in two decades. India had by then become the world's largest options market by contract count, dominated by retail weekly-expiry speculation. SEBI responded with successive curbs: from late 2024 each exchange was limited to a single weekly index-option expiry (BSE kept Sensex weeklies; NSE kept Nifty), lot sizes were raised, and in 2025 SEBI standardized expiry days across exchanges. These rules reshuffled volume between NSE and BSE and are a live regulatory variable for any India index-options strategy. As of 2026, tradable index derivatives are concentrated in five NSE indices (Nifty, Bank Nifty, FinNifty, Midcap Select, Nifty Next 50) and two BSE indices (Sensex, Bankex).

## Market Snapshot (June 2026)

- **Sensex**: ~73,700-73,900 in early June 2026, down roughly 10.8% year-to-date (Nifty 50 down ~9.5%) in a broad Indian-equity drawdown.
- **Market capitalization**: India's total listed market cap fell from ~$5.3 trillion at the start of 2026 to ~$4.77 trillion by June — a loss of more than $533 billion, the largest such drop in nearly 15 years.
- **Listed companies**: ~5,500 on BSE, still among the most of any exchange globally.
- **BSE Ltd stock**: BSE shares (listed on NSE) were among India's standout exchange-sector performers of 2023-2025 on the derivatives revival, and remain a direct play on Indian retail options volumes and SEBI regulatory risk.

## Trading Relevance

- The **Sensex** is one of the two benchmark indices for the world's most active options market; expiry-day flows and SEBI rule changes around weekly options are tradable events.
- **BSE Ltd equity** itself functions as a leveraged proxy on Indian retail derivatives activity — its revenue swings directly with index-options premium turnover and regulatory caps.
- **GIFT Nifty** (offshore) plus BSE/NSE cash-open dynamics matter for global macro traders using India exposure; the 2026 drawdown alongside ~$533B of market-cap loss made India a key EM underweight story.
- BSE's **StAR MF** platform data is a useful read on Indian retail fund flows, one of the largest persistent equity bid sources globally (monthly SIP flows).
