# LBMA (London Bullion Market Association)

The London Bullion Market Association (LBMA) oversees the London bullion market — the global center for over-the-counter (OTC) [[gold]] and [[silver]] trading. Founded in 1987 to formalize a market that had operated for centuries, the LBMA sets the benchmark prices for precious metals, maintains the Good Delivery List of approved refiners, and provides the regulatory and logistical framework for the world's largest physical gold market.

## Role and Functions

### Benchmark Pricing

The LBMA administers the two most important precious metals benchmarks in the world:

- **LBMA Gold Price**: Set twice daily (10:30 AM and 3:00 PM London time) via an electronic auction operated by ICE Benchmark Administration (IBA). This replaced the historic "London Gold Fix" in 2015 following manipulation scandals. The LBMA Gold Price is THE global reference price for [[gold]] — used by central banks, mining companies, jewelers, and financial institutions worldwide for contracts, settlements, and valuations.
- **LBMA Silver Price**: Set once daily (12:00 noon London time) via an electronic auction, also operated by IBA. The global benchmark for [[silver]] pricing.
- **LBMA Platinum and Palladium Prices**: Set twice daily via electronic auction. The global benchmarks for [[platinum]] and [[palladium]].

### Good Delivery List

The LBMA maintains the Good Delivery List — a register of approved gold and silver refiners whose bars meet strict standards for purity, weight, and markings:

- **Gold**: Bars must be 995+ fine (99.5% purity), weighing 350-430 troy ounces (~10.9-13.4 kg)
- **Silver**: Bars must be 999 fine (99.9% purity), weighing 750-1,100 troy ounces (~23.3-34.2 kg)

Good Delivery accreditation is essential for refiners — only Good Delivery bars are acceptable for settlement in the London market and most international gold transactions. The list currently includes approximately 70 gold refiners and 80 silver refiners worldwide.

### Vault Holdings

London vaults (operated by LBMA members including HSBC, JPMorgan, ICBC Standard Bank, and the Bank of England) store one of the largest concentrations of gold in the world. Per LBMA monthly vault data, London held roughly **8,000 tonnes of gold** (~7,996 t in recent 2026 reporting) and approximately **22,000 tonnes of silver**. The Bank of England alone holds approximately 5,000 tonnes (largely sovereign gold reserves held on behalf of other central banks).

## Market Structure

The London bullion market is primarily an OTC market, not an exchange:

- **Trading**: Bilateral transactions between LBMA members (banks and dealers), settled in "loco London" — meaning physical gold stored in London vaults or unallocated gold accounts at LBMA clearing banks
- **Clearing**: The London Precious Metals Clearing Limited (LPMCL) clears trades, with HSBC, ICBC Standard Bank, JPMorgan, Scotiabank, and UBS as clearing members
- **Settlement**: T+2 settlement in unallocated gold accounts (book entries) or allocated (specific bar numbers assigned)
- **Not exchange-traded**: Unlike [[cme-group|COMEX]] futures, London bullion is traded OTC with no central order book

## Daily Trading Volume

The London gold market trades an estimated $30-50 billion per day in gold, making it the largest gold trading center globally. Combined with COMEX futures and global OTC markets, daily gold trading volume exceeds $100 billion.

## Historical Context

London has been the center of the global gold market since the late 17th century. The original London Gold Fix was established in 1919 by N.M. Rothschild & Sons, with five banks meeting in Rothschild's offices to set the daily gold price by telephone. This system persisted until 2015, when it was replaced by the electronic IBA auction following investigations into price manipulation by participating banks (Deutsche Bank, Barclays, and others paid significant fines).

## 2025–2026: The Great London–New York Gold Dislocation

In early 2025, fears that US import tariffs might apply to bullion triggered a historic dislocation between London OTC gold and [[cme-group|COMEX]] futures:

- COMEX futures traded at unusually wide premiums to London spot, blowing out the **Exchange for Physical (EFP)** spread and making the New York–London arbitrage briefly one of the most profitable physical trades in decades.
- Hundreds of tonnes of gold were flown and shipped from London (and refined via Switzerland into COMEX-deliverable kilobars and 100 oz bars) into New York vaults; COMEX registered inventories surged to records while **London vault holdings drew down**, and waiting times to withdraw gold from the Bank of England reportedly stretched to several weeks.
- The episode demonstrated that "loco London" unallocated gold and COMEX delivery are not perfectly fungible in stress — a key lesson for [[basis-arbitrage]] and futures-spot convergence trades.

The backdrop was a record bull market in the metal: gold broke **$3,000/oz in March 2025** and **$4,000/oz in October 2025**, driven by central-bank buying, ETF inflows, and dollar-diversification flows — elevating the importance of the LBMA auction benchmarks for valuation, collateral, and central-bank accounting worldwide.

## Why It Matters to Traders

- **Benchmark fixings**: the LBMA Gold/Silver Prices are the settlement references for OTC options, miner hedges, ETF NAVs, and many structured products — auction-time flows can move intraday price.
- **EFP and vault data as signals**: the COMEX-London EFP spread and LBMA monthly vault statistics are monitorable stress indicators for physical tightness (as in early 2025).
- **Market plumbing**: understanding allocated vs unallocated accounts and Good Delivery standards is essential for interpreting "paper vs physical" gold narratives and for any cash-and-carry or [[contango]] trade in precious metals.
