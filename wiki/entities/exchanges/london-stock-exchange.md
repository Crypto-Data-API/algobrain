The London Stock Exchange (LSE) is one of the oldest and largest stock exchanges in the world, with a total market capitalisation of listed companies of approximately $3.5 trillion. It is the primary listing venue for UK and many international companies and the home of the FTSE 100 benchmark. The exchange is owned by London Stock Exchange Group (LSEG), itself a FTSE 100 company (~£45 billion market cap, April 2026) whose revenue is now dominated by data and analytics (the 2021 Refinitiv acquisition) rather than listings.

## Overview

The LSE traces its origins to 17th-century coffee-house trading and was formally constituted in 1801. Today it operates two main equity markets: the Main Market (premium and standard listings, source of the FTSE 100 and FTSE 250 indices) and AIM (the Alternative Investment Market for growth companies). Trading is fully electronic via the SETS order book. LSEG also owns FTSE Russell (index provider), LCH (clearing), and the Refinitiv data business; since a 2023 ten-year strategic partnership, LSEG's data infrastructure runs on Microsoft Azure, with Microsoft holding an approximate 4% equity stake.

## The London Listings Exodus (2023–2026)

The defining story of the LSE in the mid-2020s is the migration of listings to New York, driven by persistently lower UK valuations and deeper US liquidity:

- **2024** — 88 companies delisted or transferred their primary listing away from the Main Market, the most since 2009 (EY data). Departures cited declining liquidity and lower valuations
- **Flutter Entertainment** (May 2024) moved its primary listing to the [[nyse|NYSE]], citing "the world's deepest and most liquid capital markets"
- **CRH** completed full delisting of its London shares in April 2026, having moved its primary listing to NYSE in 2023
- **Wise** announced in June 2025 it would move its primary listing from London to New York
- **AstraZeneca** — the LSE's largest company (~$287B market cap, June 2026) — adopted a "harmonised listing structure" in late 2025 (99.36% shareholder approval), completing a direct listing of ordinary shares on the NYSE effective January 30, 2026, while remaining UK-listed, UK-headquartered, and in the FTSE 100. The move was widely read as a warning shot about London's competitiveness
- **IPO drought** — through the first three quarters of 2025, London's IPO capital raised was the weakest in more than 35 years, dropping the venue out of the global top 20 (to ~23rd, behind Mexico and Oman per Bloomberg, October 2025)

UK policy responses have included the FCA's 2024 overhaul of listing rules (the largest in three decades) and pension-reform proposals to channel domestic capital into UK equities.

## Trading Relevance

- **Valuation-gap trades** — the persistent UK-vs-US valuation discount underpins long-running arbitrage themes: take-private activity by US private equity, cross-listing announcements as re-rating catalysts (an NYSE listing announcement by a UK name is typically bought), and FTSE 100 vs S&P 500 relative-value positioning
- **FTSE 100 composition** — heavily weighted to energy, miners, banks, and pharma; behaves as a value/commodity proxy with high dividend yield, and benefits from GBP weakness (most constituent revenue is non-sterling)
- **Index events** — delistings and listing migrations force FTSE Russell index rebalances that create tradeable flow events
- **Hours** — 08:00–16:30 London; the LSE open overlaps the European session, and the 14:30 London / 09:30 New York overlap is the highest-liquidity window for dual-listed names
