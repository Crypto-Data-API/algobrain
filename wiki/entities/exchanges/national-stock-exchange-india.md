The National Stock Exchange of India (NSE) is India's largest [[stocks|stock exchange]] by trading volume and one of the largest exchanges in the world by number of equity trades. Established in 1992 and commencing operations in 1994, the NSE was a deliberate modernization effort — designed from the ground up as a fully electronic, screen-based trading platform at a time when India's established [[bombay-stock-exchange|Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE)]] still relied on open-outcry floor trading. The NSE operates the benchmark Nifty 50 index and lists approximately 2,000 companies across its equity, debt, and derivatives segments.

## History and Electronic Trading Pioneer

The NSE was incorporated in 1992 at the initiative of the Government of India and the Indian securities regulator SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India) to bring transparency, efficiency, and fairness to Indian capital markets. When it launched equity trading on November 3, 1994, it became one of the first fully electronic exchanges in the world — a move that fundamentally disrupted the decades-old open-outcry system of the [[bombay-stock-exchange|BSE]]. The NSE's electronic platform enabled investors across India to trade from anywhere via satellite-linked terminals, breaking the geographic monopoly of Mumbai-based brokers and democratizing market access. This infrastructure also dramatically reduced settlement failures, price manipulation, and the rampant insider trading that had plagued Indian markets.

The introduction of the Nifty 50 index in 1996 gave India a modern, free-float market-capitalization-weighted benchmark comparable to international standards. The Nifty 50 tracks the 50 largest and most liquid companies on the NSE across 13 sectors, and has become the primary reference point for Indian equity performance, both domestically and for international investors accessing India through ETFs and [[futures]] contracts listed on exchanges like the [[cme-group|CME]] and SGX.

## Derivatives Dominance

The NSE's [[futures]] and [[options]] (F&O) segment is the single most remarkable aspect of the exchange from a global perspective. India's F&O market, overwhelmingly concentrated on the NSE, is the world's largest by number of contracts traded — surpassing the [[cme-group|CME Group]], [[cboe|Cboe]], and all other global exchanges. This extraordinary volume is driven by retail participation in index and single-stock options, particularly Nifty 50 and Bank Nifty options, with very short-dated (weekly expiry) contracts generating massive turnover. The notional value traded in NSE's F&O segment routinely exceeds multiple trillions of dollars per month, though much of this is driven by speculative retail activity with small contract sizes rather than institutional hedging.

SEBI has periodically expressed concern about the speculative nature of F&O trading and its potential impact on retail investor wealth, leading to regulatory interventions including increased margin requirements and position limits. For international traders and researchers, the NSE's derivatives market is a fascinating case study in how electronic access, low transaction costs, and regulatory permissiveness can create explosive growth in derivatives participation among retail populations.

## SEBI's F&O Crackdown (2024–2026)

After studies showed the vast majority of retail F&O traders losing money, SEBI imposed a package of curbs phased in from **October–November 2024**: weekly expiries restricted to one benchmark index per exchange, **index derivative lot sizes roughly tripled (from ~₹5 lakh to ~₹15–20 lakh minimum contract value from November 2024)**, higher margins on expiry day, and tighter position limits and risk-management norms. The impact was immediate and large:

- NSE average daily F&O notional turnover fell **~38% month-on-month in December 2024**
- India's total options turnover declined **15% in 2025 to $1,909 billion** (from $2,247 billion in 2024), and India's share of global equity derivatives activity dropped to roughly 20%
- F&O growth remained sluggish through FY26, with cash-market turnover also dipping
- India VIX hit record lows in late 2025 as speculative churn drained out of the market

Despite the curbs, retail participation and aggregate retail losses in derivatives remained substantial through 2025, prompting continued SEBI scrutiny.

## NSE IPO

The NSE's own listing — among the most anticipated IPOs in Indian market history given the exchange's near-monopoly economics — remains pending. In November 2025 the NSE set aside nearly **₹13 billion to settle long-pending regulatory cases** (including the co-location matter) to clear the path. As of mid-2026 the timeline remains conditional on SEBI settlement outcomes and approval; NSE shares trade actively in India's unlisted/grey market in the interim.

## Trading Relevance

- **Nifty 50 and Bank Nifty options** remain among the world's most liquid index options despite the volume reset; the Nifty 50 returned roughly 10% in 2025
- International access routes: GIFT Nifty futures (formerly SGX Nifty) at GIFT City, India-focused ETFs (INDA, EPI), and ADRs of NSE-listed companies
- The post-curb volume collapse is a live natural experiment in how regulation reshapes derivatives microstructure — relevant to anyone modeling exchange revenues or Indian volatility markets
