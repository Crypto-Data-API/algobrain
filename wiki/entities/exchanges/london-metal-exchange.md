The London Metal Exchange (LME) is the world's largest market for base and industrial metals futures and options. It serves as the global pricing benchmark for non-ferrous metals and is distinguished by its unique open-outcry ring trading, daily prompt date system, and global warehouse network.

## History

The LME was formally established in 1877, though metals trading in London dates back to the opening of the Royal Exchange in 1571. The exchange evolved from informal trading in London coffee houses where merchants would draw a circle on the floor (the origin of the "ring") and call out bids and offers.

The exchange was acquired by **Hong Kong Exchanges and Clearing (HKEX)** in 2012 for $2.2 billion, giving Asian investors greater influence over global metals pricing.

The most significant modern event was the **2022 nickel squeeze** ([[2022-lme-nickel-squeeze]]), when [[nickel]] prices doubled in hours due to a massive short squeeze involving Chinese producer Tsingshan Holding (led by Xiang Guangda). The LME controversially cancelled approximately $3.9 billion in trades and halted nickel trading for over a week. The decision drew lawsuits, regulatory scrutiny, and damaged confidence in the exchange, with some participants shifting volume to [[cme-group]] nickel contracts.

### Nickel Litigation Resolved (2023–2025)

The legal fallout from the 2022 squeeze concluded decisively in the LME's favour:

- **November 2023** — The High Court dismissed the judicial-review claims brought by Elliott Associates (~$456M claimed) and Jane Street, with Mr Justice Males calling the March 8, 2022 price move "a once in a generation event" and ruling the LME acted lawfully in cancelling roughly $12 billion of trades
- **October 7, 2024** — The Court of Appeal rejected Elliott's appeal
- **January 29, 2025** — The UK Supreme Court refused Elliott permission to appeal, ending the litigation; all three claimant groups subsequently discontinued their claims

### Recovery (2024–2026)

The LME has largely recovered from the post-squeeze confidence crisis. Trading volumes rose **18% in 2024 to the fourth-highest year on record**, and combined pre-tax profit at the LME and LME Clear rose 68% to **$193 million in 2024**. The open-outcry Ring survives — as of April 2025 only **eight member firms** still trade in it, and the LME has committed to keeping in-person sessions while more than six members participate. Official Settlement Prices are still discovered in the Ring's second session.

**Unique features of LME contracts:**

- **Daily prompt dates** — Unlike most futures exchanges that use monthly expiry, LME contracts can settle on any business day up to three months out, then on monthly dates to 123 months forward. This gives hedgers precise date-matching capability
- **Open-outcry ring trading** — The LME maintains one of the last physical trading rings, where official settlement prices are determined during two daily sessions
- **LME-approved warehouses** — A global network of ~400+ warehouses across 30+ locations. Warehouse stock levels (published daily) are closely watched leading indicators of physical supply/demand balance

## Market Impact

LME prices serve as the global benchmark for physical metals contracts. The **LME Official Settlement Price** is used in billions of dollars' worth of physical supply contracts worldwide. Key market dynamics include:

- **LME warehouse stock reports** — Daily stock changes signal shifts in physical supply/demand balance. Declining stocks in key warehouses (particularly in Asia) can indicate tightening supply and support prices
- **Backwardation signals** — When nearby LME prices trade above forward prices (backwardation), it indicates immediate physical tightness and often precedes price rallies
- **Cancelled warrants** — Metal earmarked for withdrawal from LME warehouses; a rise in cancelled warrants signals impending stock declines
- **Open interest and positioning** — Speculative positioning on the LME, particularly by trend-following CTAs, can amplify moves in base metals

The LME's influence extends beyond trading — its warehouse system effectively functions as a buffer stock mechanism for the global metals industry.
