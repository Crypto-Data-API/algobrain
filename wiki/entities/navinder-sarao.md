Navinder Singh Sarao (born 1979) is a British trader who operated from his parents' house in Hounslow, west London, and was charged with contributing to the [[flash-crash-2010|2010 Flash Crash]] through [[spoofing]] — placing and rapidly cancelling large orders to manipulate prices. Dubbed "The Hound of Hounslow" by the press, Sarao's case became the landmark prosecution under the Dodd-Frank Act's anti-spoofing provisions and demonstrated that a single retail-scale trader with off-the-shelf software could materially affect the world's largest futures market.

## Trading Activity

Sarao traded E-mini S&P 500 futures from his bedroom using modified commercial trading software. His strategy involved:

1. **Layering large sell orders** above the current market price — creating the appearance of massive supply
2. **Rapidly cancelling** these orders before they could be filled (spoofing)
3. **Trading the opposite direction** — buying when the spoofed sell orders pushed prices down
4. **Repeating** thousands of times per day

Between 2010 and 2014, Sarao reportedly earned approximately $40M from this strategy. On May 6, 2010 — the day of the Flash Crash — his spoofing activity was exceptionally aggressive, with layered sell orders totaling ~$200M in notional value in the E-mini.

## Role in the 2010 Flash Crash

The CFTC alleged that Sarao's spoofing on May 6, 2010 was a contributing factor to the Flash Crash:

- He placed approximately 19,000 spoofed E-mini contracts (notional ~$3.5B) across the session
- His orders represented a significant fraction of visible sell-side depth in the E-mini order book
- The spoofed orders created the illusion of overwhelming selling pressure, discouraging legitimate buyers
- When combined with the institutional sell algorithm identified in the CFTC/SEC report ($4.1B E-mini sell program), Sarao's spoofing may have accelerated the liquidity withdrawal

Whether Sarao was a *primary cause* of the Flash Crash or merely an aggravating factor remains debated. The CFTC/SEC's original 2010 report did not mention him; his role emerged only after a 2014 whistleblower tip.

### Sentencing

The lenient sentence (no additional prison time despite charges carrying up to 30 years) reflected:
- Sarao cooperated extensively with authorities after his guilty plea
- He was diagnosed with Asperger syndrome, which the judge considered as context
- He had already spent significant time under house arrest and supervision
- Much of his $40M in profits had been lost to fraudulent investment schemes
- Prosecutors noted his cooperation helped identify other market manipulation schemes

## Significance

1. **First major spoofing prosecution.** Sarao was the highest-profile test of Dodd-Frank's anti-spoofing statute (Section 747), establishing that spoofing in futures markets is a criminal offense.

2. **Demonstrated retail-scale systemic risk.** A single trader with commercial software, operating from a bedroom, materially affected the S&P 500 futures market. The case shattered assumptions about the scale required to move major markets.

3. **Revealed detection gaps.** Sarao spoofed daily for four years before detection. The CFTC did not identify his activity through its own surveillance — it required a whistleblower.

4. **Reshaped market surveillance.** Post-Sarao, exchanges and regulators invested heavily in pattern-detection algorithms for spoofing and layering, using order-to-trade ratios and cancellation analysis.
