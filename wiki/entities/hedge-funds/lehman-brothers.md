# Lehman Brothers

Lehman Brothers was a 158-year-old global investment bank whose bankruptcy on September 15, 2008 — the largest in U.S. history at $639 billion in assets — became the defining moment of the [[2008-global-financial-crisis]] and a permanent symbol of systemic financial failure.

## Overview

Founded in 1847 as a commodities trading firm in Montgomery, Alabama, Lehman Brothers evolved into one of the "Big Five" Wall Street investment banks (it was the fourth-largest at the time of its failure). By the mid-2000s, under CEO Richard "Dick" Fuld, the firm had become heavily concentrated in mortgage-backed securities and commercial real estate, funded with extreme [[leverage]] ratios exceeding 30:1 and heavy reliance on short-term repo funding. The 2010 bankruptcy examiner's report (Valukas Report) later documented the firm's use of "Repo 105" transactions to temporarily move ~$50 billion off its balance sheet at quarter-ends, masking its true leverage.

## The Collapse

As the U.S. housing market deteriorated in 2007-2008, Lehman's massive exposure to [[subprime-mortgage]] securities generated mounting losses. Unlike [[bear-stearns]], which the [[federal-reserve]] arranged to be acquired by JPMorgan in March 2008, Lehman received no government rescue. Weekend rescue talks with Bank of America and Barclays collapsed, and on September 15, 2008, the firm filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy with $639 billion in assets against roughly $613 billion in debts.

The decision to let Lehman fail — whether deliberate policy or failed negotiation — sent shockwaves through global markets. Interbank lending froze, the Reserve Primary money market fund "broke the buck" the next day, credit markets seized, and the [[s-and-p-500]] began its 57% peak-to-trough decline. The Lehman bankruptcy is widely considered the trigger that transformed a serious financial crisis into a near-collapse of the global financial system. Barclays subsequently bought Lehman's North American investment banking business out of bankruptcy; Nomura took most of its Asian and European operations.

## Trading Relevance

Lehman's failure is the canonical example of [[counterparty-risk]] — the risk that the other side of your trade ceases to exist. It demonstrated that even century-old institutions can fail overnight, that [[leverage]] transforms losses from painful to fatal, and that [[liquidity]] can vanish completely in a crisis. Clients with assets trapped at Lehman's London prime brokerage waited years for recovery — the event that taught hedge funds to diversify prime brokers. These lessons were echoed in the [[ftx-collapse]] and remain central to modern [[risk-management]]. The phrase "Lehman moment" has entered the trading lexicon as shorthand for a systemic counterparty failure.
