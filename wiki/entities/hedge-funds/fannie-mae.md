# Fannie Mae & Freddie Mac

Fannie Mae (Federal National Mortgage Association, FNMA) and Freddie Mac (Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corporation, FHLMC) are government-sponsored enterprises (GSEs) that dominate the U.S. mortgage market. Their placement into federal conservatorship on September 7, 2008 was a critical early event in the [[2008-global-financial-crisis]], and the 2025-2026 push to take them public again has made FNMA/FMCC one of the most-watched speculative trades in U.S. markets.

## Overview

Fannie Mae was created in 1938 and Freddie Mac in 1970 to provide [[liquidity]] to the mortgage market. They buy mortgages from lenders, package them into [[mortgage-backed-securities]] (MBS), and guarantee the principal and interest payments. By 2008, they owned or guaranteed approximately $5 trillion in mortgage debt — roughly half of all U.S. mortgages. Though publicly traded, they operated with an implicit government guarantee that investors assumed would prevent them from failing.

## The Conservatorship

As the [[subprime-mortgage]] crisis intensified, Fannie and Freddie's losses mounted. Their thin capital cushions — they operated with [[leverage]] ratios far exceeding private banks — proved inadequate. On September 7, 2008, the Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA) placed both entities into conservatorship, wiping out common and preferred shareholders. The U.S. Treasury committed up to $200 billion (later expanded) to keep them solvent.

The conservatorship sent a powerful signal: the housing crisis was far worse than authorities had publicly acknowledged. It preceded the [[lehman-brothers]] bankruptcy by just eight days and set the stage for the most acute phase of the financial crisis.

## The 2025-2026 Privatization Push

After 17 years in conservatorship, re-privatization became a live policy in 2025 under the second Trump administration:

- **FHFA Director Bill Pulte** (appointed 2025) and President Trump spent 2025 publicly floating a public offering of the GSEs, with the administration "opportunistically evaluating" an IPO — initially targeted for end-2025, then signaled for as early as **Q2 2026**.
- The structure discussed: sell roughly **5-15% of the companies' shares**, raising on the order of **$30 billion** at a combined valuation **above $500 billion** — which would rank among the largest IPOs ever. Crucially, Pulte stated the GSEs would **remain in conservatorship** even after an offering, with the government retaining its stake and the implied guarantee.
- OTC-traded FNMA and FMCC common shares rallied dramatically through 2025 on privatization speculation; hedge fund holders such as Bill Ackman's Pershing Square (a long-time FNMA bull) publicly championed the trade.
- Critics (e.g., Fortune, Dec 2025) warned that a hasty, ill-structured exit could raise mortgage rates and recreate the pre-2008 moral-hazard structure. Key unresolved questions as of June 2026: treatment of Treasury's senior preferred stock and warrants, the status of the government guarantee, and capital requirements — all of which determine whether common shareholders capture any value.

As of June 2026, both entities **remain in conservatorship**; no public offering has been completed.

## Trading Relevance

For traders, Fannie/Freddie are relevant on three fronts:

1. **The privatization trade** — FNMA/FMCC common and junior preferred shares are event-driven instruments whose value hinges almost entirely on policy decisions (Treasury warrant treatment, capital rules, IPO terms). Headlines from Pulte, Trump, or Treasury move the stocks violently.
2. **Agency MBS** — their MBS issuance remains central to the [[bond-market]] and is a key transmission mechanism for [[federal-reserve]] monetary policy (QE/QT); MBS spreads vs. Treasuries are a core macro indicator.
3. **Moral hazard case study** — their story illustrates how government-backed entities can accumulate enormous risk precisely because of the belief that they are "too big to fail."
