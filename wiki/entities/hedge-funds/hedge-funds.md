# Hedge Funds

Hedge funds are private, lightly regulated investment vehicles that pool capital from accredited investors and institutions and deploy a wide range of strategies -- [[leverage]], [[short-selling]], [[derivatives]], and [[algorithmic-trading]] -- to pursue returns that are ideally **uncorrelated** with the broader market ("absolute return"). The name derives from the original Alfred Winslow Jones structure (1949): hold long positions and *hedge* them with short positions to neutralise market direction and isolate stock-picking skill. The industry now manages roughly $4-5 trillion globally.

## Key Characteristics

- **Accredited / qualified investors only** -- minimum investments typically range from $250,000 to $10 million+. Funds rely on private-placement exemptions, which is why they can use tools mutual funds cannot.
- **Flexible mandates** -- unlike mutual funds, hedge funds can short, use [[leverage]], trade [[derivatives]], hold concentrated positions, and invest across asset classes and geographies.
- **Lock-ups and gates** -- investors often cannot withdraw for 1-3 years (lock-up), and "gates" can limit redemptions in stress to prevent forced fire-sales.
- **Alignment via co-investment** -- managers typically have substantial personal capital in the fund.

## Fee Structure: "2 and 20"

The traditional model charges a **2% annual management fee** on assets plus a **20% performance fee** on profits. Two refinements protect investors:

- **High-water mark** -- performance fees are only charged on new profits above the fund's prior peak, so investors don't pay twice to recover a drawdown.
- **Hurdle rate** -- some funds only take performance fees on returns above a benchmark (e.g. the T-bill rate).

Fee compression has pushed many funds below 2-and-20 (e.g. "1.5 and 15") amid competition from cheaper index and factor products -- though elite, capacity-constrained quant funds (e.g. [[renaissance-technologies|Renaissance's]] Medallion) have charged far more (reportedly 5-and-44) because performance justified it.

## Major Strategy Buckets

| Strategy | What it does | Edge / risk | Notable practitioners |
|----------|--------------|-------------|----------------------|
| **[[long-short-equity]]** | Long undervalued stocks, short overvalued; net exposure dialled up or down | Stock selection; reduces market beta | Tiger Management & "Tiger Cubs", [[citadel]] |
| **[[global-macro]]** | Top-down bets on rates, currencies, commodities, indices | Macro/policy views; can be very leveraged | [[george-soros]], Ray Dalio ([[bridgewater-associates]]), Paul Tudor Jones |
| **Quant / [[statistical-arbitrage]]** | Systematic, data-driven models exploiting statistical price relationships | Speed, breadth, math; crowding risk | [[renaissance-technologies]], DE Shaw, Two Sigma |
| **Event-driven** | Merger arbitrage, restructurings, spin-offs, special situations | Deal/legal analysis; deal-break risk | Elliott Management, Paulson & Co |
| **Distressed / credit** | Buying debt of troubled companies, often to control restructuring | Legal & credit work; illiquidity | Oaktree Capital |
| **Managed futures / CTA** | Systematic [[trend-following]] across futures markets | Diversifying, crisis-alpha in trends | Winton, AHL, Millennium-style pods |

Large **multi-strategy** ("pod-shop") funds -- Citadel, Millennium, Point72 -- run dozens of independent teams across these buckets with tight risk limits, aiming for steady, low-volatility returns.

## Prime Brokerage and Leverage

Hedge funds access markets through **prime brokers** -- the institutional divisions of large banks (Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, JPMorgan) that provide trade execution, custody, securities lending (essential for [[short-selling]]), and **financing/leverage**. The prime broker is also the fund's risk gatekeeper: if positions move against the fund, margin calls force liquidation. This linkage is a key channel through which a single fund's blow-up can transmit stress to its banks -- as in the 2021 collapse of **Archegos Capital**, whose concentrated, swap-financed bets cost prime brokers (notably Credit Suisse) over $10 billion.

## Market Impact and Notable Blow-Ups

Hedge funds are significant participants whose positioning, [[leverage]], and forced [[derisking]] can amplify market moves. The implosion of **Long-Term Capital Management** (1998) -- a Nobel-laureate-led fund undone by extreme leverage and correlated bets after the Russian default -- required a Fed-orchestrated bailout. The losses of [[melvin-capital]] during the [[gamestop-short-squeeze]] (2021) showed the danger of crowded shorts, and Archegos (2021) the danger of hidden leverage. Each episode underscores both the power and the fragility of leveraged strategies.

## Why It Matters for Traders

Hedge funds are often the "smart money" counterparties in markets. Understanding their strategies, positioning, and behavioural patterns -- quarter-end window dressing, crowded trades, factor exposures, and forced deleveraging cascades -- provides valuable context for retail and institutional traders alike. Crowding and deleveraging in particular create predictable, recurring dislocations.
