# Bear Stearns

Bear Stearns was a major Wall Street investment bank whose collapse in March 2008 served as the opening act of the [[2008-global-financial-crisis]]. Its emergency acquisition by JPMorgan Chase, brokered by the [[federal-reserve]], marked the first time since the Great Depression that the government intervened to rescue a failing investment bank.

## Overview

Founded in 1923, Bear Stearns grew into the fifth-largest U.S. investment bank, with particular strength in mortgage-backed securities trading and fixed income. The firm had a reputation for aggressive risk-taking and a combative corporate culture. By the mid-2000s, it was heavily exposed to the booming — and increasingly toxic — [[subprime-mortgage]] market.

## The Collapse

In June 2007, two Bear Stearns hedge funds heavily invested in subprime mortgage-backed securities collapsed, losing nearly all of their $1.6 billion in value. This was an early warning signal of the coming crisis. By March 2008, rumors about the firm's solvency triggered a classic [[bank-run]] — clients and counterparties pulled their assets and refused to trade with the firm, draining its [[liquidity]] in days.

On March 14, 2008, the [[federal-reserve]] and JPMorgan Chase arranged an emergency rescue. On March 16, JPMorgan agreed to buy the firm for **$2 per share** — raised to **$10 per share** on March 24, 2008 after shareholder outrage — a stunning collapse from Bear Stearns' 52-week high of about $170 (and a $133 close as recently as January 2008). The deal was backed by a **$29 billion** [[federal-reserve]] loan facility against Bear's mortgage assets (the vehicle later known as **Maiden Lane LLC**). The acquisition was completed on **May 30, 2008**, ending Bear Stearns' 85-year run as an independent firm.

## Trading Relevance

Bear Stearns' collapse demonstrated that a loss of market confidence can destroy a firm faster than actual insolvency. The speed of the [[bank-run]] — from apparent health to near-death in 72 hours — underscored the fragility of financial institutions dependent on short-term funding and [[leverage]] (Bear ran at roughly 35:1 assets-to-equity). For traders, Bear Stearns is a lesson in [[counterparty-risk]], the importance of monitoring credit default swap spreads as early warning indicators (Bear's CDS blew out days before the equity fully repriced), and the reality that government intervention can fundamentally alter the outcome for shareholders — both the $2 "nationalization print" and the renegotiated $10 illustrate that terminal equity value in a rescue is a political variable, not a fundamental one. The June 2007 hedge-fund failures are also the canonical example of early-warning signals from structured-credit markets preceding the broader [[2008-global-financial-crisis]] by over a year.
