# AIG (American International Group)

American International Group (AIG) was the world's largest insurance company and a central player in the [[2008-global-financial-crisis]], requiring a $182 billion government bailout — the largest in U.S. history — after its massive exposure to [[credit-default-swaps]] (CDS) on mortgage-backed securities threatened to trigger a chain reaction of defaults across the global financial system. Today AIG is a slimmed-down, underwriting-focused global property & casualty insurer that returned to consistent profitability in the 2020s under CEO Peter Zaffino, who handed the CEO role to Eric Andersen on June 1, 2026.

## Overview

Founded in 1919 in Shanghai, AIG grew into a global insurance conglomerate operating in over 130 countries. While its core insurance operations were sound, its Financial Products division (AIG-FP), based in London, sold enormous quantities of CDS — essentially insurance contracts guaranteeing the value of mortgage-backed securities and [[collateralized-debt-obligations]] (CDOs). AIG collected premiums on these contracts without holding adequate reserves against potential payouts, treating the risk as negligible.

## The Crisis

When the [[subprime-mortgage]] market collapsed, AIG faced collateral calls on its CDS contracts that it could not meet. By September 2008, following the [[lehman-brothers]] bankruptcy, AIG was hours from collapse. Because AIG was the counterparty to CDS contracts held by virtually every major bank globally, its failure would have triggered cascading losses across the entire financial system.

On September 16, 2008, the [[federal-reserve]] extended an $85 billion emergency loan in exchange for a 79.9% equity stake — effectively nationalizing the company. The total bailout eventually reached $182 billion. Controversy erupted when it was revealed that bailout funds were used to pay CDS claims at par (100 cents on the dollar) to counterparties including Goldman Sachs.

## The Modern AIG (2012-2026)

AIG fully repaid the bailout by the end of 2012, with the U.S. government booking a reported ~$22.7 billion positive return. The subsequent decade was a long restructuring:

- **2021-2026 (Zaffino era)**: Peter Zaffino became CEO in March 2021 and re-underwrote the entire General Insurance book, delivering five consecutive years of underwriting profitability after years of losses.
- **2022**: AIG spun off its life & retirement business as **Corebridge Financial** (NYSE: CRBG) via IPO, subsequently selling down its stake; Corebridge is deconsolidated, leaving AIG a focused property & casualty insurer.
- **FY2025 results**: Net profit of **$3.1 billion** (reversing a $1.4 billion net loss in 2024), General Insurance combined ratio of **90.1%**, core operating ROE of **11.1%**, adjusted after-tax EPS up 43% to **$7.09**, and **$6.8 billion** of capital returned to shareholders ($5.8B buybacks, ~$1.0B dividends).
- **June 1, 2026**: Eric Andersen (former Aon president, joined as CEO-elect February 16, 2026) became CEO; Zaffino moved to executive chairman.

## Trading Relevance

AIG's failure is the definitive lesson in [[counterparty-risk]] and the danger of selling insurance on [[tail-risk]] events. The firm collected steady premiums for years while accumulating catastrophic hidden risk — a pattern [[nassim-taleb]] describes as "picking up pennies in front of a steamroller." For traders, AIG demonstrates why understanding the full chain of counterparty exposure matters, and why instruments like CDS can concentrate systemic risk in ways that are invisible until crisis strikes. The post-2021 AIG is also a case study in an underwriting-turnaround equity thesis: combined-ratio improvement plus aggressive buybacks drove the stock's re-rating through 2025, and CEO transitions (Zaffino to Andersen, June 2026) are watched as a continuity risk for that thesis.
