The Australian Prudential Regulation Authority (APRA) is Australia's prudential regulator, responsible for supervising banks, insurance companies, and superannuation (pension) funds to ensure they remain financially sound and can meet their obligations to depositors, policyholders, and fund members. APRA oversees institutions holding approximately **AUD $8 trillion** in total assets, making it one of the most significant financial regulators in the Asia-Pacific region.

## Overview

APRA was established in 1998 alongside [[asic|ASIC]] as part of Australia's **"Twin Peaks" regulatory model** — a framework recommended by the 1997 Wallis Inquiry. Under this model, financial regulation is split between a **conduct regulator** (ASIC — responsible for market integrity, disclosure, and consumer protection) and a **prudential regulator** (APRA — responsible for the financial soundness of institutions). This separation ensures that the regulator focused on whether institutions are financially healthy is distinct from the regulator focused on whether they are treating customers fairly. See [[australian-regulatory-framework]] for how all Australian regulators fit together.

APRA's core mandate is to ensure that regulated financial institutions:
- Maintain sufficient **capital reserves** to absorb losses
- Manage **risk** appropriately (credit risk, market risk, operational risk, liquidity risk)
- Have sound **governance** structures and accountable leadership
- Can meet their financial promises to depositors, insurance policyholders, and super fund members

## What APRA Regulates

### Authorised Deposit-taking Institutions (ADIs)
- All Australian banks, building societies, and credit unions
- Includes the "Big Four": Commonwealth Bank (CBA), Westpac, NAB, ANZ
- Also includes foreign bank branches operating in Australia
- APRA sets minimum capital requirements (CET1 — Common Equity Tier 1) and conducts regular stress tests
- Australian banks are required to be "**unquestionably strong**" — maintaining CET1 ratios of at least **10.5%**, well above the Basel III minimum of 4.5%

### Insurance Companies
- General insurers (property, motor, commercial)
- Life insurers
- Private health insurers
- APRA ensures insurers hold sufficient reserves to pay claims, including under stressed scenarios (catastrophic events, pandemics)

### Superannuation Funds (APRA-regulated)
- Industry funds, retail funds, corporate funds, public sector funds
- **NOT** self-managed super funds ([[smsf|SMSFs]]) — those are regulated by the ATO
- APRA supervises approximately **AUD $2.2 trillion** in APRA-regulated super assets (out of ~$3.9 trillion total super)
- Focus areas: investment governance, member outcomes, fees, insurance within super

## Key Regulatory Actions

### Macroprudential Controls on Housing Lending (2014-2017)
APRA implemented targeted controls on housing-related lending to cool Australia's overheating property market:
- **December 2014**: Capped annual investor lending growth at **10%** for each ADI
- **March 2017**: Required ADIs to limit new interest-only loans to **30%** of new residential mortgage lending
- **Effect**: Meaningfully slowed investor lending growth, contributed to a 10-15% property price correction in Sydney and Melbourne (2017-2019)
- **Removed**: Benchmarks were removed in 2018-2019 as conditions stabilised
- **Significance**: Demonstrated APRA's willingness to use macroprudential tools to address systemic risk — these controls directly affected property investors and those using [[negative-gearing]] strategies

### CBA Prudential Inquiry (2018)
Following the [[austrac]] $700 million AML/CTF penalty and broader governance concerns, APRA commissioned an independent **Prudential Inquiry** into CBA's governance, culture, and accountability frameworks:
- Found **widespread complacency** — CBA had become reactive rather than proactive on risk management
- Identified weak accountability structures, inadequate attention to non-financial risks, and a "can do" culture that didn't adequately consider risk
- CBA was required to implement all 35 recommendations and was subject to a **$1 billion** additional capital requirement (CET1 add-on) until remediation was complete
- The capital add-on was removed in 2021 after APRA was satisfied with CBA's reforms

### IOOF Trustee Removal (2018)
APRA took the extraordinary step of seeking court orders to remove IOOF's superannuation trustee directors for:
- Conflicts of interest between IOOF's corporate interests and member outcomes
- Using super fund reserves to compensate members for compliance failures (rather than using the company's own funds)
- Case ultimately settled, but signalled APRA's willingness to intervene directly in super fund governance

### Super Fund Performance Heatmap (Annual)
Since 2019, APRA publishes an annual **MySuper Product Heatmap** comparing fund performance, fees, and sustainability:
- Funds rated on investment performance (relative to benchmark), fees, and sustainability
- Underperforming funds receive formal letters requiring them to explain or justify continued operation
- Has driven significant consolidation — poorly performing funds pressured to merge with better-performing funds or close
- As of 2025, the number of APRA-regulated super funds has decreased from ~200+ to fewer than 140 through mergers

## Capital Framework

APRA implements the **Basel III** framework (and Basel III.1 reforms) in Australia, with significant domestic additions:

| Metric | APRA Requirement | Basel III Minimum | Notes |
|--------|-----------------|-------------------|-------|
| **CET1 Ratio** | 10.5%+ (majors) | 4.5% | "Unquestionably strong" benchmark |
| **Tier 1 Capital** | 12%+ (majors) | 6% | Includes CET1 + Additional Tier 1 |
| **Total Capital** | 14%+ (majors) | 8% | All capital classes |
| **Leverage Ratio** | 3.5%+ (majors) | 3% | Non-risk-weighted |
| **Liquidity Coverage Ratio** | 100%+ | 100% | 30-day stressed liquidity |
| **Net Stable Funding Ratio** | 100%+ | 100% | 1-year structural liquidity |

The significantly higher CET1 requirements mean Australian banks are among the best-capitalised in the world, which contributes to their stability but also constrains lending growth and profitability relative to less-regulated banking systems.

|--------|------|------|---------|
| **Focus** | Financial soundness | Market conduct & consumer protection | AML/CTF compliance |
| **Regulates** | Banks, insurers, super funds | Brokers, advisers, companies, markets | Reporting entities (banks, exchanges, casinos) |
| **Key concern** | Can they pay their debts? | Are they treating customers fairly? | Are they preventing financial crime? |

## Connection to Investors

APRA's actions affect investors in several direct ways:

1. **Bank stability**: APRA's capital requirements ensure Australian banks are well-capitalised — this supports the stability of deposits, mortgage markets, and banking sector share prices (the Big Four banks represent ~20% of ASX 200 market cap)
2. **Interest rates on savings and loans**: APRA's capital and liquidity rules affect how much banks can lend and at what rates — tighter rules generally mean higher mortgage rates and lower savings rates
3. **Superannuation performance**: APRA's heatmap and performance testing drive fund mergers and fee reductions, directly benefiting [[superannuation]] fund members
4. **Property market**: APRA's macroprudential controls on lending (when applied) directly affect property prices, which matters for investors using [[negative-gearing]] strategies
5. **Financial Stability**: APRA is a member of the Council of Financial Regulators (CFR) alongside the RBA, ASIC, and Treasury — coordinating Australia's systemic risk response

## Structure and Leadership

- **Chair**: Appointed by the Treasurer for a fixed term (typically 5 years)
- **Members**: Chair + two to four other APRA members, forming the APRA Executive Board
- **Staff**: ~700
- **Budget**: ~AUD $180 million (funded by industry levies — regulated entities pay for their own regulation)
- **Offices**: Sydney (headquarters), Melbourne, Brisbane, Canberra
