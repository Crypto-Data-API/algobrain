The Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC) is Australia's primary regulator of financial markets, financial services, and corporate governance. Established in 1998 as the successor to the Australian Securities Commission, ASIC's mandate combines functions that in the United States are split across the [[sec|SEC]], FINRA, CFPB, and parts of the OCC — making it one of the broadest financial regulators in the developed world. Its retail-protection rules (CFD leverage caps, binary-option ban) closely mirror the EU's [[esma|ESMA]] intervention; see [[regulation]] for the global comparison.

## Overview

ASIC is an independent Commonwealth government body responsible for:

- **Market integrity**: Surveillance of the ASX and other Australian financial markets
- **Licensing**: Issuing and enforcing Australian Financial Services Licences (AFSLs) required by all brokers, fund managers, and financial advisers
- **Consumer protection**: Ensuring financial products and services are fair, transparent, and appropriate for their target market
- **Corporate regulation**: Overseeing company registrations, corporate governance, and financial reporting
- **Credit regulation**: Supervising consumer credit providers (added to ASIC's mandate in 2010)

ASIC does **not** regulate [[superannuation]] funds or [[smsf|SMSFs]] — that responsibility falls to the ATO (Australian Taxation Office) for SMSFs and APRA (Australian Prudential Regulation Authority) for APRA-regulated super funds. However, ASIC does regulate the financial products and advice associated with superannuation.

## Key Powers and Functions

### Market Surveillance

ASIC monitors trading activity on the ASX, Chi-X Australia, and other licensed markets for:

- Insider trading
- Market manipulation
- Unusual trading patterns around corporate announcements
- Wash trading and spoofing
- Short selling compliance

ASIC operates the Market Analysis and Intelligence (MAI) system for automated surveillance of trading data across all Australian markets.

### Licensing (AFSL)

Every entity that provides financial services in Australia must hold an **Australian Financial Services Licence (AFSL)** or be authorised under someone else's AFSL. This includes:

- Stockbrokers (commsec, nabtrade, bell-direct, morgans, selfwealth)
- CFD providers (cmc-markets, ig-markets)
- Fund managers and super fund trustees
- Financial advisers and planners
- Insurance providers
- Managed investment scheme operators

AFSL holders must meet ongoing requirements including adequate financial resources, competent staff, internal dispute resolution, and membership of an external dispute resolution scheme (Australian Financial Complaints Authority, or AFCA).

### Product Intervention Powers

Since 2019, ASIC has had the power to intervene directly in financial product markets by banning, restricting, or imposing conditions on products it considers harmful to consumers — without needing to prove that a specific law has been broken.

### Enforcement

ASIC can:

- Issue infringement notices and civil penalty proceedings
- Seek court orders including injunctions, asset freezing, and disqualification of directors
- Ban individuals from providing financial services or managing corporations
- Refer matters to the Commonwealth Director of Public Prosecutions for criminal prosecution

## Notable Regulatory Actions

### CFD and Derivatives Restrictions (2021)

In March 2021, ASIC implemented sweeping restrictions on CFD and binary options products offered to retail investors:

| Product | Restriction |
|---------|------------|
| **CFDs — major forex pairs** | Maximum leverage 30:1 |
| **CFDs — minor forex pairs, gold** | Maximum leverage 20:1 |
| **CFDs — commodities (excl. gold), indices** | Maximum leverage 10:1 |
| **CFDs — shares** | Maximum leverage 5:1 |
| **CFDs — cryptocurrency** | Maximum leverage 2:1 |
| **Binary options** | **Banned entirely** for retail investors |
| **Negative balance protection** | **Required** — retail accounts cannot go below zero |
| **Margin close-out** | Required at 50% of total margin required |

These restrictions aligned Australia with similar rules in the EU (ESMA) and UK (FCA), and significantly impacted cmc-markets, ig-markets, and other CFD brokers operating in Australia.

### Design and Distribution Obligations (DDO)

Effective October 2021, DDO requires financial product issuers and distributors to:

- Define a **target market** for each product (Target Market Determination, or TMD)
- Take reasonable steps to ensure the product reaches the target market
- Monitor and review the appropriateness of distribution
- Report complaints and significant dealings outside the target market to ASIC

This means brokers must assess whether products like CFDs, options, or leveraged ETFs are appropriate for the investors using them — not just make them available to anyone who opens an account.

## MoneySmart

ASIC operates **MoneySmart** (moneysmart.gov.au), a consumer education website providing:

- Fee comparison tools for [[superannuation]], credit cards, home loans, and insurance
- Investment calculators (compound interest, super projections, borrowing capacity)
- Warnings about scams and unlicensed operators
- Guidance on choosing financial products and advisers
- SMSF resources and warnings

MoneySmart is widely regarded as one of the best government-operated financial education resources globally.

## ASIC vs SEC Comparison

| Feature | ASIC (Australia) | [[sec|SEC]] (United States) |
|---------|-----------------|------------------------------|
| **Scope** | Markets + corporate + consumer credit + insurance | Markets + corporate only |
| **SRO model** | No SRO — ASIC directly regulates (no equivalent of FINRA) | SEC oversees FINRA (SRO for broker-dealers) |
| **Budget** | ~AUD $500 million | ~USD $2.2 billion |
| **Staff** | ~1,800 | ~5,000 |
| **Enforcement posture** | Historically lighter; strengthened post-Royal Commission | Aggressive — large fines, frequent enforcement actions |
| **Product intervention** | Yes — can ban products proactively | Limited — generally requires proving violations |
| **Crypto regulation** | Evolving — case-by-case assessment, no specific crypto legislation | SEC vs CFTC jurisdictional battles, aggressive enforcement |
| **CFD/binary options** | Restricted/banned for retail | Mostly banned for retail (via CFTC) |
| **Super/retirement** | Does not regulate super funds (ATO/APRA), but regulates products and advice | SEC regulates some retirement products (securities) |

## SMSF Regulation Split

A common source of confusion: ASIC does **not** regulate [[smsf|SMSFs]]. The regulatory framework is split:

| Regulator | What They Regulate |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **ATO** | SMSFs — compliance, annual returns, audits, trustee obligations |
| **APRA** | APRA-regulated super funds (industry, retail, corporate, public sector) |
| **ASIC** | Financial products that super funds invest in, financial advice about super, AFSL holders who provide super-related services |

This means an SMSF trustee investing in ASX shares through a CHESS-sponsored broker interacts with ATO regulation (for the fund itself), ASIC regulation (for the broker and the shares), and ASX regulation (for the market and settlement system).

## Structure and Leadership

- **Chair**: Appointed by the Treasurer for a fixed term (typically 5 years)
- **Commissioners**: Multiple commissioners with specific portfolio responsibilities
- **Regional offices**: Sydney (headquarters), Melbourne, Brisbane, Perth, Adelaide, Hobart, Darwin
- **Funded by**: Industry levies (since 2017) — financial services firms pay levies to fund ASIC's regulation of their sector

## How ASIC Affects Traders

For an Australian retail or professional trader, ASIC's rules shape day-to-day trading in concrete ways:

| Area | Practical effect for traders |
|------|------------------------------|
| **CFD leverage caps (2021)** | A retail CFD account in Australia is capped at 30:1 (major FX) down to 2:1 (crypto); offshore alternatives carry counterparty/recovery risk (see table above) |
| **Negative-balance protection** | Retail CFD accounts cannot go below zero — the broker absorbs adverse gaps, a free downside option similar to [[esma\|ESMA]]'s EU rule |
| **Binary options ban** | Retail traders cannot legally buy binary options from an AU-licensed provider |
| **Design & Distribution (DDO)** | Brokers must restrict complex products (CFDs, leveraged ETFs, options) to their defined target market — affects who can open what account |
| **Wholesale / sophisticated investor test** | Meeting the wholesale-client thresholds (e.g., A$2.5M net assets or A$250K gross income, or a A$500K product investment) unlocks products and leverage unavailable to retail — Australia's analogue to ESMA's Professional-Client uplift |
| **Market-integrity surveillance** | Insider trading, [[market-manipulation\|manipulation]], spoofing, and wash trading on the ASX are policed via ASIC's MAI system; enforcement can void trades and ban participants |
| **AFSL requirement** | Only [[afsl\|AFSL]]-licensed (or authorised) entities can legally offer broking or advice — a quick AFSL-status check is the first counterparty-risk filter for any AU broker |

These overlap heavily with the global retail-derivative protection wave that [[esma|ESMA]] (EU) and the FCA (UK) implemented around the same period — see [[risk-management]] for why leverage caps and negative-balance protection matter to survivorship.
