# FINRA (Financial Industry Regulatory Authority)

FINRA is the largest independent **self-regulatory organization (SRO)** in the United States, overseeing virtually all US broker-dealers and their registered representatives. It was created in 2007 from the consolidation of the National Association of Securities Dealers (NASD) and the member-regulation, enforcement, and arbitration functions of the New York Stock Exchange. FINRA derives its authority from the [[sec|SEC]], which delegates day-to-day broker-dealer oversight to FINRA under Section 15A of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934.

## Overview

FINRA regulates approximately **3,200 brokerage firms** and roughly **640,000 registered representatives**. Per FINRA's 2025 Industry Snapshot (published July 2025), registered representatives reached **639,723** — a fourth consecutive year of growth, with 40,000-45,000 people entering the industry annually — while the number of member firms continued a long consolidation trend, declining from 3,394 in 2021 to around 3,200. Every US broker-dealer that interacts with retail or institutional public customers must be a FINRA member, and every individual representative must pass FINRA-administered qualification exams (Series 7, Series 63, Series 65, Series 66, etc.). FINRA has been led by President and CEO **Robert W. Cook** since 2016.

FINRA is **not a government agency**. It is a not-for-profit organization funded by member firm dues, transaction-based fees, and fines — not by Congressional appropriations. This funding model gives FINRA operational independence but also creates a complex incentive structure: it polices the same firms that fund it.

## What FINRA Does

| Function | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Rule-making** | Drafts and amends the FINRA Rulebook, subject to SEC approval. Rules cover sales practices, trading conduct, financial responsibility, communications with the public, and reporting obligations. |
| **Examination** | Conducts routine cycle exams (typically every 1-4 years depending on firm size and risk) and cause exams triggered by complaints or red flags. |
| **Enforcement** | Investigates rule violations, brings disciplinary actions, levies fines, suspends or bars individuals, and expels firms. |
| **Market Surveillance** | Operates surveillance over equities markets and OTC trading on behalf of multiple exchanges through Regulatory Service Agreements (RSAs). |
| **Dispute Resolution** | Operates the largest securities arbitration and mediation forum in the US — most retail brokerage disputes are resolved through FINRA arbitration. |
| **Registration and Licensing** | Administers qualification exams and maintains the registration database for representatives and firms. |
| **Public Disclosure** | Operates BrokerCheck (free public lookup of broker and firm history) and the Central Registration Depository (CRD). |

## Key Rules for Options Traders

FINRA's rulebook contains several rules of direct relevance to options traders:

| Rule | Subject | Trader Impact |
|------|---------|---------------|
| **Rule 2360** | Options | Governs broker-dealer options activity. Requires firms to assign customers an options approval level (commonly Level 1-4 or 1-5 depending on broker) before allowing them to trade options. Mandates delivery of the Options Disclosure Document (ODD) before account approval. |
| **Rule 4210** | Margin Requirements | Sets minimum margin requirements for equity, options, and security futures positions. Includes the **Pattern Day Trader (PDT) rule**: any margin account that executes 4 or more day trades within 5 business days must maintain minimum equity of $25,000. |
| **Rule 5310** | Best Execution and Interpositioning | Requires brokers to use reasonable diligence to obtain the best market for customer orders given prevailing conditions. Underpins debates over [[payment-for-order-flow]] and order routing. |
| **Rule 2111** | Suitability | Requires reasonable basis to believe a recommended transaction or strategy is suitable for the customer based on their investment profile. Largely subsumed by SEC's [[regulation|Regulation Best Interest]] (Reg BI) for retail recommendations as of 2020, but the FINRA suitability rule still applies in institutional and non-recommendation contexts. |
| **Rule 3110** | Supervision | Requires firms to establish and maintain a supervisory system reasonably designed to achieve compliance with securities laws and FINRA rules. |
| **Rule 2210** | Communications with the Public | Governs marketing, social media posts, and any public-facing communication by a broker-dealer or its representatives. Heavy fines are common for non-compliant influencer-style content. |
| **Rule 6432** | Compliance with the SEC's OTC Quotation Rule (15c2-11) | Constrains broker-dealers from publishing quotes in OTC securities without adequate current information about the issuer. |

## Relationship with SEC

FINRA operates **under [[sec|SEC]] oversight** as a registered SRO. The relationship is hierarchical but cooperative:

- The SEC has authority to approve, disapprove, or amend FINRA rule proposals.
- The SEC can review FINRA disciplinary decisions on appeal.
- The SEC retains enforcement authority over the same firms FINRA regulates and may pursue parallel actions.
- FINRA frequently refers significant cases (e.g., suspected criminal fraud, large insider trading rings) to the SEC and the Department of Justice.
- FINRA provides examination resources and surveillance data the SEC relies on heavily.

In practice, most retail brokerage discipline flows through FINRA. The SEC focuses on issuers, advisers, market structure, and the largest cases.

## BrokerCheck and CRD

The **Central Registration Depository (CRD)** is the licensing and registration database FINRA jointly operates with state securities regulators (NASAA). It contains the employment, qualification, customer complaint, regulatory action, and bankruptcy history of every registered representative and member firm.

**BrokerCheck** (https://brokercheck.finra.org) is the free public-facing portal into CRD. Any retail customer can look up:

- A broker's firm history and tenure
- Customer complaints (pending, settled, or denied)
- Regulatory disclosures (fines, suspensions, bars)
- Criminal disclosures
- Bankruptcy filings

For options traders, BrokerCheck is a useful tool for vetting independent advisors and confirming that the rep handling a complex options account does not have a pattern of customer disputes or suitability violations.

## Enforcement Powers

FINRA's enforcement powers are **civil and regulatory in nature**, not criminal. FINRA cannot indict, prosecute, or imprison anyone — those powers belong to the DOJ and US Attorneys.

| Sanction | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Censure** | Formal written rebuke recorded on the rep's CRD permanently |
| **Fine** | Monetary penalties, ranging from a few thousand dollars to multi-million-dollar firm-level fines |
| **Suspension** | Temporary prohibition from associating with a member firm (days to years) |
| **Bar** | Permanent prohibition from associating with any FINRA member in any capacity |
| **Expulsion** | Termination of a firm's FINRA membership; functionally ends its broker-dealer business |
| **Restitution** | Order requiring repayment to harmed customers |
| **Heightened Supervision** | Conditions on continued employment requiring enhanced oversight |

Disciplinary decisions are appealable internally to the National Adjudicatory Council, then to the SEC, then to federal courts of appeals.

## Why It Matters for Traders

1. **Broker accountability** — Every US broker you can legally use (interactive-brokers, charles-schwab, [[robinhood]], etrade, tastytrade, webull, etc.) is a FINRA member subject to its rules. FINRA fines and suspensions create real consequences for misconduct.
2. **Complaint mechanism** — Retail customers can file complaints directly with FINRA, which will investigate and may force broker action. This is independent of any private litigation.
3. **Public record of rep history** — Before opening an account with a financial advisor or trusting a tip from a registered rep, a 60-second BrokerCheck lookup can surface multiple prior customer disputes or regulatory violations.
4. **Arbitration default** — Nearly every retail brokerage account agreement contains a mandatory FINRA arbitration clause. Disputes go to FINRA's arbitration forum, not federal court. Award outcomes vary, but the forum is the standard for broker-customer disputes.
5. **Options approval gating** — A trader cannot legally trade options at a US broker without going through FINRA Rule 2360 approval. The ODD must be delivered, and the broker must collect financial and experience information. This is why every broker asks for income, net worth, and trading experience before enabling options.
6. **PDT rule constraints** — Sub-$25K margin accounts cannot day-trade more than 3 times in 5 business days without restriction. This rule shapes the entire low-capital active trading landscape.

## Limitations

- **Not a federal agency** — FINRA's authority is delegated and bounded; it cannot make law, levy criminal charges, or compel non-members to do anything.
- **Limited to broker-dealer member firms** — FINRA does **not** regulate registered investment advisers (RIAs), banks, futures-only firms, hedge funds (unless they have a broker-dealer affiliate), or non-US firms operating offshore.
- **Funded by the regulated** — Critics note the inherent tension in member-funded regulation; FINRA has been criticized for under-enforcing against major firms relative to small ones.
- **Arbitration concerns** — Mandatory arbitration limits customers' access to courts and class actions, a perennial source of consumer-advocate criticism.
- **No jurisdiction over crypto-only platforms** — FINRA's authority covers securities and broker-dealers. Pure crypto exchanges that do not handle securities are outside its scope (unless they hold a broker-dealer registration).

## Recent Developments (2025-2026)

- **FINRA Forward (March 2025)** — A broad modernization initiative announced by CEO Robert Cook, comprising a comprehensive review of FINRA's rulebook to eliminate outdated or duplicative requirements, expanded support for member-firm compliance (especially smaller firms), and intensified work combating financial fraud against investors (imposter websites, social-media scams). It builds on the earlier FINRA 360 self-review.
- **Continued industry consolidation** — The 2025 Industry Snapshot confirmed fewer firms but more representatives, with growth concentrated at the largest firms and continued expansion of dual broker-dealer/RIA registration.
- **Surveillance scale** — FINRA processes on the order of hundreds of billions of market events per day across the equity and options markets it surveils under Regulatory Service Agreements, making it one of the largest market-surveillance operations in the world.
