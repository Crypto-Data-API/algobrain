# Bank of England

The **Bank of England (BoE)** is the central bank of the United Kingdom, founded in 1694. It sets UK monetary policy, manages [[interest-rates]], and serves as lender of last resort. For traders, BoE rate decisions, inflation reports, and Governor speeches are among the highest-impact scheduled catalysts for GBP [[forex]] pairs and the UK gilt market.

The BoE is notably associated with **[[black-wednesday]]** (September 16, 1992), when [[george-soros]] famously shorted the British pound, forcing the UK to withdraw from the European Exchange Rate Mechanism and earning Soros over $1 billion.

## History and Role

Founded in 1694, the Bank of England is one of the oldest [[central-bank]] institutions in the world. It was originally established to fund England's war against France and was nationalized in 1946. The BoE gained operational independence to set [[interest-rates]] in 1997 under the Blair government, a pivotal reform that insulated monetary policy from political interference.

The **Monetary Policy Committee (MPC)**, composed of nine members, meets eight times per year to set the **Bank Rate** -- the UK's benchmark interest rate that influences borrowing costs throughout the economy. The BoE also serves as prudential regulator of UK banks and insurers (via the Prudential Regulation Authority), and manages the stability of the British pound (GBP) and the broader UK financial system. **Andrew Bailey** has been Governor since March 2020.

## Black Wednesday and Market Significance

The events of [[black-wednesday]] in 1992 remain one of the most dramatic episodes in central banking history. The UK had pegged the pound to the Deutsche Mark through the ERM, but the rate was unsustainably high. [[george-soros]] and other speculators built massive short positions against the pound. Despite the BoE raising interest rates twice in a single day (from 10% to 15%) and spending billions in reserves to defend the peg, the pound collapsed and the UK was forced to exit the ERM. The episode demonstrated the limits of [[central-bank]] intervention against determined market forces.

For traders, BoE rate decisions, inflation reports, and speeches by the Governor are key catalysts for GBP [[forex]] pairs and UK gilt (government bond) markets.

## The 2021-2026 Cycle

After raising Bank Rate from 0.10% (December 2021) to a peak of **5.25%** (August 2023) to fight post-pandemic inflation, the MPC began a gradual easing cycle in August 2024:

| Date | Decision | Bank Rate | Vote |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aug 2024 | Cut 25bp (first of cycle) | 5.00% | 5-4 |
| Nov 2024 | Cut 25bp | 4.75% | 8-1 |
| Feb 2025 | Cut 25bp | 4.50% | 7-2 |
| May 2025 | Cut 25bp | 4.25% | 5-4 |
| Aug 2025 | Cut 25bp | 4.00% | 5-4 |
| Sep 2025 | Hold | 4.00% | 7-2 |
| Dec 2025 (announced Dec 18) | Cut 25bp | 3.75% | 5-4 |
| Apr 30, 2026 | Hold | 3.75% | — |

As of June 2026 Bank Rate stands at **3.75%**, with the next MPC decision scheduled for **June 18, 2026**. The December 2025 cut was a knife-edge 5-4 vote with Governor Bailey siding with the doves while inflation was still running around 3.2%. Several 2024-2026 decisions were decided by a single vote, making MPC member speeches and vote splits unusually market-moving for GBP and short sterling (SONIA) futures.

On the balance-sheet side, the BoE has been running **quantitative tightening** (active gilt sales plus passive run-off) since 2022; at the September 2025 meeting the MPC slowed the annual QT pace from £100bn to £70bn for the 2025-26 year, partly in response to long-gilt yield pressure.

## Trading Relevance

- **GBP pairs** (GBP/USD "Cable", EUR/GBP) reprice on rate decisions, vote splits, and CPI/labour data that shift the expected BoE path
- **Gilts and SONIA futures** are the direct instruments for trading the BoE path; the 30-year gilt yield hit multi-decade highs in 2025 amid fiscal worries and QT supply
- **Vote-split asymmetry**: with repeated 5-4 decisions in 2024-2026, the composition of dissent is itself a signal traders parse within seconds of release
- **Rate differentials vs the [[federal-reserve]] and ECB** drive medium-term GBP trends — a core input for [[macro-trading]]
