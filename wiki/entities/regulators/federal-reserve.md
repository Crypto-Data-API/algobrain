# Federal Reserve

The Federal Reserve (commonly "the Fed") is the central banking system of the United States and arguably the single most powerful institution in global financial markets. Its decisions on [[interest-rates]], money supply, and emergency lending programs move every asset class — stocks, bonds, [[commodities]], [[forex]], and crypto.

## Overview

Established by the Federal Reserve Act of 1913, the Fed operates through a Board of Governors, 12 regional Federal Reserve Banks, and the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC), which sets monetary policy. The Fed has a dual mandate: maximum employment and stable prices (targeting approximately 2% inflation).

## Key Functions

- **Federal Funds Rate**: The FOMC sets the target range for the overnight lending rate between banks, which ripples through all borrowing costs, asset valuations, and economic activity.
- **Quantitative Easing (QE) / Tightening (QT)**: The Fed buys or sells Treasury and mortgage-backed securities to expand or contract the money supply. QE following the [[2008-global-financial-crisis]] and COVID-19 pandemic fundamentally reshaped markets.
- **Lender of Last Resort**: Provides emergency [[liquidity]] to prevent systemic collapses, as seen with bear-stearns, aig, and during the 2023 banking crisis.
- **Banking Regulation**: Supervises and regulates bank holding companies and financial institutions.

## Policy Path 2022-2026

After the fastest hiking cycle in four decades (0-0.25% in March 2022 to a peak of **5.25-5.50%** by July 2023) to fight post-pandemic inflation, the Fed pivoted to easing:

| Period | Action | Target range after |
|---|---|---|
| Jul 2023 - Aug 2024 | Hold at peak | 5.25-5.50% |
| Sep 2024 | Cut 50bp (first of cycle) | 4.75-5.00% |
| Nov 2024 / Dec 2024 | Cut 25bp each | 4.25-4.50% |
| Jan - Jul 2025 | Hold (five consecutive meetings) | 4.25-4.50% |
| Sep 17, 2025 | Cut 25bp | 4.00-4.25% |
| Oct 29, 2025 | Cut 25bp | 3.75-4.00% |
| Dec 10, 2025 | Cut 25bp | 3.50-3.75% |

As of June 2026 the target range stands at **3.50-3.75%**, with the June 16-17, 2026 FOMC meeting the first chaired by the new Fed Chair.

## Leadership Transition 2026

Jerome Powell's term as Chair expired in May 2026 after eight years (2018-2026) marked in the final stretch by sustained public pressure from President Trump for faster rate cuts. **Kevin Warsh** — a former Fed governor (2006-2011) — was confirmed by the Senate on **May 13, 2026** in a 54-45 vote, the most divisive confirmation ever for a Fed chair, and was sworn in on **May 22, 2026** for a four-year term running to May 2030. Powell served as chair pro tempore during the one-week gap and, in a break with tradition, remained on the Board as a governor (his separate governor term runs to January 2028) rather than leaving the Fed.

For traders, the transition is itself a regime variable: markets price not just the rate path but the new chair's reaction function, his perceived independence from the White House, and the unusual dynamic of a former chair still voting on the FOMC.

## Trading Relevance

"Don't fight the Fed" is one of the oldest maxims in trading. FOMC meeting dates are among the most important events on any trader's calendar. Rate decisions, press conferences, dot plots, and meeting minutes can trigger massive moves across all markets. Key instruments and signals:

- **Fed funds futures and SOFR futures** (CME) — the market's live probability distribution over future FOMC decisions (the basis of the CME FedWatch tool)
- **2s10s and the Treasury curve** — repricing of the Fed path drives curve steepeners/flatteners
- **DXY and major [[forex]] pairs** — rate differentials vs the ECB, [[bank-of-england]], and BoJ
- **Equity factor rotation** — duration-sensitive growth vs value rotates on Fed-path surprises

Understanding the Fed's reaction function — how it responds to inflation, employment, and financial stability risks — is foundational to [[macro-trading]] and essential context for interpreting the strategies of traders like [[george-soros]], [[ray-dalio]], and [[paul-tudor-jones]].
