The **Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC)** is a U.S. government agency that insures bank deposits (up to **$250,000 per depositor, per insured bank, per ownership category**) and acts as the **receiver** for failed banks. Created by the Banking Act of 1933 during the [[great-depression|Great Depression]] to halt the bank runs that had destroyed confidence in the banking system, the FDIC is a cornerstone of U.S. financial stability. For traders, the FDIC matters most during banking crises: its decisions on which banks to close, whether to invoke the systemic-risk exception, and how to treat uninsured depositors directly move bank equities, credit spreads, and broad risk sentiment.

## How the FDIC Works

- **Deposit Insurance Fund (DIF)**: The FDIC does not run on taxpayer money in the first instance. It is funded by **risk-based assessments (premiums)** levied on insured banks, accumulated in the DIF. The DIF maintains a target reserve ratio (a statutory minimum of 1.35% of insured deposits).
- **The $250,000 limit**: Raised from $100,000 during the [[2008-global-financial-crisis|2008 crisis]] and made permanent by the Dodd-Frank Act. The limit applies per ownership category, so a household can structure accounts to insure more.
- **Resolution and receivership**: When a bank fails, the FDIC is appointed receiver. It typically arranges a **purchase-and-assumption (P&A)** transaction — a healthy acquirer buys the failed bank's deposits and good assets over the weekend — so depositors regain access by Monday. Failing that, it pays insured depositors directly.
- **Supervision**: The FDIC is the primary federal supervisor of state-chartered banks that are not members of the Federal Reserve System, and shares oversight with the [[federal-reserve|Fed]] and OCC.

## Role in Market Events

### 2008 Global Financial Crisis

During the [[2008-global-financial-crisis|2008 GFC]], the FDIC:
- Closed 25 banks in 2008 and 140 in 2009 (versus ~3 per year in 2005–2007)
- Managed the resolution of **Washington Mutual** ($307B in assets — the largest bank failure in U.S. history), whose deposits were sold to JPMorgan
- Implemented the **Temporary Liquidity Guarantee Program (TLGP)**, guaranteeing newly issued bank debt and non-interest-bearing transaction accounts to unfreeze credit markets
- Saw the DIF fall into negative territory, forcing banks to prepay three years of assessments

### 2023 Regional Banking Crisis

In March 2023, three large banks failed in rapid succession after deposit runs amplified by social media and mobile banking:

| Bank | Assets | Date | FDIC Action |
|------|--------|------|-------------|
| **[[silicon-valley-bank|Silicon Valley Bank]]** | ~$209B | Mar 10, 2023 | Receivership; **systemic-risk exception** invoked to guarantee *all* deposits, not just the insured $250K |
| **Signature Bank** | ~$110B | Mar 12, 2023 | Receivership; full deposit guarantee |
| **First Republic Bank** | ~$229B | May 1, 2023 | FDIC-facilitated sale to JPMorgan |

[[silicon-valley-bank|SVB]] failed because rising rates had crushed the value of its long-duration bond portfolio while concentrated, uninsured tech-startup deposits fled en masse. The FDIC, [[federal-reserve|Fed]], and Treasury jointly invoked the systemic-risk exception to backstop all SVB and Signature deposits, preventing a broader run. The episode caused regional-bank stocks (KRE ETF) to fall ~30% in weeks and triggered a flight to quality into Treasuries, gold, and large-cap banks. The DIF losses (estimated ~$20B+ for SVB and ~$2.5B for Signature) were recovered via a **special assessment** on large banks.

## Trading Relevance

1. **Friday-afternoon timing.** Banks are almost always closed by the FDIC on **Friday after market close**, giving the agency the weekend to complete a P&A. Traders watching for banking stress monitor Friday-night FDIC announcements.
2. **The $250K cliff.** The insurance limit creates a sharp incentive for **uninsured** depositors (large corporates, startups, the wealthy) to flee at the first sign of trouble — the dynamic that turned SVB's problems into a one-day run. Banks with high uninsured-deposit ratios are screened as run-prone.
3. **Systemic-risk exception as a signal.** Invoking it tells the market that authorities judge contagion risk severe — a double-edged signal that calms depositors but flags acute stress.
4. **Special assessments.** Large DIF losses are clawed back from surviving big banks, a modest but real earnings headwind worth modeling after major failures.
