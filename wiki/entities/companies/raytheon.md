RTX Corporation (NYSE: RTX) — formed by the 2020 merger of Raytheon Company and United Technologies — is one of the world's largest aerospace and [[defense]] primes, operating through Collins Aerospace, Pratt & Whitney and Raytheon. For traders it is a mega-cap [[industrials|Industrials]] defense bellwether in the [[s-and-p-500]] and Dow, with a record ~$268B backlog that makes it a core long-cycle play on global defense spending and the commercial-aerospace recovery.

## Business Overview & Segments

RTX runs three operating segments spanning commercial aerospace and [[defense]]. Roughly 60% commercial / 40% defense by backlog, giving RTX a balanced exposure to the airline-travel cycle on one side and government budgets on the other — a deliberately diversified profile versus pure-play primes like [[lockheed-martin]] or [[northrop-grumman]].

| Segment | What it is | Key programs / brands | Demand driver |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Collins Aerospace** | Avionics, interiors, mechanical & structural systems, power & controls — the largest aerospace supplier by breadth | Avionics suites, ejection seats, landing systems, cabin interiors | OEM build rates + high-margin commercial **aftermarket** (spares/MRO) |
| **Pratt & Whitney** | Commercial & military jet engines, including the geared turbofan (GTF) | GTF (A320neo family), F135 (F-35), military engines | Narrowbody build rates, engine aftermarket, fighter procurement |
| **Raytheon** (defense) | Missiles, air-and-missile defense, radars, effectors | Patriot, NASAMS, SM-3/SM-6, AMRAAM, Tomahawk, radars | Geopolitical demand, US/NATO budgets, foreign military sales (FMS) |

The Pratt & Whitney GTF powder-metal inspection program has been a multi-year cash and reputational drag that is now working through; Raytheon's missile-defense book is the segment most directly tied to elevated geopolitical demand.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

RTX holds a wide [[economic-moat|economic moat]] rooted in the structural features of the aerospace and [[defense]] complex:

- **Razor-and-blade aftermarket** — Collins and Pratt sell engines and equipment at modest OEM margins, then earn decades of high-margin spares, repairs and overhaul (MRO) on an installed base that takes 20–40 years to retire. This recurring revenue is the most valuable, lowest-cyclicality part of the franchise.
- **Programmatic lock-in & [[switching-costs]]** — once an engine or radar is designed onto an airframe or weapon system, it is effectively sole-source for the program's life; re-qualifying a competitor is prohibitively expensive and slow.
- **Certification & regulatory barriers** — FAA/EASA airworthiness certification and US security clearances create enormous barriers to entry; the qualified prime universe is a handful of firms.
- **Backlog visibility** — a record ~$268B backlog ($161B commercial / $107B defense) provides multi-year revenue visibility rare outside the defense complex.
- **Scale & IP** — decades of accumulated engineering IP in propulsion, sensing and effectors that a new entrant cannot replicate.

The constraint on the moat is execution risk (the GTF episode) and the fixed-price-development trap that periodically bites defense primes; pricing power on government contracts is bounded by budget politics.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Global defense rearmament** — elevated US/NATO budgets, European rearmament, and a strained munitions-restocking cycle drive the Raytheon segment; air-and-missile defense (Patriot, NASAMS, SM-series) is in structural demand.
- **Foreign military sales (FMS)** — geopolitical tension converts into multi-year international missile and radar orders at attractive margins.
- **Commercial aerospace recovery** — record narrowbody/widebody build rates and a fully recovered air-travel cycle drive Collins OEM and, more importantly, high-margin aftermarket.
- **GTF normalization** — as the powder-metal inspection program works through, the associated cash drag reverses, freeing free cash flow.
- **Backlog conversion & FCF recovery** — the ~$268B backlog converting into revenue and the 2026 FCF guide ($8.25–8.75B) are the central re-rating story.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly earnings/FCF prints (late Jan/Apr/Jul/Oct), defense budget cycles, large missile-defense awards, GTF inspection cadence, and commercial build-rate data.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Record backlog gives multi-year revenue visibility and the GTF cash drag is reversing — a clean FCF-recovery setup.
- Structural defense rearmament (US/NATO/Europe) plus munitions restocking underpins the Raytheon segment for years.
- Commercial aerospace aftermarket is firing on a fully recovered travel cycle — the highest-margin, most durable cash stream.
- Diversified across commercial and defense, so neither an airline downturn nor a defense-budget pause sinks the whole company.

### Bear case
- Trailing margins and PE (~51x) are depressed by prior GTF charges and merger amortization; the recovery has to actually show up.
- Fixed-price development contracts can produce surprise charges; execution risk is a recurring feature of the prime model.
- Defense revenue depends on government budgets that are politically and fiscally constrained over the long run.
- Commercial aerospace is cyclical; a travel/OEM downturn would hit Collins OEM volumes and pressure aftermarket.
- Balance-sheet metrics are tight (current ratio <1) versus quality-compounder peers.

## Key Risks

- **Execution / charge risk** — the GTF powder-metal episode showed how a single program can create a multi-billion cash and reputational drag; fixed-price development contracts carry similar tail risk.
- **Budget & political** — US/NATO defense appropriations, continuing-resolution dysfunction, and shifting procurement priorities can defer awards.
- **Commercial cyclicality** — an airline-demand or OEM build-rate downturn hits Collins OEM volumes and, with a lag, the aftermarket.
- **Supply chain & labor** — aerospace supply-chain constraints (castings, forgings, skilled labor) cap delivery rates.
- **Leverage & liquidity** — tight current/quick ratios and merger-era debt; less balance-sheet cushion than [[lockheed-martin]] or [[general-dynamics]] in a downturn.
- **Valuation** — the market prices the backlog-conversion and FCF-recovery story; any slippage de-rates a premium multiple.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

RTX trades on a trailing PE (~51x) that overstates expensiveness — the figure is depressed by prior-year GTF charges and merger amortization rather than by a rich forward multiple; the market increasingly values RTX on backlog conversion and free-cash-flow recovery (see the Fundamentals block below for the figures as pulled). Mechanically:

- **Lower beta** than the broad market — a defensive [[industrials]]/[[defense]] holding that can bid on geopolitical risk-off.
- **Dividend** — RTX pays a meaningful, regularly raised dividend (the data block shows the API-defined yield; the real market yield is roughly in the ~2% area for a large-cap defense prime); capital return is dividend-plus-buyback, not a token payout like high-growth tech.
- **Earnings behavior** — the stock trades on adjusted EPS and especially the free-cash-flow print and 2026 guide; backlog and book-to-bill commentary move it as much as the headline.
- **Index torque** — both [[s-and-p-500]] and Dow membership; moves with the defense complex on budget and conflict headlines.
