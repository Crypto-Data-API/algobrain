Wesfarmers Limited (ASX: WES) is one of Australia's largest listed conglomerates and a core [[asx-200]] [[consumer-discretionary|Consumer Discretionary]] constituent, with a diversified portfolio spanning retail ([[bunnings|Bunnings]], Kmart, Target, Officeworks), health (Priceline), chemicals/energy and fertilisers, industrial safety, and lithium (via the Covalent Lithium JV / Mount Holland project). Wesfarmers is built around a head office that allocates capital across operating divisions — acquiring, improving, retaining or divesting businesses — and is widely regarded as one of the best-managed companies on the ASX, with a multi-decade record of disciplined [[capital-allocation]]. Its crown jewel, Bunnings, dominates Australian home-improvement retail with a roughly 50% market share and a category-killer scale advantage that few peers can challenge.

Fred McNaught discusses Wesfarmers primarily through the lens of its [[demergers|demerger]] of [[coles|Coles Group]] in 2018. The Coles demerger is one of Fred's key case studies — he notes that Coles performed "much better than anticipated" as an independent company, validating the demerger thesis that embedded value can be unlocked when a subsidiary is freed from conglomerate structure. Fred also references Wesfarmers' management quality and track record of buying underperforming businesses, improving them operationally, and either retaining or divesting them at a profit. The Bunnings hardware chain, which dominates Australian hardware retail with 50%+ market share, remains the jewel in Wesfarmers' portfolio.

## Business Model & Segments

Wesfarmers reports through several principal divisions:

- **Bunnings** — Australia and New Zealand's leading home improvement and outdoor living retailer, serving consumers and trade/builder customers.
- **Kmart Group** — Kmart Australia/New Zealand and Target Australia department stores selling apparel, general merchandise, homewares, toys and consumables, plus the Anko product brand which is increasingly sold globally to third-party retailers.
- **Officeworks** — Australia-wide office supplies, technology and print/create retailer.
- **Wesfarmers Health** — Priceline Pharmacy network, Australian Pharmaceutical Industries (API) pharmacy wholesale distribution, InstantScripts digital health platform, MediAesthetics clinical cosmetic / skin care clinics and Clear Skincare.
- **Chemicals, Energy and Fertilisers (WesCEF)** — manufactures ammonia, ammonium nitrate, industrial chemicals, sodium cyanide, chlor-alkali, phosphate / nitrogen / potassium-based fertilisers, LPG and LNG (Kleenheat), and lithium hydroxide (via Covalent Lithium).
- **Industrial and Safety** — Blackwoods (industrial MRO), Workwear Group, Coregas (industrial gases) and related businesses.
- **OneDigital and Other** — includes the OnePass subscription programme, Catch marketplace, retail media network and other digital / corporate investments.

Wesfarmers retains a strategic shareholding in BWP Trust (Bunnings property) and in BHP-Tianqi JV-related entities through Covalent Lithium.

## History

- **1914**: Founded by the Farmers' and Settlers' Association of Western Australia as Westralian Farmers' Co-operative Limited, providing services and merchandise to WA farmers.
- **1984**: Restructured from cooperative to public company; listed on the ASX as Wesfarmers Limited.
- **1990s–2000s**: Acquired Bunnings (1994), the Landmark agribusiness, Howard Smith industrial business (including Bunnings assets), and diverse industrial and retail businesses.
- **23 November 2007**: Completed the A$22 billion acquisition of Coles Group — at the time the largest takeover in Australian corporate history — bringing Coles supermarkets, Kmart, Target, Officeworks and Coles Express into the group.
- **2010s**: Progressive divestment of non-core businesses (Curragh coal, Tyre & Auto, etc.) and a failed UK Bunnings / Homebase expansion (divested 2018).
- **21 November 2018**: Demerged Coles Group as a separately listed ASX company (ASX: COL), retaining approximately 15% (subsequently reduced).
- **March 2022**: Acquired Australian Pharmaceutical Industries (API), creating the Wesfarmers Health division (Priceline, Clear Skincare, Soul Pattinson pharmacy distribution).
- **2023–2024**: Covalent Lithium development — Mt Holland mine and Kwinana refinery constructed.
- **July 2025**: First lithium hydroxide product achieved at Kwinana.

## Recent Developments

Under Managing Director Rob Scott (CEO since November 2017), Wesfarmers delivered record earnings in FY25 and continued to invest in strategic growth:

- **FY25 results (year to 30 June 2025)**: Statutory net profit after tax of A$2,926 million, up 14.4% on FY24. Group revenue of A$45.7 billion, up 3.4%. Strong contributions from Bunnings, Kmart Group (including the Anko sourcing model), Officeworks and Wesfarmers Health.
- **Wesfarmers Health FY25**: Revenue up, earnings up 28% to A$64 million, with Priceline Pharmacy sales up 14.4% led by dispensary growth, network expansion and strong personal care performance.
- **Covalent Lithium**: Kwinana refinery achieved first lithium hydroxide product in July 2025; FY26 is a transitional ramp-up year with customer qualification processes ongoing.
- **1H FY26 (to 31 December 2025)**: Continued growth across retail divisions, with Wesfarmers Health revenue up 8.4% and earnings up 35.7%.
- **Infinity Pharmacy Group receivership (December 2025)**: API (a Wesfarmers Health company) appointed receivers and administrators over 56 pharmacies within the Infinity Pharmacy Group following deterioration in the group's financial position. Wesfarmers Health clarified that Priceline Pharmacy stores are not affected.
- **AI and technology investment**: Wesfarmers has accelerated AI adoption across its retail operations, including supply chain and customer-facing tools.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Wesfarmers' durable advantage is **not** a single business but the combination of dominant category positions and a disciplined head-office capital-allocation engine — a wide [[economic-moat|economic moat]] expressed through several reinforcing layers:

- **Bunnings scale / category-killer dynamics** — Bunnings holds an estimated ~50% share of Australian home improvement, with a store footprint, buying power and trade relationships that no competitor can match. Scale lets it under-price rivals while still earning high returns, a self-reinforcing flywheel (lower costs → lower prices → more volume → lower costs). For trade customers, account relationships, credit terms and the breadth of in-stock range create mild [[switching-costs]].
- **Kmart / Anko vertical sourcing** — Kmart's in-house Anko brand controls design and sourcing end-to-end, producing private-label goods at price points pure resellers cannot match; the model is now being wholesaled to third-party retailers globally, extending the moat beyond Kmart's own stores.
- **Capital-allocation track record** — the head office is the real moat: a multi-decade record of buying underperforming assets, improving them operationally, and either retaining or divesting at a profit (e.g. the [[coles]] acquisition and later [[demergers|demerger]], exits from coal, Curragh, UK Bunnings/Homebase). This compounding discipline is rare and hard to replicate.
- **Diversification / portfolio optionality** — exposure across home improvement, discount retail, health, industrial chemicals and now lithium smooths the cycle and provides redeployment optionality that a single-line retailer lacks.

The moat is **wide but cyclical** rather than impregnable: most divisions sell into discretionary consumer demand, and the newer lithium and health pillars are still proving their economics.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Bunnings expansion** — new-format and smaller-footprint stores, trade/commercial penetration, and adjacency expansion (e.g. pet, cleaning, automotive) extend the largest profit engine.
- **Kmart Anko global wholesale** — turning Anko into a third-party global sourcing/wholesale brand is a structurally higher-margin growth vector beyond Kmart's own store network.
- **Wesfarmers Health rollup** — Priceline network expansion, API integration, InstantScripts digital health and Clear Skincare build a deliberate "fourth pillar"; M&A optionality remains.
- **Lithium ramp** — Covalent Lithium (Mt Holland mine + Kwinana refinery) reached first lithium hydroxide in July 2025; the ramp through FY26 and beyond adds commodity-linked earnings and price-cycle leverage to WesCEF.
- **OneDigital / OnePass** — the OnePass subscription, Catch marketplace and retail-media network aim to build cross-divisional loyalty and data infrastructure that lifts retention and ad revenue.
- **Capital management** — disciplined balance-sheet use, buybacks and franked dividends; potential future demergers/divestitures remain part of the playbook.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year (Feb) and full-year (Aug) results, Bunnings/Kmart like-for-like sales prints, lithium pricing and Kwinana ramp updates, and consumer-spending/housing data.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Bunnings is a genuine category killer with a ~50% share, high returns and a long runway — a defensive-cyclical compounder.
- Best-in-class capital allocation: management has repeatedly created value through acquisitions, operational turnarounds and well-timed [[demergers]].
- Diversification across retail, health, chemicals and lithium smooths the cycle and provides redeployment optionality.
- Anko global wholesale and Wesfarmers Health are credible new growth pillars not fully reflected in legacy numbers.
- Strong, franked dividend stream backed by resilient cash generation.

### Bear case
- The business is ultimately leveraged to discretionary consumer spending and the housing cycle; a downturn hits Bunnings and Kmart simultaneously.
- A premium multiple (PE in the low-30s per the Fundamentals block) leaves little margin for a consumer or execution stumble.
- Lithium is a new, capital-intensive, volatile commodity exposure with an unproven Kwinana ramp.
- The health rollup faces formidable Chemist Warehouse–Sigma scale, and pharmacy integration (e.g. the 2025 Infinity Pharmacy receivership) carries execution risk.
- Regulatory/ACCC scrutiny of large retailers and any future large acquisition could constrain the playbook.

## Key Risks

- **Consumer & housing cycle** — Bunnings and Kmart earnings ride discretionary spending and home-improvement activity; a recession or housing slowdown compresses the largest profit pools.
- **Lithium price & ramp risk** — WesCEF's new lithium hydroxide line is exposed to a volatile commodity price and to operational ramp risk at the Kwinana refinery; FY26 is a transitional year.
- **Pharmacy / health integration** — integrating API and the Priceline network, plus pharmacy-sector distress (the December 2025 Infinity Pharmacy Group receivership), is an execution and reputational risk.
- **Regulatory / ACCC** — heightened scrutiny of large Australian retailers and merger review could limit acquisitions or pressure practices.
- **Valuation / de-rating** — a premium conglomerate multiple is vulnerable to multiple compression if growth disappoints or rates rise.
- **Conglomerate discount** — diversified structures can trade at a discount to sum-of-parts if capital allocation falters.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Wesfarmers trades at a premium conglomerate multiple (PE in the low-30s per the Fundamentals block) that reflects the quality of Bunnings and management's capital-allocation record rather than a cheap valuation; the debate is whether discretionary-retail earnings can keep compounding into that price. Mechanically:

- **Defensive-cyclical character** — lower beta than a pure cyclical thanks to diversification and the staples-like resilience of home improvement, but still geared to the consumer; it is not a true defensive like a grocery duopoly name.
- **Index weight** — a top-weight [[asx-200]] [[consumer-discretionary|Consumer Discretionary]] constituent; a core holding in most Australian large-cap and income portfolios, so passive/index flows matter.
- **Franked dividend** — pays a substantial, franked dividend (see the franking-credit gross-up noted in the Fundamentals block), which materially lifts the after-tax yield for Australian-resident investors and anchors much of the shareholder base.
- **Catalysts that move the stock** — half-year/full-year results and especially Bunnings and Kmart like-for-like sales, lithium-pricing and Kwinana-ramp updates, and Australian consumer-confidence/housing data.
- **Capital return** — disciplined buybacks and dividends, with periodic special distributions and the standing optionality of further demergers/divestitures.
