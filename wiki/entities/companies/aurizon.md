**Aurizon Holdings Limited** (ASX: AZJ) is an railroads company listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. Aurizon is Australia's largest rail freight business and formerly the world's largest rail transporter of coal from mine to port. Headquartered in Brisbane (Fortitude Valley), it operates a below-rail regulated track business in Queensland and above-rail haulage across Queensland, NSW, WA and South Australia.

## Business Overview & Segments

Aurizon is Australia's largest rail-freight business and an [[industrials]]-sector infrastructure asset. It combines a **regulated below-rail track monopoly** (the Central Queensland Coal Network) with **above-rail haulage** of [[coal]] and bulk commodities. The Network segment earns a regulated return on its asset base (QCA-determined), giving bond-like, low-risk cash flow; the haulage segments are contract-driven and more exposed to commodity volumes and the structural coal phase-out — the central tension in the equity.

Aurizon reports four segments:

| Segment | What it is | Driver (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **Network** | Owns/operates the Central Queensland Coal Network (CQCN) — 2,670 km multi-user track (Blackwater, Goonyella, Newlands, Moura) linking 50+ Bowen Basin mines to ports; QCA-regulated. Also the ~2,100 km SA/NT (Tarcoola-Darwin) line | Regulated allowed-return on the asset base — the bond-like, lowest-risk earnings core |
| **Coal** | Above-rail haulage of metallurgical and thermal [[coal]] in QLD and NSW | Contracted volumes; the legacy cash cow facing the long-run coal phase-out |
| **Bulk** | Haulage of iron ore, bauxite, alumina, base metals, grain, fertiliser, cement, dangerous goods; integrated supply-chain/intermodal (One Rail Australia, 2022) | The diversification/growth engine away from coal |
| **Other** | Corporate, landbridging and other services | Small residual |

Aurizon hauls more than 250 million tonnes of freight per year on an average day of over 700,000 tonnes. The investment debate is the mix shift from regulated-Network-plus-Coal toward Bulk/containerised freight.

## History

Aurizon's origins trace to the rail operations of the Queensland Government, consolidated under the QR National brand in 2004-05 to encompass coal, bulk and containerised freight. QR National listed on the ASX on 22 November 2010 via a public float that raised A$4.6 billion, at the time Australia's largest IPO since Telstra. The company rebranded from QR National Limited to Aurizon Holdings Limited in December 2012, reflecting a broader national identity. In 2022 Aurizon acquired One Rail Australia, extending its SA/NT and intermodal footprint, and has since progressively diversified away from coal into bulk and containerised freight.

## Recent Developments

For FY26 Aurizon has guided underlying group EBITDA of A$1.68-1.75 billion, with sustaining capex of A$610-660 million (including A$30 million of Transformation Capital) and growth capex of A$100-150 million. A dividend guidance of 19-20 cents per share has been introduced, reflecting a change in Network revenue recognition. Aurizon's contract to haul Whitehaven's Gunnedah coal will end in June 2026; offsetting this, Aurizon signed a new BHP Copper South Australia contract and 10-year extensions with Minara and Karara in Western Australia. Pam Bains retired as Group Executive Network after 15 years with the company; Cat Peppler succeeded her. CEO Andrew Harding continues to execute on growth in Bulk and Containerised Freight alongside defending the Network and Coal franchises.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Aurizon has a **wide moat on the regulated track and a narrower one on haulage**:

- **Regulated track monopoly (Network)** — the CQCN is the only rail link from the Bowen Basin coal mines to their export ports; it is a natural-monopoly infrastructure asset earning a QCA-regulated return on a multi-billion-dollar asset base. This is the durable [[economic-moat|moat]]: replicating 2,670 km of multi-user heavy-haul track is impossible, and the regulated framework underpins predictable returns (the price of that protection is that the QCA caps the upside).
- **Scale & integration in haulage** — as Australia's largest rail-freight operator, Aurizon has the rollingstock, crews, terminals and supply-chain footprint that sub-scale competitors lack; long-term take-or-pay contracts create [[switching-costs]].
- **High barriers** — heavy-haul rail is enormously capital-intensive with long-dated assets; new entrants are rare.
- **Diversification optionality** — One Rail Australia and Bulk/intermodal extend the network into non-coal commodities, lengthening the franchise.

The moat's weakness is **structural, not competitive**: the coal phase-out shrinks the long-run volume base for both Network and Coal, and ESG exclusion caps the multiple. The regulated track is the fortress; coal exposure is the slow leak.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **QCA regulatory resets** — the allowed-return decision on the CQCN directly sets the value of the Network segment; a favourable reset is the single biggest re-rating lever.
- **Bulk / containerised diversification** — growth in non-coal haulage (iron ore, bauxite, base metals, grain, intermodal via One Rail) is the structural re-rating story away from coal.
- **Contract wins** — new BHP Copper South Australia contract and 10-year Minara/Karara (WA) extensions offset losses (Whitehaven Gunnedah haulage ends June 2026).
- **Cost / capital discipline** — FY26 guidance: underlying group EBITDA A$1.68-1.75bn; sustaining capex A$610-660m; growth capex A$100-150m.
- **Capital returns** — franked dividends (FY26 guidance 19-20cps) and buybacks support total return.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year (Feb) / full-year (Aug) results with EBITDA/dividend guidance, QCA resets, coal-haulage volume data, weather events (QLD floods/cyclones hit tonnage), and Bulk contract wins/losses.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Regulated CQCN is a high-quality, monopoly infrastructure asset with bond-like, QCA-underpinned returns.
- Attractive, well-covered franked dividend (5y-avg yield ~4.5%) — a staple income holding.
- Bulk/intermodal diversification (One Rail) is steadily reducing coal dependence and could drive a re-rate.
- Take-private optionality: a regulated asset base makes AZJ a recurring infrastructure-bidder target.
- Trades at a structural discount, so a successful diversification or favourable QCA reset offers asymmetric upside.

### Bear case
- **Structural coal phase-out** — the long-run decline in thermal (and eventually met) coal volumes shrinks the Network and Coal base; the core bear case.
- **ESG exclusion** — coal exposure removes the name from many institutional mandates, dampening the multiple.
- **Regulatory risk** — an adverse QCA reset cuts the allowed return and de-rates the Network segment.
- **Leverage** — D/E ~1.06x (above the standard industrials bar) with rate-sensitive, infrastructure-style debt.
- **Volume shocks** — Queensland weather (floods/cyclones) and contract losses (Whitehaven Gunnedah, June 2026) hit tonnage.

## Key Risks

- **Coal-volume decline** — the structural energy transition erodes the thermal/met-coal haulage base over time.
- **QCA regulatory resets** — the allowed-return decision on the CQCN can materially raise or lower Network earnings.
- **ESG / capital access** — coal association limits the investor base and can keep the multiple structurally low.
- **Leverage / rate sensitivity** — D/E ~1.06x; infrastructure debt is sensitive to interest rates and refinancing.
- **Weather & operational** — Queensland floods/cyclones routinely disrupt tonnage and revenue.
- **Contract concentration** — losing large haulage contracts (e.g. Whitehaven Gunnedah) without offset hits volumes.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Aurizon trades as a **regulated-infrastructure / income** name carrying a structural coal discount: the market values the Network segment on its QCA-regulated asset base (a bond-like, duration-sensitive cash flow) and the haulage segments on contracted volumes, then applies a discount and ESG penalty for coal. Mechanically:

- **EBITDA & dividend guidance** — FY26 underlying group EBITDA of A$1.68-1.75bn and a 19-20cps dividend guide are the live benchmarks; results read against these.
- **Regulated-duration sensitivity** — like other regulated infrastructure ([[atlas-arteria]], [[transurban]]), the Network's value is rate-sensitive; falling yields and favourable QCA resets re-rate it.
- **Coal discount & re-rating debate** — AZJ trades at a structural discount; the pace of Bulk/containerised diversification is the key re-rating swing factor, with ESG exclusion capping the multiple.
- **Dividend / franking** — a franked dividend (5y-avg yield ~4.5% per the API note) makes it an income staple; franking materially lifts the after-tax yield for Australian residents.
- **Take-private optionality** — the regulated asset base makes AZJ a recurring infrastructure-bidder candidate, providing a valuation floor.
- **Liquidity/options** — large-cap ASX liquidity; ASX exchange-traded options exist but volume is modest, so most positioning is via the underlying.
