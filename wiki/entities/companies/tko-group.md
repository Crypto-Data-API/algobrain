TKO Group Holdings (NYSE: TKO) is a global sports-and-entertainment company that owns **UFC** (mixed martial arts), **WWE** (professional wrestling), and, since 2025, **IMG**, **On Location**, and **PBR** (Professional Bull Riders). Created in September 2023 when Endeavor merged WWE with UFC, TKO is a [[s-and-p-500]] constituent in [[communication-services|Communication Services]] (with [[consumer-discretionary]]-style live-events economics) whose value is driven almost entirely by long-dated, escalating media-rights deals — a recurring catalyst that makes it a **media-rights re-rating play** rather than a traditional trailing-earnings-multiple story. Headquartered in New York City; SEC CIK 0001973266.

## Business Overview & Segments

TKO monetises owned-and-controlled premium sports IP across four core revenue levers, layered on top of the event-experience and representation businesses absorbed from Endeavor in 2025. The combination of UFC and WWE produces **~300+ live events a year** and one of the most reliable, DVR-proof live-content libraries in media — a structural advantage precisely as linear-TV audiences fragment and streamers compete for the scarce, must-watch live programming that still aggregates a real-time audience. The 2025 absorption of **IMG** (sports media rights/representation), **On Location** (premium event hospitality), and **PBR** (Professional Bull Riders) broadened the platform from pure promotion into event experiences and rights distribution.

| Lever / segment | What it is | Role / qualitative mix |
|---|---|---|
| **Media Rights** | Multi-year, escalating domestic and international broadcast/streaming contracts for UFC and WWE programming | Largest and most predictable revenue line — the quasi-annuity that anchors the equity story |
| **Live Events / Site Fees** | Gate receipts and host-city site fees from UFC fight cards, WWE shows (incl. WrestleMania), PBR events | Meaningful and growing; sensitive to attendance, host-market bidding, and the event calendar |
| **Sponsorships** | Brand partnerships, in-arena and on-broadcast advertising across UFC/WWE/PBR | Higher-margin growth lever as the combined platform raises sponsor reach |
| **Consumer Products / Licensing** | Merchandise, video-game and toy licensing, branded consumer goods | Smaller but high-margin, IP-leverage line |
| **IMG / On Location / PBR (2025)** | Sports media-rights agency, premium hospitality, and a bull-riding circuit | Diversifies beyond core WWE/UFC into experiences and rights distribution; full-year contribution is the main 2025 revenue step-up |

FY2025 was a transformative year: preliminary revenue of ~**$4.71 billion** (vs. ~$2.80 billion in 2024) and adjusted EBITDA of ~**$1.58 billion** (margin ~33.5%), reflecting the full-year contribution of WWE plus the newly acquired IMG/On Location/PBR businesses. ## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

TKO holds a genuine but concentrated [[economic-moat|economic moat]], built on the scarcity of premium, owned-and-controlled live IP rather than on cost or scale alone:

- **Owned-and-controlled premium IP** — TKO does not license the rights to someone else's league; it *owns* UFC and WWE outright. That control over the underlying content (calendar, talent, format, brand) is the durable asset and the thing rivals cannot replicate.
- **Scarcity of must-watch live content** — as on-demand and DVR viewing erode the value of scripted catalogs, real-time live sports/entertainment is one of the few formats that still commands a guaranteed, advertiser-friendly audience. UFC and WWE are among the most reliable recurring live franchises in the world.
- **League/promotion network effects** — a flywheel of athletes/wrestlers, fans, sponsors, and broadcasters: the biggest stars want the biggest stage, which draws the biggest audience, which attracts the richest sponsors and rights bids, which funds the biggest stars.
- **Brand equity** — UFC and WrestleMania are globally recognised marquee brands with decades of accumulated fan loyalty and cultural footprint.
- **Escalating rights contracts as a quasi-annuity** — multi-year media deals provide highly visible, contractually escalating revenue that smooths the model and underwrites the valuation.

The moat is **real but narrow and concentrated**: value is heavily tied to a handful of rights contracts and a few marquee properties, and the business is exposed to talent/athlete dependence (star power, injuries, departures) in a way a diversified media platform is not.

## Competitors / Peer Set

TKO sits at an unusual intersection: the large media/streaming platforms are simultaneously its **customers** (they buy its rights) and the source of the **bidding tension** that re-rates those rights higher. Its direct IP peers are other leagues and promotions.

Framed simply: streamers and broadcasters are both the buyers of TKO's product and the auction dynamic that lifts its price — continued competition among them is central to the bull case.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **UFC + WWE media-rights repricing** — the dominant value driver; each rights cycle resets the revenue base higher when multiple streamers/broadcasters bid.
- **Netflix–Raw ramp** — the move of WWE's flagship Monday Night Raw to [[netflix]] under a ~$5 billion, 10-year deal is a landmark live-sports streaming pact and a step-change in WWE media-rights economics; execution and audience delivery are watch items.
- **New UFC domestic media deal** — TKO secured a new domestic UFC media-rights agreement, a major catalyst given UFC rights had been a long-anticipated repricing event.
- **EBITDA / margin expansion** — operating leverage as high-margin rights and sponsorship dollars scale faster than cost.
- **Sponsorship & site-fee growth** — the combined platform raises sponsor reach and host-city bidding for marquee events.
- **IMG / On Location / PBR integration** — full-year contribution and cross-sell into experiences and rights distribution.
- **International expansion** — global rollout of events and rights across new markets.
- **Recurring catalysts** — rights-deal renewals/announcements (the news flow that moves the stock most), quarterly EBITDA prints, and major event windows.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Scarce, owned live IP is structurally advantaged as linear fragments and streamers compete for the few formats that still deliver a real-time audience.
- Escalating, multi-year rights contracts behave like a compounding annuity; each cycle re-rates revenue higher as streamers ([[netflix]], Amazon) and broadcasters bid.
- Demonstrated margin expansion (FY2025 adjusted EBITDA margin ~33.5%) with operating leverage still ahead as the platform scales.
- Owned-content model plus IMG/On Location/PBR adds optionality in new properties, experiences, and rights distribution.
- Combat sports and pro wrestling have durable, global, recurring fan bases.

### Bear case
- Extreme reliance on a handful of rights contracts and a few marquee properties — concentration risk if a key deal disappoints or a cycle re-rates flat.
- Talent/athlete dependence: star power, injuries, and departures directly affect audience and value.
- Live-event economics are sensitive to attendance, sponsor demand, and host-city site fees.
- The whole thesis assumes streamers keep bidding up sports rights — a cooling of the streaming-content arms race would cap the re-rating.
- Controlled-company governance (Endeavor / Silver Lake influence) limits minority-shareholder leverage and introduces related-party considerations.
- Valuation rests on EV/EBITDA and forward rights growth, not GAAP earnings — a richer bar to clear and harder to anchor.

## Key Risks

- **Rights-contract concentration** — value is tied to a small number of large, long-dated media deals; any single renewal that disappoints can move the equity hard.
- **Live-event / attendance / sponsor sensitivity** — gate, site-fee, and sponsorship revenue depend on event demand, host-market economics, and the ad cycle.
- **Dependence on streamer competition** — the re-rating thesis requires [[netflix]], Amazon, [[disney]], [[comcast]] and peers to keep competing for live rights.
- **Controlled-company governance** — Endeavor/Silver Lake influence over the board and strategy; related-party transactions (e.g., the IMG/On Location/PBR acquisition).
- **Talent dependence** — reliance on marquee athletes/performers and a healthy pipeline of stars.
- **Integration risk** — absorbing IMG, On Location, and PBR while running the core UFC/WWE engine.
- **Regulatory / athlete-classification** — combat-sports regulation and athlete-classification/labor questions are a structural overhang.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

TKO is a **media-rights re-rating play**, and the single most important framing for traders is that its **headline GAAP multiple is meaningless**: the ~10,222x trailing P/E and near-breakeven net margin shown in the Fundamentals block below are artifacts of merger/transaction accounting (2024 GAAP net income was near break-even after the WWE+UFC combination), not a read on the underlying business. Mechanically:

- **Trade it on EV/EBITDA and rights-deal news flow**, not GAAP P/E — analysts and the market value TKO on adjusted EBITDA and the trajectory of media-rights revenue.
- **Event/catalyst-driven** — the stock moves most on rights-deal renewals and announcements (UFC and WWE repricing, the Netflix–Raw transition) rather than on routine quarterly EPS.
- **No dividend** — TKO pays none (see the Fundamentals block); total return is a capital-appreciation, re-rating story, not income.
- **Controlled-company float dynamics** — Endeavor/Silver Lake control and a comparatively limited public float shape liquidity, index treatment, and how the stock reacts to governance news.
- **Sector classification** — sits in [[communication-services]] / media-entertainment baskets, with live-events economics that rhyme with [[consumer-discretionary]] experiential names.
