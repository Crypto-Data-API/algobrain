AES Corporation (NYSE: AES) is a global power generator and utility — one of the largest US corporate suppliers of renewable power to data-center hyperscalers — and a constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]] [[utilities]] sector. **It is being taken private**: on 2 March 2026 a consortium led by Global Infrastructure Partners (GIP, part of [[blackrock]]) and EQT agreed to acquire AES for **$15.00 per share in cash** ($10.7B equity value, ~$33.4B enterprise value including debt). The trade today is a [[merger-arbitrage]] spread, not a fundamental utility story.

## Business Overview & Segments

AES, founded in 1981 and headquartered in Arlington, Virginia, is a hybrid of regulated utilities and a fast-growing renewables-development platform. The renewables/PPA platform — not the legacy generation — is what made AES a strategic target for infrastructure capital chasing AI-driven power demand.

| Segment / strand | What it is | Role in the model |
|---|---|---|
| **US & international utilities** | Regulated utilities — AES Indiana, AES Ohio, plus international operations (e.g., El Salvador) | Stable, rate-regulated cash flows; the steadier base of the business |
| **Renewables & energy-infrastructure development** | Solar, wind and battery storage built under long-term power-purchase agreements (PPAs) | The growth engine and the strategic prize — long-dated contracted cash flows to blue-chip corporate buyers |
| **Legacy / conventional generation** | Thermal and other conventional generation being wound down | Declining; AES has been exiting coal and shifting the mix toward renewables |

AES has been a top renewable-energy supplier to [[amazon]], [[google]] and [[microsoft]] data centers under long-term PPAs — precisely the AI-power-demand angle that attracted infrastructure capital. Years of heavy renewables capex left it with elevated leverage and a 2023–2025 equity de-rating (dividend yield pushed toward 7%+), setting up the private bid.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

As a [[utilities]] business, AES's [[economic-moat|economic moat]] is moderate and contract-based rather than wide:

- **Regulated-utility franchises** — AES Indiana and AES Ohio are rate-regulated monopolies in their service territories, providing protected, predictable returns and a barrier to competition.
- **Long-term PPAs / contracted cash flows** — multi-year power-purchase agreements with creditworthy hyperscaler buyers ([[amazon]], [[google]], [[microsoft]]) lock in revenue and insulate the renewables platform from merchant-power volatility — a contractual moat.
- **Development scale and pipeline** — a large renewables-development organization with grid-interconnection queue positions and project pipeline is hard to replicate quickly, a real advantage as power demand surges.
- **Data-center relationships** — established status as a preferred clean-power supplier to hyperscalers is a distribution edge in the fastest-growing power-demand segment.

The moat is offset by heavy leverage and capital intensity: AES competes with every independent power producer and renewables developer for projects, capital and PPAs, and its de-rated equity (high dividend yield) reflected balance-sheet stress before the buyout. The take-private resolves that by moving the leverage onto patient private-infrastructure capital.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

> For AES specifically the "bull/bear" debate is now a **merger-arbitrage** question — will the $15.00 cash deal close, and when — overlaid on the underlying power-demand thesis.

### Bull case (deal closes / thesis works)
- A definitive, board-approved, all-cash deal at $15.00/share from well-capitalized buyers (GIP/[[blackrock]], EQT) with co-underwriters (CalPERS, Qatar Investment Authority) — high deal certainty for arbitrageurs.
- The take-private validates the AI-power-demand thesis: contracted renewable generation to hyperscalers is a scarce, strategic asset.
- Private-infrastructure ownership can fund the renewables build-out without public-market leverage scrutiny.
- Spread capture offers a defined, relatively low-volatility return versus the deal price.

### Bear case (deal risk / standalone weakness)
- Multi-jurisdiction regulatory approval (FERC, state PUCs, foreign) creates a long closing tail into late 2026/early 2027 and real break risk.
- On a standalone basis AES carries heavy leverage, thin coverage and volatile returns (see fundamentals) — the reason the equity de-rated pre-deal.
- A deal break would re-expose holders to that levered, de-rated fundamental story.
- Rising rates or financing-market stress could pressure the buyers or the terms.

## Key Risks

- **Deal / regulatory completion risk** — approvals across FERC, state PUCs and foreign regulators; a long closing tail and the possibility of conditions or a break.
- **Leverage** — high balance-sheet leverage and thin interest coverage on a standalone basis (see fundamentals); covenant headroom in a downturn.
- **Rate-cycle exposure** — as a capital-intensive, leveraged utility/IPP, AES is sensitive to interest rates and refinancing costs.
- **Merchant / PPA counterparty risk** — dependence on hyperscaler off-takers and contract terms; merchant-power exposure where uncontracted.
- **Regulatory / political shifts** — changes to renewable incentives, the leverage envelope, or utility rate frameworks.
- **Index-deletion flow** — on closing, AES exits the [[s-and-p-500]], forcing index-fund selling (a known but mechanical risk for holders into the event).

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

AES no longer trades on a conventional valuation framework: with a definitive all-cash take-private at $15.00/share agreed, the stock is now a **merger-arbitrage instrument** whose price hugs the deal consideration and reflects deal-completion probability and the time-to-close, not earnings multiples. The low ~6x trailing P/E and ~46% reported ROE in the fundamentals below are artifacts of a leveraged, volatile-earnings utility and are not the basis on which the equity trades today. Mechanically:

- **Merger-arb spread** — the live return is the spread between the market price and the $15.00 cash consideration, weighed against regulatory/closing risk; the spread tightens as approvals clear and widens on any deal-risk headline.
- **Dividend in run-off** — AES historically paid a meaningful dividend (yield pushed toward 7%+ during the 2023–2025 de-rating); under the deal, the dividend is effectively a component of the arb carry until close rather than a standalone income thesis.
- **Compressed volatility / low beta to the market** — IV crushed post-announcement and liquidity migrated to near-dated strikes around $15; the stock decoupled from utility-sector and broad-market beta and now trades on deal news.
- **Index-event flow** — on closing AES exits the [[s-and-p-500]], a mechanical forced-selling event index-rebalance strategies trade around.
- **Catalysts** — the 26 June 2026 stockholder vote, FERC and state-PUC filings/approvals, and any deal-financing or terms news.
