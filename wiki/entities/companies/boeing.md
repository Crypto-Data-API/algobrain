Boeing (NYSE: BA) is one half of the global large-commercial-aircraft duopoly with Airbus, plus a top-tier US defense contractor. A [[s-and-p-500]] and Dow Jones Industrial Average constituent, it is a turnaround/recovery trade: the stock is driven less by current earnings than by 737 MAX production rates, FAA certification milestones, and free-cash-flow inflection after the 2019–2024 crisis years (MAX groundings, COVID, the January 2024 Alaska Airlines door-plug incident, and the 2024 machinists' strike). It sits in the [[industrials]] sector with a heavy [[defense]] overlay, and because of its crisis-distorted financials it trades far more on operational news flow than on trailing earnings multiples.

## Business Overview & Segments

Boeing, founded in 1916, runs three reporting segments. **Commercial Airplanes (BCA)** is the swing factor for the equity — it carries a multi-year backlog of thousands of aircraft but has been the epicentre of the quality crisis and the cash drain. **Defense, Space & Security (BDS)** provides a more stable, government-funded counterweight, though several fixed-price development programs have generated large charges. **Global Services (BGS)** — the aftermarket of parts, maintenance/MRO and training — is the consistent, high-margin cash generator that the market values most cleanly. Kelly Ortberg took over as CEO in August 2024 to lead the safety-and-quality rebuild. The fundamentals section below reflects the crisis legacy: deeply negative book equity until recently (hence distorted ROE/D/E) and thin margins that only turned positive again in FY2025.

| Segment | What it is | Role in the model |
|---|---|---|
| **Commercial Airplanes (BCA)** | 737 MAX, 787 Dreamliner, 767, 777/777X; thousands of aircraft in backlog | The growth and recovery engine — and the source of the crisis; FAA-capped 737 output gates revenue and cash |
| **Defense, Space & Security (BDS)** | F-15EX, KC-46 tanker, T-7A trainer, MQ-25, P-8, satellites, F-47 sixth-gen fighter (selected 2025), Starliner legacy | Stable government demand, but dogged by fixed-price development charges (KC-46, T-7A, Starliner) |
| **Global Services (BGS)** | Spares, MRO, modifications, digital tools, pilot/crew training | The steady, high-margin aftermarket — the cleanest cash generator and least controversial part of the franchise |

The franchise rests on the world's largest installed base of commercial jets, which feeds a decades-long services annuity, plus prime positions on US defense platforms. The durable advantage is structural (duopoly + installed base); the near-term reality is an execution-and-balance-sheet repair story.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Boeing holds a wide but currently dented [[economic-moat|economic moat]] built on several reinforcing layers:

- **Duopoly structure** — large commercial aircraft is effectively a two-firm market (Boeing and Airbus). New entrants face enormous capital, certification and reputational barriers; China's COMAC remains regional/sub-scale for now. This makes demand far less competitive than the headlines suggest.
- **Installed base + services annuity** — thousands of in-service jets generate decades of high-margin spares, MRO and training revenue ([[switching-costs]] are high once a fleet standardizes on a type).
- **Backlog visibility** — a multi-year order book provides revenue visibility few industrials can match, even when delivery timing slips.
- **Defense franchise and certification know-how** — entrenched positions on US military platforms and the regulatory expertise to certify clean-sheet aircraft are themselves a barrier.

The moat has narrowed because of self-inflicted quality and execution failures: the 737 MAX groundings (2019–2020), the January 2024 Alaska Airlines door-plug blowout, repeated 777X delays, and chronic fixed-price defense charges have eroded customer trust, ceded delivery share to Airbus, and forced an FAA-imposed production cap. The bull thesis is that the duopoly and installed base make the moat self-repairing once production stabilizes; the bear thesis is that the credibility damage and balance-sheet leverage are semi-permanent.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **FAA 737 production-cap relief** — the single biggest lever; each step up in the monthly cap (from the current ~38/month toward 42 and beyond) flows almost directly to cash.
- **777X certification and entry into service** — FAA type certification targeted for H2 2026, first deliveries (Lufthansa) in early 2027; clearing it removes a multi-billion-dollar overhang.
- **737 MAX 7 / MAX 10 certification** — unlocks large Southwest and United order books.
- **Free-cash-flow inflection** — the recovery thesis is fundamentally a cash story: turning years of cash burn into sustained positive FCF as deliveries normalize.
- **Defense margin normalization** — working off legacy fixed-price charges (KC-46, T-7A, Starliner) and ramping the F-47 sixth-gen fighter win.
- **Services growth** — the steady BGS aftermarket compounding with the recovering installed base.
- **Order intake** — air-show cycles (Paris/Farnborough/Dubai), China delivery resumptions, and wide-body demand recovery.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- It is one of two firms in a global duopoly with a multi-year backlog — demand is not the problem, throughput is, and throughput is fixable.
- FAA cap relief and 777X/MAX 7/10 certifications are identifiable catalysts that each re-rate the cash trajectory.
- New CEO Kelly Ortberg and the Spirit re-acquisition target the root cause (quality control), and the 2024 equity raise plus Jeppesen divestiture have shored up liquidity.
- Defense and services provide a stable floor while commercial recovers; FY2025 was the first profitable year since 2018.
- An eventual return to normalized cash generation supports a much higher equity value off a depressed base.

### Bear case
- The balance sheet is stretched — deeply distorted ROE/D/E, thin margins, and heavy debt leave little error margin (see fundamentals below).
- Quality and certification slippage has been chronic; any new safety event, whistleblower disclosure, or 777X delay resets the timeline and the narrative.
- Production is gated by suppliers (CFM/[[ge-aerospace]] engines, Spirit fuselages) and by the FAA, both outside Boeing's full control.
- Fixed-price defense programs keep generating charges, capping BDS profitability.
- The stock already discounts a successful recovery; execution stumbles de-rate a name with little valuation cushion.

## Key Risks

- **Certification slippage** — 777X and MAX 7/10 timelines have repeatedly slipped; further delays carry program charges and order risk.
- **Safety / quality escapes** — any new in-service incident or whistleblower event (post-Alaska door plug) can re-ground aircraft and re-cap production.
- **Supplier bottlenecks** — engine supply from CFM ([[ge-aerospace]]) and fuselage quality from Spirit AeroSystems cap deliverable volume.
- **Balance-sheet fragility** — high leverage and thin interest coverage (see fundamentals) mean limited tolerance for another cash-burn cycle.
- **Defense fixed-price charges** — KC-46, T-7A, Starliner legacy programs continue to absorb cash.
- **Geopolitics / trade** — China periodically suspends MAX deliveries amid trade tensions, removing a major demand channel.
- **Labor relations** — the 2024 machinists' strike showed how quickly labor disputes halt production.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Boeing's trailing earnings multiple is not a meaningful valuation tool: the company only returned to profitability in FY2025, so the ~86x trailing P/E (per the fundamentals below) reflects a tiny, recovering earnings base rather than expensive growth. Likewise ROE/D/E are distorted by years of negative or near-zero book equity. The market values Boeing on **forward free-cash-flow potential** at a normalized production rate — a sum-of-the-parts of a recovering commercial franchise, a stable defense book, and a high-quality services annuity — not on trailing GAAP. Mechanically:

- **It trades on operational news, not earnings** — monthly delivery/orders data, FAA cap decisions, certification milestones and FCF guidance move the stock more than the headline EPS print.
- **No dividend** — Boeing suspended its dividend in 2020 and has not reinstated it; reinstatement would itself be a recovery signal. There is no income case here, only capital-recovery.
- **High beta / event-driven volatility** — a deep, liquid Tier-1 options market sees IV spikes around delivery data, earnings, FAA announcements and air-show order news (Paris/Farnborough/Dubai).
- **Correlated basket** — moves with Airbus, [[ge-aerospace]]/CFM, Spirit AeroSystems, [[rtx]], [[lockheed-martin]], and a supplier basket (Howmet, Hexcel) that trades on Boeing rate announcements.

## 2025–2026 Developments

- **Return to profitability**: FY2025 was Boeing's first positive-net-income year since 2018 (net margin 2.5%, per fundamentals below), aided by recovering 737/787 deliveries and a ~US$24 billion 2024 equity raise that shored up the balance sheet.
- **FAA cap still binding**: 737 output remains constrained by an FAA-imposed monthly cap; in **April 2026** Boeing delivered 47 aircraft including 34 737 MAX (eight below the cap), with its YTD delivery lead over Airbus narrowing to nine aircraft.
- **777X delayed again**: FAA type certification now targeted for **H2 2026** with first deliveries (Lufthansa) slipping to **early 2027** — roughly seven years late, with multi-billion-dollar program charges along the way.
- **737 MAX 7 / MAX 10 certification** expected during 2026 (MAX 7 around mid-2026, MAX 10 later) — key for the Southwest and United order books.
- **Portfolio**: 2024–2025 re-acquisition of fuselage supplier Spirit AeroSystems progressed to bring 737 quality control in-house; Jeppesen/Digital Aviation Solutions divestiture (~US$10.6B, 2025) funded deleveraging.
