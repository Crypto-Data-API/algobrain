Nomura Holdings (TSE: 8604, NYSE ADR: NMR) is Japan's largest investment bank and brokerage, spanning retail wealth management, investment management, and global wholesale (markets and investment banking) operations. For traders it is the prime listed proxy for the Japanese capital-markets cycle — retail flow into equities, Bank of Japan policy normalisation, and yen-rate volatility all flow through its earnings. It sits in the [[financials]] sector and is a constituent of the [[nikkei-225]].

## Business Overview

Founded in 1925 (Nomura Securities, Osaka) and headquartered in Tokyo, Nomura operates three core divisions:

- **Wealth Management** — Japan's leading retail brokerage network, the dominant earnings base; benefits from the "savings to investment" shift and the expanded NISA program.
- **Investment Management** — asset management arm; record assets under management of approximately ¥136.9 trillion as of 31 March 2026, boosted by the Macquarie acquisition (below).
- **Wholesale** — global markets (fixed income and equities) and investment banking; the volatile leg of the franchise (it absorbed an approximately US$2.9 billion loss in the 2021 Archegos collapse).

## Business Segments

Nomura's reportable structure has converged on a small number of large divisions. The mix matters enormously for the stock: stable fee-based earnings (Wealth + Investment Management) are rewarded with a higher multiple than the volatile Wholesale leg.

| Division | What it does | Earnings character |
|---|---|---|
| **Wealth Management** | Domestic retail brokerage, advisory, and distribution to Japanese households | Stable, recurring fee + commission base; geared to Japanese retail equity participation |
| **Investment Management** | Asset management (Nomura Asset Management + acquired Macquarie public AM); ~¥136.9T AUM | Stickiest fee revenue; the strategic growth pillar |
| **Wholesale — Global Markets** | Fixed income and equities trading, financing, prime services | Cyclical, capital-intensive; the source of tail-risk events (e.g. Archegos) |
| **Wholesale — Investment Banking** | M&A advisory, ECM/DCM underwriting | Cyclical, deal-flow dependent; leveraged to Japan-cross-border activity |

**Strategic tilt.** Management has spent a decade trying to shift the earnings mix away from volatile Wholesale toward stable, fee-based Wealth and Investment Management — the Macquarie public-AM acquisition is the clearest expression of that pivot.

## 2025-2026 Developments

- **Macquarie asset management acquisition:** announced 22 April 2025 and completed 1 December 2025 — Nomura acquired [[macquarie-group|Macquarie]]'s US and European public asset management business for approximately US$1.8 billion (~¥281.4 billion), adding roughly US$180 billion in client assets across equities, fixed income, and multi-asset strategies. This is Nomura's largest pivot toward stable fee-based revenue since the 2008 Lehman international acquisition.
- **FY2026 results (year ended 31 March 2026):** net revenue rose 14.5% to ¥2.17 trillion; income before income taxes up 14.4% to ¥539.8 billion; net income attributable to shareholders up 6.3% to ¥362.1 billion — a record revenue year. March-quarter momentum softened versus the strong first half.
- **AUM record:** Investment Management assets climbed to a record ¥134.7 trillion during the year, ¥136.9 trillion at fiscal year end including Macquarie assets.

## Competitive Positioning / Peers

Nomura competes on two fronts: as Japan's domestic brokerage champion, and as a global-markets/investment-bank also-ran outside the US bulge bracket.

| Peer | Ticker | Arena | Comparison |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Nomura** | NMR / 8604 | Global IB + Japanese retail | #1 Japanese brokerage; sub-scale globally vs US bulge bracket |
| Daiwa Securities | 8601 (TSE) | Japanese retail + IB | Closest domestic rival; the natural Japan-capital-markets pair trade |
| [[goldman-sachs]] | GS | Global IB/markets | Higher ROE, deeper global franchise; the benchmark Nomura measures against |
| [[morgan-stanley]] | MS | Global IB + wealth | Wealth-management mix is the model Nomura's pivot emulates |
| [[macquarie-group]] | MQG (ASX) | Asset mgmt + IB | Seller of the public-AM business Nomura acquired; otherwise a global peer |
| MUFG / SMFG / Mizuho | (TSE megabanks) | Universal banks | Compete in Japanese capital markets but bank-led, not brokerage-led |

**Positioning.** Domestically Nomura is the clear leader (vs Daiwa and the megabanks' securities arms). Globally it has never matched the consistent profitability of [[goldman-sachs]] or [[morgan-stanley]]; its overseas Wholesale business has historically been the drag on group ROE and the source of tail-loss events. The Macquarie deal and the broader fee-mix pivot are explicitly aimed at narrowing that ROE gap with the US peers.

## Bull vs Bear Case

**Bull case**
- Leveraged proxy for BoJ normalisation: rising Japanese rates lift net interest income and bond-trading franchise after decades of zero/negative rates.
- "Savings to investment" structural shift plus expanded NISA pulls Japanese household cash into equities, feeding Wealth Management.
- Macquarie acquisition durably raises the stable fee-revenue share and AUM scale.
- Record revenue year demonstrates operating momentum; trades at a discount to book/US peers, leaving re-rating room.

**Bear case**
- Wholesale tail risk — the Archegos ~US$2.9B loss is the cautionary template for the volatile global-markets leg.
- Persistently sub-US-peer ROE; the global IB business has long struggled to earn its cost of capital.
- Yen strength compresses translated overseas earnings and the value of acquired non-yen AUM.
- Integration/execution risk on the Macquarie business.
- March-quarter momentum already softened versus the strong first half — cyclical peak risk.

## Valuation Framework

Like most investment banks, Nomura is valued on **return on equity relative to book value** rather than earnings multiples alone. Qualitative drivers:

- **ROE vs cost of equity** — the central gauge; Nomura has historically traded below book partly because ROE lagged peers. The fee-mix pivot is about lifting and stabilising ROE.
- **Price-to-book (P/B)** — Japanese financials are frequently valued on P/B; a re-rating toward book/above-book hinges on sustained ROE improvement.
- **Japan-rates leverage** — BoJ policy normalisation is a key earnings swing factor (net interest income, JGB trading); the stock is a rate-normalisation play.
- **Fee-revenue share** — the higher the stable Wealth + Investment Management contribution, the higher the deserved multiple.
- **AUM & flows** — record ¥136.9T AUM drives recurring management fees; net flows and market levels move it.
- **Capital & buybacks** — capital adequacy and the pace of buybacks/dividends bound shareholder returns.

## Trading & Options Playbook

- **Listings & index:** primary liquidity is TSE: 8604 (a [[nikkei-225]] and TOPIX constituent); NMR ADRs on NYSE give US-hours exposure. Found in Japan-equity and EAFE/MSCI Japan ETF baskets, plus financials sector funds.
- **ADR access & liquidity:** NMR ADR liquidity is moderate and options are thin — most institutional flow is in Tokyo on 8604. US-listed options are not a primary expression; positioning is usually via the ADR or Tokyo line.
- **Catalysts calendar:** quarterly earnings (roughly late April/July/October/January for the Japanese fiscal cadence), BoJ policy meetings, Japanese retail flow data, Nikkei/TOPIX momentum, integration progress on the Macquarie business, and any Wholesale-division risk events.
- **Volatility behavior:** trades with Daiwa (8601) and the Japanese megabanks (MUFG, SMFG, Mizuho); high sensitivity to BoJ surprises and to global risk-off episodes that hit Wholesale.
- **Risk events:** trading-loss tail risk in Wholesale (Archegos precedent); yen strength compressing translated overseas earnings.

## Capital Return / Dilution

Nomura returns capital through dividends and share buybacks, with the pace governed by capital adequacy and the cyclical earnings of the Wholesale division. As a bank holding company its distributions flex with regulatory capital and the trading-results cycle rather than following a fixed policy. The Macquarie acquisition was funded primarily with cash rather than large equity issuance, so it was not a meaningful dilution event for common holders.

## Risks

- **Wholesale trading tail risk** — counterparty/blow-up events (Archegos precedent).
- **ROE shortfall** — global IB business may continue to earn below cost of capital.
- **FX translation** — yen strength compresses overseas earnings and acquired AUM value.
- **Cyclicality** — capital-markets-dependent revenue; deal and trading volumes swing with sentiment.
- **Integration risk** — realising synergies on the acquired Macquarie public-AM business.
- **Policy risk** — adverse or delayed BoJ normalisation removes a key earnings tailwind.
