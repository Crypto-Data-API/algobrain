# Overend, Gurney & Company

Overend, Gurney & Company was a London discount house founded in 1800 that became the largest bill broker in the world during the mid-19th century. Nicknamed "the bankers' bank" for its role as de facto [[lender-of-last-resort]] to smaller British banks, its collapse on May 10, 1866 ("Black Friday") produced the [[panic-of-1866]] and directly shaped Walter Bagehot's *Lombard Street* (1873), which codified modern central-bank lender-of-last-resort doctrine.

## Overview

The firm originated as Richardson, Overend & Company in Norwich in 1800, relocating to 65 Lombard Street in London. Under the leadership of Samuel Gurney (a member of the Quaker Gurney banking family related by marriage to [[barclays]]), it grew into the dominant bill-broking house of the period 1825-1865. At its peak, Overend, Gurney handled more bills of exchange than the [[bank-of-england]] itself and had call deposits from hundreds of country banks.

## Business Model: Bill Broking and Discount Arbitrage

The core business was [[bill-broking-arbitrage]]:

- **Bills of exchange** — short-term commercial paper, typically 30-90 days, backed by goods in transit or commercial receivables.
- **Discounting** — buying a bill at face value minus interest; the spread between the buy rate and the bill's yield to maturity was the broker's gross margin.
- **Re-discounting** — selling bundles of bills to banks seeking short-term yield, or to the [[bank-of-england]] for cash.
- **Call deposits** — funding the inventory with deposits from country banks, payable on demand.

This business is a classic **maturity mismatch** — short-duration assets (bills) funded by even shorter-duration liabilities (call deposits). It works only when counterparties trust the firm's solvency. When they don't, the run is faster than the bill book can be liquidated.

During 1825-1865 Overend, Gurney was viewed as so safe that the [[bank-of-england]]'s own governor relied on it to smooth market liquidity, and the firm was profitable enough to make the Gurney family one of the wealthiest in England.

## The Drift into Railway and Speculative Finance

From the late 1850s onward, under new partners, the firm expanded beyond first-class bills into:

- Long-dated railway debentures (illiquid, speculative)
- Loans against [[shipping-certificates]] and cargo
- Ship financing and shipbuilding advances
- Speculation in Greek, Italian, and Turkish sovereign paper

By 1865, latent losses were estimated at £4 million against capital of roughly £500,000. A July 1865 conversion to a limited-liability joint-stock company (Overend, Gurney & Co. Ltd.) placed the concealed losses inside a public vehicle, with an IPO that raised fresh capital from outside shareholders.

## Black Friday, May 10, 1866

A cascade of bill defaults and rumors triggered a run on call deposits. On **May 10, 1866**, after the firm had exhausted its lines, the directors requested emergency support from the [[bank-of-england]]. Governor Lancelot Holland refused — the [[bank-of-england]] held that extending aid would reward recklessness. Overend, Gurney suspended payments that afternoon.

Impact:

- **Losses** — roughly £11 million on failure (~£1.4 billion in 2020 money), a scale comparable to [[jay-cooke-co]]'s 1873 collapse in the US.
- **Panic of 1866** — ~200 companies failed in the following weeks; [[bank-of-england]] discount rate spiked to 10%; consols (long UK gilts) fell sharply.
- **Criminal trial** — directors were tried for fraud at the Old Bailey in 1869 and acquitted, though found to have been "grossly negligent."

## Intellectual Legacy: Bagehot's Dictum

The collapse convinced Walter Bagehot, then editor of *The Economist*, that the [[bank-of-england]] needed a formalized doctrine of emergency liquidity provision. His 1873 book *Lombard Street* (Source: [[book-lombard-street]]) produced what became **Bagehot's dictum**:

> In a panic, lend freely, against good collateral, at a penalty rate.

This remains the operational core of modern [[lender-of-last-resort]] policy — invoked during 1907, the Great Depression (inadequately), 1998 ([[ltcm]]), 2008 ([[2008-global-financial-crisis]]), and 2020 (COVID). The Overend, Gurney failure is therefore the founding case study of modern central banking.

## Trading Relevance

Overend, Gurney anchors several core concepts in [[market-microstructure]] and [[risk-management]]:

1. **Bill-broking arbitrage** — the canonical pre-telegraph discount-rate [[structural-edge]]; see [[bill-broking-arbitrage]].
2. **Liquidity risk vs. solvency risk** — the firm was arguably solvent on a mark-to-model basis but illiquid; the distinction is the heart of [[liquidity-risk]] frameworks.
3. **Maturity mismatch** — funding illiquid long assets with on-demand short liabilities is a recurring pattern: [[jay-cooke-co]] (1873), [[creditanstalt-1931]], [[continental-illinois]] (1984), [[northern-rock]] (2007), [[silicon-valley-bank]] (2023).
4. **Moral hazard and the [[bank-of-england]] no-bailout signal** — a deliberate policy of non-rescue that, after panic proved too costly, was replaced by Bagehot's more structured doctrine.
5. **Franchise drift** — a profitable discount house destroyed by expansion into adjacent but misunderstood markets ([[railway-bubble]], sovereign speculation).
