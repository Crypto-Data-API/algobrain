Edison International (NYSE: EIX) is the holding company of Southern California Edison (SCE), one of the largest US electric utilities, delivering power to ~15 million people across central and southern California. It is a constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]] index. For traders, EIX is the sector's defining **event-driven wildfire-liability story**: the January 2025 Eaton Fire turned a sleepy regulated utility into a litigation-handicapping trade, and the stock has traded on settlement and cost-recovery headlines ever since.

## Business Overview & Segments

Edison International is, for practical purposes, a single-utility holding company: nearly all of its earnings come from **Southern California Edison (SCE)**, a rate-regulated electric transmission and distribution (T&D) utility. SCE largely exited generation ownership years ago, so it is a wires-and-poles business — it earns a regulated return on the capital it invests in the grid, not on the price of power. The small non-regulated **Edison Energy / Trio** advisory arm is immaterial to the [[s-and-p-500]]-level investment case.

| **Edison Energy / Trio** | Small non-regulated energy-advisory arm serving large commercial/industrial customers | Immaterial to the equity story; optionality at best |

California's **AB 1054 wildfire fund** (~$21B) and the associated liability-cap framework are central to the equity story. The fund pools capital across the state's investor-owned utilities and, for utilities that operate prudently (and hold a valid safety certificate), caps shareholder exposure to catastrophic wildfire claims — but the [[event-driven\|event-driven]] 2025 Eaton Fire is the framework's single biggest stress test. The interaction of inverse-condemnation liability, AB 1054 reimbursement, and CPUC cost recovery is what the stock actually trades on (see [[risk-management]]).

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

A regulated electric utility holds a genuine, durable [[economic-moat|economic moat]] — but the *source* of the moat is regulation, not technology or a fleet. SCE has a legal/natural **monopoly franchise** over its service territory: customers in central and southern California cannot choose another wires provider, and duplicating the grid is economically impossible. On top of that franchise sits a **regulated rate base** — the depreciated capital invested in the network — on which the [[cpuc|CPUC]] and FERC authorize a **return on equity**. Growth is therefore mechanical and low-variance: invest more capex into the grid, grow the rate base, grow earnings at the authorized return. This is the opposite of a merchant generator like [[vistra]] or [[constellation-energy]], which are **price-takers** exposed to power and fuel prices; Edison's moat is the regulatory compact, not the assets it operates.

The California-specific caveat is decisive and is what separates EIX from a sleepy regulated peer: under California's **inverse-condemnation** doctrine, a utility can be held strictly liable for wildfire damage caused by its equipment *even if it was not negligent*. That single structural risk can overwhelm the entire regulated-return moat — a few catastrophic fire seasons can dwarf decades of authorized earnings. **AB 1054** (the ~$21B wildfire fund plus a prudency-based liability cap) is the mitigant designed to keep that tail from forcing a [[pg-and-e|PG&E]]-style bankruptcy, but it is a backstop on a structural hazard, not the removal of it. For Edison, the moat and the central risk are two sides of the same regulatory coin.

## 2025–2026 Developments

- **Eaton Fire (2025-01-07)** — one of the most destructive and deadly fires in California history swept Altadena/Pasadena. Evidence and subsequent investigation pointed to SCE transmission equipment; SCE has acknowledged its equipment was likely associated with ignition. Estimated total economic losses run well into the tens of billions of dollars; EIX stock fell ~40% in early 2025.
- **Direct-settlement strategy (2025–2026)** — SCE launched a voluntary Wildfire Recovery Compensation Program to pay claimants directly and bypass years of litigation. By spring 2026 SCE had extended roughly 1,500 settlement offers totaling more than $500 million, enhanced the program (attorney-fee coverage to 20% of net damages, expanded tenant housing coverage), and reported over $117M in issued claims payments in single updates.
- **Strategy/outlook (2026-02-18 and 2026-04-28 8-Ks)** — Edison detailed its wildfire strategy and a 5–7% core EPS growth outlook, alongside expected reimbursement from the AB 1054 wildfire fund for eligible claims.
- **Dividend maintained and raised** — board declared $0.8775/share quarterly (payable 2026-04-30), a ~6% hike marking a 22nd consecutive year of dividend growth, targeting 45–55% of SCE core earnings; yield ~5% at April 2026 prices.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Rate-base growth** — the core engine: wildfire-mitigation capex (covered conductor, undergrounding), grid modernization, and electrification / EV load growth all expand the rate base on which SCE earns its authorized return.
- **5–7% core EPS growth outlook** — management's framing (per the 2026-02-18 and 2026-04-28 8-Ks) for compounding earnings off rate-base growth, alongside expected AB 1054 reimbursement for eligible claims.
- **AB 1054 wildfire-fund reimbursement** — recovery of eligible Eaton Fire claims from the ~$21B fund is a direct catalyst for bounding the liability and re-rating the equity.
- **Settlement-program resolution** — the voluntary Wildfire Recovery Compensation Program paying claimants directly (bypassing years of litigation) is the mechanism by which the discount-to-peers can narrow.
- **Dividend growth** — a 22nd consecutive year of dividend increases (board declared $0.8775/share quarterly, ~6% hike, payable 2026-04-30; ~5% yield at April 2026 prices), targeting 45–55% of SCE core earnings — supports the income shareholder base.
- **Recurring catalysts** — cumulative settlement totals and program take-up, [[cpuc|CPUC]] prudency/cost-recovery rulings, wildfire-fund reimbursement decisions, California fire season (June–November), legislative changes to AB 1054, and general rate-case outcomes.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- **PCG-style recovery** — as Eaton Fire liability gets bounded by AB 1054 reimbursement and CPUC cost recovery, the [[pg-and-e|PG&E]] post-2019 playbook suggests the discount-to-peers narrows and the equity re-rates.
- **Regulated rate-base growth** — low-variance, mechanical EPS compounding (~5–7% core) from grid capex, largely insulated from commodity prices.
- **Electrification load growth** — California's EV and electrification push is a multi-decade demand tailwind that expands the investable rate base.
- **Income durability** — a 22-year dividend-growth streak and a clear payout target underpin a yield-oriented shareholder base.

### Bear case
- **Liabilities exceeding the framework** — if total Eaton Fire damages overwhelm the AB 1054 fund/cap, shareholder exposure balloons regardless of prudence.
- **Inverse condemnation** — California's strict-liability regime means SCE can be liable for fire damage *without* negligence, an open-ended structural hazard.
- **CPUC prudency-disallowance risk** — regulators can deny cost recovery, forcing shareholders rather than ratepayers to absorb costs.
- **Recurring fire-season tail risk** — every June–November fire season is a fresh draw on the same catastrophic-liability distribution.
- **High leverage** — utility-level D/E (2.35x) and financing needs in a higher-rate environment pressure the equity if access to capital tightens.

## Key Risks

- **Wildfire liability exceeding the AB 1054 cap/fund** — the dominant risk; catastrophic claims that breach the ~$21B fund and prudency-cap framework transfer directly to shareholders.
- **Inverse condemnation** — strict liability for equipment-caused fire damage even absent negligence.
- **CPUC cost-recovery disallowance** — prudency findings that deny rate recovery of fire or mitigation costs.
- **Annual fire-season recurrence** — California's June–November fire season is a structurally recurring tail risk, not a one-off.
- **Leverage & financing** — heavy utility debt load and ongoing capex funding needs are sensitive to rates and credit access (see [[risk-management]]).
- **Regulatory / political shifts to AB 1054** — any legislative weakening of the fund or cap framework would remove the central mitigant of the equity story.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

In normal conditions EIX is a rate-sensitive **regulated-utility yield and rate-base story** — it trades with the [[utilities]] complex (XLU), inversely to interest rates, and on the durability of its dividend and authorized return. Since the Eaton Fire, however, it has traded as an **[[event-driven|event-driven]] liability-handicapping name**: the market prices the distribution of ultimate Eaton liability against AB 1054 protection and CPUC cost recovery, so the stock moves on settlement and cost-recovery headlines far more than on rate-base fundamentals. Mechanically:

- **Options & volatility** — unusually active [[options]] for a utility, with elevated implied [[volatility]] and persistent put skew as the market handicaps the liability tail.
- **Recovery template** — [[pg-and-e]] (PCG) post-2019 is the reference path: bankruptcy was avoided here, and the bull thesis is the discount-to-peers narrowing as liability gets bounded.
- **Correlated names** — PCG and Sempra (California regulatory beta) on fire/regulatory days; the rate-sensitive [[utilities]] complex ([[duke-energy]], [[dte-energy]], XLU) on quiet days.
- **Yield** — dividend yield ~5% at April 2026 prices, supporting the income shareholder base; the payout itself is a watched signal of management's confidence in the liability outcome.
