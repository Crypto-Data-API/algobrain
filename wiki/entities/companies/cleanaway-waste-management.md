## Business Overview & Segments

Cleanaway is Australia's largest waste-management company and an [[industrials]]-sector infrastructure asset: a vertically integrated network of collection fleets, transfer stations, material-recovery facilities, treatment plants and landfills. Its earnings are annuity-like — long-dated municipal and commercial contracts plus disposal "post-collection" assets (landfills, treatment) that competitors must use. Revenue is supplemented by recovered-commodity sales and landfill-gas electricity.

Cleanaway operates through three principal business lines:

| Segment | What it is | Role (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **Solid Waste Services** | Municipal/commercial/industrial collection and disposal (general, putrescible, recyclables, C&D, organics); fleet, transfer stations, MRFs, landfills | The largest segment — collection density + landfill ownership is the core moat |
| **Liquid Waste & Health Services** | Hazardous/non-hazardous liquids, hydrocarbons, chemicals, grease-trap/oily water, used-oil refining, and clinical/healthcare waste | Higher-margin, specialised; regulated, sticky treatment franchises |
| **Industrial & Waste Services** | Industrial cleaning, vacuum loading, high-pressure water, pipeline maintenance, non-destructive digging for resources/oil-and-gas/infrastructure | More project- and cycle-linked services revenue |

The company also generates revenue from the sale of recovered commodities (paper, cardboard, metals, plastics) and from electricity generated at landfill gas facilities. [[fred-mcnaught|Fred McNaught]] holds it as a defensive infrastructure asset (see lead and Sources).

## History

Cleanaway's origins trace back to 1969. The modern entity was formed through the 2016 transformation of Transpacific Industries Group – a debt-laden waste roll-up – which renamed itself Cleanaway Waste Management Limited to align with its long-standing operating brand. Since then the company has grown materially, most notably through the A$2.5 billion acquisition of Toxfree Solutions in 2018, the 2021 acquisition of SKM Recycling assets in Victoria, and the 2021 acquisition of the Sydney Resource Network (SRN) from Suez, following the Veolia/Suez merger.

## Recent Developments

For the full year ended 30 June 2025 (FY25), Cleanaway reported net revenue of A$3,302.7 million (up 3.4%) and statutory EBIT of A$353.7 million (up 3.6%). The company declared a fully franked final dividend of 3.2 cents per share, taking total FY25 dividends to 6.0 cents per share (up 20.0%). For the half year ended 31 December 2025 (1H FY26), Cleanaway delivered 13% net revenue growth, driven by disciplined pricing and strategic acquisitions, and upgraded its FY26 underlying EBIT guidance to a range of A$470–500 million, including approximately A$30 million of EBIT contribution from acquisitions. Management has pointed to improved safety performance alongside the financial result.

The most recent filing (annual) was lodged on 2023-06-30. See [[#Financial Reports]] below.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Cleanaway has a **moderate [[economic-moat|economic moat]]** built on the two structural advantages of the waste industry — route density and disposal assets:

- **Route density** — waste collection is a fixed-route logistics business: the operator with the most bins per truck-kilometre in a given area has the lowest unit cost. Cleanaway's national scale and dense urban collection networks create a cost advantage a sub-scale entrant cannot match — the classic waste-industry [[economic-moat|moat]].
- **Disposal infrastructure (landfills + treatment)** — landfills and licensed treatment facilities are scarce, capital-intensive and near-impossible to permit anew (NIMBY + environmental approvals). Owning the "post-collection" assets lets Cleanaway capture the full value chain and forces competitors to pay it for disposal — a durable, regulated barrier.
- **Long contracts & switching costs** — multi-year municipal and commercial contracts with embedded escalators create sticky, annuity-like revenue and [[switching-costs]].
- **Vertical integration** — combining collection, transfer, recovery and disposal compounds the density and disposal advantages.

The moat is real but not wide: thin net margins (~4%) leave little buffer, recovered-commodity prices are volatile, and there is no second domestic operator of similar scale to discipline pricing — but EPA/landfill-levy regulation and cost inflation can erode returns. [[fred-mcnaught|Fred McNaught]] holds it precisely for the defensive, infrastructure-like quality of this network (Source: [[fred-sam-session-2024-01-02]]).

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Pricing discipline** — CPI-plus contract escalators and disciplined pricing drove ~13% net-revenue growth in 1H FY26; pricing is the primary organic lever in a low-margin business.
- **Bolt-on M&A** — a long record of accretive acquisitions (Toxfree 2018, SKM and Sydney Resource Network 2021) consolidates a fragmented industry; ~A$30m of acquisition EBIT in FY26 guidance.
- **EBIT margin recovery** — FY26 underlying EBIT guidance upgraded to A$470-500m; operating leverage as volumes and pricing flow through.
- **Resource recovery / circular economy** — investment in MRFs, organics, energy-from-waste and used-oil refining, plus landfill-gas electricity — structural tailwinds from recycling mandates.
- **Landfill-levy and regulation** — higher landfill levies push waste up the value chain toward recovery, where Cleanaway is positioned.
- **Recurring catalysts** — February (half-year) and August (full-year) results, FY26 EBIT guidance (A$470-500m), bolt-on announcements, recovered-commodity prices, and EPA/landfill-levy changes.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Defensive, annuity-like infrastructure: waste volumes are non-discretionary; should fall far less than the market in a downturn (the reason [[fred-mcnaught|Fred McNaught]] holds it).
- Route density + scarce landfill/treatment assets are a genuine, hard-to-replicate moat.
- Pricing discipline driving double-digit revenue growth and an upgraded FY26 EBIT range.
- Consolidator of a fragmented industry with a track record of accretive bolt-ons.
- Structural circular-economy tailwinds (recycling mandates, landfill levies, energy-from-waste).

### Bear case
- **Thin margins (~4% net)** leave little buffer for labour, fuel and cost inflation.
- High PE (~34x) against a modest ROE (~5%) — a full valuation for a low-return business.
- Serial-acquisition integration risk and goodwill on the balance sheet.
- Landfill remediation and environmental/EPA liabilities are long-tailed and uncertain.
- Recovered-commodity price volatility (paper, metals, plastics) swings recovery-segment economics.

## Key Risks

- **Thin net margins** — ~4% net margin means small cost moves (labour, fuel, energy) hit earnings disproportionately.
- **Environmental / landfill liabilities** — remediation, leachate and post-closure obligations are long-dated and can surprise.
- **Regulatory** — EPA conditions and landfill-levy changes alter both costs and the economics of disposal.
- **Integration risk** — a steady cadence of acquisitions carries execution and goodwill-impairment risk.
- **Commodity prices** — recovered-material prices are volatile and outside management's control.
- **Valuation** — a high (~34x) multiple versus a modest ROE leaves limited margin for disappointment.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Cleanaway trades as a **defensive infrastructure compounder** — a low-beta, annuity-like industrial valued on EBIT/EBITDA growth and the durability of its network rather than on a single year's earnings. Mechanically:

- **EBIT guidance is the live benchmark** — FY26 underlying EBIT of A$470-500m anchors the bull/bear debate; results are read against guidance and margin trajectory.
- **Premium multiple, modest returns** — ~34x PE against ~5% ROE; the market pays up for defensiveness and consolidation optionality, so the stock de-rates if margins or guidance disappoint.
- **Defensive beta** — non-discretionary volumes mean it should fall less than the market in a sell-off (the infrastructure thesis); it lags in strong risk-on rallies.
- **Dividend / franking** — fully franked dividends (FY25 total 6.0cps, +20%) are material to after-tax yield for Australian residents (see the franking line in Fundamentals); total return = modest yield + earnings growth.
- **Catalysts** — half-year (Feb) / full-year (Aug) results, EBIT guidance, bolt-on M&A, recovered-commodity prices, EPA/landfill-levy changes.
- **Liquidity/options** — large-cap ASX liquidity; no deep single-name options market, so most positioning is via the underlying or industrials/infrastructure ETFs.
