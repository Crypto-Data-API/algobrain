Westpac Banking Corporation (ASX: WBC) is Australia's oldest bank, founded in 1817 as the Bank of New South Wales — making it one of the oldest continuously operating banks in the world. It is the second-largest Australian bank by assets and the third-largest by market capitalisation (~AUD $100 billion+), operating under multiple brands including Westpac, St.George, Bank of Melbourne, BankSA, and RAMS.

## Overview

Westpac is a full-service bank offering retail banking, business banking, institutional banking, and wealth management. While it lacks the retail broker market share of [[commsec|CommSec]] (CBA), Westpac maintains a significant wealth management presence through BT Financial Group and its BT Panorama wrap platform. Westpac's multi-brand strategy — with St.George, Bank of Melbourne, and BankSA operating as distinct brands — gives it broad geographic coverage and brand differentiation.

Westpac typically trades at a discount to [[commonwealth-bank|CBA]] and offers the highest [[dividend]] yield among the Big Four banks, making it popular with income-focused investors.

## History

- **1817**: Founded as the Bank of New South Wales — Australia's first bank, predating Australian federation by 84 years
- **1982**: Renamed to Westpac Banking Corporation
- **1992**: Near-collapse during Australian recession — bad debts almost sank the bank, requiring emergency capital raising
- **1995**: Launched online banking — one of Australia's first
- **2002**: Merged with St.George Bank (AUD $18.5 billion deal) — added significant retail presence in NSW and Queensland
- **2008**: Navigated GFC relatively well compared to global peers
- **2018-2019**: Royal Commission revelations — fee-for-no-service, inappropriate financial advice
- **2020**: Paid $1.3 billion fine to [[austrac|AUSTRAC]] for 23 million AML/CTF breaches — largest corporate fine in Australian history. CEO Brian Hartzer resigned after tone-deaf comments about the scandal
- **2022**: Peter King era — strategic simplification, cost reduction, technology investment
- **2024**: Market cap recovered to ~$100B+ range, continued focus on simplification and digital investment

## Investment Services

### Westpac Online Investing

Westpac's self-directed share trading platform. Less popular than [[commsec]] but offers a solid feature set:

- **ASX shares**: Full access to all ASX-listed equities, [[chess-sponsorship|CHESS-sponsored]]
- **ETFs**: All ASX-listed ETFs
- **International shares**: Access to international markets via partnership arrangements
- **Brokerage fees**: $19.95 per trade up to $10,000, 0.11% for larger trades
- Integrated with Westpac banking — instant fund transfers for Westpac customers

### BT Financial Group

Westpac's wealth management arm, providing:

- **BT Super**: Retail and corporate [[superannuation]] products
- **BT Panorama**: Comprehensive wrap platform for advisers and investors — consolidates super, pension, investments, and insurance in a single platform. Used by financial advisers and directly by sophisticated investors
- **Managed funds**: Access to a wide range of managed fund options
- **Financial advice**: Scaled back significantly post-Royal Commission, but advisory services remain available through BT

### Margin Lending

Available through BT — investors can borrow against their share portfolio to invest, using listed securities as collateral. Standard margin lending risks apply (see [[risk-management]]).

### SMSF Services

Westpac offers [[smsf|SMSF]]-specific banking and investment products, including dedicated SMSF transaction accounts, BT Panorama SMSF super, and integration with Westpac Online Investing for share trading within the SMSF structure.

### Dividend History

Westpac has a long history of paying fully [[franking-credits|franked]] dividends, though it cut its dividend during the 2020 COVID downturn (halved the interim dividend) and during the AUSTRAC scandal period. Dividends have since recovered. Westpac typically offers the highest dividend yield of the Big Four, reflecting its lower valuation multiple.

## Regulatory and Legal

### AUSTRAC Fine (2020)

Westpac paid a $1.3 billion fine to [[austrac|AUSTRAC]] in 2020 — the largest corporate fine in Australian history — for 23 million breaches of AML/CTF laws. The breaches were far more serious than [[commonwealth-bank|CBA's]] earlier case:

- **23 million International Funds Transfer Instructions (IFTIs)** not reported to AUSTRAC
- **Failure to conduct due diligence on transactions** to the Philippines and Southeast Asia, including transactions consistent with child exploitation payment patterns
- **Inadequate transaction monitoring** across millions of transactions
- CEO Brian Hartzer resigned after publicly downplaying the scandal. Chairman Lindsay Maxsted also departed.

The scandal permanently damaged Westpac's reputation and triggered a fundamental overhaul of compliance systems and culture.

### Crypto Restrictions

Westpac has imposed some of the most restrictive crypto policies among the Big Four banks — a $10,000 monthly limit on transfers to cryptocurrency exchanges, with some customers reporting transfers blocked entirely. This reflects Westpac's heightened sensitivity to AML/CTF risk following the AUSTRAC fine.

## For Investors and Traders

Westpac is typically the "value play" among the Big Four banks — it trades at the lowest price-to-earnings ratio and offers the highest [[dividend]] yield.

**Bull case**: Highest dividend yield among Big Four (4.5-5%), fully [[franking-credits|franked]]. Trades at a discount to peers — potential for valuation re-rating. Multi-brand strategy (St.George, Bank of Melbourne, BankSA) provides diversified retail presence. Significant cost reduction opportunity through digital transformation.

**Bear case**: AUSTRAC scandal damaged brand and required massive remediation spending. Historically weaker technology platform than [[commonwealth-bank|CBA]]. Near-collapse in 1992 is a reminder that even Big Four banks are not invincible. Lower ROE than CBA suggests structural efficiency gap.

**Income investor perspective**: Westpac's higher yield makes it particularly attractive for [[smsf|SMSF]] investors in accumulation phase (where the 15% tax rate maximises the benefit of franking credits) and retirees seeking maximum income. The fully franked dividend at a ~5% yield represents an effective gross yield of ~7.1% for investors in the 30% tax bracket.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Westpac shares the wide collective [[economic-moat|economic moat]] of the Australian banking oligopoly, anchored by Australia's oldest banking franchise:

- **Deposit franchise & funding cost** — a large base of low-cost transaction and savings deposits funds the bulk of the loan book, the foundational moat in retail banking.
- **Multi-brand reach** — Westpac, St.George, Bank of Melbourne and BankSA give it differentiated brand presence across NSW, Victoria and South Australia, broadening distribution and raising [[switching-costs]].
- **Scale in mortgages & wealth** — the #2 Australian bank by assets, with a major wealth platform in BT Panorama (~A$150B funds under administration after the 2026 migration).
- **Regulatory oligopoly** — [[apra|APRA]] capital rules and the "four pillars" policy protect the Big Four collectively; Westpac is one of the four protected incumbents.

The moat is **wide but Westpac runs a structural efficiency gap**: its ROE has persistently trailed [[commonwealth-bank|CBA]], reflecting a historically weaker technology platform and the drag of post-AUSTRAC remediation. The multi-year "UNITE" technology programme (targeted completion FY29) is the central effort to close that gap — successful execution is the bull case; slippage is the bear case.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **UNITE technology transformation** — the multi-year programme to simplify core systems and lower the cost-to-income ratio; the single biggest internal lever and re-rating catalyst.
- **Net interest margin (NIM)** — repricing of loans and deposits as the [[reserve-bank-of-australia|RBA]] moves the cash rate; the dominant earnings swing factor.
- **Cost reduction** — workforce restructuring and process simplification to narrow the efficiency gap to peers.
- **Australian credit growth** — system home-loan and business-credit growth tied to population, immigration and house prices.
- **Capital management** — surplus [[apra|APRA]] CET1 capital funds buybacks and supports the high dividend; recurring catalyst.
- **Portfolio simplification** — RAMS divestment and exits from non-core wealth lines sharpen the franchise.
- **Recurring events** — full-year results (Nov, fiscal year ends 30 Sep), half-year (May), quarterly updates, dividend declarations and [[reserve-bank-of-australia|RBA]] decisions.

## Trading Relevance

- **Index weight** — a top [[asx-200]] constituent; trades as part of the "bank basket" with [[commonwealth-bank|CBA]], [[nab]] and [[anz]].
- **Value/yield rotation** — as the highest-yielding, lowest-multiple major, Westpac tends to outperform in "value" and income-seeking rotations and lag in growth-led tape.
- **Rate sensitivity** — trades off [[reserve-bank-of-australia|RBA]] expectations and bond yields.
- **Dividend dynamics** — the largest ex-dividend gaps of the majors (highest yield); franking is central to domestic income demand.
- **Beta** — a moderate, income-style large-cap, with re-rating optionality tied to UNITE execution.

## Key Risks

- **Australian housing concentration** — residential mortgages dominate the book; a house-price downturn or unemployment spike would hit impairments and capital.
- **Technology execution (UNITE)** — the transformation is expensive (A$850-950M in FY26) and multi-year; cost overruns or delays would extend the ROE gap to peers.
- **Net interest margin compression** — broker-driven mortgage competition and deposit competition squeeze NIM.
- **Regulatory / [[apra|APRA]] capital & conduct** — the record A$1.3B [[austrac|AUSTRAC]] fine (2020) is a stark reminder of conduct tail risk; capital-ratio increases and macroprudential rules constrain returns.
- **Lower ROE / structural efficiency gap** — Westpac's persistently lower returns versus [[commonwealth-bank|CBA]] cap its valuation until proven otherwise.
- **Macro & funding** — recession, rising unemployment and wholesale-funding/credit-spread shocks are the classic bank-cycle risks. The 1992 near-collapse is a reminder even majors are not invincible.

## Business Model & Segments

Westpac reports through four customer-facing segments plus a Group Businesses segment:

- **Consumer** — banking products and services for retail customers in Australia, including home loans, personal loans, credit cards, cash and transactional banking, insurance, superannuation, investments and share trading.
- **Business & Wealth** — business banking (micro, SME, commercial and agribusiness), Westpac's wealth and private banking businesses, and Westpac Pacific operations serving Pacific Island nations.
- **Institutional (Westpac Institutional Bank / WIB)** — corporate, institutional and government banking, transaction banking, corporate and structured finance, financial markets, and trade and supply chain finance.
- **New Zealand** — banking and wealth services to consumer, business and institutional customers across New Zealand.
- **Group Businesses** — includes Treasury, Group Technology and corporate functions.

Key brands include Westpac (core Australian bank), St.George (NSW), Bank of Melbourne (Victoria), BankSA (South Australia), Westpac Pacific, and (historically) RAMS, which the group agreed to sell in 2025.

## Recent Developments

Under new CEO Anthony Miller, who took over from Peter King in December 2024, Westpac has focused on the multi-year "UNITE" technology transformation programme and portfolio simplification:

- **FY25 results (year to 30 September 2025)**: Full-year net profit after tax of approximately A$7 billion, down approximately 2% on FY24 as margins softened but cost growth was contained.
- **1Q FY26 update**: Unaudited statutory net profit of approximately A$1.9 billion, up 5% on the 2H FY25 quarterly average. Net profit excluding notable items approximately A$1.9 billion, up 6%.
- **UNITE transformation**: Multi-year technology programme to simplify and modernise core systems, with investment of A$850–950 million expected in FY26 and full delivery targeted by FY29. BT Panorama migration completed in March 2026, holding more than 150,000 accounts and approximately A$150 billion in funds under administration.
- **Cost management**: Significant workforce restructuring announced in 2025 including more than 1,500 role reductions as part of the UNITE-aligned cost programme, partially offset by hiring of more customer-facing bankers.
- **RAMS divestment**: Agreed to sell the RAMS mortgage portfolio (approximately A$21 billion book) to streamline mortgage operations.

The most recent filing (annual) was lodged on 2025-06-30. See [[#Financial Reports]] below.
