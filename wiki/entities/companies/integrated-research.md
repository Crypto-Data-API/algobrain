Integrated Research Ltd (ASX: IRI) is an Australian enterprise software company that builds performance-management and monitoring tools for business-critical computing, payments networks, and unified-communications infrastructure. Its flagship Prognosis platform watches the health of systems run by major banks, payment processors, telcos, and enterprises worldwide. For traders, IRI is a thinly-traded ASX small-cap and a textbook case study in how deteriorating revenue growth can flip a long thesis into a high-conviction sell.

## Business Overview

IRI sells software (historically licence-and-maintenance, now shifting toward subscription/recurring revenue) under the Prognosis brand across three domains: payments (transaction monitoring for card networks and processors), unified communications (call-quality and collaboration-platform monitoring for Microsoft Teams, Cisco, etc.), and infrastructure/transaction processing (mainframe and high-volume systems). Customers are large enterprises with mission-critical uptime requirements, which makes revenue sticky but also concentrated. As of June 2026 (approximate), IRI is a micro/small-cap with annual revenue in roughly the A$80-90M range and a market capitalisation under A$200M; exact figures should be confirmed against the latest ASX filings.

The investment story for the past several years has been a transition from perpetual-licence sales to a recurring-revenue model — a transition that compresses reported revenue in the near term (deferred recognition) and has weighed on the share price and dividend.

## 2025-2026 Developments

IRI has continued to push its subscription/SaaS transition while managing cost discipline; the company remains profitable but small, and growth has been uneven across its payments, communications, and infrastructure lines. Traders should treat ASX results announcements (half-year in February, full-year in August) as the primary catalysts, alongside any commentary on recurring-revenue mix, net retention, and dividend policy. Because liquidity is thin, single large orders can move the price materially.

## Product & Segments

IRI's flagship **Prognosis** platform monitors the health and performance of mission-critical computing systems. The portfolio spans three domains, each with different growth and concentration characteristics:

| Segment | What it monitors | Customer type | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Payments** | Transaction monitoring for card networks, switches, and processors | Card schemes, banks, payment processors | High-value, sticky, highly concentrated; the franchise jewel |
| **Unified Communications** | Call quality / collaboration-platform performance (Microsoft Teams, Cisco, etc.) | Large enterprises, telcos | Tied to enterprise UC spend; competitive |
| **Infrastructure / Transaction processing** | Mainframe and high-volume transaction systems | Banks, large enterprises | Legacy-heavy; mature, slow-growth |

The mission-critical nature of the workloads makes revenue **sticky** (customers do not casually rip out monitoring on a payments switch), but it also makes it **concentrated** — a handful of very large customers drive a meaningful share of revenue, so a single contract loss or renewal renegotiation can move the numbers disproportionately.

## Competitive Positioning & Peers

IRI is a small, specialist vendor competing against far larger observability and APM (application performance monitoring) platforms whose breadth and R&D budgets dwarf its own.

| Competitive set | Examples | Implication for IRI |
|---|---|---|
| Broad observability / APM | Datadog, Dynatrace, Splunk, New Relic | Far larger, faster-growing; can bundle monitoring and out-invest IRI |
| Cloud-native incumbents | Hyperscaler-native tooling (AWS/Azure/GCP) | Erodes the case for third-party niche tools as workloads move to cloud |
| Niche payments/UC monitoring | Specialist transaction- and UC-monitoring vendors | Direct overlap in IRI's strongest verticals |

IRI's defensible position is its **deep specialisation in payments and legacy/high-volume transaction processing** — domains the broad observability players have historically under-served. The strategic risk is that the broader platforms move down into these niches, or that customers consolidate vendors onto a single observability suite. Structurally deteriorating revenue growth (the core of the [[revenue-growth]] case study below) is the market's read on whether that competitive position is being lost.

## Bull vs Bear Case

**Bull case**
- Profitable and cash-generative — unusual for a small-cap "story" stock; not dependent on dilutive raises.
- Sticky, mission-critical payments franchise with high switching costs and a concentrated, high-value customer base.
- Subscription/recurring-revenue transition, if it lands, should re-accelerate reported revenue and improve quality of earnings once the deferred-recognition drag rolls off.
- Cost discipline supports the dividend and downside valuation; a successful stabilisation could re-rate a deeply de-rated stock.

**Bear case**
- Multi-year revenue stagnation/decline is the central concern — in software, persistent revenue-growth deterioration usually signals lost competitive position, not a cyclical dip (the [[revenue-growth]] lesson).
- Competes against vastly larger, faster-growing observability/APM platforms with far bigger R&D budgets.
- Customer concentration: loss or renegotiation of a major payments customer would hit revenue hard.
- The subscription transition has compressed reported revenue and weighed on price and dividend for years; execution risk remains.
- Thin liquidity caps institutional interest and amplifies downside moves.

## Valuation Framework (qualitative)

Unlike the speculative micro-caps, IRI is a **profitable, dividend-paying** small-cap, so it can be anchored to fundamentals — but the key variable is the *direction of revenue*, not the current multiple:

- **Revenue trajectory is the master variable.** Per the [[revenue-growth]] case study, structurally declining software revenue is the dominant signal; a low earnings multiple on a shrinking-revenue base is a value trap, not value. Watch the recurring-revenue mix and net retention before the headline number.
- **Quality of the transition:** distinguish *deferred-recognition* revenue compression (a timing effect from the licence→subscription shift, which should reverse) from *genuine* demand loss (which will not). The market's willingness to pay hinges on which one is occurring.
- **Cashflow & dividend support:** as a cash-generative business, free cashflow and the sustainability of the dividend provide a valuation floor that the speculative names lack — but only if revenue stabilises.
- **Balance sheet:** unlike the pre-revenue micro-caps, IRI's funding risk is low (it is profitable); the risk is earnings erosion, not a capital raise.
- **ASX small-cap liquidity discount:** thin trading and no index support warrant a structural discount; this is intrinsic to the small-cap universe (see [[small-cap-investing]]).

Treat the ~A$80–90M revenue range and sub-A$200M market-cap figures in the [[Business Overview]] as approximate; confirm against the latest ASX results before relying on any multiple.

## Capital Structure & Funding

- IRI is **profitable and cash-generative**, so — unlike the speculative micro-caps in this register — it does **not** rely on recurring dilutive equity raises. Funding risk is correspondingly low.
- It has historically paid a **dividend**, which has been pressured by the revenue/subscription transition; dividend changes are themselves a signal about management's confidence in cashflow.
- The investor risk here is **earnings/revenue erosion and a value-trap multiple**, not dilution or going-concern — a materially different risk profile from [[artrya|AYA]] and [[adisyn|AI1]].
- Register: a thinly-traded small-cap; low free float and modest institutional ownership mean liquidity is the binding constraint on position sizing.

## Trading Playbook (ASX small-cap)

- **Liquidity is the binding constraint.** IRI is thinly traded with wide spreads; single large orders move the price materially. Use **limit orders** and size for the exit, not the entry.
- **No meaningful listed-options market.** There is no exchange-traded options chain to hedge or leverage IRI. Do not assume one exists.
- **Scheduled catalysts.** The primary catalysts are **half-year results (February)** and **full-year results (August)**, plus commentary on recurring-revenue mix, net retention, contract wins/losses, and dividend policy. These are the windows where the thin stock gaps.
- **Revenue-growth signal.** Per the Fred McNaught case study below, a structural reversal in revenue growth is the highest-conviction sell trigger — act decisively when the growth thesis breaks rather than averaging down on a low multiple.
- **Customer-concentration headlines.** A major payments-customer win or loss can move the stock disproportionately given concentration.
- **Not in the [[asx-200]].** IRI sits in the broader All Ordinaries / small-cap universe, so it lacks index-fund support and screens out of many institutional mandates — a structural liquidity headwind.

## Risks

- **Structural revenue decline / value trap:** the central risk — persistent software-revenue deterioration signalling lost competitive position (see [[revenue-growth]]); a cheap multiple on a shrinking base is not value.
- **Competition:** far larger, faster-growing observability/APM platforms (Datadog, Dynatrace, Splunk, etc.) and hyperscaler-native tooling encroaching on IRI's niches.
- **Customer concentration:** loss or renegotiation of a major payments/UC customer would hit revenue disproportionately.
- **Transition execution:** the licence→subscription shift compresses reported revenue near-term and carries execution risk; a failed transition prolongs the de-rating.
- **Dividend risk:** dividend has been pressured by the transition; further cuts would signal cashflow concern and likely hit the share price.
- **Liquidity / volatility:** thin trading, wide spreads, gap risk around results; difficult to exit in size.
- **Listing compliance:** standard ASX continuous-disclosure and listing-rule obligations; lower acute risk than the pre-revenue names given profitability, but still a background consideration.

## Trading Relevance

- **Index membership:** small-cap; not in the ASX 200 as of June 2026 — sits in the broader ASX All Ordinaries / small-cap universe.
- **Liquidity:** low average daily volume; wide spreads; no meaningful listed-options market. Position sizing and limit orders matter.
- **Catalysts:** half-year and full-year results, subscription-transition metrics, contract wins/losses (customer concentration risk), dividend changes.
- **Fred McNaught case study:** McNaught uses IRI as a cautionary tale on [[revenue-growth]] reversal as a sell signal. He bought around A$6 and held as the business grew; when revenue began declining he sold, and the stock eventually fell toward ~38 cents. The lesson: in a software company, structurally deteriorating revenue growth usually signals lost competitive position rather than a cyclical dip — act decisively when the growth thesis breaks.
