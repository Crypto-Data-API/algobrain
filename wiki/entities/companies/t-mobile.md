T-Mobile US (NASDAQ: TMUS) is one of the three dominant US wireless carriers, serving over 130 million customers and known as the "Un-carrier" for its disruptive, value-led marketing. Majority-controlled by Germany's Deutsche Telekom, T-Mobile transformed itself via the 2020 Sprint merger into the US 5G coverage leader and has since posted industry-best postpaid phone net additions. For traders it is the growth-and-cash-return story among US telecoms — high EBITDA margins, aggressive buybacks, and a relatively clean balance sheet versus AT&T and Verizon.

## Business Overview

T-Mobile's core business is postpaid and prepaid wireless service, with the key operating metrics being postpaid phone net adds, churn (industry-leading low), and average revenue per account. Its competitive edge is a deep mid-band 5G spectrum position (largely from Sprint's 2.5 GHz holdings), which gave it a multi-year head start in 5G capacity and coverage. The growth narrative has broadened beyond phones into two adjacencies: **5G fixed wireless access (FWA) home internet**, where it leads the industry in net adds and is taking share from cable, and **fiber**, via joint ventures (e.g., with EQT/Lumos and the Metronet acquisition) to add a wireline broadband leg. Margins are very strong for a telecom (EBITDA margin ~38-41%), and management has run one of the largest buyback programs in the market.

The strategic logic is **convergence** — pairing the leading 5G network with FWA and fiber to sell wireless-plus-broadband bundles that raise household value and lower churn.

## 2025-2026 Developments

- **UScellular acquisition:** T-Mobile agreed in 2024 to acquire substantially all of UScellular's wireless operations and a large slice of its spectrum (~$4.4 billion), closing in 2025 — consolidating regional subscribers and bolstering its spectrum stack.
- **Fiber/broadband push:** continued build-out of FWA home internet and fiber JVs to position wireless+broadband convergence bundles.
- **2024 results / momentum (reported through 2025):** record postpaid phone net adds, ~$80 billion revenue scale, net margin expanding to ~14%, and a multi-year, multi-tens-of-billions capital-return authorization. The FY2024 fundamentals (period end 31 Dec 2024) are detailed below.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

T-Mobile holds a **wide [[economic-moat|moat]]** rooted in the structural characteristics of US wireless — a capital- and spectrum-intensive oligopoly of three national carriers with enormous barriers to entry. Its specific edges: (1) a **mid-band 5G spectrum lead** (largely Sprint's 2.5 GHz), giving a multi-year capacity-and-coverage advantage that translates into the best network experience per dollar; (2) **scale economics** — fixed network costs amortized over 130M+ customers, with the Sprint merger having removed a competitor and added density; (3) **[[switching-costs]]** — device-financing balances, bundled lines, and account inertia keep churn industry-low; and (4) a differentiated **"Un-carrier" brand** built on value and customer-friendly moves. The oligopoly structure itself is the deepest part of the moat: spectrum scarcity and the cost of a national network make new national entrants effectively impossible, so the competitive set is just [[verizon]] and [[att]]. T-Mobile's relative position within that oligopoly has been improving — it has out-added the incumbents on postpaid phones for years and now extends the network advantage into FWA broadband, where cable incumbents have no spectrum answer.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Postpaid phone net adds & low churn** — the core growth and quality metric; T-Mobile has led the industry.
- **Fixed wireless access (FWA)** — leading home-internet net adds, taking share from cable at high incremental margin; the standout growth vector.
- **Fiber / convergence** — JV-built fiber (EQT/Lumos) plus Metronet add a wireline leg for bundles that lower churn.
- **UScellular integration** — adds regional subscribers and spectrum; synergy realization is a catalyst.
- **Capital return** — one of the market's largest buyback programs plus a growing dividend; the cash-return story is central.
- **Margin expansion** — Sprint synergies and scale continue to lift EBITDA and free cash flow.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly postpaid net adds/churn, FWA subscriber growth, spectrum auctions, M&A integration, and capital-return updates.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Best competitive position of the three US carriers: spectrum lead, leading net adds, lowest churn, and the "Un-carrier" brand.
- FWA and fiber broaden the growth runway into broadband, taking share from cable with a structural spectrum advantage.
- Strong telecom margins (EBITDA ~38-41%) plus a massive buyback and growing dividend — a rare growth-and-cash-return telecom.
- Relatively cleaner balance sheet and better growth than [[att]]/[[verizon]].

### Bear case
- **Wireless is maturing** — US penetration is high; growth ultimately depends on share gains and FWA, not market expansion.
- **Capital intensity** — 5G, fiber, and spectrum require continuous heavy capex; FWA capacity has limits.
- **Leverage** — D/E ~1.3x (sector-typical but real); rate-cycle and refinancing exposure.
- **Deutsche Telekom overhang** — majority owner's stake and potential share dispositions are a structural consideration.
- **Competitive escalation** — promotional intensity from [[verizon]]/[[att]] or cable-MVNO pressure can compress ARPU and adds.

## Key Risks

- **Competitive intensity** — promotional wars with [[verizon]] and [[att]], plus cable-MVNO ([[comcast]]/[[charter-communications]]) pressure on wireless and FWA on broadband.
- **Capital intensity & FWA limits** — heavy ongoing 5G/fiber/spectrum capex; FWA depends on spare mid-band capacity that has a ceiling.
- **Leverage / rate cycle** — telecom-typical leverage (D/E ~1.3x) and refinancing exposure; covenant headroom in a downturn.
- **Deutsche Telekom overhang** — majority ownership and any stake changes are a structural consideration.
- **Regulatory** — spectrum policy, M&A approvals, and broadband/telecom regulation.
- **Maturity** — limited subscriber-market growth means the story rides on share, FWA, and capital return rather than secular expansion.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

T-Mobile trades as the **growth-and-cash-return story among US telecoms** — valued on net adds, FWA growth, EBITDA/free-cash-flow expansion, and the size of its buyback rather than as a pure yield play. Mechanically:

- **Lower-beta megacap** — defensive relative to the broad market but with more growth than [[att]]/[[verizon]]; it tends to outperform peers on operating momentum.
- **Cash-return-driven** — one of the largest buyback programs in the market plus a growing (still modest-yield) dividend; total return leans on repurchases more than the dividend.
- **Telecom-leverage context** — D/E ~1.3x is sector-normal; read leverage and coverage against telecom peers, not the standard <1.0 bar (see Fundamentals).
- **Catalysts** — quarterly postpaid net adds/churn and FWA subscriber prints move the stock; spectrum auctions and M&A integration are episodic catalysts.
- **Correlated** — with [[att]]/[[verizon]] (peers) and inversely with cable broadband ([[comcast]]/[[charter-communications]]) as FWA takes share.
