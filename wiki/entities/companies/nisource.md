NiSource (NYSE: NI) is a regulated multi-utility serving roughly 3.7 million natural gas and electric customers across six states through Columbia Gas and NIPSCO (Northern Indiana Public Service Company). It is an [[s-and-p-500]] constituent that re-rated through 2025-2026 from a sleepy gas utility into one of the market's cleaner **data-center electricity demand** plays via its Northern Indiana service territory.

## Business Overview & Segments

Two regulated platforms: **Columbia Gas** distribution utilities (Ohio, Pennsylvania, Virginia, Kentucky, Maryland) and **NIPSCO** (gas + electric in Northern Indiana). Nearly all earnings come from regulated rate base, making the stock primarily a function of allowed-ROE rate cases, capex execution, and interest rates — with the new data-center load pipeline adding a growth kicker uncommon for gas-heavy utilities. There is essentially no merchant or unregulated commodity exposure: unlike [[nrg-energy]] or [[vistra]], NiSource does not earn on power-price swings, so the earnings stream is unusually defensive for a name now associated with the AI-power theme.

| Segment / platform | What it is | Earnings / rate-base mix (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **Columbia Gas** (distribution) | Regulated natural-gas distribution LDCs across Ohio, Pennsylvania, Virginia, Kentucky and Maryland — pipes, mains, meters, safety/modernization capex | The larger, more geographically diversified leg; steady rate-base growth driven by pipeline replacement and safety programs |
| **NIPSCO — Gas** | Regulated gas distribution in Northern Indiana | Smaller, stable distribution rate base under Indiana (IURC) jurisdiction |
| **NIPSCO — Electric** | Regulated electric generation, transmission and distribution in Northern Indiana; fleet transitioning from coal to wind/solar/storage + new gas | The growth engine — the coal-to-renewables build-out plus data-center load (new dedicated gas turbines + storage) is where the incremental rate base and the AI narrative concentrate |

The economic logic is the regulated compact: NiSource invests capital into the grid (rate base), regulators set an allowed return on that capital, and earnings grow roughly with the rate base — independent of weather-driven commodity prices. The 2025-2026 re-rating reflects the market pricing in a *larger* rate base (data-center-driven capex) rather than any change in business model.

## 2025-2026 Developments

- **FY2025 results** (reported 11 February 2026) met guidance; the company **reaffirmed 2026 non-GAAP adjusted EPS guidance of $2.02-$2.07**, and Q1 2026 EPS beat on data-center momentum.
- **Capital plan:** ~**$28.0 billion of investment for 2026-2030**, including nearly $7.0 billion of strategic data-center infrastructure; supports a raised long-term adjusted EPS CAGR of **8-9%** and rate base growth of 9-11% through 2033.
- **Amazon partnership (Indiana):** commercial agreement including development of two 1,300 MW gas turbines plus 400 MW of storage and transmission at an aggregate cost of $6-7 billion, under a 15-year initial contract expected to begin serving demand in 2027.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

NiSource's [[economic-moat]] is the classic regulated-utility franchise: it holds an exclusive, government-granted right to distribute gas and electricity within defined service territories, and replicating its installed pipes, wires and substations is economically and legally prohibitive. The moat is **wide but capped** — regulators grant the monopoly in exchange for price control, so the upside is bounded by the allowed return on equity rather than by competition.

- **Regulated-monopoly franchise** — no direct competitor can string parallel infrastructure into Columbia Gas or NIPSCO territory; customers are effectively captive, giving extremely high revenue visibility.
- **Rate-base / allowed-ROE model** — earnings scale with invested capital at a regulator-set return. Growth is a function of *how much capex regulators approve*, not market share. This makes the earnings stream predictable but rate-sensitive.
- **Regulatory compact & jurisdiction quality** — the durability of the moat depends on constructive regulators. Indiana (IURC) has been a notably **constructive jurisdiction for large-load / data-center economics**, allowing structures that let hyperscaler-driven generation be added to rate base — a genuine competitive advantage over utilities in less accommodating states.
- **Switching costs are structural, not behavioral** — there is simply no alternative supplier, so the moat does not erode the way a consumer brand's might; the threat is regulatory (adverse rate cases, disallowed costs), not competitive.

The competitive question for NiSource is therefore not "will it lose customers" but "will regulators let it earn an adequate return on a rapidly growing capital base" — the defining variable for the whole regulated-utility group.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **~$28.0 billion capital plan (2026-2030)** — including ~$7.0 billion of strategic data-center infrastructure; the largest capex program in NiSource's history and the engine of rate-base growth.
- **Rate-base growth of 9-11% through 2033** — translates into a raised long-term **adjusted EPS CAGR of 8-9%**, exceptional for a gas-heavy regulated utility.
- **Amazon Indiana partnership** — two ~1,300 MW gas turbines plus 400 MW of storage and transmission ($6-7 billion) under a 15-year initial contract, serving load from 2027; the template for converting the data-center pipeline into rate base.
- **Data-center pipeline** — up to **9 GW of potential capacity in Northern Indiana by 2035**; each signed agreement adds approved capex and de-risks the growth outlook.
- **Constructive Indiana (IURC) regulation** — approvals for data-center-related generation and cost-allocation structures are the gating catalyst.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly earnings and guidance (Feb/May/Aug/Nov), rate-case outcomes in Ohio/Pennsylvania, new hyperscaler contract announcements, and 10-year Treasury moves (utility multiples are rate-sensitive).

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- A genuine, regulated-utility growth story: 8-9% EPS CAGR and 9-11% rate-base growth materially above the typical gas-LDC, with downside protection because nearly all earnings are regulated.
- Data-center load converts speculative demand into *approved, rate-based* capex — earnings growth that is contracted and regulator-blessed rather than commodity-dependent.
- Constructive Indiana jurisdiction and the Amazon template give a credible path to monetize up to 9 GW of pipeline.
- Defensive, low-beta profile with a growing dividend — a rare combination of bond-proxy stability and secular growth.

### Bear case
- The premium multiple already prices in the data-center growth; if the pipeline does not convert to signed contracts, the re-rating reverses.
- Rate-sensitive: rising long-term yields compress the multiple regardless of operational execution.
- Heavy capex requires large, sustained financing — equity issuance risk dilutes the EPS growth it is meant to fund.
- Regulatory cost-allocation fights (who pays — hyperscalers or ratepayers) could slow approvals or trigger political backlash.

## Key Risks

- **Interest-rate / multiple compression** — as a bond-proxy, NiSource de-rates when 10-year Treasury yields rise; the AI-driven premium multiple amplifies the downside if rates back up.
- **Demand-conversion risk** — the 9 GW pipeline and 8-9% EPS CAGR depend on data-center load translating into *signed* contracts and *approved* capex; slippage would unwind the growth premium.
- **Regulatory cost-allocation pushback** — disputes over allocating data-center infrastructure costs between hyperscalers and existing ratepayers could delay approvals, invite political scrutiny, or compress allowed returns.
- **Capex execution & financing** — the ~$28B plan carries construction, supply-chain and financing risk; persistent equity needs dilute shareholders and the program must be delivered on time and on budget to earn the planned returns.
- **Adverse rate cases** — lower allowed ROE or disallowed costs in any jurisdiction directly cap earnings.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

NiSource trades like a **regulated-utility bond-proxy with a growth overlay**: low beta, a rate-sensitive multiple, and a steadily growing dividend, but commanding a premium versus sleepier gas LDCs because of the data-center growth narrative layered on since 2025. Mechanically:

- **Low beta, rate-sensitive** — the stock trades with the XLU/utilities complex and inversely with 10-year Treasury yields; the regulated earnings stream means it behaves defensively versus the broad [[s-and-p-500]].
- **Dividend** — a meaningful, regularly growing dividend is a core part of total return (see the API-definition caveat in the Fundamentals section; the *market* yield is modest and typical of a regulated utility, not the inflated API figure).
- **Premium multiple from the AI narrative** — since 2025 NiSource has carried a higher multiple than its gas-utility history would suggest, reflecting the 8-9% EPS CAGR and data-center optionality; that premium is the swing factor and the first thing to compress if the growth story stumbles.
- **Catalyst-driven, not gap-prone** — IV rises around earnings, IURC approvals and rate-case milestones, but moves are modest versus high-beta merchant power names.
