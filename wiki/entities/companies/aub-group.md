**AUB Group Limited** (ASX: AUB) is an insurance broking and underwriting-agency group listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. AUB operates from roughly 579 locations globally across Australia, New Zealand, the UK, US, continental Europe and Asia, combining equity-owned brokerages, underwriting agencies and international wholesale broking under a "network-of-networks" model. Crucially, AUB is an **intermediary**, not a risk carrier: its earnings come from **commission and fee income geared to commercial property-&-casualty (P&C) premium rates**, not from underwriting risk on its own balance sheet — making it a capital-light, high-cash-conversion play on the [[financials|insurance]] value chain rather than a balance-sheet insurer like [[insurance-australia-group]] or [[qbe-insurance]].

## Business Overview & Segments

AUB reports five operating segments. The unifying economic model: AUB takes **commission and fee income** for placing risk with licensed insurers — it does not warehouse insurance risk on its own balance sheet — so revenue is geared to commercial P&C premium **rates** and policy volumes rather than to claims experience.

| Segment | What it is | Earnings mix (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **Australian Broking** | Retail insurance broking and advisory to SMEs through a network of equity-owned and aligned partner brokerages; complemented by member services, life broking, premium funding and claims management | The largest profit pool — the core engine, anchored to Australian commercial P&C rates and SME relationships |
| **Agencies** | Underwriting agencies that design, distribute and manage products/portfolios on behalf of licensed insurers in specialist niches (binder authority, not own-balance-sheet risk) | High-margin and growing; "manufactures" product the network distributes — deepens the value-chain capture |
| **International (Tysers)** | London-based Tysers Lloyd's wholesale broking business (acquired 2022), providing international placement and reinsurance broking capability | Material and a structural diversifier; introduces GBP/AUD sensitivity and exposure to global/Lloyd's rate cycles |
| **New Zealand Broking** | Retail broking partnerships across New Zealand | Smaller, steady; mirrors the Australian network model in a second geography |
| **Support Services** | Automated quoting/binding, white-labelling, technology support for partner brokerages, plus the BizCover/ExpressCover digital SME platforms | Smaller but strategic — the "rails" that lower partner costs and bind the network together |

The franchise is a **distribution and intermediation** business, not an underwriter: AUB earns a slice of every premium dollar it places without bearing the insurance risk, which is why its [[economic-moat|economic moat]] and capital intensity look nothing like a carrier's.

## History

AUB Group was founded in 1985 as Austbrokers Holdings Limited, pioneering the "network-of-networks" model in Australian insurance broking, and listed on the ASX. In November 2015 Austbrokers Holdings was renamed AUB Group Limited to reflect its expansion into underwriting agencies and international markets. In May 2022 AUB announced the A$880 million (GBP500 million) acquisition of London-based Tysers from Odyssey Investment Partners, the sixth-largest wholesale broker at Lloyd's, writing about A$3.6 billion in annual gross written premium. The Tysers acquisition completed later in 2022 and transformed AUB from a regional broker network into a global-focused insurance services group. The company has continued to bolt on partner brokerages and agencies, including Prestige.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

AUB's [[economic-moat|economic moat]] is built on distribution scale and relationship stickiness rather than on capital or brand — the classic intermediary moat:

- **Network-of-networks structure** — AUB does not own all its brokerages outright; it takes **equity stakes in partner brokerages** and binds them into a shared services, placement and technology network. Partners keep local ownership and incentive, while AUB aggregates their placement volume to negotiate better terms with carriers. This aligns owner-operators with the group and is hard for a pure roll-up or a single carrier to replicate.
- **Switching costs & sticky SME relationships** — commercial SME clients rely on their broker for advice, claims advocacy and renewals; the broker, not the insurer, owns the client relationship. Re-broking is friction-heavy, so retention is high and revenue is recurring. These are genuine [[switching-costs]] at the client level.
- **Scale in placement** — aggregated premium volume gives AUB leverage with insurers ([[insurance-australia-group]], [[qbe-insurance]] and others) on commission terms, binder authorities for its agencies, and access to capacity — advantages a sub-scale independent broker cannot match.
- **Agencies = value-chain capture** — by running underwriting agencies under delegated authority, AUB earns manufacturing-style economics on specialist product its own network distributes, without taking the underlying insurance risk.
- **Capital-light, high-cash economics** — because it is an intermediary, AUB converts earnings to cash at a high rate and does not carry the catastrophe/reserve risk that constrains a balance-sheet underwriter; that funds the bolt-on acquisition flywheel and the dividend.

The moat is **wide but rate-cycle-exposed**: the durable defence is client relationships and network scale, but commission income still rises and falls with the commercial P&C premium-rate cycle, which AUB does not control.

## Recent Developments

For H1 FY26 (six months to 31 December 2025) AUB reported underlying NPAT up 13.9% to A$90.4 million, with underlying margin expanding to 33.9% and revenue up 6.6% for the half. CEO Mike Emmett said the result reinforced the durability of AUB's model, citing resilience, capital discipline and future-proofing. The Board declared an interim dividend of 27 cents per share, up 8% on the prior corresponding period. Following the Prestige acquisition and other bolt-ons, AUB upgraded its FY26 UNPAT guidance to A$220-230 million (growth of 9.9-14.9% on FY25). The Group has deployed more than 35 AI solutions across its businesses.

The most recent filing (annual) was lodged on 2023-06-30. See [[#Financial Reports]] below.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Bolt-on acquisition flywheel** — AUB's capital-light cash generation funds a continuous stream of partner-brokerage and agency acquisitions (Prestige and others); buying earnings at sensible multiples and folding them into the network is the core compounding engine.
- **Commercial P&C premium-rate cycle** — commission income is geared to rates; a hard or firming market lifts revenue with little extra cost, while soft pricing is the main bear risk (see below).
- **Tysers / Lloyd's of London** — the 2022 acquisition gave AUB international wholesale placement and reinsurance broking reach; growth in international placement and cross-network referrals is a structural diversifier (with attendant GBP/AUD sensitivity).
- **Agencies expansion** — scaling delegated-authority underwriting agencies captures more of the value chain at high margin without adding balance-sheet risk.
- **Operating leverage & technology** — shared services, automated quote-and-bind (BizCover/ExpressCover) and AI deployment (the group has rolled out 35+ AI solutions across its businesses) lower partner costs and lift margin.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year (February) and full-year (August) results, UNPAT guidance upgrades/downgrades, acquisition announcements, and reads on the global P&C rate cycle from the broker majors.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Capital-light, high-cash intermediary economics — no catastrophe or reserve risk, strong free-cash conversion funding both the dividend and the acquisition flywheel.
- Network-of-networks alignment and sticky SME relationships give durable, recurring revenue and high retention.
- Tysers/Lloyd's diversifies the earnings base internationally and adds placement reach few domestic peers have.
- Consolidation runway: a long tail of independent brokers and agencies to acquire and integrate.
- Operating leverage and AI/automation can expand margin (UNPAT margin already expanding) through the cycle.

### Bear case
- **P&C rate softening** — the central bear case: because commission income is geared to premium rates, a softening commercial P&C market compresses revenue with little offset, even if volumes hold.
- Acquisition-led growth carries integration, goodwill and overpayment risk; a missed bolt-on cadence slows the compounding story.
- Tysers adds GBP/AUD translation risk and exposure to global/Lloyd's rate cycles outside management's control.
- Mid-cap liquidity and a no-options market make the stock harder to trade and hedge than the large-cap financials.
- Insurtech and direct-distribution disruption could, over the long run, disintermediate parts of the SME broking pool.

## Key Risks

- **Premium-rate cycle** — soft commercial P&C pricing is the dominant earnings risk; commission income falls with rates the group does not control.
- **Acquisition / integration risk** — the model depends on continuously buying and integrating brokerages and agencies; overpayment, goodwill impairment or integration missteps would impair returns.
- **FX / Tysers** — GBP/AUD movements and UK/Lloyd's market conditions affect the International segment's reported contribution.
- **Regulatory** — commissions, conflicts-of-interest and remuneration in insurance broking are perennial regulatory watch items in Australia and the UK.
- **Liquidity / positioning** — mid-cap (~A$3B) ASX liquidity with no meaningful single-stock options market; exposure is via shares only.
- **Disruption** — long-dated insurtech / direct-distribution risk to the SME broking pool AUB consolidates.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

AUB trades as a quality-growth financial-intermediary compounder rather than a cyclical underwriter; the market rates it on the durability of its commission income and the bolt-on growth runway (see the data block in [[#Fundamentals]] for the figures as pulled). Mechanically:

- **Commission income geared to premium rates** — the single most important valuation driver is the commercial P&C rate cycle; a firming market lifts revenue with little extra cost, while soft pricing is the de-rating risk. The market reads AUB partly off the global broker majors' rate-cycle commentary.
- **Capital-light, high free-cash economics** — because it is an intermediary, not a risk carrier, AUB converts earnings to cash efficiently and carries none of the catastrophe/reserve drag of [[insurance-australia-group]] or [[qbe-insurance]]; cash funds both the dividend and acquisitions.
- **Fully-franked dividend** — a fully-franked payer (franking credits gross up the after-tax yield for Australian residents — see the franking row in [[#Fundamentals]]); income plus acquisition-led growth is the total-return story.
- **GBP/AUD via Tysers** — the International segment introduces sterling translation sensitivity, an unusual feature for an ASX domestic financial.
- **Mid-cap liquidity** — ~A$3B market cap; one of the more liquid ASX mid-cap financials, but with **no meaningful single-stock options market** — exposure is via shares only, and the stock can gap on guidance changes.
- **Trading behaviour** — moves on half-year (Feb) and full-year (Aug) results, UNPAT guidance revisions, and acquisition news; pairs with [[steadfast-group]] on the broking-cycle read.
