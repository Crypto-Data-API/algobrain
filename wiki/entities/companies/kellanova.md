Kellanova (formerly NYSE: K) was the global snacking company spun out of the Kellogg Company split in October 2023 (Pringles, Cheez-It, Pop-Tarts, Eggo, RXBAR, plus international cereal). **It no longer trades**: Mars, Incorporated completed its ~$36 billion all-cash acquisition on 2025-12-11 at $83.50 per share, and the stock was delisted from the NYSE the same day. The page is retained as a completed [[merger-arbitrage]] case study and for historical reference.

> **Status note (2026-06-10):** Kellanova was removed from the [[s-and-p-500]] upon deal completion in December 2025. The fundamentals below are the final public-company financials and will not update.

## Business Overview

Kellanova held the snacking, international cereal, and frozen-foods portfolio of the old Kellogg Company: Pringles, Cheez-It, Pop-Tarts, Eggo, RXBAR, Nutri-Grain and the Kellogg's brand outside North America. Roughly 80% of net sales came from snacks and emerging-market cereal; the slower-growth North American cereal business was separated into WK Kellogg Co (which Ferrero subsequently agreed to acquire in 2025). Annual revenue ran around $13 billion.

### Why the Split Happened — Strategic Logic

The October 2023 separation of Kellogg Company into Kellanova (snacks + international + frozen) and WK Kellogg Co (North American cereal) was a textbook **conglomerate-discount unlock**: separate a faster-growing, higher-multiple snacking franchise from a low-growth, cash-generative cereal business so each can be valued on its own merits and pursue its own capital allocation. The bet was that the market would assign Kellanova a snacks multiple (closer to Mondelez/PepsiCo Frito-Lay economics) while WK Kellogg traded as a slow-and-steady dividend/cash play. The structure also made each piece a cleaner acquisition target — which is precisely what happened: Mars bought Kellanova and Ferrero agreed to buy WK Kellogg, both within ~2 years of the split. For event-driven traders, **spin-offs that create clean strategic-fit targets are a recurring source of takeout setups** (see [[spin-off]], [[event-driven]]).

This shelf overlapped heavily with Mars's existing snacking ambition (Wrigley gum, chocolate, plus the prior KIND and Nature's Bakery deals), which is why Mars — not a financial sponsor — was the logical strategic buyer.

## The Mars Acquisition (2024–2025) — Merger-Arb Case Study

- **2024-08-14** — Mars announced the acquisition at **$83.50/share cash (~$36B total)**, a ~33% premium to the undisturbed price. One of the largest consumer-staples deals ever.
- **2025** — The deal required 28 regulatory approvals globally. The main risk was the **European Commission**, which opened an in-depth Phase 2 review (portfolio-power/retailer-leverage concerns), pushing the spread wider and the timeline past initial guidance.
- **2025-12-08** — Final regulatory clearance received (unconditional EU approval); Mars moved to close.
- **2025-12-11** — Deal **closed**; shareholders received $83.50 cash per share; NYSE delisting effective the same day.

Lessons for the merger-arb book: a financially solid strategic buyer (private, family-owned Mars) with committed financing meant low financing risk; the spread was driven almost entirely by antitrust timeline uncertainty, and patient holders captured the full spread plus interim dividends.

### Anatomy of the Spread — What the Arb Book Watched

This was an **all-cash, fixed-price, strategic-buyer** deal — the cleanest archetype in [[merger-arbitrage]]. The risk decomposition:

| Risk vector | Assessment in this deal | Effect on spread |
|---|---|---|
| Financing risk | Very low — Mars is private, family-owned, committed financing | Negligible |
| Buyer-walk / MAC risk | Low — strategic fit, no overlap competitor | Negligible |
| Antitrust / regulatory | The dominant risk — 28 approvals, EU Phase 2 | Drove the entire spread |
| Timing / duration risk | Long — Phase 2 extended the calendar past initial guidance | Widened spread (lower annualized IRR) |
| Shareholder-vote risk | Low — 33% premium, board-supported | Negligible |

Because antitrust *approval probability* was high but *timing* was uncertain, the spread behaved like a high-confidence-outcome, long-duration position: the dollar spread stayed modest but the annualized return eroded each time the EU clock reset. The wider spread during the Phase 2 review (mid-2025) was the best entry for patient capital, and the deal-break tail risk was concentrated almost entirely on a remote EU prohibition scenario.

### Lessons & Templates

- **Strategic + cash + private buyer = lowest financing/walk risk.** The work is almost entirely antitrust handicapping.
- **Phase 2 is a timing event, not usually a break event** for non-horizontal consumer deals — but it crushes annualized IRR; size accordingly.
- **Collect the dividend.** In long-dated cash deals the target keeps paying its dividend until close; that interim yield is part of the total return and was material here over the ~16-month timeline.
- **The break-price floor matters.** Underwriting the downside means estimating where K trades if the deal dies (undisturbed price ~$62 area implied by the ~33% premium) versus the $83.50 deal price.
