TeraWulf Inc. (NASDAQ: WULF) is a digital-infrastructure company that began as a low-cost, predominantly zero-carbon (nuclear/hydro-powered) Bitcoin miner and has pivoted into high-performance-computing (HPC) and AI data-center hosting at its Lake Mariner campus in Western New York and Abernathy, Texas. The transformation — anchored by multibillion-dollar contracts with Fluidstack and a backstop from Google — turned WULF from a pure crypto-mining proxy into one of the highest-profile "bitcoin miner to AI compute" rerating stories. For traders it is a high-volatility name driven by both Bitcoin price and the AI data-center capex narrative.

## Business Overview

TeraWulf has two pillars:

- **HPC / AI data-center hosting** — now the primary growth engine. Long-term colocation agreements lease critical IT (power) capacity to AI/cloud tenants, generating contracted, recurring, infrastructure-style revenue with far higher visibility than mining.
- **Bitcoin mining** — its legacy business: operating ASIC fleets to mine BTC, powered by low-cost, largely carbon-free energy at Lake Mariner. Increasingly run opportunistically as power is reallocated toward higher-value HPC hosting.

The strategic edge is **powered land and energy access** — secured grid interconnects and low-cost, clean power that AI hyperscalers desperately need. This scarcity is what lets a former small-cap miner sign hyperscale-grade contracts.

## Business Model

TeraWulf's economics rest on converting **scarce, low-cost, low-carbon megawatts** into the highest-value use available — increasingly AI hosting rather than mining:

- **HPC / AI data-center hosting (primary engine).** Long-term colocation leases provide critical IT (power) capacity to AI/cloud tenants. The tenant supplies GPUs; TeraWulf supplies powered shells, cooling, and operations. Per-MW revenue from a creditworthy tenant is far higher and more predictable than mining margin, and the contracts are long-dated — which is what re-rates capacity from a mining multiple to an infrastructure/REIT-style multiple. See [[data-center]] and [[ai-data-center-power-demand]].
- **Bitcoin mining (legacy, run opportunistically).** Self-mining converts power into hashrate into BTC; unit economics turn on **fleet efficiency (J/TH)**, **all-in power cost ($/kWh)**, and **hash price** (BTC price × network difficulty). At Lake Mariner, low-cost nuclear/hydro power gives WULF one of the lower cost curves in the cohort — but mining is increasingly the *swing* use of power, reallocated toward HPC when hosting economics are better.
- **The scarce asset: energized, interconnected, low-carbon power.** In a multi-year interconnection-queue backlog, secured grid interconnects plus clean baseload are the moat — and the reason hyperscalers (and Google) backstop TeraWulf's contracts.

The throughline matches the cohort ([[core-scientific]], [[hut-8]], [[mara]], [[applied-digital]], [[iren]], [[hive-digital-technologies]]): use mining as the cash-flowing bridge and migrate from *price-taker on BTC* to *landlord of compute*. WULF's distinctive angle is **clean power** — a selling point as AI buyers face carbon scrutiny.

## 2025-2026 Developments

- **Fluidstack HPC contracts (2025).** Two 10-year colocation agreements at Lake Mariner for >200 MW of critical IT load, representing ~US$3.7B of contracted revenue over the initial terms (up to ~US$8.7B with extensions). In August 2025 Fluidstack exercised an option for the **CB-5** building (+160 MW), targeting commencement in H2 2026.
- **Abernathy, Texas JV (Oct 2025).** A 25-year HPC joint venture with Fluidstack to develop 168 MW of critical IT load in Texas, delivery expected H2 2026.
- **Google backstop and equity stake.** Google agreed to backstop a large portion of Fluidstack's lease obligations (escalating to ~US$3.2B) and received warrants for TeraWulf shares, taking its pro-forma equity ownership to roughly 14%. This Google validation was a major catalyst, derisking the contracted revenue and lifting the stock.
- **Business mix shift.** Through 2025 management explicitly made HPC hosting the primary growth engine while running legacy mining opportunistically.

## Bull vs Bear Case

**Bull case**
- Multibillion-dollar contracted HPC backlog (Fluidstack ~$3.7B base, up to ~$8.7B with extensions; Abernathy JV) converting to revenue from H2 2026 — visible, recurring, infrastructure-grade cash flow.
- Google backstop (escalating to ~$3.2B) and ~14% pro-forma equity stake validate the demand and lower counterparty risk.
- Low-carbon, low-cost power at Lake Mariner is a structurally scarce asset as AI power demand tightens and carbon scrutiny rises.
- Optionality to flex power between mining and HPC depending on which pays more.

**Bear case**
- Counterparty concentration: Fluidstack is the dominant tenant; a stumble there is a single point of failure even with the Google backstop.
- Execution risk converting secured power into operational, energized HPC capacity on schedule (CB-5, Abernathy targeted H2 2026).
- Capital intensity and dilution to fund the buildout; still unprofitable during the transition.
- BTC drawdowns still pressure the residual mining business and sentiment.
- Small/mid-cap volatility — sentiment-driven, prone to sharp drawdowns.

## Capital Structure & Dilution Risk

- **Equity-funded buildout.** Converting secured power into energized HPC capacity is capital-intensive; WULF has relied on equity issuance (including ATM-style programs) and project/debt financing, both of which dilute or lever the company.
- **Google warrants.** The hyperscaler's equity stake (~14% pro-forma) came via warrants — derisking demand but adding to the diluted share count.
- **No large BTC treasury buffer.** Unlike [[mara]] or [[hut-8]], WULF does not run a large strategic BTC reserve to self-fund, so it leans harder on external financing — making dilution a more acute swing factor.
- **Implication for traders:** track the financing calendar and warrant overhang alongside lease/energization milestones; raises into weakness are recurring drawdown catalysts.
