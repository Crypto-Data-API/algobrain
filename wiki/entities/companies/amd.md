Advanced Micro Devices (NASDAQ: AMD) is the second-largest designer of x86 CPUs and AI-accelerator GPUs, and the primary credible challenger to [[nvidia]] in data-center AI compute. It is a fabless semiconductor company (manufacturing outsourced primarily to [[tsmc|TSMC]]) and a constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]] and [[nasdaq|Nasdaq]]-100. For traders it is one of the highest-volume, most actively optioned AI-trade vehicles in US equities — a high-beta way to express the data-center capex theme, trading sympathetically with [[nvidia]] while carrying its own roadmap-execution and China-policy risk. Under CEO Lisa Su (since 2014), AMD engineered one of the great corporate turnarounds in tech, going from near-bankruptcy to a credible two-horse race in both server CPUs and AI silicon.

## Business Overview & Segments

AMD reports four operating segments. Data Center is the growth engine and the segment the market watches; the legacy Client, Gaming, and Embedded businesses provide diversification and cash flow. FY2025 (reported Feb 2026) delivered record revenue of US$34.6B, of which Data Center was US$16.6B — roughly half the company.

| Segment | What it is | Revenue / mix (real figures where disclosed) |
|---|---|---|
| **Data Center** | EPYC server CPUs and Instinct AI accelerators (MI300/MI325/MI350 series; MI400 "Helios" rack-scale platform ramping in 2026) | US$16.6B in FY2025 (~half of revenue); Q1 2026 Data Center US$5.8B (+57% y/y) — the engine of the growth story |
| **Client** | Ryzen CPUs for desktop and notebook | Meaningful and growing; a steady share-gainer against [[intel]] in PCs |
| **Gaming** | Radeon discrete GPUs and semi-custom silicon for Sony PlayStation and Microsoft Xbox consoles | Mature/cyclical; tied to console cycle and discrete-GPU demand |
| **Embedded** | Former Xilinx FPGA business (acquired 2022 for ~US$49B in stock), serving industrial, aerospace, comms, automotive | Higher-margin, design-win-driven; subject to industrial-cycle softness |

The strategic thrust is selling Data Center "platforms," not just chips: EPYC CPUs + Instinct accelerators + Pensando DPUs + the ROCm software stack, increasingly assembled into rack-scale systems (MI400/Helios) to compete with [[nvidia]]'s integrated NVL racks rather than at the merchant-chip level.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

AMD's [[economic-moat|moat]] is narrower than [[nvidia]]'s but real, and concentrated in two arenas:

- **x86 CPU duopoly with [[intel]]** — the x86 instruction set is effectively a two-player licensed franchise, and AMD has spent the Zen era taking durable server (EPYC) and client (Ryzen) share from Intel. The moat here is process-node leadership (via [[tsmc]]), a multi-generation design cadence, and the deep [[switching-costs]] of an entrenched x86 software/validation ecosystem in enterprise datacenters.
- **#2 merchant AI-GPU supplier** — in accelerators AMD is the clear second source behind [[nvidia]], competing on memory capacity/bandwidth, total cost of ownership, and an open (non-proprietary) software story. The structural weakness is the **ROCm software gap**: [[nvidia]]'s CUDA is a ~two-decade developer ecosystem, and the [[switching-costs]] of re-porting AI workloads off CUDA are the single biggest brake on AMD share gains. Every percentage point of accelerator share AMD wins depends on ROCm maturity catching up.

The moat is a *challenger's* moat: AMD does not command [[nvidia]]-style pricing power, but its existence as a credible second source is strategically valuable to hyperscalers who want to avoid single-vendor dependence — which is precisely why the [[openai]] deal mattered.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **MI400 / Helios rack-scale systems (2026)** — the first full rack-level competitor to [[nvidia]]'s NVL platforms; raises average system value (selling racks, not chips) and is the key 2026 catalyst.
- **[[openai]] partnership (Oct 2025)** — multi-year agreement for OpenAI to deploy up to **6 GW** of Instinct GPUs beginning with the MI450 series in 2026, paired with a warrant structure allowing OpenAI to acquire up to **~160M AMD shares** tied to deployment and share-price milestones. The deal re-rated AMD as a genuine second source for frontier AI compute.
- **EPYC server-CPU share gains** — continued structural share taking from [[intel]] in the datacenter.
- **Inference demand** — as deployed AI models scale, inference (where TCO and memory capacity matter most) plays to AMD's competitive strengths.
- **Sovereign AI** — nation-state datacenter programs as a demand pool seeking vendor diversity, favorable to a credible #2.
- **Embedded/Pensando** — DPU and FPGA attach in networking and datacenter infrastructure.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly earnings and Data Center GPU revenue disclosures, MI400/Helios launch milestones, OpenAI/hyperscaler deployment news, China export-policy headlines, [[tsmc]] capacity/pricing, and [[nvidia]] results (AMD trades sympathetically).

## 2025–2026 Developments

- **FY2025 results (reported Feb 2026):** record revenue of US$34.6B, with Data Center revenue of US$16.6B. Q4 2025 included ~US$390M of MI308 revenue from China and a ~US$360M release of previously reserved inventory charges after export-license relief.
- **OpenAI partnership (Oct 2025):** the 6 GW Instinct deployment and ~160M-share warrant structure described above.
- **China export controls:** April 2025 restrictions on MI308 shipments forced an ~US$800M charge; partial license relief later in 2025 restored some China revenue (see Q4 figures above). Policy remains a swing factor.
- **Q1 2026 (reported May 2026):** revenue US$10.3B, Data Center US$5.8B (+57% y/y) on EPYC and the Instinct ramp. Q2 2026 guidance: US$11.2B ± $300M.
- **Product cycle:** MI350 ramped through H2 2025; MI400 / Helios rack-scale systems are the 2026 catalyst.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- A credible #2 in AI accelerators with a multi-year roadmap (MI350 → MI400/Helios) and a marquee [[openai]] anchor customer validating the platform.
- Structural x86 server share gains against [[intel]] keep compounding the Data Center base independent of the GPU ramp.
- Hyperscalers *want* a second source to break [[nvidia]] dependence — the strategic value of being the only credible merchant alternative.
- Net-cash balance sheet (D/E ~0.03x) and strong interest coverage; the company can self-fund the roadmap and buybacks.
- Optionality in inference (TCO/memory advantages), sovereign AI, and Embedded/Pensando attach.

### Bear case
- The **ROCm software gap** versus CUDA is the persistent governor on accelerator share; closing it is hard and slow.
- Valuation prices a multi-year AI ramp (trailing GAAP PE ~475x on FY2024 EPS — see Fundamentals); any roadmap slip de-rates the stock hard.
- China export controls are an unpredictable swing factor on guidance (the ~US$800M MI308 charge is the template).
- Customer/financing circularity in the AI-capex complex; the [[openai]] warrant structure ties upside to a counterparty's own funding trajectory.
- Single-point dependence on [[tsmc]] advanced nodes and packaging plus HBM supply ([[micron]], SK Hynix) caps shippable volume.

## Key Risks

- **ROCm software gap** — the durable competitive disadvantage versus [[nvidia]]'s CUDA; the biggest brake on accelerator share.
- **China export controls** — recurring, hard-to-forecast restrictions on advanced-chip shipments (the ~US$800M MI308 charge in 2025); policy can move guidance overnight.
- **Execution / roadmap risk** — the AI thesis rests on landing MI350 → MI400/Helios on schedule and at competitive performance-per-watt; a slip is the single most likely shock.
- **Valuation / positioning** — a trailing GAAP PE of ~475x (FY2024 EPS) prices years of AI growth; high beta amplifies drawdowns on any disappointment.
- **Supply chain** — dependence on [[tsmc]] advanced nodes and CoWoS packaging plus HBM ([[micron]], SK Hynix); any constraint caps shipments.
- **Competitive substitution** — hyperscaler in-house ASICs (Trainium/TPU/MTIA/Maia) and [[broadcom]]-style custom silicon erode the merchant-GPU TAM over time.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

AMD trades as a high-beta, AI-capex expression rather than on near-term earnings: the trailing GAAP PE of ~475x (on depressed FY2024 EPS — see the Fundamentals block below) reflects a market pricing a multi-year accelerator ramp, not current profitability. Mechanically:

- **Beta:** high versus the [[s-and-p-500]] — among the more volatile large-cap semis; drawdowns are amplified on roadmap or policy disappointments.
- **Options:** among the most liquid single-name option chains in the market — penny-wide weeklies, enormous open interest; a favorite for earnings straddles and AI-theme leverage. See [[options]].
- **Earnings behavior:** large pre-print implied moves; the stock routinely gaps on Data Center GPU revenue and guidance more than on the headline print.
- **Correlated names:** [[nvidia]], [[broadcom]], [[tsmc]], [[micron]], [[intel]] (inverse share-shift dynamics), [[super-micro-computer]]; AMD trades sympathetically with [[nvidia]] on AI-capex headlines.
- **Capital return:** no dividend; the balance sheet is net-cash and capital return is via buybacks. Reinvestment in the roadmap, not yield, is the story.
- **Index membership:** [[s-and-p-500]], Nasdaq-100 (NASDAQ-listed; some legacy sources show NYSE — AMD moved to NASDAQ in 2015). See [[ai-trading-overview]] for the broader theme.
