**Fisher & Paykel Healthcare Corporation Limited** (ASX/NZX: FPH) is a global medical device company dual-listed on the NZX and the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]], a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index and a top-weight member of the NZX 50. Headquartered in Auckland, New Zealand, FPH holds a leading global position in respiratory care (nasal high flow, humidification, ventilation accessories) and obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) therapy, designing and manufacturing hardware *and* recurring single-use consumables used in hospitals (general ward and ICU) and home-care settings across more than 120 countries. Its commercial engine is a razor/razorblade model — every device placed pulls years of high-margin consumable sales — which underpins one of the most durable [[economic-moat|economic moats]] in [[healthcare]] on the ASX. It is widely grouped with global med-tech ([[resmed]], [[cochlear]], [[csl-limited]]) rather than the local economic cycle.

## Business Model & Segments

FPH's product portfolio is organised into two broad groupings:

- **Hospital:** Devices and consumables used in acute and chronic respiratory care, surgery and related clinical settings. Flagship platforms include Optiflow (nasal high flow oxygen therapy), AIRVO humidification systems, breathing circuits and chambers, infant resuscitation / CPAP devices, invasive and non-invasive ventilation accessories, and surgical humidification products. Single-use consumables represent a significant recurring revenue stream that rises with device placements.
- **Homecare:** Devices and masks for the treatment of obstructive sleep apnea (OSA), including CPAP devices, humidifiers, masks, nasal cannulas, software and data management products.

Revenue mix is weighted toward the United States, Europe and the Asia Pacific, with the balance of revenue split between Hospital and Homecare.

| Grouping | Core products | Note |
|---|---|---|
| **Hospital — devices** | Optiflow (nasal high flow oxygen therapy), AIRVO humidification systems, infant resuscitation / CPAP devices, invasive & non-invasive ventilation accessories, surgical humidification | The hardware that seeds the installed base; placements drive the consumables annuity that follows |
| **Hospital — consumables** | Breathing circuits, chambers, single-use Optiflow cannulas and interfaces | High-margin recurring revenue that scales with the installed base — the razorblade in the razor/razorblade model |
| **Homecare — devices** | CPAP machines, humidifiers for OSA therapy | Competes head-to-head with [[resmed]] in the OSA-therapy duopoly |
| **Homecare — masks & accessories** | Nasal cannulas, OSA masks, software and data-management products | Recurring replacement-mask sales; new mask ranges are a key consumer-side growth lever |

The defining economics are the **razor/razorblade** structure: device placements (Optiflow, AIRVO, CPAP) are the "razor," and the single-use consumables they consume for years afterward — circuits, chambers, cannulas, masks — are the high-margin "razorblade." Each device placed locks in a multi-year stream of consumable revenue, so the installed base, not any single hardware sale, is the value driver.

## History

Fisher & Paykel Healthcare's heritage stems from the wider Fisher & Paykel appliances business in New Zealand. The healthcare division was separated and listed as a standalone entity in 2001, retaining the Fisher & Paykel name. The company has been consistently innovation-led, investing a material share of revenue each year in R&D, and has grown substantially during and after the COVID-19 pandemic, which materially expanded the installed base of Optiflow/AIRVO devices.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

FPH holds one of the widest [[economic-moat|economic moats]] in ASX/NZX [[healthcare]], built on a reinforcing set of advantages rather than a single edge:

- **Consumables annuity / installed-base flywheel** — the core of the moat. Every Optiflow, AIRVO or CPAP device placed pulls years of high-margin, single-use consumables (circuits, chambers, cannulas, masks). The larger the installed base, the larger the recurring revenue base — a flywheel where past device wins compound into future consumable sales independent of new hardware orders.
- **Clinical evidence & protocol embedding** — FPH invests heavily in clinical trials and education so that its therapies (notably nasal high flow) become written into hospital protocols and clinician training. Once a therapy is embedded in standard-of-care workflows and a clinician is trained on a specific interface, swapping vendors imposes real [[switching-costs]] — retraining, revalidation and clinical risk — that protect the consumables stream.
- **Sustained R&D leadership & IP** — a consistent, material share of revenue reinvested in R&D funds a steady cadence of device and mask improvements, protected by a deep patent portfolio. This keeps FPH ahead on performance (humidification quality, comfort, ease of use) in categories where clinical outcomes matter.
- **Regulatory approvals & manufacturing scale** — medical-device approvals across 120+ countries and vertically integrated manufacturing are slow, costly barriers that a new entrant cannot easily replicate.

The moat is **wide but not impregnable**: in OSA homecare FPH operates in an effective duopoly with [[resmed]], the larger global OSA player, and competes in hospital respiratory against larger diversified med-tech. The consumables lock-in is the durable defence; the long-term watch items are competitive innovation cadence and reimbursement pressure.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Nasal high flow (Optiflow) adoption** — expanding the installed base of Optiflow/AIRVO and broadening clinical applications (new patient populations and care settings) is the central growth engine; each placement seeds the consumables annuity.
- **Consumables pull-through** — as the installed base grows, recurring single-use consumable revenue compounds even without new device wins.
- **New OSA mask ranges** — refreshed Homecare mask portfolios drive replacement-cycle revenue and share gains against [[resmed]].
- **Hospital new-product approvals** — regulatory clearances for new devices, circuits and humidification products open new markets and refresh the catalogue.
- **Geographic penetration** — deepening sales across the 120+ countries already served, with the United States, Europe and Asia Pacific the largest pools.
- **Capacity expansion** — manufacturing build-out to support a structurally larger installed base.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year (November) and full-year (May) results, northern-hemisphere respiratory/flu-season severity (drives Hospital consumables), product approvals, and NZD/USD moves.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Durable, high-margin consumables annuity that compounds with the installed base — recurring revenue with limited reordering friction.
- Secular tailwind in respiratory care and OSA as nasal high flow penetrates new clinical applications and the global installed base grows.
- Near-debt-free balance sheet (D/E ~0.02x) and strong free cash flow — can self-fund R&D and capacity through any cycle.
- Consistent innovation cadence protected by clinical evidence, IP and protocol embedding — wide moat with high [[switching-costs]].
- Quality defensive-growth profile that holds up through economic cycles; FY2026 delivered a record result with margin recovery and a higher dividend.

### Bear case
- Premium valuation (PE ~40x) leaves little room for error — highly sensitive to rate-driven multiple compression when bond yields rise.
- COVID-era demand normalisation and respiratory-season variability make revenue lumpy year to year (visible in the FY2025 dip before the FY2026 recovery).
- FX: most revenue is offshore, so NZD/USD strength compresses reported NZD earnings and guidance.
- Competitive pressure from [[resmed]] in OSA and larger diversified med-tech in hospital respiratory; any erosion of the innovation lead threatens the consumables lock-in.
- Reimbursement and regulatory shifts in major markets could pressure pricing and volumes.

## Key Risks

- **Valuation / multiple compression** — at a persistent premium multiple, FPH behaves like a long-duration growth asset; rising bond yields can de-rate it independent of operating performance.
- **FX translation** — the majority of revenue is earned offshore (US, Europe, Asia Pacific) while results are reported in NZD; currency swings move reported earnings and guidance.
- **Respiratory-season variability** — Hospital consumables demand is tied to northern-hemisphere flu/respiratory season severity, which is unpredictable and creates half-to-half lumpiness.
- **Competitive / IP** — [[resmed]] in OSA and larger players in hospital respiratory; loss of clinical/innovation leadership would weaken the consumables annuity and pricing.
- **Regulatory & reimbursement** — device approvals and payer reimbursement across 120+ countries are a recurring source of risk to both volumes and pricing.
- **Product-recall risk** — as a medical-device maker, a quality or recall event (as seen across the OSA/ventilation industry) could damage revenue and reputation.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

FPH trades at a persistent premium multiple (PE ~40x — see the Valuation & Returns and Fundamentals tables above for figures as pulled) that reflects its quality defensive-growth profile and durable consumables annuity rather than a cheap valuation. Mechanically:

- **Trades like global med-tech, not the local cycle** — FPH is benchmarked against global respiratory/med-tech peers ([[resmed]], [[cochlear]]) more than the domestic ANZ economy; it is a long-duration growth asset sensitive to multiple compression when bond yields rise.
- **Dual NZX/ASX listing** — one of the most liquid New Zealand-domiciled equities, a top-weight NZX 50 name frequently traded via the ASX line; index membership in both [[asx-200]] and NZX 50 drives passive flows.
- **Low beta** — defensive demand profile means modest realised volatility outside major results and respiratory-season surprises.
- **Capital return** — a modest, franked dividend (raised again in FY2026) supplements a primarily reinvestment-and-growth story; franking credits lift the after-tax yield for Australian-resident holders.
- **Earnings behaviour** — the stock reacts to half-year (November) and full-year (May) results, respiratory-season commentary, and NZD/USD moves; [[resmed]] news is a frequent cross-read.
