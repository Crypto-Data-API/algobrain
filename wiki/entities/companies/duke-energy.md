Duke Energy (NYSE: DUK) is one of the largest regulated electric [[utilities]] in the US, serving roughly 8.6 million electric customers across the Carolinas, Florida, Indiana, Ohio and Kentucky, plus about 1.7 million gas customers. For traders it is the flagship of the data-center-driven utility capex supercycle: its 2026–2030 capital plan of $103 billion is the largest regulated capital program in the industry. It is a [[utilities|Utilities]] constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]] index. Founded 1904; headquartered in Charlotte, North Carolina.

## Business Overview & Segments

Duke is a **regulated, rate-regulated utility** — a fundamentally different business from a commodity producer or a competitive merchant generator. It does not take price risk on a commodity; instead, state regulators let it earn an *allowed return* (typically ~9.5-10.5% ROE) on its **rate base** — the depreciated value of the poles, wires, power plants and pipes it builds to serve customers. Growth is therefore mechanical and low-risk: the more capital it invests in approved infrastructure, the larger the rate base, and earnings compound roughly in step. This is the core of the utility "EPS algorithm."

| Segment | What it is | Role / mix (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **Electric Utilities & Infrastructure** | Regulated generation, transmission and distribution across six states (Carolinas, Florida, Indiana, Ohio, Kentucky) | The dominant segment; rate-base growth here funds essentially all of the EPS algorithm |
| **Gas Utilities & Infrastructure** | Piedmont Natural Gas and Midwest gas distribution | Smaller, also regulated; steady rate-base contributor |

Duke sold its commercial renewables business in 2023 to become an **(almost) pure-play regulated utility** — a deliberate de-risking that removed merchant/competitive earnings volatility. Growth is now driven by rate-base expansion, with regulators in North Carolina, South Carolina, Florida and Indiana setting allowed returns; the constructive regulatory relationships in those jurisdictions are themselves an asset.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Duke's [[economic-moat|moat]] is the classic **regulated-monopoly** moat — the most durable structure in the utility sector and entirely different from a commodity player's cost-curve edge:

- **Legal monopoly** — Duke is the sole provider of electricity (and gas) in its service territories; customers cannot choose a competitor, so revenue is exceptionally stable and visible.
- **Regulated rate-base returns** — regulators guarantee the opportunity to earn an allowed return on prudently invested capital, converting capital spending directly into earnings growth with little demand risk.
- **Enormous barriers to entry** — no one builds a competing grid; the replacement cost and permitting of transmission/distribution networks make the franchise effectively unassailable.
- **Scale and constructive regulation** — size lowers financing and operating cost per customer, and long, generally constructive relationships with Carolina/Florida/Indiana regulators support timely cost recovery.

The constraint, not the moat: returns are *capped* by regulators, the business is **capital-intensive and heavily leveraged**, and the equity is highly sensitive to interest rates (the discount rate on a bond-like cash-flow stream). The risk is regulatory and rate-driven, not competitive.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Record capital plan** — the $103B 2026-2030 program (raised ~$16B / ~18% in Feb 2026) is the largest regulated capex plan in the industry; rate-base growth off this plan underpins the EPS algorithm.
- **Data-center load** — dedicated large-load teams signed ~3 GW of new electric-service agreements, including 2.7 GW of contracted data-center load in Q1 2026 alone; the [[ai-data-center-power-demand|AI power-demand]] theme is the structural growth story.
- **Generation build-out** — ~14 GW of new capacity planned by 2030, including accelerated gas (the Anderson plant).
- **Constructive rate cases** — favorable NC/SC/FL/IN outcomes (allowed ROEs, rider mechanisms) convert capex into earnings.
- **Recurring catalysts** — capital-plan updates, data-center contract announcements, rate-case rulings, hurricane season (storm-cost recovery), and Treasury-yield moves.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Largest regulated capex plan in the industry ($103B) drives years of visible, low-risk rate-base and EPS growth.
- Data-center load in the Carolinas/Indiana adds structural demand growth to a historically flat-load business.
- (Almost) pure-play regulated model with constructive regulators removes merchant earnings volatility.
- Defensive, dividend-paying bond proxy that benefits if/when long-end yields fall.

### Bear case
- Heavy equity-funded capex risks shareholder dilution to finance the rate-base build.
- High leverage (D/E ~1.68x) and thin interest coverage (~2.3x) make the balance sheet rate-sensitive.
- Rising Treasury yields de-rate bond-proxy utilities and raise financing cost.
- Regulatory risk — adverse rate-case outcomes or disallowed costs can break the EPS algorithm.

## Key Risks

- **Rate-case / regulatory risk** — unfavorable allowed ROEs or cost disallowances in NC/SC/FL/IN undercut the rate-base earnings model.
- **Interest-rate sensitivity** — as a bond proxy, rising long-end yields compress the multiple and raise financing cost on a debt-heavy balance sheet.
- **Capex funding / dilution** — the $103B plan requires substantial equity issuance, a standing overhang on per-share growth.
- **Storm / operational risk** — hurricanes and storms in Florida/the Carolinas create large recovery-and-restoration costs.
- **Leverage** — D/E ~1.68x with ~2.3x interest coverage leaves limited headroom in a downturn (see Fundamentals).

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Duke trades as a **regulated-utility bond proxy** — valued on rate-base growth, allowed ROE, dividend yield, and the level of long-term interest rates rather than on commodity prices or business-quality multiples. Mechanically:

- **Rate-base / EPS-algorithm lens** — the market pays a P/E that reflects the credibility of the 5-7% long-term EPS-growth target funded by the $103B capex plan; capital-plan upgrades that extend visible rate-base growth re-rate the stock.
- **Bond-proxy behavior** — DUK trades inversely to long-end [[treasuries|Treasury]] yields; a falling-rate environment supports the multiple, a rising-rate one pressures it.
- **Dividend yield as anchor** — a long, steady dividend record makes the ~3-4% yield a valuation floor for income buyers and dampens drawdowns.
- **Low beta, defensive** — held for stability and income; it outperforms in risk-off/defensive rotations and lags in risk-on cyclical rallies. See the Fundamentals block below for the figures as pulled.
