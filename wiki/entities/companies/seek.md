**SEEK Limited** (ASX: SEK) is an internet content & information company listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. SEEK Limited (ASX: SEK) is one of the world's leading online employment marketplace operators, with a multinational presence across Australia, New Zealand, Hong Kong SAR, Southeast Asia and other emerging jurisdictions. The company is headquartered at 60 Cremorne Street, Melbourne, Victoria and employs approximately 3,245 people.

## Business Model & Segments

SEEK's core business is online employment marketplaces, operated under the SEEK brand in Australia and New Zealand and the Jobstreet and Jobsdb brands across Southeast Asia, Hong Kong SAR and other Asian markets. In February 2024, SEEK unified its APAC employment marketplaces onto a single AI-powered technology platform following a three-year development effort and approximately A$180 million investment. The unified platform leverages AI models to assess talent suitability, generate personalised recommendations for candidates and hirers, and speed up shortlisting and placement.

SEEK's product suite includes job advertisements, ad enhancements (branding, immediate start badges), Talent Search access to a searchable candidate database, and hiring tools such as SEEK Pass (credential verification), Employer Brand Boost (targeted company profile promotion), GradConnection (early career graduate recruitment) and Reference Checks (automated candidate reference management).

Beyond the core marketplaces, SEEK offers placement support products such as Pay Per Hire, JobAdder applicant tracking system, Sidekicker contingent labour marketplace and various HR SaaS products. SEEK also operates SEEK Business (online marketplace for businesses for sale in Australia) and SEEK Volunteer (volunteer opportunities platform in ANZ).

## History

SEEK was founded in November 1997 in Melbourne by brothers Andrew Bassat and Paul Bassat, alongside Matt Rockman, with a vision to create an online version of newspaper employment classifieds. The company was incorporated on 16 September 1997 and its website launched in March 1998. SEEK rapidly became Australia's leading online employment platform as the market transitioned from print to digital.

SEEK floated on the ASX on 18 April 2005, providing capital at a market capitalisation of approximately A$587 million to fund regional expansion. Over the following decade, SEEK built a major international footprint via acquisitions, including a 10% initial stake in JobStreet Corporation in 2008, rising to 21.3% in 2010 and full acquisition in 2014 for approximately A$580 million. SEEK also invested in Chinese online recruiter Zhaopin, taking majority ownership before a subsequent privatisation transaction in 2017.

Andrew Bassat remained CEO of SEEK until 2021, when he was succeeded by former Commonwealth Bank CEO Ian Narev. In February 2024, SEEK completed the merger of the SEEK, Jobsdb and Jobstreet platforms into a single unified AI-powered marketplace, a major organisational and technology milestone.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

SEEK holds a wide [[economic-moat]] built primarily on a two-sided [[network-effects|network effect]]: the more candidates that use the marketplace, the more attractive it is to hirers, and the more hirers (and job ads) on the platform, the more candidates it draws — a self-reinforcing flywheel that makes the leading job marketplace very hard to dislodge once established. Reinforcing layers:

- **Brand / default behaviour** — in Australia and New Zealand, "SEEK" is effectively the default verb for job search, giving it unmatched candidate liquidity and pricing power.
- **Unified AI platform** — the ~3-year, ~A$180m consolidation of SEEK, Jobstreet and Jobsdb onto a single AI-powered tech stack (2024) improves matching quality, shortlisting speed and personalised recommendations, which in turn supports "yield" (price-per-ad) expansion — the core monetisation lever. See [[artificial-intelligence]].
- **Switching costs** — moderate rather than absolute. Employers accumulate data, integrations (e.g. JobAdder ATS), and reliance on the Talent Search candidate database, raising the friction of leaving; but recruitment ad spend is ultimately discretionary, so [[switching-costs]] are a supporting, not dominant, factor.

This is the same marketplace network-effect model that underpins ASX classifieds peers [[rea-group]] (property) and [[carsales]] (autos). The moat is strongest in ANZ; it is more **contested in Southeast Asia** (Jobstreet/Jobsdb against regional boards), and the **China** exposure (Zhaopin, equity-accounted) is structurally challenged by a deep hiring slump — the source of recent non-cash impairments. As a sector ([[communication-services]] / online classifieds) and technology-platform name, SEEK sits at the intersection of [[technology]] and media.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **AI-driven yield expansion** — price-per-ad ("yield") is the central growth lever; AI matching and product tiers let SEEK raise effective ad pricing faster than ad volumes.
- **Unified platform monetisation** — extracting revenue and cost synergies from the single AI tech stack across SEEK/Jobstreet/Jobsdb.
- **Asia penetration** — deeper monetisation of Jobstreet/Jobsdb across Southeast Asia and Hong Kong.
- **Adjacent products** — HR SaaS and placement tools (SEEK Pass credential verification, Talent Search, Sidekicker contingent labour, JobAdder ATS, Pay Per Hire) broaden the revenue base beyond core job ads.
- **Structural shift online** — continued migration of recruitment advertising spend from offline to digital.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year/full-year results (Feb/Aug), monthly ANZ job-ad and unemployment data, China hiring trends and Zhaopin marks, RBA rate decisions, and AI-monetisation commentary.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Dominant ANZ marketplace with a self-reinforcing [[network-effects|network effect]] that is very hard to displace.
- AI-driven **yield expansion** runway — management's "shares up, yields up" framing — lets revenue outgrow job-ad volumes.
- High-margin, recurring marketplace economics produce strong operating leverage as yield rises faster than costs.
- Strong balance sheet (net leverage within target) and a record interim dividend signal confidence and capital-return capacity.
- Optionality in adjacent HR SaaS/placement products and deeper Asian monetisation.

### Bear case
- Cyclical exposure to labour markets — job-ad volumes fall in downturns, and earnings track the ANZ/Asian hiring cycle.
- China/Zhaopin is in structural decline; the deep hiring slump has driven sizeable non-cash impairments and could prompt more.
- Southeast Asia is more competitive and lower-yielding than the ANZ core.
- Global platforms (LinkedIn, Indeed) are persistent threats for employer recruiting spend and passive candidates.
- Leverage and rate sensitivity mean a downturn could pressure covenant headroom and the dividend.

## Key Risks

- **Labour-market cyclicality** — job-ad volumes swing with hiring conditions; a synchronised ANZ/Asia slowdown hits revenue and the multiple together.
- **China / Zhaopin** — further deterioration in Chinese hiring risks additional impairments on the equity-accounted stake.
- **SE Asia competition** — regional boards contest Jobstreet/Jobsdb share in the faster-growing but fragmented Asian markets.
- **Global platform threat** — LinkedIn and Indeed compete for recruiting budgets and candidate attention.
- **Execution on AI monetisation** — the yield-expansion story depends on continued AI/product execution against customer price sensitivity.
- **Leverage / rate sensitivity** — covenant headroom and dividend cover could tighten in a downturn if leverage rises.
- **Leadership / board turnover** — recent senior departures (chair, head of IR) introduce continuity risk.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

SEEK trades on a growth/marketplace multiple (around the high-teens-to-low-20s P/E range as shown in the data above, qualitatively) that reflects high-margin recurring marketplace economics and the optionality of AI-driven yield expansion rather than a cheap valuation. Mechanically:

- **Cyclical earnings** — profits track the ANZ and Asian labour markets; job-ad volumes and yield are the swing variables, so the stock is sensitive to monthly job-ad and unemployment prints and to China-hiring data.
- **Income** — pays a partly-franked dividend (a record interim was declared in the most recent half), adding some income appeal for Australian-resident investors via franking, though it trades primarily as a growth name.
- **Index / peers** — an [[asx-200]] [[communication-services]] holding, grouped with online-classifieds peers [[rea-group]] and [[carsales]] as the canonical ASX network-effect marketplaces.
- **Liquidity / positioning** — reasonable ASX liquidity but thin single-name options, so most positioning is outright equity rather than derivatives.

## Recent Developments

SEEK released its **H1 FY26** financial results on **17 February 2026**, reporting net revenue up ~12% and EBITDA up ~19%, with ~35% adjusted-profit growth — clear operating leverage as AI-enabled yield expansion across ANZ and Asia outpaced cost growth. CEO Ian Narev highlighted "shares up, yields up," a strong balance sheet, and a record dividend.

A major development during the half was a **A$356 million non-cash impairment** against SEEK's Zhaopin investment (comprising ~A$284m on the 23.5% equity-accounted stake and ~A$72m on a consideration receivable), reflecting a deepening China hiring slump. The carrying value of SEEK's Zhaopin investment fell from A$529 million at June 2025 to A$182 million at December 2025.

The Board declared a **record interim dividend of 27 cents per share, up 13%** on the prior period. Net leverage improved to 2.0x from 2.3x a year earlier, within SEEK's <2.5x target.

On outlook, SEEK **tightened FY26 guidance** and expects revenue and EBITDA to land in the upper half of the original ranges: net revenue ~A$1.19-1.23B, EBITDA ~A$530-550M, and adjusted profit ~A$195-215M. SEEK also flagged an exit from its Employment Hero stake.

Narev noted two significant departures: Chair Graham Goldsmith and Head of Investor Relations Dan McKenna.
