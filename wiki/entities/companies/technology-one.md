**Technology One Limited** (ASX: TNE), trading as TechnologyOne, is Australia's leading enterprise software company, a [[technology|Information Technology]] constituent of the [[asx-200]] (and ASX 50) listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]]. Headquartered in Fortitude Valley, Brisbane, the company develops, markets, implements and supports a deeply integrated Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) platform for public-sector, education, health and asset-intensive customers across the ANZ and UK markets. TechnologyOne is the archetypal ASX quality compounder: a single-codebase, verticalised ERP sold on multi-year contracts with very high retention, giving it a deep [[switching-costs|switching-cost]] [[economic-moat|moat]] and a roughly two-decade record of doubling in size about every five years.

## Business Model & Segments

TechnologyOne reports through two segments:

- **Software** — recurring SaaS subscriptions and legacy on-premises licences. The product suite includes:
  - **OneCouncil** — for local government.
  - **OneEducation** (with DxP Student and DxP Essentials) — for higher education.
  - **OneGovernment** — for state/federal/central government.
  - **OneHealth** — for health and community services.
  - **Enterprise Suite** — financials, HR & payroll, enterprise budgeting, enterprise asset management, supply chain management, property and rating, enterprise content management, business analytics, spatial and corporate performance management.
- **Consulting** — implementation, integration and professional services supporting customer deployments.

Over the past decade, TechnologyOne has transitioned from on-premises licensing to its SaaS+ delivery model, which includes application management and managed SaaS operations as a bundled subscription.

The franchise is a verticalised, single-codebase ERP — one product configured for each market vertical rather than a sprawl of separate codebases — which keeps R&D efficient while making the software deeply embedded in each customer's mission-critical operations:

| Product / segment | Market served | Note |
|---|---|---|
| **Software (SaaS+)** | All verticals | Recurring SaaS subscriptions plus bundled application management and managed operations — the core ARR engine and the primary growth driver |
| **OneCouncil** | Local government | Flagship vertical; rates, property, finance, asset and citizen services for councils |
| **OneEducation** (DxP Student, DxP Essentials) | Higher education | Student management and digital experience platform for universities (ANZ and UK) |
| **OneGovernment** | State / federal / central government | Finance, HR/payroll and enterprise functions for public-sector agencies |
| **OneHealth** | Health & community services | Enterprise platform for health and community-services providers |
| **Enterprise Suite** | Asset-intensive & cross-vertical | Financials, HR & payroll, budgeting, enterprise asset management, supply chain, property & rating, content management, business analytics, spatial and corporate performance management |
| **Consulting** | All verticals | Implementation, integration and professional services supporting deployments — lower-margin, deliberately capped relative to Software |

The strategic emphasis is verticalised SaaS+ for government, education, health and asset-intensive customers — segments with long budget horizons, regulatory complexity and a strong preference for proven, locally supported platforms over generic global ERP.

## History

- **1987**: Adrian Di Marco founded TechnologyOne from a demountable office in the carpark of JL Mactaggart Industries' hide processing plant in Hemmant, Brisbane.
- **1990s**: Built a next-generation accounting and ERP software stack using relational database technology and expanded into local government and higher education.
- **1999**: Listed on the Australian Securities Exchange.
- **1999–2019**: TechnologyOne approximately doubled in size every five years, driven by verticalised ERP strategy and high customer retention.
- **2012**: Began transition from on-premises software to SaaS.
- **2014**: First iteration of TechnologyOne's cloud SaaS offering launched.
- **May 2017**: Founder Adrian Di Marco stepped down as CEO; Edward (Ed) Chung appointed CEO. Di Marco remained Executive Chairman and Chief Innovation Officer.
- **September 2021**: First international acquisition — UK-based higher education software company Scientia — for £12 million.
- **June 2022**: Adrian Di Marco stepped down as Executive Chairman after 35 years.
- **2025**: Promoted to the ASX 50.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

TechnologyOne's [[economic-moat|economic moat]] is built almost entirely on **[[switching-costs|switching costs]]**, reinforced by a verticalised single-codebase product and decades of compounding scale:

- **Mission-critical embedding** — the ERP runs core finance, payroll, rating, student management and asset systems for councils, universities and government agencies. Ripping it out means re-platforming the operational backbone of the organisation, retraining staff and re-certifying compliance — rarely worth the risk once live. This produces very high customer retention and long multi-year contract tails.
- **Verticalised single codebase** — one product configured per vertical (OneCouncil, OneEducation, OneGovernment, OneHealth) rather than dozens of bespoke systems. This is cheaper to maintain than competitors' fragmented stacks and compounds the product advantage with every release, deepening the moat over time.
- **SaaS+ lock-in** — bundling application management and managed operations into the subscription means TechnologyOne runs the customer's environment, not just licenses software — extending switching costs from data and process into operations.
- **Reference-density / trust** — in conservative public-sector procurement, an installed base of comparable councils or universities is itself a moat: buyers favour the platform their peers already trust.
- **Scale compounding** — the company has roughly doubled in size about every five years for two decades, funding R&D no local rival can match while keeping margins high.

The moat is **wide but addressable at the edges**: large global ERP vendors can win greenfield tenders, and any prolonged public-sector budget freeze slows new logos. But for existing customers the switching cost is the durable defence, and net revenue retention plus the SaaS+ migration tailwind are the clearest evidence the moat is intact.

## Recent Developments

TechnologyOne's growth has accelerated, underpinned by SaaS+ adoption and a strong AI product agenda under CEO Ed Chung:

- **SaaS+ momentum**: Continued strong customer uptake of the bundled SaaS+ offering (SaaS plus application management).
- **AI product launches**:
  - **PLUS** — an enterprise agentic AI platform integrated across the ERP suite, designed to predict, learn and automate workflows.
  - **Guide** — AI-powered conversational interface for students within OneEducation, enabling self-service access to university services, task completion and query resolution.
- **FY26 upgraded guidance (issued February 2026)**: Profit before tax growth target raised to 18–20% (from a prior 13–17% range) and ARR growth target of 16–18%, citing SaaS+ and AI momentum.
- **Geographic momentum**: Continued customer wins in the UK following the Scientia acquisition, with expanding higher education and local government pipeline.
- **ARR milestone tracking**: TechnologyOne reiterated its long-standing goal of achieving A$1 billion of annual recurring revenue by the end of the decade.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **SaaS+ migration & ARR ramp** — the structural conversion of the legacy on-premises base to bundled SaaS+ is the central growth engine, driving recurring revenue toward the stated **A$1 billion ARR by the end of the decade** goal. Each migrated customer lifts ARR per account and deepens [[switching-costs|switching costs]].
- **AI product suite** — **PLUS** (an enterprise agentic AI platform integrated across the ERP, designed to predict, learn and automate workflows) and **Guide** (an AI conversational interface for students in OneEducation) add monetisable capability on top of the existing footprint.
- **UK expansion** — momentum following the Scientia acquisition, with an expanding higher-education and local-government pipeline; the UK is the primary geographic optionality beyond the mature ANZ base.
- **Upgraded FY26 guidance** — in February 2026 management raised its profit-before-tax growth target to 18–20% (from 13–17%) and set an ARR growth target of 16–18%, citing SaaS+ and AI momentum; TechnologyOne also has a long-standing habit of issuing conservative guidance and then beating it.
- **Operating leverage** — the single-codebase model means incremental ARR drops through at very high margins; scale steadily lifts profitability.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year (March) and full-year (September) results, ARR and SaaS+ disclosures, PBT guidance updates, new-vertical/UK contract wins and AI roll-out milestones.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Sticky, high-retention recurring revenue from mission-critical public-sector ERP — among the most durable revenue bases on the ASX.
- High and rising margins (gross ~86%, operating ~29%) with strong [[economic-moat|moat]]-protected economics and meaningful operating leverage still to come.
- Long runway: SaaS+ migration, UK expansion, AI monetisation and the A$1B ARR goal give a multi-year compounding story.
- Conservative guidance culture — management has a track record of under-promising and over-delivering, which supports the premium multiple.

### Bear case
- Premium valuation (P/S ~15x, P/B ~20x) leaves little room for any growth disappointment; a single soft ARR or PBT print can de-rate the stock hard.
- Revenue is exposed to government and education **budget cycles**; a public-sector spending freeze slows new logos.
- UK expansion adds execution and **FX** risk (UK earnings translate back to AUD).
- Founder-era succession is now well past, but execution under the current CEO remains the key bet for a stock priced for sustained compounding.

## Key Risks

- **Valuation / growth-miss sensitivity** — a high-multiple compounder; any miss on ARR, PBT or guidance can trigger an outsized de-rate.
- **Public-sector budget cycles** — government/education spending freezes or procurement delays slow new-customer wins.
- **UK execution & FX** — international expansion is the main growth lever but carries delivery risk and currency translation exposure on UK earnings.
- **Key-person / succession** — founder Adrian Di Marco's departure is well in the past; continued execution under the current leadership is the principal bet for a premium-priced name.
- **Competitive displacement** — global ERP vendors (SAP/Oracle/Workday) or vertical specialists narrowing TechnologyOne's vertical advantage on new tenders.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

TechnologyOne trades at a persistent **quality-compounder premium** — the multiple reflects durable recurring revenue, high margins and a long growth runway rather than a cheap valuation (see the Valuation & Returns and Fundamentals tables above for the figures as pulled; P/S and P/B sit well above market). Mechanically:

- **Premium multiple** — the market pays up for switching-cost-protected ARR and the conservative-guidance habit; the risk is entirely valuation/growth-miss rather than business quality.
- **Franked dividends** — distributions carry franking credits, materially lifting the after-tax yield for Australian-resident investors (see Fundamentals); the total-return story is compounding-plus-franked-income, not a high headline yield.
- **Liquidity & options** — a large-cap ASX name with reasonable turnover, but single-stock options are thin relative to US peers, so most positioning is expressed via the underlying.
- **Reporting cadence** — a March half-year and a September full-year; results, ARR/SaaS+ disclosures and PBT guidance updates are the key repricing events.
- **Capital return** — steady, growing franked dividends alongside reinvestment in product (SaaS+, AI) and UK growth; the dividend complements rather than dominates the return.
