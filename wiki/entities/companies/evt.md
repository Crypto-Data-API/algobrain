**EVT Limited** (ASX: EVT) is a diversified, more-than-century-old Australian entertainment, hospitality and leisure group listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. It runs one of Australia's largest cinema networks (Event Cinemas, BCC Cinemas, Rialto, Embassy Theatre) plus the German CineStar chain, an Australasian hotels platform (QT, Rydges, Atura, LyLo), the Thredbo Alpine Resort in the Australian Alps, and a large directly held property portfolio. Long classified under [[communication-services]] for its cinema-exhibition heritage, EVT trades in practice as a [[consumer-discretionary]] leisure name — and, more than most, as an **asset-backed** play: its ~A$2B+ property book, irreplaceable Thredbo concession and prime hotel/cinema real estate underpin a sum-of-the-parts story beneath modest operating margins. The Rydge family is the long-standing controlling shareholder, which keeps free float tight.

## Business Model & Segments

EVT reports through five segments:

- **Entertainment (Australia & New Zealand):** Cinema operations under the Event Cinemas, BCC Cinemas, Rialto, Embassy Theatre and Moonlight Cinema (outdoor) brands, together with licensing of Skyline Drive-In.
- **Entertainment Germany:** Cinema operations under the CineStar brand.
- **Hotels and Resorts:** Ownership, operation and management of hotels under the QT, Rydges, Atura, LyLo and Thredbo Alpine brands, primarily in Australia and New Zealand but with selected assets in Singapore.
- **Thredbo Alpine Resort:** Ownership and operation of the Thredbo ski resort, including lifts, accommodation, food and beverage, and retail.
- **Property and Other Investments:** Property investment and development activities supporting the group's operating brands, together with the SpaQ, Ubika Spa and related ancillary businesses.

EVT earns revenue from ticketed entertainment admissions and food and beverage, hotel room revenue, food and beverage and gaming/function income, Thredbo ski-pass and accommodation revenue, and property rental.

## History

EVT was founded in 1910 as Greater Union, initially a cinema exhibitor. Over more than a century the business evolved into a diversified leisure and hospitality group, accumulating major hotel brands (Rydges), ski resort assets (Thredbo), and a property portfolio. The company was previously known as Amalgamated Holdings Limited and then Event Hospitality & Entertainment Limited, before adopting its current name EVT Limited in October 2022 to reflect its portfolio-wide branding. The Rydge family has long been EVT's largest shareholder.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

EVT's [[economic-moat|economic moat]] is **asset- and real-estate-backed rather than a high-return operating moat** — a distinction central to how the stock is valued. The durable advantages are scarce physical assets that are extremely hard to replicate:

- **Thredbo alpine concession** — EVT operates Australia's premier snow resort under a long-dated lease/concession. There is no second Thredbo: the combination of terrain, snowmaking, lifts, accommodation and a captive catchment is effectively irreplaceable, and a strong snow season drops disproportionately to the bottom line.
- **Prime cinema and hotel real estate** — many flagship Event Cinemas and hotel sites sit on owned, well-located land. The ~A$2B+ property book provides hard **asset backing** that anchors the [[sum-of-the-parts]] / NAV case and worth a large share of the market cap.
- **Scale in ANZ cinema exhibition** — EVT is one of the two dominant Australasian exhibitors, giving it negotiating weight with film distributors and the ability to invest in premium formats (Gold Class, Vmax) ahead of smaller chains.
- **Brand and premiumisation** — the QT hotel brand, Gold Class and Vmax cinema tiers, and Rydges/Atura allow yield-led ("premiumisation") growth that lifts revenue per customer above commodity leisure pricing.

The moat's limits are real and structural: cinema exhibition faces secular **attendance decline and streaming substitution**; the operating businesses run thin operating margins (~4-5%) and are cyclical; and returns on the underlying asset base are modest. The investment case rests far more on asset value and disciplined capital allocation than on compounding operating returns. See [[consumer-discretionary]] and [[asset-backing]] framing.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Hollywood box-office slate** — a strong blockbuster calendar lifts both the ANZ (Event Cinemas) and German (CineStar) exhibition divisions; a weak slate is a direct drag.
- **Hotel premiumisation & RevPAR** — the Hotels and Resorts division delivered record revenue of A$413.3 million and record normalised EBITDA on strong occupancy and premiumisation, led by the QT brand; continued RevPAR and occupancy growth is a core driver.
- **Thredbo investment & snow quality** — record Thredbo results in the period; ongoing capex and a strong Australian snow season (June–September) are high-margin tailwinds.
- **Property-portfolio monetisation/development** — surfacing value from the ~A$2B+ property book (development, revaluation or selective sales) is the key sum-of-the-parts re-rating catalyst.
- **Cinema premiumisation yield** — Gold Class and Vmax drive admission yield growth even where attendance volumes are flat to declining.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year (February) and full-year (August) results, snow-season quality (Jun–Sep), the box-office release calendar, and property revaluations.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Deep **asset backing**: the ~A$2B+ property portfolio plus the irreplaceable Thredbo concession underpin a sum-of-the-parts value that can exceed the operating-business valuation, providing downside support.
- Hotels and Thredbo are delivering **record results** — record Hotels & Resorts revenue (A$413.3M) and record Thredbo results — showing the non-cinema portfolio can carry growth.
- Premiumisation (QT hotels, Gold Class/Vmax) lifts yield per customer and partly offsets structural cinema-volume pressure.
- A tightly held register (Rydge family) aligns long-term capital allocation; any property monetisation could close the holding-company/asset discount.

### Bear case
- **Structural cinema decline** — secular attendance erosion and streaming substitution threaten the largest legacy division.
- **Thin operating margins** (~4-5%) and high operating leverage mean small revenue swings hit profit hard.
- **Cyclical and weather-exposed** — consumer-spending downturns hit leisure first, and a poor Thredbo snow season is a measurable earnings hit.
- **Capex intensity** — hotels and cinemas need ongoing reinvestment to sustain the premium positioning.
- **Tight free float** — the large Rydge-family stake limits liquidity and can suppress the multiple.

## Key Risks

- **Structural cinema-attendance decline / streaming substitution** — the central long-term threat to the exhibition business.
- **Consumer-spending downturn** — discretionary leisure (cinema, hotels, snow) is early-cyclical and sensitive to household budgets.
- **Thredbo weather/climate risk** — a poor or short snow season (and longer-run climate risk to alpine seasons) directly reduces high-margin resort earnings.
- **Operating leverage** — on a 4-5% operating-margin base, modest revenue softness compresses profit sharply.
- **Reinvestment capex** — sustaining premium hotels and cinemas requires continuous capital, constraining free cash flow.
- **Box-office slate volatility** — earnings depend on a film release calendar EVT does not control.
- **Free-float / ownership concentration** — the controlling Rydge-family stake reduces liquidity and limits the influence of minority holders.
