OPEC (Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries) is an intergovernmental cartel of oil-producing nations that coordinates production quotas to influence [[crude-oil]] prices. Its production decisions represent the single largest discretionary supply variable in global crude oil markets.

## History

OPEC was founded in 1960 by five original members: Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, and Venezuela, in response to unilateral price cuts by Western oil companies. The organization currently comprises 13 member nations. In 2016, the broader OPEC+ alliance was formed to include non-OPEC producers such as Russia, significantly expanding the group's control over global oil supply.

Key historical events in OPEC's influence on markets include:

- **[[1973-oil-crisis]]** — The Arab oil embargo against nations supporting Israel triggered a quadrupling of oil prices and reshaped global energy politics
- **2014 price war** — Saudi Arabia refused to cut production to defend prices, targeting US shale producers, sending [[crude-oil]] from over $100 to below $30
- **2020 COVID price war** — A breakdown in OPEC+ negotiations with Russia coincided with pandemic demand destruction, briefly sending WTI futures negative
- **2022+ post-pandemic management** — Coordinated production cuts to support prices during uncertain demand recovery

## Key Functions

OPEC's primary mechanism is the **production quota system**, where member nations agree to target output levels (measured in barrels per day) to manage supply and influence prices. Compliance with quotas varies and is itself a closely watched market variable — overproduction by members like Iraq or Nigeria frequently undermines agreed cuts.

The **Monthly Oil Market Report (MOMR)** is OPEC's flagship publication, providing:

- Member production levels (based on secondary sources and direct communication)
- Global oil demand forecasts
- OECD commercial inventory data
- Supply/demand balance projections

Traders closely watch OPEC ministerial meetings (typically held in Vienna) for quota decisions. The pre-meeting diplomacy and post-meeting press conferences are significant sources of [[crude-oil]] price volatility.

(Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Market Impact

OPEC's decisions move [[crude-oil]] futures on the [[cme-group]] (WTI) and [[intercontinental-exchange]] (Brent) more than virtually any other single factor. The mechanism operates through supply expectations:

- **Production cuts** tighten supply forecasts, pushing prices higher and flattening the futures curve (or shifting it into backwardation)
- **Production increases or quota non-compliance** signal surplus, pushing prices lower and steepening contango
- **Surprise decisions** (unexpected cuts or refusals to cut) generate the largest moves — the 2014 Saudi decision to maintain production caused a multi-year bear market in oil

OPEC's influence also creates a persistent [[geopolitical-risk-premium]] in oil prices, as internal tensions among members (e.g., Saudi-Iran rivalry, UAE capacity ambitions) introduce uncertainty about future supply commitments.

(Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## 2025-2026 Production Policy

The 2025-2026 period has been defined by OPEC+ unwinding its post-2022 voluntary cuts into a soft demand backdrop:

- **April 2025** — OPEC+ cuts peaked at roughly 5.85 million b/d, of which ~2.2 million b/d were the second-tranche voluntary cuts by eight members. The group then began returning barrels faster than its original schedule.
- **August-September 2025** — OPEC+ agreed to fully unwind the 2.2 million b/d tranche of voluntary cuts by September 2025, marking the effective end of the price-defense phase that began in 2023.
- **November 2025** — agreed a final 137,000 b/d increase for December 2025, then **suspended further increases for Q1 2026**, citing seasonality; the 30 November 2025 ministerial reaffirmed the pause.
- **December 2025 / 2026 plan** — announced a framework to gradually return the remaining ~1.65 million b/d tranche (the April 2023 voluntary cuts) over 18 months starting April 2026, conditions permitting. The 3.66 million b/d of group-wide mandatory cuts remain in place through at least end-2026.
- **April 2026 onward** — the phased return of the ~1.65 million b/d tranche commenced as planned from April 2026, on a monthly phase-in schedule with initial increments of approximately 137,000 b/d per month (Verified via Perplexity (sonar), 2026-06-10).
- **Market context** — the IEA projected Q1 2026 as one of the largest surpluses in recent years (inventory builds approaching 4-5 million b/d at the extreme), and banks including JPMorgan warned OPEC+ may need to re-cut in 2026 to prevent Brent sliding toward $40.

**Trading takeaway**: the regime has shifted from "OPEC put" (defending a floor with cuts) to **market-share mode** (returning barrels and tolerating lower prices). In this regime, OPEC+ meeting surprises skew bearish-to-neutral, the contango/backwardation structure of the [[crude-oil]] curve is the cleaner signal than spot, and compliance data (Kazakhstan, Iraq, UAE overproduction) matters as much as headline quota decisions.
