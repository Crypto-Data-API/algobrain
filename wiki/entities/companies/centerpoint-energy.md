CenterPoint Energy (NYSE: CNP) is a regulated electric and natural gas utility holding company headquartered in Houston, serving roughly 7 million metered customers across Texas, Indiana, Minnesota, and Ohio. A constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]], its Houston Electric transmission-and-distribution franchise sits at the centre of one of the fastest-growing load pockets in the US — driven by data centres, electrification, and Gulf Coast industrial expansion — making CNP one of the higher-growth narratives in the [[utilities]] sector.

## Business Overview & Segments

CenterPoint is a **regulated electric-and-gas utility holding company** whose earnings come almost entirely from regulated rate-base growth. The company runs a multi-year capital plan in the tens of billions of dollars focused on Houston-area grid resiliency (post-Hurricane Beryl, 2024) and system growth, recovered through rate cases and interim mechanisms. Houston Electric's load-growth forecast — boosted by data-centre interconnection requests measured in gigawatts — underpins one of the strongest organic rate-base growth profiles among US utilities. The EBITDA margin (40%, see Fundamentals) is typical of regulated wires/gas utilities, with leverage (D/E ~2.0x) standard for the sector.

| Segment | What it is | Regulatory / risk profile (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **Houston Electric (T&D)** | A wires-only transmission-and-distribution utility serving the greater Houston area | The crown jewel — no generation/commodity price risk (pure regulated wires); load growth from data centers and Gulf Coast industry drives the strongest rate-base growth |
| **Indiana Electric** | Vertically integrated electric generation and distribution in Indiana | Smaller; carries generation transition (coal retirement / new build) and associated capex |
| **Natural Gas distribution** | Regulated gas LDC across Texas, Indiana, Minnesota and Ohio | Stable regulated distribution; multi-state rate-case cadence; non-discretionary heating demand |

A regulated utility earns an allowed return on its invested capital ("rate base"); CenterPoint's thesis is that exceptional Houston-area load growth — measured in gigawatts of data-centre and industrial interconnection requests — lets it grow rate base, and therefore earnings, faster than the typical US utility while recovering heavy resiliency capex through constructive Texas regulation.

## 2025–2026 Developments

- **Resiliency-driven capex acceleration:** following the 2024 Hurricane Beryl outage criticism, CenterPoint executed the Greater Houston Resiliency Initiative and repeatedly raised its 10-year capital plan, with mobile generation, automation, and stronger poles/undergrounding across 2025.
- **Q1 2026 (reported April 23, 2026):** non-GAAP EPS of $0.56; management reiterated 2026 non-GAAP EPS guidance, targeting at least the midpoint of **$1.89–$1.91**, and reaffirmed its long-term 6–8% annual EPS growth framework.
- **Load growth story:** continued disclosure of multi-gigawatt data-centre and industrial interconnection queues in the Houston Electric service territory through 2025–2026 supported guidance raises and made CNP a frequent feature in "AI power demand" utility baskets.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

CenterPoint's [[economic-moat|economic moat]] is the classic **regulated-utility monopoly moat**, sharpened by the quality of its franchise mix:

- **Regulated monopoly franchises** — exclusive, rate-regulated service territories for both electric T&D and gas distribution; customers are captive and revenue is set by regulators, not the market.
- **Wires-only Houston Electric** — a pure transmission-and-distribution franchise with *no generation or commodity price risk* — among the lowest-risk, highest-quality regulated business models, earning a return purely on the wires.
- **Rate-base compounding on premium load** — Houston-area data-centre and Gulf Coast industrial load (gigawatts of interconnection requests) lets CenterPoint grow rate base faster than the typical utility, the core of the growth-kicker thesis.
- **Replacement cost & essentiality** — the T&D and gas network is effectively impossible to duplicate, and electricity/gas demand is non-discretionary, giving defensive cash flows.

The binding constraints are the **regulators** (Texas PUC/Railroad Commission and the Indiana/Minnesota/Ohio commissions), which set allowed returns and decide recovery of the heavy resiliency capex, and **Gulf hurricane exposure**, which periodically tests reliability and the political/regulatory appetite for cost recovery (as the 2024 Beryl episode showed).

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Houston load growth** — multi-gigawatt data-centre and industrial interconnection queues drive the strongest organic rate-base growth profile among US utilities (see [[ai-data-center-power-demand]]).
- **Resiliency capex** — the Greater Houston Resiliency Initiative (post-Beryl) and repeated 10-year capital-plan raises expand rate base.
- **Constructive Texas regulation** — favourable rate-case and cost-recovery outcomes convert capex into earnings.
- **Long-term EPS framework** — management's reaffirmed 6-8% annual EPS growth target, among the higher rates in the regulated space.
- **Recurring catalysts** — Texas rate-case outcomes and resiliency-cost-recovery rulings, hurricane season (June-November), data-centre interconnection announcements, equity-issuance needs to fund capex, and quarterly EPS prints.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- **Premium load growth** — gigawatts of Houston data-centre/industrial interconnection demand support sector-leading rate-base and 6-8% EPS growth.
- **High-quality wires franchise** — Houston Electric is a pure T&D, commodity-risk-free regulated business — a low-risk earnings base.
- **Resiliency capex visibility** — a clear, large, multi-year capital plan with regulatory recovery mechanisms.
- **Defensive income** — regulated cash flows plus a growing dividend; outperforms in risk-off rotations.

### Bear case
- **Hurricane / reliability risk** — Gulf Coast landfall (Beryl, 2024) creates outage, cost and political/regulatory-recovery risk; a recurring event-risk discount.
- **Regulatory & recovery lag** — heavy capex must be recovered through rate cases; adverse outcomes or political pushback cap returns.
- **Equity dilution** — funding a large capital plan may require equity issuance, diluting per-share growth.
- **Rate sensitivity** — as a bond proxy, the valuation compresses when long Treasury yields rise.

## Key Risks

- **Hurricane / extreme-weather** — Gulf landfall risk drives outages, restoration cost and recovery uncertainty (the Beryl precedent).
- **Regulatory lag** — allowed ROE and cost-recovery timing across Texas, Indiana, Minnesota and Ohio commissions.
- **Capex funding / dilution** — large plan may require equity issuance.
- **Rising interest rates** — compress the bond-proxy valuation and lift financing costs.
- **Load-growth realisation** — the thesis depends on data-centre/industrial interconnections actually energising and being served profitably.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

CenterPoint trades as a **regulated-utility bond proxy with a growth kicker** — valued on yield plus premium Texas-driven EPS growth rather than on cyclical earnings:

- **Yield + above-average growth** — total return is the dividend plus management's targeted 6-8% EPS growth, a premium rate within the regulated space that supports a relative valuation premium when load growth is credible.
- **Bond-proxy behaviour** — rate-sensitive; correlates inversely with the 10-year Treasury yield, with low beta to the [[s-and-p-500]].
- **Event-risk discount** — Gulf hurricane season (June-November) adds a recurring weather/event-risk overhang absent from inland utilities.
- **Thematic basket member** — features in utility / "AI power demand" baskets alongside merchant names [[vistra]] and [[constellation-energy]], though CNP is the lower-risk regulated expression of the theme.
- **Catalyst-driven** — rate-case and resiliency-recovery rulings and data-centre interconnection news move the stock more than the headline quarterly print.
