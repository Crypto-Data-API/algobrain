eBay Inc. (NASDAQ: EBAY) is one of the world's largest online marketplaces, connecting buyers and sellers across ~190 markets, classified Broadline Retail in the [[consumer-discretionary|Consumer Discretionary]] sector and a constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]] index. Unlike [[amazon]], it runs a **pure third-party marketplace** — it owns no inventory and takes no warehousing or fulfilment risk, monetising other people's transactions via a take-rate on gross merchandise volume (GMV) plus advertising. For traders it is a high-margin, cash-returning [[technology]] platform whose stock re-rated through 2025–2026 as GMV growth reaccelerated from years of stagnation into double digits, shifting the narrative from "value/capital-return" to "growth at a reasonable price."

## Business Overview & Segments

Founded by Pierre Omidyar in 1995 (CIK 0001065088; headquartered in San Jose, California), eBay is an asset-light, two-sided marketplace: buyers and sellers transact, eBay takes a cut, and the company carries no first-party inventory. That structure is the source of its ~72% gross margins and heavy free cash flow — most of which is returned through buybacks and a dividend (initiated 2022). Strategy under CEO Jamie Iannone centres on **focus categories** — verticals where eBay can build a defensible, specialist marketplace rather than compete head-on with [[amazon]] in commodity retail — layered with a fast-growing first-party advertising business and a fully owned managed-payments stack.

| Segment / line | What it is | Mix & dynamics (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **Marketplace take-rate (GMV)** | Final-value and listing fees charged on goods sold across ~190 markets; the core engine | The overwhelming majority of revenue; revenue ≈ GMV × take-rate, so GMV growth and take-rate are the two levers traders watch |
| **First-party advertising** | Promoted Listings and ad products sold to sellers on eBay's own surfaces | High-margin and fast-growing; a structural take-rate uplift layered on top of marketplace fees |
| **Managed payments** | eBay's in-house payments stack (post-[[paypal]] separation) processing transactions end-to-end | Captures payments economics formerly ceded to PayPal; deepens data and seller relationships |
| **Focus categories** | Collectibles/trading cards, motors parts & accessories, luxury (authenticated handbags/watches/sneakers), refurbished/certified goods | The strategic growth tilt — higher-value, repeat-purchase, enthusiast verticals where authentication and depth create an edge |
| **Geo-specific marketplaces** | Localised sites and investments in priority international markets | Targeted GMV reacceleration via localised inventory, trust and shipping |

The thesis is that eBay can't out-scale [[amazon]] in horizontal commerce, so it concentrates on **enthusiast and resale verticals** — trading cards, watches, sneakers, auto parts — where buyer/seller liquidity, condition grading and authentication (e.g. authenticated luxury) matter more than two-day shipping. Advertising and owned payments raise the effective take-rate on every transaction without adding inventory risk.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

eBay's [[economic-moat]] rests primarily on **two-sided [[network-effects]]**: a large installed base of buyers attracts sellers (and their inventory), and deep, varied inventory attracts buyers — a liquidity flywheel that is hard to bootstrap from zero. Reinforcing layers:

- **Marketplace liquidity** — for hard-to-find, used, vintage and enthusiast goods, eBay is often the deepest pool of both supply and demand; thin categories elsewhere are liquid here.
- **Category-specialist depth + authentication** — in focus verticals (trading cards, luxury, sneakers, watches), authentication services and condition grading build buyer trust that a generic marketplace can't easily replicate, raising [[switching-costs]] for serious sellers.
- **Seller tooling & reputation systems** — established seller accounts, feedback/reputation scores and listing histories are sticky; rebuilding standing on a rival platform is costly.
- **Brand & habit** — three decades of recognition as *the* place to buy and sell secondhand and collectible goods.
- **Owned payments + data** — managed payments keeps transaction economics and behavioural data in-house, sharpening ad targeting and risk management.

Critically, the moat is **narrower than [[amazon]]'s in horizontal commerce** — eBay cannot match Amazon's Prime logistics, first-party selection or ad scale, and in commodity new-goods retail it has structurally lost share. That reality *is* the strategy: rather than defend an indefensible horizontal position, eBay retreats to verticals where marketplace liquidity and authentication, not fulfilment speed, decide the winner. Against [[etsy]] (handmade/craft) and resale specialists ([[poshmark]], [[mercari]], [[stockx]], [[depop]]) it competes vertical-by-vertical; against social commerce ([[facebook-marketplace]]) it competes on trust, payments and buyer protection.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Focus categories** — collectibles/trading cards, motors P&A, authenticated luxury and refurbished goods drive the GMV reacceleration; these higher-value, repeat-purchase verticals lift both volume and take-rate.
- **Depop acquisition (~$1.2B cash)** — adds Gen-Z fashion resale (Depop did ~$1B GMV in 2025, ~60% US growth); expected to add ~1–2pp to FX-neutral GMV growth in 2026, EPS-dilutive initially and accretive by ~2028.
- **Advertising ramp** — first-party Promoted Listings continue to scale as a high-margin, take-rate-accretive layer.
- **Authentication expansion** — extending authenticity guarantees into more categories deepens buyer trust and unlocks higher-value transactions.
- **AI-driven listing & search** — generative tools that auto-create listings from a photo and improve discovery lower seller friction and improve conversion.
- **Managed payments + financial services** — owned payments and adjacent financing/seller-services economics.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly GMV and take-rate prints vs guidance, Depop closing/integration milestones, advertising-revenue disclosures, consumer-discretionary data, and buyback pace.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- GMV growth has reaccelerated from years of stagnation to double digits — if durable, the multiple re-rates further toward growth peers.
- The focus-category + advertising + owned-payments combination structurally lifts the take-rate on every transaction without adding inventory risk.
- Asset-light model throws off heavy free cash flow; aggressive buybacks shrink the share count and a growing dividend adds total-return support.
- Authentication and enthusiast-vertical liquidity create a defensible niche that Amazon's logistics advantage doesn't neutralise.
- Depop and resale momentum tap the secular Gen-Z/secondhand-shopping shift.

### Bear case
- The GMV reacceleration may prove cyclical (a collectibles/trading-card boom) rather than structural, and could fade.
- [[amazon]] and social commerce keep pressuring horizontal demand; resale is fragmenting across specialist apps ([[poshmark]], [[mercari]], [[stockx]], [[depop]] pre-deal).
- Take-rate has a practical ceiling — push fees/ads too hard and sellers defect to lower-cost channels ([[shopify]] DTC, social).
- Consumer-discretionary cyclicality: a spending slowdown hits GMV directly.
- Depop integration adds execution and near-term dilution risk for a relatively small GMV contribution.

## Key Risks

- **GMV-growth durability** — the entire re-rating rests on double-digit GMV growth being structural, not a collectibles-cycle spike; a reversion to low-single-digit growth would de-rate the stock.
- **Competitive pressure** — [[amazon]]'s horizontal dominance and [[facebook-marketplace]]/social commerce on casual C2C supply; resale fragmentation across specialist platforms.
- **Consumer-discretionary cyclicality** — GMV is directly geared to discretionary spending and big-ticket/enthusiast purchases that consumers defer in downturns.
- **Take-rate ceiling** — monetisation (fees + ads) can only rise so far before seller economics break and supply migrates off-platform.
- **Depop integration risk** — acquisition execution, near-term EPS dilution and the risk the resale bet underwhelms.
- **FX translation** — a large international GMV base means a strong dollar drags reported (vs FX-neutral) growth.

## Trading Relevance

- **GMV reacceleration through 2025** — quarterly results beat through FY2025, with momentum building each quarter; collectibles (notably trading cards) and geo-specific investments drove growth.
- **Q4 2025 results / 2026 guidance (2026-02-18)** — eBay guided Q1 2026 GMV to $21.5–21.9B (10–12% FX-neutral growth) and revenue to $3.0–3.05B, with non-GAAP EPS of $1.53–$1.59.
- **Depop acquisition** — definitive agreement to acquire Gen-Z fashion-resale marketplace Depop for ~$1.2 billion cash (Depop did ~$1B GMV in 2025, with 60% US growth); expected to add 1–2pp to FX-neutral GMV growth in 2026, slightly dilutive to non-GAAP EPS initially, accretive by 2028.
- **Q1 2026 beat (reported 2026-04/05)** — GMV $22.2B, up 18% as reported (14% FX-neutral) and revenue $3.1B, up 19%, both above guidance — the strongest growth in years.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

eBay has historically traded at a discount to growthier internet peers — a "value/capital-return" multiple reflecting years of flat-to-low GMV growth, fat margins and heavy buybacks. The 2025–2026 GMV reacceleration into double digits shifted it toward **growth-at-a-reasonable-price**, and the multiple re-rated as the market began to weigh whether the higher growth is structural. Mechanically:

- **GMV and take-rate prints are the drivers** — revenue ≈ GMV × take-rate, so the quarterly GMV growth number (and the FX-neutral vs as-reported split) is what moves the stock; advertising and payments monetisation drive the take-rate line.
- **Capital return** — an asset-light model throws off heavy free cash flow funnelled into aggressive buybacks (a meaningful share-count reducer) plus a dividend initiated in 2022; the API ROE is flattered by buyback-shrunken book equity (see the data block above for the figures as pulled).
- **Beta** — historically a relatively low-beta, defensive-ish discretionary name; the re-rating has added growth sensitivity, so reactions to GMV surprises are now sharper.
- **Cycle sensitivity** — geared to the resale/collectibles cycle (trading cards, sneakers, luxury) and to broad consumer-discretionary spending; a discretionary slowdown hits GMV directly.
- **Earnings behaviour** — gaps on whether GMV growth beats and holds; guidance on FX-neutral GMV and the take-rate trajectory matter more than the headline revenue line.
