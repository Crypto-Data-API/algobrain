Sempra (NYSE: SRE) is a San Diego-based energy infrastructure holding company and one of the largest regulated utility owners in North America, operating across two of the highest-growth U.S. utility markets — Texas and California — plus a major [[lng|LNG]] export business. It is a [[s-and-p-500]] constituent in the [[utilities|Utilities]] sector. For traders it is a large-cap, lower-beta [[utilities|utility]] with an unusual growth angle: rising electricity demand from AI data centers in Texas and a sizeable [[liquefied-natural-gas|LNG]] development pipeline give it more catalysts than a typical regulated utility.

## Business Overview & Segments

Sempra is a **regulated-utility-plus-LNG** holding company: the bulk of earnings come from low-risk, rate-base-driven regulated utilities in Texas and California, with a higher-upside [[lng|LNG]]/infrastructure leg as an option on global gas. The qualitative mix below is indicative, not a precise current breakdown — see the data disclaimer in Fundamentals.

| Platform | What it is | Mix (qualitative) | Profile |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sempra Texas** | ~80% economic interest in **Oncor**, the largest regulated T&D utility in Texas | Core growth engine | Regulated; surging ERCOT load (population + data-center demand) |
| **Sempra California** | **San Diego Gas & Electric (SDG&E)** and **Southern California Gas (SoCalGas)** | Large | Regulated electric + gas; wildfire-liability-exposed |
| **Sempra Infrastructure** | LNG export (Cameron LNG; Port Arthur LNG under construction) + Mexico energy networks (IEnova / Sempra Infrastructure Partners) | Higher-upside leg | Non/less-regulated; tied to global LNG demand; 45% sold to [[kkr]] (2026) |

Sempra's investment thesis rests on a very large, multi-year **regulated capital plan funded into rising rate base** (a record ~$65B plan), with [[lng|LNG]] as an option on global gas markets and Texas data-center load as a structural demand tailwind.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Sempra holds a **wide** [[economic-moat|economic moat]] of the kind intrinsic to regulated utilities — a regulated monopoly franchise rather than a competitive edge that must be defended in a market:

- **Regulated monopoly franchises** — Oncor (Texas T&D), SDG&E and SoCalGas operate as exclusive franchises in their service territories; there is no head-to-head competition for customers.
- **Rate base + authorized ROE model** — utilities earn a regulator-set return on invested capital ("rate base"); growth comes from deploying capex into a growing rate base, a durable, low-risk compounding mechanism.
- **High barriers to entry** — building duplicate transmission, distribution or LNG infrastructure is economically and politically infeasible; regulatory approval is itself a moat.
- **LNG scale & contracts** — long-term LNG offtake contracts and scarce export-terminal capacity provide a differentiated, harder-to-replicate asset.

The moat's limit is **regulatory dependence**: returns are capped and set by commissions (Texas PUCT, California CPUC), so the risk is unfavorable rate cases and (in California) wildfire liability — not competitive displacement. The Texas/data-center load growth and LNG optionality are what give Sempra more upside than a vanilla regulated peer.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Record ~$65B capital plan** — deploying capex into a growing rate base is the core regulated-earnings growth engine.
- **Oncor / ERCOT load growth** — Texas population growth plus **AI data-center demand** drive transmission/distribution investment; the April 2026 Oncor rate settlement (9.75% authorized ROE) is constructive.
- **[[lng|LNG]] development** — Port Arthur LNG construction and further FIDs are options on global gas demand and a non-regulated upside leg.
- **[[kkr]] stake sale** — selling 45% of Sempra Infrastructure Partners for ~$10B recycles capital into the regulated utilities (the single largest 2026 catalyst).
- **California rate cases** — SDG&E/SoCalGas regulatory outcomes.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly earnings, the KKR/Sempra Infrastructure close, rate-case decisions, ERCOT load/data-center data, LNG project FIDs and global gas prices, and interest-rate moves.

## 2025-2026 Developments

- **FY2025 results** (reported Feb 2026): adjusted earnings of ~US$3.07B, or ~US$4.69 per diluted share (vs. US$4.65 in 2024); GAAP EPS US$2.75. Sempra invested ~US$13B in 2025, mostly into its Texas and California utilities.
- **2026 guidance**: adjusted EPS of US$4.80-5.30. Management laid out a record ~US$65B capital plan and lifted long-term (2030) earnings growth targets, anchored by Oncor.
- **KKR stake sale**: Sempra agreed to sell a **45% equity stake in Sempra Infrastructure Partners to [[kkr]] affiliates for ~US$10B**, with closing expected in Q2-Q3 2026 (pending approvals) — a portfolio-reshaping deal that recycles capital into the regulated utilities and is the single largest 2026 catalyst.
- **Oncor rate case**: in April 2026 the Texas PUCT adopted Oncor's base rate settlement, setting an annual revenue requirement of ~US$6.97B with a 9.75% authorized ROE — a constructive outcome supporting the Texas growth story.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- A record ~$65B regulated capital plan deploying into rising rate base drives durable, low-risk earnings growth.
- Oncor/ERCOT load growth (Texas population + AI data centers) gives Sempra structural demand a typical utility lacks.
- LNG optionality (Port Arthur, FIDs) and the ~$10B [[kkr]] capital recycling sharpen the regulated-growth story.
- Lower-beta, dividend-paying defensive profile with above-peer growth catalysts.

### Bear case
- Regulated returns are capped by commissions; unfavorable Texas/California rate cases would cap growth.
- California wildfire liability (SDG&E/SoCalGas) is a tail risk for any CA utility.
- High leverage (D/E ~1.08x) and a low quick ratio make the stock rate-sensitive; utilities trade inversely to long-end yields.
- LNG execution and global gas prices are non-regulated, more volatile earnings drivers.

## Key Risks

- **Regulatory** — adverse rate-case outcomes (Texas PUCT, California CPUC) directly cap authorized returns and rate-base growth.
- **California wildfire liability** — SDG&E/SoCalGas face the catastrophic-liability risk endemic to California utilities.
- **Interest-rate sensitivity** — as a levered, dividend-paying utility, SRE trades inversely to long-end yields; higher rates pressure the multiple and raise financing costs.
- **Leverage & financing** — D/E ~1.08x with a sub-1 quick ratio (read against the wider utility leverage bar); large capex plan requires continuous capital access.
- **LNG / commodity** — Port Arthur execution, FID timing and global gas prices drive the non-regulated leg.
- **Execution** — delivering the ~$65B capital plan and closing the [[kkr]] transaction on schedule.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Sempra trades as a **regulated utility with a growth kicker** — valued on rate-base growth, authorized ROE and dividend yield rather than earnings multiples in the growth sense (see Fundamentals). Mechanically:

- **Beta & rate sensitivity** — lower-beta, defensive [[utilities|Utilities]] name; like all utilities it trades **inversely to long-end yields**, so interest-rate moves are a primary driver.
- **Dividend** — a core attraction: a steady, growing dividend funded by regulated cash flows; total return is yield-plus-rate-base-growth.
- **Growth premium** — the Texas/Oncor data-center load and [[lng|LNG]] optionality give Sempra more growth catalysts than pure regulated peers, supporting a relative premium within the sector.
- **Leverage** — utility-appropriate leverage (D/E ~1.08x, within the wider utility bar) funds the large capital plan; covenant headroom matters in a downturn.
- **Catalysts that move the stock** — rate-case decisions, the [[kkr]] close, ERCOT/data-center load data, LNG FIDs/global gas prices, and macro rate moves more than headline EPS.
- **Correlated names** — regulated-utility peers [[american-electric-power]], [[ameren]], [[atmos-energy]]; the LNG leg tracks global natural-gas pricing.
