Everest Group (NYSE: EG, formerly Everest Re) is a Bermuda-based global reinsurer and specialty insurer, one of the world's largest property-catastrophe reinsurance writers, and a constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]]. For traders it is a leveraged play on reinsurance pricing cycles and catastrophe outcomes — and since late 2025, a reserve-credibility turnaround story after a $1.7B US casualty reserve charge.

## Business Overview & Segments

Everest operates two segments: **Reinsurance** (the core franchise — property-cat, casualty, and specialty treaty business written out of Bermuda, the US, and international hubs) and **Insurance** (primary specialty lines built out aggressively during 2018-2023). The reinsurance book benefited from the hard market of 2023-2025, with record property-cat pricing; the insurance build-out, however, produced the US casualty under-reserving (social-inflation-driven) that forced the 2025 charge and a strategic retrenchment. FY2025 gross written premium was $17.7B with operating income of $1,591M, a year management itself called one "of profound change." At ~10x trailing earnings (see Fundamentals) the stock trades at a discount to reinsurance peers reflecting that reserve overhang.

As a Bermuda reinsurer Everest sits in the [[financials]] sector of the [[s-and-p-500]]. Its economics are the textbook reinsurance model — collect premium up front, hold **float** invested against future claims, and earn an underwriting margin set by the combined ratio — which makes it a leveraged bet on the catastrophe-pricing cycle and on reserve adequacy.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Everest's [[economic-moat]] is narrow and rests on **scale, capital, and underwriting franchise** rather than any proprietary product — reinsurance is a capacity/relationship business where the durable advantages are:

- **Balance-sheet scale and ratings** — the ability to write large, diversified property-cat lines and absorb tail losses; strong financial-strength ratings are the license to compete for the best treaties.
- **Cycle-timing and underwriting discipline** — reinsurers that deploy capacity into hard markets (and pull back in soft ones) compound book value through cycles; Everest's reinsurance book did this well in 2023-25, even as the insurance build-out did not.
- **Global platform and data** — Bermuda/US/international hubs and decades of loss data support catastrophe modeling and pricing.

The 2025 $1.7B casualty reserve charge is precisely a moat/credibility wound: it showed the insurance expansion was underwritten less disciplined than the reinsurance core, and the stock's persistent discount to peers like [[arch-capital]] and RenaissanceRe is the market pricing that lost trust until reserve development proves clean.

## 2025–2026 Developments

- **CEO change (Jan 2025):** Jim Williamson was appointed CEO, succeeding Juan Andrade; a broader management reshuffle followed.
- **$1.7B reserve charge (announced Oct 2025):** Everest strengthened US casualty loss reserves by $1.7B, confirming long-running market suspicion about its 2019-2023 casualty accident years.
- **Adverse development cover:** simultaneously bought an adverse-development reinsurance agreement effective 2025-10-01 providing $1.2B of gross limit above the strengthened reserves — capping further downside from the legacy book.
- **Retreat from retail commercial insurance:** sold renewal rights for its Global Retail Commercial Insurance business (~$2B of gross premiums) to [[aig|AIG]], refocusing the company on reinsurance and specialty/wholesale lines.
- **FY2025 results (reported Feb 2026):** $17.7B gross written premium; operating income $1,591M; insider buying by directors followed the reserve reset.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Property-cat pricing** — capacity remained tight and pricing near multi-decade highs through 2023-25; how fast it softens into 2026 renewals is the key top-line swing.
- **Clean reserve development** — each quarter of no further casualty adverse development (post-charge, post-ADC) rebuilds credibility and is the catalyst for the discount to close.
- **Investment income** — higher reinvestment yields on the large bond portfolio lift net investment income as the book turns over.
- **Refocus on reinsurance/specialty** — exiting retail commercial (renewal rights to [[aig]]) and the legacy ADC cap concentrate capital on the higher-return reinsurance core.
- **Capital return** — buybacks/dividends supported once reserve adequacy is re-established; insider buying by directors followed the reset.

## Bull vs Bear

### Bull case
- The $1.7B reserve charge plus the $1.2B adverse-development cover should be a clean-up event — capping legacy downside and letting the high-quality reinsurance franchise show through at ~10x earnings.
- Property-cat pricing is still historically strong, and a refocused, reinsurance-weighted Everest earns attractive returns on float in a high-rate world.
- A discount to [[arch-capital]]/RenaissanceRe that closes as quarterly reserve development proves benign offers re-rating upside.

### Bear case
- Casualty social inflation could push losses *beyond* the ADC limit, reopening the reserve wound and destroying remaining credibility.
- A major catastrophe year hits a property-cat-heavy book hard.
- Property-cat rates soften faster than expected into 2026 renewals, eroding the hard-market tailwind just as the company leans into it.
- New-CEO execution risk on the strategic retrenchment.

## Key Risks

- **Casualty reserve adequacy** — further adverse development on 2019-2023 accident years above the $1.2B ADC limit is the single biggest idiosyncratic risk.
- **Catastrophe loss** — a large hurricane/earthquake/wildfire season can wipe out an underwriting year for a property-cat writer.
- **Pricing cycle** — accelerating property-cat rate softening compresses the core margin.
- **Investment / credit risk** — the bond portfolio carries duration and credit exposure that flows straight to book value.
- **Execution** — new management's strategic refocus and the AIG renewal-rights transition must be delivered cleanly.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Everest trades on **price-to-book** and a normalized return-on-equity through the cycle, not on noisy GAAP EPS — the standard lens for a reinsurer whose results are dominated by catastrophe timing. The persistent discount to peers reflects the 2025 reserve charge; the bull thesis is mean-reversion of that discount as **combined ratio** and reserve development normalize. Key mechanics:

- **Combined ratio / float** — below 100% means underwriting profit; the reinsurance book runs well below that ex-catastrophe, while investment income on the float is the second, increasingly large earnings engine in a high-rate environment.
- **Two recurring event windows** — **hurricane season** (June-November, cat-loss headlines) and **January 1 reinsurance renewals** (pricing data) frame the year.
- **Dividend / beta** — pays a steady dividend; the stock is a higher-beta, cyclical financial whose price swings on cat events and reserve disclosures rather than on macro rates alone.
- **Catalysts:** reserve-development disclosures each quarter (the key credibility test post-charge), cat events, renewal pricing (signs of soft-market erosion after the 2023-2025 peak), and execution of the AIG renewal-rights transition.
- **Options:** liquid enough for event structures around the cat/renewal windows.
- **Correlated names:** [[arch-capital]] (ACGL), RenaissanceRe (RNR), [[chubb]] (CB), Munich Re, Swiss Re; the EG-vs-ACGL/RNR spread is the standard pair trade.
