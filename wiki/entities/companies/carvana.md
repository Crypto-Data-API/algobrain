Carvana (NYSE: CVNA) is an American online used car retailer founded in 2012 by Ernie Garcia III, headquartered in Tempe, Arizona. The company pioneered the e-commerce model for used car sales, allowing customers to browse inventory online, secure financing, and have vehicles delivered to their door — or famously, dispensed from multi-story glass "car vending machines" built in several US cities. Carvana went public in 2017 and quickly became a high-profile growth stock before experiencing one of the most dramatic boom-bust-recovery cycles in modern equity markets.

## Business Model

Carvana's vertically integrated platform handles the entire used car transaction: acquisition (buying from consumers and auctions), reconditioning (at proprietary inspection centres), online retail (website and app), financing (Carvana-originated auto loans), and delivery logistics. The company also operates ADESA, a wholesale auction business acquired through the 2022 purchase of KAR Global's US physical auction business. Revenue is driven by retail unit sales and ancillary products including vehicle service contracts, GAP coverage, and finance receivables. The company processes hundreds of thousands of retail units annually.

## Business Segments

Carvana's profit pool comes from several stacked revenue streams per transaction, not just the car price:

| Segment / stream | What it is | Trading lens |
|---|---|---|
| **Retail vehicle sales (units)** | Used cars sold online and delivered/dispensed; the headline volume metric (retail units sold) | The growth engine — unit growth vs. the market is the share-gain story against [[carmax]] |
| **Retail GPU (gross profit per unit)** | Total gross profit — vehicle margin + financing + ancillary — divided by units sold | The core profitability lever; rising GPU on rising units drives operating leverage |
| **Financing / loan sales** | Carvana-originated auto loans, packaged and sold (securitized) to investors; gain-on-sale economics | High-margin but cyclical — sensitive to interest rates, securitization spreads and subprime credit performance; retained exposure adds credit risk |
| **Ancillary products** | Vehicle service contracts (VSC), GAP coverage and add-ons attached at point of sale | Steady, high-margin attach revenue that lifts GPU |
| **ADESA (wholesale auctions)** | Physical/digital wholesale auction network (acquired from KAR Global, 2022); reconditioning and logistics backbone | Vertical integration that lowers reconditioning cost and shortens inventory cycle; a structural cost-and-supply advantage |
| **Wholesale vehicle sales** | Trade-ins and lower-grade inventory sold into wholesale channels | Lower-margin but supports inventory turns and sourcing |

The strategic edge is **vertical integration** — sourcing, reconditioning (proprietary inspection centres), ADESA logistics, in-house financing and last-mile delivery — which lets Carvana capture margin at each step and scale SG&A across more units.

## Competitive Positioning & Peers

Carvana is the clear share-gainer in US used-car retail, taking volume from incumbents while expanding margins.

| Company | Ticker | Positioning vs. Carvana |
|---|---|---|
| [[carvana]] | CVNA | E-commerce-native, vertically integrated; fastest unit growth; high GPU trajectory |
| [[carmax]] | KMX | Largest used-car retailer; omnichannel but store-anchored; the primary share donor |
| Traditional franchise dealers | (e.g. AutoNation, Penske, Lithia, Group 1) | New + used + service/parts; local, fragmented; slower online conversion |
| Online/peer entrants | (e.g. Vroom — exited retail) | Largely failed to match Carvana's logistics/financing integration |

Carvana's moat versus traditional dealers is the **scale + integration** combination (ADESA logistics, in-house financing, national inventory pooling) that point-of-sale dealers and store-bound omnichannel players like [[carmax]] struggle to replicate at cost. The cleanest expression is a long CVNA / short [[carmax]] relative-value pair.

## Bull vs Bear Case

**Bull case**
- Industry-leading unit growth while [[carmax]] posts declining units — durable share gains in a huge, fragmented market.
- Powerful operating leverage: record GPU on rising volume against a flatter SG&A base drives outsized EBITDA and EPS growth.
- ADESA integration and vertical supply chain lower reconditioning cost and improve inventory turns.
- Turnaround completed — from near-bankruptcy to record profitability, [[s-and-p-500]] inclusion and improved balance sheet.

**Bear case**
- Still-substantial debt from the 2023 restructuring; turnaround is real but leverage remains elevated.
- Valuation premium prices continued hypergrowth; any unit-growth or GPU disappointment de-rates sharply.
- Financing/gain-on-sale and subprime credit exposure is cyclical — vulnerable to higher rates, wider securitization spreads or rising loan losses.
- Related-party and governance overhang (DriveTime / Garcia family ties, insider selling) has historically sparked sharp drawdowns.
- Used-car pricing (Manheim index) cycles affect both volume and per-unit margin.

## Valuation Framework

Carvana is valued as a high-growth, operating-leverage turnaround; the qualitative levers:

- **GPU per unit × units** — the core profit identity; the market pays up for rising GPU on accelerating volume.
- **SG&A leverage** — the turnaround thesis; spreading a roughly fixed cost base over more units converts revenue growth into outsized EBITDA growth.
- **Adjusted EBITDA margin trajectory** — the headline profitability metric the Street tracks post-recovery.
- **Debt load / net leverage** — the residual from the 2023 restructuring; deleveraging (and refinancing of high-coupon notes) is a key part of the turnaround narrative and a swing factor in equity value.
- **Financing economics** — gain-on-sale margins, securitization market access and subprime credit performance.
- **Free cash flow conversion** — the proof that the model self-funds growth rather than burning cash as in 2022.

- **Catalysts calendar**: quarterly earnings (large historical gap moves — Q4 2025 EPS was a ~4x beat), used-car price data (Manheim index), index-flow events, convertible/refinancing actions, and related-party/insider-selling headlines.

## Capital Structure & Dilution

- **Residual debt from the 2023 restructuring** — the debt exchange reduced obligations by $1.2 billion but left a still-substantial, high-coupon debt stack; deleveraging and refinancing are central to the equity story.
- **Convertible / refinancing actions** — the company has used converts and refinancings; these affect share count and interest expense and are recurring catalysts.
- **Securitization funding** — the finance arm relies on access to the asset-backed securities market; spreads and investor appetite gate financing-segment profitability.
- **Related-party structure** — DriveTime/Garcia-family ties and insider selling are a persistent governance overhang that periodically pressures the stock.

## Stock Price History

Carvana's equity is one of the most dramatic [[volatility]] stories in recent market history. The stock surged from its 2017 IPO price of $15 to approximately $370 in August 2021, fuelled by pandemic-era used car demand, low interest rates, and growth-stock euphoria. It then collapsed over 99% to a low of approximately $3.55 in December 2022 as rising interest rates crushed used car demand, the company burned cash, and a $9 billion debt load raised existential solvency concerns. Against widespread expectations of bankruptcy, Carvana executed a dramatic turnaround in 2023-2024 through aggressive cost-cutting, a debt exchange that reduced obligations by $1.2 billion, and a recovery in used car market fundamentals. The stock recovered to over $250 by late 2024, making it one of the largest short squeezes and turnaround stories of the decade.
