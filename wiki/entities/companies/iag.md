**Insurance Australia Group Limited** (ASX: IAG) is the largest general insurer in Australia and New Zealand, underwriting more than A$17 billion of gross written premium annually across brands including NRMA Insurance, CGU and NZI. A constituent of the [[asx-200]], it is the benchmark ASX play on P&C insurance pricing cycles, catastrophe/weather risk and reinsurance costs.

## Business Model & Segments

IAG writes both short-tail and long-tail general insurance products:

- **Short-tail products:** motor vehicle, home and contents, lifestyle and leisure (boats, caravans, classic cars), small business packages, farm, commercial motor, commercial property, fleet motor, construction and engineering, rural, marine, and niche pleasure craft/boat/caravan insurance.
- **Long-tail products:** professional indemnity, compulsory third party (motor injury liability), workers' compensation, directors' and officers' liability, public and products liability, and commercial liability.

Distribution is through branches, call centres, online channels, insurance brokers, authorised representatives, motor dealerships, and financial institution partners. Key brands include:

- **Australia:** NRMA Insurance, CGU, WFI, Swann Insurance, ROLLiN', and SGIO/SGIC.
- **New Zealand:** NZI, State, AMI, and Lumley Insurance.

The group also manages a substantial investment portfolio that backs insurance liabilities and shareholder funds. IAG reports along two main operating divisions — Direct Insurance Australia (DIA) and Intermediated Insurance Australia (IIA) — plus New Zealand, with the technical insurance result driven by the **combined ratio** (loss ratio + expense ratio) and the reported **insurance margin** layered with investment income earned on the **float** (premiums collected and reserves held before claims are paid).

| Division / line | What it is | Revenue / earnings mix (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct Insurance Australia (DIA)** | Consumer motor, home and contents sold direct under NRMA Insurance, SGIO/SGIC, and the digital-native ROLLiN' brand; the post-RACQI Queensland book sits here | Largest and most valuable division — high-retention personal lines, strong brand pricing power, the core earnings engine |
| **Intermediated Insurance Australia (IIA)** | Commercial and SME lines distributed through brokers and authorised representatives under CGU, WFI and Swann Insurance | Material; commercial-lines pricing cycle and broker relationships drive the swing in margins |
| **New Zealand** | Personal and commercial general insurance under NZI (intermediated), and State, AMI and Lumley (direct/intermediated) | Meaningful contributor; exposed to NZ earthquake and weather risk (e.g. Auckland floods, Cyclone Gabrielle history) |
| **Investment portfolio / float** | Technical reserves and shareholder funds invested largely in fixed income; income on the float | A second profit lever distinct from underwriting — rises with bond yields, cushioning weak underwriting years |

The economics are those of a general insurer: profit = underwriting result (premiums earned less claims and expenses, summarised in the combined ratio) **plus** investment income on the float. A combined ratio below 100% means underwriting itself is profitable; the reported [[insurance-margin]] guidance (see below) is the headline the market trades on.

## History

IAG's heritage traces back to 1920 when the National Roads and Motorists' Association (NRMA) was established in New South Wales. NRMA began providing insurance to members around 1925, growing into one of Australia's largest general insurers.

In July 2000, the NRMA Insurance business was demutualised and listed on the ASX as NRMA Insurance Group Limited, with shares issued to NRMA members. On 15 January 2002 the company changed its name to Insurance Australia Group Limited.

Following its demutualisation IAG pursued a sustained acquisition strategy to expand in Australia and New Zealand:

- Acquired CGU (including Swann Insurance) in Australia.
- Acquired NZI in New Zealand.
- Acquired New Zealand's largest general insurer, State Insurance.
- Acquired HIH's Australian workers' compensation policies and renewal rights, and Zurich's NSW workers' compensation business.
- **2014:** Completed the acquisition of Wesfarmers' underwriting businesses for approximately A$1.845 billion, bringing the WFI and Lumley brands into the group.

The CGU business line itself has insurance roots dating back to the 1860s, giving IAG one of the longest operating histories in Australasian insurance.

## Recent Developments

For the 2025 financial year (year ended 30 June 2025), IAG reported a net profit after tax of approximately A$1.36 billion (up from A$898 million in FY2024). Gross written premiums rose to A$17.1 billion from A$16.4 billion, supported by higher net earned premiums and below-allowance natural peril costs.

In 2025 IAG completed the acquisition of the insurance underwriting business of the Royal Automobile Club of Queensland (RACQI). Following completion, IAG upgraded its FY2026 guidance:

- Gross written premium growth expected at around 10% (up from low-to-mid single digit previously).
- Reported insurance profit guidance lifted to A$1.55-1.75 billion (from A$1.45-1.65 billion), implying a reported insurance margin of 14-16%.

For 1H FY2026 (half year ended 31 December 2025), IAG reported net profit after tax of A$505 million. The result included a one-off A$174 million impact from severe seasonal weather events affecting the newly acquired RACQI book.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

IAG sits inside one of the clearest [[economic-moat|economic moats]] available on the [[asx-200]]: Australian general insurance is a tight oligopoly, and IAG is the largest player in personal lines. The moat rests on several reinforcing layers rather than a single advantage:

- **Scale & cost advantage** — the largest motor/home book in the country amortises fixed claims, reinsurance and technology costs over the widest premium base, and gives IAG buying power with repairers and suppliers that smaller insurers cannot match.
- **Brand** — **NRMA Insurance** is among the most trusted consumer brands in Australia, supporting high renewal retention and pricing power in personal lines; sticky personal-lines customers are a soft but real [[switching-costs|switching cost]].
- **Distribution breadth** — direct (NRMA, SGIO/SGIC, ROLLiN'), intermediated/broker (CGU, WFI, Swann) and bancassurance/partner channels cover the full distribution map.
- **Data & pricing** — decades of claims and pricing data feed risk-selection and rate-setting, a compounding informational edge in a business where mispricing the tail is fatal.
- **Reinsurance access** — size secures favourable reinsurance and quota-share terms (including arrangements with global reinsurers), capping catastrophe volatility on terms unavailable to sub-scale rivals.

The moat is **wide but not impregnable**: personal lines are commoditising at the margin via aggregators and digital challengers (Youi, Hollard/Honey), and the structural exposure to Australian/NZ natural catastrophe means a few bad weather years can overwhelm pricing discipline regardless of moat. IAG competes in a comfortable oligopoly with [[suncorp]] and, in commercial lines, [[qbe-insurance]].

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Premium-rate hardening** — multi-year rate increases across motor, home and commercial lines to recover claims inflation and reinsurance cost; the gap between rate and claims inflation is the core margin lever.
- **RACQI acquisition** — the 2025 RACQ Insurance underwriting deal expands the Queensland personal-lines book and underpinned the upgraded FY2026 guidance (gross written premium growth around 10%).
- **GWP growth** — gross written premium rising on both rate and volume; FY2026 reported insurance profit guidance was lifted to **A$1.55–1.75 billion** (implying a reported insurance margin of **14–16%**).
- **Investment income on the float** — higher bond yields lift investment returns on technical reserves, a second earnings lever independent of underwriting.
- **Reinsurance/quota-share structure** — quota-share arrangements (including with global reinsurers) stabilise earnings and free capital for buybacks/dividends.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year (February) and full-year (August, June year-end) results, catastrophe events, the January 1 annual reinsurance renewal, natural-peril budget over/under-runs, and APRA/regulatory actions.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Sustained premium-rate hardening outpaces claims inflation, expanding the underwriting margin toward (and through) the FY2026 14–16% insurance-margin guidance.
- Oligopoly structure with [[suncorp]] limits price wars; scale and the NRMA brand defend share and pricing power.
- Higher bond yields lift investment income on the float, adding a second profit lever on top of underwriting.
- RACQI and disciplined capital management (quota share, buybacks, fully-franked dividends) support per-share earnings and returns.
- A benign natural-peril year — claims below the catastrophe allowance — can produce a large positive earnings surprise.

### Bear case
- Australian/NZ catastrophe exposure means a single severe season (east-coast floods/storms, cyclones, NZ earthquakes) can blow through the natural-peril allowance and gut the result — the 1H FY2026 A$174M RACQI weather hit is the live example.
- Claims inflation (motor repair, parts, construction/rebuild costs) can outrun rate increases, compressing the combined ratio.
- Rising reinsurance costs raise the floor on expenses and reduce retained margin.
- Regulatory/political pressure on premium affordability, plus aggregator/digital competition, can cap pricing power in personal lines.
- As a value/income name, the rating de-rates quickly if underwriting discipline slips or a capital event (large catastrophe, reserve strengthening) emerges.

## Key Risks

- **Catastrophe / weather** — the dominant risk; concentrated Australian and New Zealand natural-peril exposure (floods, storms, cyclones, earthquakes) can overwhelm the natural-peril allowance in any single year.
- **Claims inflation vs rate** — if motor-repair, parts and construction-cost inflation outruns premium-rate increases, the loss ratio (and combined ratio) deteriorates.
- **Reinsurance cost & availability** — hardening global reinsurance markets raise costs and can shift more tail risk back onto IAG's balance sheet.
- **Reserve adequacy (long-tail lines)** — professional indemnity, CTP, workers' comp and liability require accurate long-tail reserving; under-reserving surfaces as later earnings hits.
- **Investment / market risk** — the float is largely fixed income; rate and credit-spread moves swing investment income and shareholder-fund returns.
- **Regulatory & affordability** — APRA capital rules, conduct regulation, and political pressure on premium affordability can constrain pricing and returns.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

IAG trades as a value/income financial rather than a growth compounder, and the market prices it primarily off the **combined ratio** and the reported **insurance margin** — not headline revenue. Mechanically:

- **Combined ratio is the scoreboard** — loss ratio + expense ratio; below 100% means underwriting is profitable before investment income. The market reacts most to where the combined ratio lands versus expectations, and to whether natural-peril claims came in **over or under the catastrophe allowance** (an under-run is a positive earnings surprise; an over-run, like the 1H FY2026 A$174M RACQI weather hit, erodes the result).
- **Insurance margin guidance is the anchor** — FY2026 guidance of **A$1.55–1.75 billion** insurance profit (a **14–16% reported insurance margin**) frames the bull/bear debate; beats/misses against it move the stock.
- **Float / investment income** — a second, less-volatile profit lever; higher bond yields lift returns on technical reserves and shareholder funds, partly offsetting weak underwriting years.
- **Capital return** — IAG is a **fully-franked dividend** payer; franking credits gross up the after-tax yield for Australian residents, and quota-share arrangements free capital for buybacks. Income, not growth, is the total-return story.
- **Beta** — moderate (well below a high-beta growth name); it is one of the more liquid ASX financials outside the big-four banks, with reasonable single-stock options, and is the benchmark ASX expression of the P&C pricing cycle and catastrophe risk.
