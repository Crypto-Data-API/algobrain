Propel Funeral Partners (ASX: PFP) is the second-largest private death-care operator in Australia and New Zealand, running funeral homes, crematoria and cemeteries, and — following InvoCare's 2023 privatisation — the only pure-play funeral provider listed on the ASX. For traders it is a defensive, demographically-driven roll-up: a small/mid-cap whose growth comes from acquiring independent funeral businesses and from the structural rise in deaths as the population ages. It is also the primary worked example used throughout [[alfred-fundamental-analysis|Alfred's fundamental-analysis]] framework.

## Business Overview

Propel operates a network of funeral, cremation and cemetery businesses across Australia and New Zealand, growing through a disciplined acquire-and-integrate strategy alongside organic volume growth. Revenue is driven by funeral volumes (closely tied to death rates), average revenue per funeral (pricing/mix), and the cadence of bolt-on acquisitions. The death-care sector is prized for its defensiveness and predictability — demand is largely non-discretionary and supported by long-run demographic tailwinds — though year-to-year volumes fluctuate with mortality (e.g. post-pandemic normalisation). As of FY25 the network spanned ~205 operating locations, including 41 cremation facilities and 9 cemeteries. Approximate scale (FY25): revenue ~A$225.8m, operating EBITDA ~A$56.2m, operating NPAT ~A$21.6m (as of June 2026, approximate, from FY25 results).

### Revenue drivers & business model

| Driver | Description | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Funeral volumes** | Number of services performed, tied to regional death rates | The base demand variable; non-discretionary but mortality fluctuates year to year |
| **Average revenue per funeral** | Pricing and service mix (e.g. cremation vs burial, prepaid plans) | The pricing lever; comparable growth signals organic strength |
| **Bolt-on acquisitions** | Acquiring independent funeral businesses and integrating them | The primary growth engine in a fragmented market — the roll-up thesis |
| **Crematoria & cemeteries** | Higher-margin owned infrastructure (41 crematoria, 9 cemeteries FY25) | Vertical integration improves margin and captures more of the value chain |

### Why death-care is defensive

Demand for funeral services is largely **non-discretionary** and **demographically underpinned** — the ageing population structurally lifts long-run deaths. This gives Propel bond-like, low-beta revenue characteristics. The trade-off is short-term volume lumpiness: mortality normalised lower after the COVID period (Australia saw a ~3% contraction in death volumes in 2H FY25), so year-to-year volumes can disappoint even within a rising long-run trend.

## 2025-2026 Developments

- **FY25 (year to 30 June 2025):** record result, ahead of guidance — revenue **+7.9% to A$225.8m** on a 4.4% rise in funeral volumes and 2.3% higher comparable average revenue per funeral; operating EBITDA **+1.4% to A$56.2m** (cash conversion ~102%); operating NPAT **+2.2% to A$21.6m**. Fully-franked final dividend of 7 cents/share. Australia saw a ~3% contraction in death volumes in 2H FY25, a headwind to industry volumes.
- **Acquisitions:** added nine locations in FY25, deploying ~A$13m across three acquisitions, with ~A$143m of funding capacity to support further roll-up.
- **Competitive position:** with InvoCare taken private by TPG Capital in 2023, Propel is the last listed pure-play funeral operator on the ASX, leaving it the default public-market exposure to the sector.

## Competitive Positioning & Peers

The Australian/NZ death-care market is fragmented, with two scaled consolidators (InvoCare and Propel) sitting above a long tail of independent operators — the structural set-up for a roll-up.

| Operator | Status | Position vs Propel |
|---|---|---|
| [[invocare]] | Private (TPG Capital, 2023) | Historically the #1 operator and Propel's main scaled rival; its delisting made Propel the sole listed pure-play |
| Independent funeral homes | Private, fragmented | The acquisition pipeline — Propel's roll-up targets |
| Service Corporation International | SCI (NYSE) | The US death-care consolidator; the global read-across for the roll-up model, not a direct AU/NZ competitor |

Propel's edge is scale in a fragmented market (purchasing power, shared infrastructure, crematoria ownership) plus the scarcity value of being the only ASX-listed pure-play. Its constraints are a finite domestic acquisition pipeline, acquisition-multiple discipline, and dependence on mortality trends it cannot control.

## Bull vs Bear Case

**Bull case**
- Demographic tailwind: ageing population lifts long-run death volumes, a structural revenue underpin.
- Fragmented market gives a long bolt-on acquisition runway, funded by ~A$143m capacity.
- Defensive, low-beta, fully-franked dividend appeal for income/defensive Australian funds.
- Scarcity premium as the only listed pure-play after InvoCare's delisting.

**Bear case**
- Post-COVID mortality normalisation pressures near-term volumes (2H FY25 ~3% contraction).
- Roll-up risk: overpaying for acquisitions or integration missteps erode returns.
- Wage and cost inflation squeeze margins in a labour-intensive service business.
- Regulatory/consumer-protection scrutiny of funeral pricing and prepaid plans.

## Valuation Framework (qualitative — consolidation / roll-up lens)

Propel is valued as a defensive consolidator, so the relevant lenses combine roll-up economics with standard value metrics (the very metrics it is used to teach in [[alfred-fundamental-analysis]]):

- **Acquisition cadence & returns** — A$ deployed per year, locations added, and the return on acquired businesses; funding capacity (~A$143m FY25) sets the runway.
- **Organic growth** — comparable volume and average-revenue-per-funeral growth, the non-acquisition engine.
- **EBITDA margin & cash conversion** — ~102% cash conversion in FY25 signals high earnings quality; margin trend reflects cost/wage pressure.
- **[[price-to-earnings-ratio|P/E]] vs sector** and **dividend yield (fully franked)** — the income/defensive frame, explicitly taught in the case studies below.
- **[[return-on-equity|ROE]] and gearing** — whether debt-funded acquisitions are accretive (see the market-share-via-borrowing discussion below).

No multiple or price target is asserted; the quantitative anchors are the FY25 figures above, drawn from the cited sources.

## Trading & Options Playbook

- **Index membership**: ASX-listed (small/mid-cap, [[asx-200]] vicinity); held in small-cap and consumer/defensive Australian funds. A low-beta defensive with bond-like, demographically underpinned demand.
- **Liquidity & options**: options liquidity is negligible; exposure is via the ordinary shares, so it trades as a buy-and-hold defensive rather than a derivatives vehicle.
- **Catalysts:** half-year and full-year results (volume, average revenue per funeral, EBITDA margin), acquisition announcements, death-rate/mortality data, and dividend declarations.
- **Correlated names:** historically [[invocare]] (now private); broadly a defensive consumer-services name, low correlation to cyclicals; global read-across to Service Corporation International (SCI).

## Capital Return & Dilution

Propel returns capital through a **fully-franked dividend** (FY25 final dividend 7 cents/share), part of its appeal to Australian income/defensive investors who value franking credits. Growth is funded by debt and, periodically, equity — so the dilution watch-item is any capital raising to fund the roll-up, weighed against the ~A$143m of existing funding capacity. Acquisition discipline (price paid vs returns) is the key determinant of whether growth is accretive to per-share value.

## Risks

- Lumpy death-volume trends (post-COVID mortality normalisation).
- Integration risk and acquisition-multiple discipline as the roll-up scales.
- Wage/cost inflation on margins in a labour-intensive service model.
- Regulatory/consumer-protection scrutiny of funeral pricing and prepaid arrangements.
- Finite domestic acquisition pipeline; growth could slow as the market consolidates.
