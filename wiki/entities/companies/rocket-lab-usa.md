Rocket Lab USA, Inc. (NASDAQ: RKLB) is an end-to-end space company that provides small-satellite launch services (the Electron rocket), spacecraft manufacturing and a broad space-systems business (satellite components, solar cells, reaction wheels, and full spacecraft). Founded in 2006 by Peter Beck and headquartered in Long Beach, California, with launch sites in New Zealand and Virginia, it is the second-most-active US orbital launch provider after SpaceX. RKLB is a mid-cap, high-beta favorite among traders playing the commercial-space theme, with the upcoming medium-lift **Neutron** rocket as its central growth catalyst.

## Business Overview

Rocket Lab runs two segments: **Launch Services** (the workhorse Electron small-lift rocket and, in development, the reusable medium-lift Neutron) and **Space Systems** (the larger and faster-growing segment — spacecraft buses, the Photon platform, satellite components and the planned in-house satellite constellation/space-applications business). The strategy is vertical integration across the space value chain, moving up from launch into higher-margin systems and eventually end-to-end space services. Customers include NASA, the US Space Force and defense agencies, and commercial constellation operators. The bull case rests on Neutron opening the much larger medium-lift market (constellation deployment, national-security launch) where SpaceX dominates.

## 2025-2026 Developments

- After a record-cadence 2025 for Electron launches, **Neutron's debut slipped to 2026** following a setback in qualification testing (a Stage-1 propellant tank rupture during a hydrostatic pressure trial). Management targeted having the first Neutron on the pad at Launch Complex 3 (Virginia) and a first launch thereafter, with realistic timing pushed toward late 2026.
- Continued backlog growth in Space Systems and defense/national-security work; Space Systems is the larger revenue contributor while launch provides visibility and brand.
- Latest annual revenue is roughly in the **hundreds of millions of dollars**, with market cap in the **several-billion-dollar** range (approximate, as of June 2026) — a valuation that prices in successful Neutron commercialization.

## Business Segments / Products

Rocket Lab is structured as two reporting segments, with a deliberate strategy of vertical integration up the space value chain — owning launch, the spacecraft, the components inside the spacecraft, and ultimately the data/services the spacecraft generate. This integration is the company's core differentiator versus pure-play launchers or pure-play component suppliers.

| Segment | What it includes | Strategic role |
|---|---|---|
| **Launch Services** | **Electron** — operational small-lift orbital rocket; **Neutron** — in-development reusable medium-lift rocket; HASTE suborbital variant; launch infrastructure at **Launch Complex 1** (Mahia, New Zealand), **Launch Complex 2 (LC-2)** and **Launch Complex 3 (LC-3)** (Wallops/Virginia) | Brand, cadence, and visibility; the gateway product. Electron anchors the dedicated small-sat niche; Neutron is the growth engine into the larger medium-lift market |
| **Space Systems** | Spacecraft buses and full satellites; the **Photon** in-space platform; satellite **components** — solar cells, **reaction wheels**, **star trackers**, radios, flight software, separation systems, propulsion, composite structures; in-house **constellation / space-applications** ambition | The larger and faster-growing revenue contributor; higher-margin recurring/component business that monetizes the rest of the space economy regardless of who launches |

**Reusability strategy.** Electron is a small, largely expendable workhorse (with partial recovery experiments). **Neutron** is designed from the outset for first-stage reusability — the cost lever needed to compete in the constellation-deployment and national-security medium-lift market that [[spacex]] currently dominates with reusable Falcon 9. Neutron success is therefore as much an economic question (per-kg cost, refurbishment cadence) as a technical one.

**Vertical integration as moat.** By acquiring and building component suppliers (solar, reaction wheels, star trackers, separation systems, radios, software), Rocket Lab can build its own spacecraft cheaply and quickly, sell components to competitors, and eventually deploy its own constellation — capturing margin at every layer rather than competing only on launch price.

## Competitive Positioning / Peers

Rocket Lab occupies an unusual position: the most credible *public* end-to-end alternative to [[spacex]], but a fraction of SpaceX's scale. It competes on two fronts — dedicated small-launch (where it leads) and the much larger medium-lift market (which it is trying to enter with Neutron).

**Small- vs medium-launch dynamics.** The dedicated small-launch market is real but capacity-constrained and price-pressured by SpaceX rideshare. The strategic prize is medium-lift, where constellation operators and national-security customers need an alternative to SpaceX — a "second source" demand that favors a credible #2. Neutron is Rocket Lab's bid to capture that, and the entire bull thesis hinges on it.

## Bull vs Bear Case

**Bull case**
- Neutron opens a far larger TAM (medium-lift, constellations, national security) and re-rates the stock if it flies and proves reusable economics.
- Space Systems already provides a real, growing, higher-margin recurring revenue base that monetizes the whole industry, not just Rocket Lab launches.
- Vertical integration is a durable moat; few competitors own the full stack from components to launch.
- The clearest *public* pure-play on the commercial-space theme besides private [[spacex]]; scarcity value plus government "second source" tailwinds.

**Bear case**
- Neutron is unproven, capital-hungry, and has already slipped; execution/delay risk is the dominant variable.
- Valuation prices in Neutron success today — disappointment compresses the multiple sharply.
- Heavy capital intensity drives recurring dilution; not yet sustainably profitable.
- [[spacex]] is a structurally cheaper, faster-iterating incumbent; competing on cost is brutal.

## Valuation Framework

RKLB is a pre-profitability, narrative-driven space name, so traditional earnings multiples don't anchor it. The qualitative levers that matter:

- **Backlog / contract pipeline** — total backlog and the Space Systems vs Launch mix signal revenue visibility; Space Systems backlog is the more durable, higher-margin component.
- **Revenue mix shift** — the market rewards a growing Space Systems share (recurring, higher margin) over launch (lumpy, project-based).
- **Neutron as embedded option value** — a large slice of the market cap is effectively an option on Neutron reaching commercial cadence and proving reusable unit economics; this option's value swings violently on milestone news.
- **Extreme EV/sales multiple** — the stock typically trades on a high, narrative-driven EV/sales basis rather than earnings; multiple compression is the key downside risk if growth or Neutron disappoints.
- **Capital intensity & path to profitability** — fabs, pads, and Neutron development consume cash; investors watch gross-margin trajectory, free-cash-flow burn, and the timeline to self-funding.

## Capital Structure & Dilution Risk

Building Neutron, fabs, and launch infrastructure is enormously capital-intensive, and Rocket Lab is not yet self-funding from operations. Like most growth-stage space companies, it finances the buildout through the capital markets — equity raises (including at-the-market / ATM programs that sell stock into strength) and convertible notes. The practical consequence for shareholders:

- **Dilution is the recurring cost of growth** — share count tends to grind higher as the company funds Neutron toward commercialization; raises often follow milestone-driven rallies.
- **Convertible debt** adds optionality/overhang and potential future dilution at conversion, while keeping near-term cash burn funded.
- **Watch the runway** — cash position vs. quarterly burn, plus any new ATM or convert announcements, are key signals; financing news typically pressures the stock short-term even when it de-risks the program.

## Risks

- **Neutron execution & delay risk** — the central variable; further slips, a failed first launch, or worse-than-expected reusable economics would hit the thesis hardest.
- **Launch failure** — an Electron or Neutron failure damages cadence, customer confidence, and the multiple.
- **Customer / government concentration** — reliance on NASA, the US Space Force, and defense programs ties revenue to federal budget cycles and procurement timing.
- **Competition from [[spacex]]** — a cheaper, faster-iterating incumbent with reusable launch and its own constellation; rideshare pricing pressures small-launch economics.
- **Capital intensity & dilution** — ongoing cash needs and share issuance (see above).
- **Valuation compression** — a narrative-/option-driven multiple is vulnerable to sharp de-rating if growth, backlog, or Neutron disappoint, or if risk appetite for speculative space names fades.
