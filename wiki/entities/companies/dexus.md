**Dexus** (ASX: DXS) is an office-anchored [[reits|REIT]] listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. Dexus is one of Australasia's largest fully integrated [[real-estate|real estate]] groups, combining direct property ownership with a substantial funds management business. The group manages an estimated A$51.5 billion of real estate and infrastructure assets, including approximately A$15.3 billion in its listed Dexus portfolio and A$36.2 billion in its funds management platform. It is the benchmark listed proxy for Australian premium-CBD office, and a core holding for [[australian-investing|Australian]] income and A-REIT investors.

## Business Overview & Segments

Dexus operates across two principal activities:

- **Listed Dexus Portfolio:** Direct and indirect ownership of office, industrial, retail, healthcare, infrastructure, alternatives and other investments, primarily in Australia and New Zealand. The portfolio is anchored by premium CBD office towers in Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane and Perth, together with a growing industrial and logistics platform and an evolving healthcare and alternatives exposure.
- **Funds Management:** Management of a suite of sector-specific and diversified wholesale and institutional real estate and infrastructure funds, connecting third-party capital with the Dexus platform's deal sourcing and asset management capability. This business was materially expanded through the 2022 acquisition of AMP Capital's real estate and domestic infrastructure equity businesses.

Dexus also has a real estate development pipeline of approximately A$11.5 billion across the listed and funds portfolios, providing visibility over future organic growth. The business is supported by more than 37,000 investors from 26 countries.

| **Development pipeline** | ~A$11.5B of projects across listed and funds portfolios | Organic growth engine and capital-recycling source; execution risk in a high-rate environment |

The strategic shape of the group is a **rent-collecting balance sheet (the listed portfolio) bolted to a capital-light funds-management engine**. The funds business earns recurring management and performance fees on third-party capital, transaction and development fees on deal flow, and lets Dexus run a larger asset footprint than its own balance sheet could fund — the structural reason it screens differently from a single-asset-class [[reits|REIT]].

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Dexus's [[economic-moat|economic moat]] is **moderate** — wider than a single-asset rent collector but narrower than the franchises that dominate their sub-sectors — and rests on a few reinforcing advantages:

- **Scale as a premium-CBD office landlord** — one of the largest owners of A-grade and premium office towers in Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane and Perth. Trophy assets are scarce, costly to replicate, and attract the highest-credit tenants, giving Dexus pricing and leasing leverage in a recovering market.
- **Integrated funds-management platform** — managing ~A$36.2B of third-party capital is the real differentiator. Institutional mandates are sticky (multi-year, with lock-ups and co-investment), generate recurring fees with little balance-sheet capital, and create a flywheel: more capital → more deal flow → more fees and development opportunity. This is a genuine [[switching-costs|switching-cost]]/relationship moat versus pure landlords.
- **Development & asset-management capability** — a ~A$11.5B pipeline and in-house development, leasing and capital-transactions teams let Dexus manufacture stock at a margin to acquisition and recycle capital, rather than only buying completed assets.

The qualifier is the office tilt. Structural work-from-home demand uncertainty, secular pressure on B-grade space, and acute cap-rate sensitivity to interest rates mean the balance-sheet portfolio has **no pricing power in a soft leasing market** and re-rates hard with bond yields. The moat lives in the funds platform and the quality of the assets, not in the office exposure per se — which is why the market has been willing to hold DXS at a persistent discount to [[net-asset-value|NTA]].

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Funds-management inflows & fee growth** — raising and deploying third-party capital is the highest-return, lowest-balance-sheet growth lever; institutional appetite returning to real assets directly lifts recurring fees.
- **Development pipeline delivery** — converting the ~A$11.5B pipeline into income-producing, valued assets at a development margin.
- **Office-value trough & recovery** — the 2023–2025 writedown cycle stabilising (HY26 office valuations +0.7%) is the inflection the market waited for; a turn from "falling" to "rising" marks re-rates the whole book and NTA.
- **Industrial / logistics tailwind** — e-commerce and supply-chain demand keeps logistics cap rates tighter and rents growing.
- **Healthcare & alternatives expansion** — diversifying away from office into structurally growing, less-cyclical property and infrastructure.
- **Rate cuts re-rating REITs** — A-REITs are long-duration; RBA easing lowers discount rates, supports valuations and narrows the [[net-asset-value|NTA]] discount.
- **Capital recycling / asset sales above book** — selling assets at or above carrying value is the cleanest proof-point that NTA is real and the discount unjustified.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Office values are troughing (HY26 valuations +0.7%, NTA up 1.6% to A$8.95) — the cyclical bottom the market feared is passing, and DXS trades at a deep discount to NTA (P/B ~0.65) that should mean-revert as values rise.
- The funds-management platform is a capital-light, recurring-fee engine that the market under-credits when it values DXS like a pure office landlord.
- Rate cuts are a direct tailwind: A-REIT duration works in reverse as yields fall, lifting valuations and shrinking the discount.
- Diversification into industrial, healthcare and alternatives reduces office dependence over time.
- Distributions (FY26 ~37.0c) are covered by AFFO (44.5–45.5c guided) with gearing toward the low end of target — income is durable.

### Bear case
- Office is structurally challenged: work-from-home permanently lowers demand, incentives stay elevated, and B/secondary grade keeps de-rating — a value trap, not a recovery.
- The persistent NTA discount may be the market correctly pricing further writedowns rather than a mispricing to harvest.
- REIT duration cuts both ways — if rates stay higher-for-longer, cap rates expand and NTA falls further.
- Funds-management fees are pro-cyclical; institutional outflows or redemptions in a downturn hit the highest-margin earnings stream.
- Development execution and gearing add risk in a high-cost-of-capital environment.

## Key Risks

- **Office structural decline / WFH** — the central existential question: a permanent step-down in office demand would impair the core portfolio and the funds platform's flagship assets.
- **Rate / cap-rate sensitivity** — as a long-duration [[reits|REIT]], DXS valuations, NTA and the share price move inversely with bond yields; cap-rate expansion is the dominant driver of the 2023–25 writedowns.
- **NTA-discount value-trap risk** — a P/B near 0.65 can persist or widen if asset sales fail to validate book values.
- **Funds-management outflows** — redemptions, weak fundraising, or fee compression would erode the capital-light earnings the bull case relies on.
- **Development execution** — cost inflation, leasing risk and timing on the ~A$11.5B pipeline.
- **Gearing in a downturn** — even at ~33.9% look-through gearing, further valuation falls raise the gearing ratio and pressure covenant headroom.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Dexus is a [[reits|REIT]], so the way it trades differs from an industrial or growth stock — statutory net profit is dominated by non-cash property revaluations (the swing from A$10.3M to A$348.5M NPAT between HY25 and HY26 was revaluation-driven), so the market values it on **distributable cash earnings and asset backing, not headline EPS or the GAAP P/E**. Mechanically:

- **FFO / AFFO over statutory earnings** — Funds From Operations (and AFFO after maintenance capex/incentives) is the cash-earnings metric the distribution is paid from. FY26 guidance of **AFFO 44.5–45.5c** and **distributions 37.0c** per security is what income investors anchor to, not the volatile statutory result.
- **NTA backing & discount to NTA** — net tangible asset backing rose to **A$8.95** per security at HY26, yet DXS trades at a **P/B around 0.65** — a deep, persistent discount to NTA. That discount frames the entire bull/bear debate: mean-reversion as office values recover versus a justified mark-down for further writedowns. **Asset sales at or above book** are the cleanest evidence on which side is right.
- **Cap-rate & rate-sensitivity** — valuations are an inverse function of capitalisation rates, which track bond yields. As a long-duration asset, DXS de-rates when yields rise (the 2023–25 writedown cycle) and re-rates when the RBA eases — REIT "duration" behaviour.
- **Distribution yield** — held substantially for income; the security trades on yield versus bonds and versus the A-REIT peer set, with the franking and after-tax yield relevant to [[australian-investing|Australian]] investors.
- **A-REIT index behaviour** — DXS moves with the S&P/ASX 200 A-REIT index (XPJ) and inversely with AU bond yields; it is the index's go-to expression of the **office** trade specifically. Beta to the broad market is moderate, but rate sensitivity is high.

The key reads for traders are therefore valuation/NTA marks at half-year (February) and full-year (August) results, RBA decisions, office vacancy/incentive data, funds-management flows, and — above all — whether assets sell at, above, or below book.

## Notable History & Milestones

Dexus was incorporated in 2004 following the spin-off of Deutsche Office Trust from Deutsche Bank's Australian property platform (formerly known as DB RREEF). Over two decades it has grown through acquisitions and internal restructures to become one of the largest landlords of premium-grade CBD office space in Australia.

## Recent Developments

For the full year ended 30 June 2025 (FY25), Dexus delivered Adjusted Funds From Operations (AFFO) of 45.0 cents per security and distributions of 37.0 cents per security. For the half year ended 31 December 2025 (HY26), Dexus reported statutory net profit after tax of A$348.5 million, compared with just A$10.3 million in HY25, reflecting a recovery in property valuations. Net Tangible Asset (NTA) backing per security rose by 14 cents, or 1.6%, to A$8.95. Office portfolio valuations increased by 0.7% on prior book values over the half, with office occupancy exceeding the Australian market average and industrial delivering strong like-for-like income growth. Dexus announced an estimated HY26 distribution of 19.3 cents per security.

### HY26 detail (reported 18 February 2026)
