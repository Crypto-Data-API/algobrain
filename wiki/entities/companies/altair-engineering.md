Altair Engineering Inc. (formerly NASDAQ: ALTR) was a provider of simulation, high-performance computing (HPC), data analytics and AI-powered engineering software (HyperWorks, RapidMiner, PBS Professional). **It no longer trades: Siemens AG completed its acquisition of Altair on 26 March 2025**, ending an eight-year run as a public company. The page is retained for historical reference and as a case study in software-sector M&A. This is a **completed-acquisition case study**, written in the past tense; ALTR is delisted and no standalone security exists.

## What the Business Was

Altair was an industrial/engineering software company spanning computer-aided engineering (CAE) simulation, high-performance computing (HPC) workload management, and data-science/[[artificial-intelligence|AI]] tooling — sold largely as recurring software licences under a distinctive "units-based" flexible licensing model.

| Product family | What it was |
|---|---|
| HyperWorks | Core CAE simulation suite (structural, fluid, electromagnetic, multiphysics) — the founding franchise |
| PBS Works / PBS Professional | HPC job scheduling and workload management for compute clusters |
| RapidMiner | Data-science and machine-learning analytics platform (acquired 2022) |
| World Programming (WPS) | SAS-language-compatible analytics compiler (acquired 2021) |
| Units-based licensing | Flexible, pooled-token licensing model — a notable commercial differentiator |

## The Siemens Acquisition (2024-2025) — The Deal

- **Announced:** 30 October 2024 — Siemens agreed to acquire Altair for **$113.00 per share in cash**, roughly **$10.6B equity value / ~$10B enterprise value**, a ~19% premium to the pre-leak price (shares had already run up on reports of strategic interest).
- **Completed:** **26 March 2025**, after shareholder and regulatory approvals. Altair became a wholly owned subsidiary of Siemens and ALTR was delisted from Nasdaq.
- **Rationale:** Siemens added mechanical/electromagnetic simulation, HPC workload management, and data-science/AI tooling to its Digital Industries Software arm (alongside the earlier Mentor Graphics EDA acquisition), creating what Siemens called "the most complete AI-powered portfolio of industrial software."
- The deal valued Altair at roughly 14x forward revenue — a benchmark multiple still cited in simulation-software M&A comps (e.g., versus Synopsys/Ansys, Cadence, Dassault Systèmes).

## Competitive Positioning at the Time

Altair was a mid-cap simulation-software vendor competing in the CAE/EDA/PLM software complex against far larger design-and-simulation peers. The deal slotted Altair into Siemens Digital Industries Software alongside the earlier Mentor Graphics EDA acquisition.

## Merger-Arb / Trading Postmortem

Siemens-Altair was an **all-cash deal** — the cleanest merger-arb structure (see [[merger-arbitrage]]):

- Post-announcement, ALTR traded at a narrow cash spread to **$113.00**, with the return driven by **time-to-close and break risk**, not acquirer-share volatility (no stock leg to hedge).
- The pre-announcement run-up on strategic-interest reports compressed the realized premium to ~19% — a reminder that arbs entering after a leak capture less of the spread.
- Spread sensitivity was to **regulatory/antitrust timeline** (cross-border, multi-jurisdiction review) and to short-rate carry on the cash position over the ~5-month pendency.
- Close was dateable (2025-03-26), with ALTR delisting from Nasdaq at completion — a hard event for arbs to size the carry against.

## Company Background

Founded in 1985 in Troy, Michigan by James Scapa (long-time CEO), Altair began in CAE simulation (HyperWorks) and expanded via 40+ acquisitions into HPC scheduling (PBS Works), IoT, and data analytics/AI (acquiring RapidMiner in 2022 and World Programming — SAS-language compatibility — in 2021). It IPO'd on Nasdaq in November 2017 at $13/share; the $113 takeout delivered roughly a 9x return over eight years. Revenue at acquisition was ~$650M/year, mostly recurring software licences, with the "units-based" flexible licensing model a notable differentiator.

## Trading Relevance (historical / read-through)

- **No direct exposure remains** — exposure is now via [[siemens]] (XETRA: SIE / OTC: SIEGY), where Altair sits inside Digital Industries Software.
- **Read-throughs:** the deal validated premium multiples for simulation/industrial-AI software; comparable listed names include Ansys (acquired by Synopsys, 2025), Cadence, Dassault Systèmes, Hexagon, Bentley Systems, and [[ptc]]. M&A speculation in this niche prices off the Siemens-Altair and Synopsys-Ansys comps.
- **Lesson for screens:** any wiki/screen rows still carrying ALTR as an active ticker should be purged — quotes after 26 March 2025 are stale.

## Lessons / Why It Was Acquired

- **Strategic premium for industrial-AI software**: Siemens paid a full multiple (~14x forward revenue) to complete an "AI-powered portfolio of industrial software," validating premium valuations across the simulation niche.
- **Consolidation wave**: Siemens-Altair and [[synopsys]]-[[ansys]] (both 2025) marked a clustering of large simulation-software takeouts — strategic buyers absorbing standalone simulation/CAE assets into broader EDA/PLM platforms.
- **Recurring-revenue quality**: ~$650M of mostly-recurring software licences with a differentiated units-based model made Altair an attractive, predictable asset for a strategic acquirer.

## Risks That Applied (historical)

- **Cross-border regulatory risk (during pendency)**: multi-jurisdiction antitrust review was the main thing standing between announcement and the 2025-03-26 close.
- **Cyclicality**: engineering/simulation software demand tracks industrial and manufacturing capex cycles.
- **Acquisition-heavy history**: 40+ acquisitions implied integration risk and goodwill on the standalone balance sheet.
- **Concentration in industrial end-markets**: exposure tied to manufacturing, automotive, and aerospace design budgets.
