Veeva Systems Inc. (NYSE: VEEV) is a leading provider of cloud-based software solutions purpose-built for the global life sciences industry, including pharmaceutical, biotech, and medical device companies. Founded in 2007 by Peter Gassner (a former [[salesforce]] and [[oracle]] executive) and Matt Wallach and headquartered in Pleasanton, California, Veeva pioneered the first cloud CRM for pharma and expanded into content management (Veeva Vault), clinical data management, and regulatory submissions. In 2021 Veeva became the first publicly traded company to convert to a public benefit corporation; the company serves over 1,400 customers (including Eli Lilly, Novartis, Merck, and Gilead Sciences), generates ~$2.7B in annual revenue, and increasingly integrates [[artificial-intelligence|AI]] into its platform for drug development workflow automation and data analytics.

## Business Overview

Veeva is the dominant vertical-SaaS provider to the life-sciences industry — a textbook example of an "industry cloud" that wins by depth of domain specialisation rather than horizontal scale. Its moat is deep regulatory/workflow specialisation and high switching costs: drug developers embed Veeva across the entire commercialisation and clinical-development lifecycle, and ripping it out would mean re-validating GxP-compliant systems with regulators (FDA/EMA), a multi-year undertaking. A pivotal strategic event was Veeva's move off the [[salesforce]] platform: it built its own **Vault CRM** to replace the legacy Salesforce-based CRM, with customer migrations underway, removing a major third-party dependency and improving long-run gross margin and platform control.

Veeva's business divides into two reporting segments, each anchored by the proprietary **Vault** platform:

### Business Segments & Products

| Segment | What it does | Representative products | Customer buyer |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Commercial Solutions** | Software for the go-to-market side of drug companies — sales-rep CRM, multichannel marketing, sample management, master data, and reference data | Veeva CRM / **Vault CRM**, Veeva Crossix (analytics), Veeva Link (reference data), Veeva OpenData, Veeva Nitro/Network | Commercial, sales, marketing, and medical-affairs orgs |
| **R&D Solutions (Development Cloud)** | Software for the science/regulatory side — clinical trials, regulatory submissions, quality, and safety | Vault Clinical (CTMS, eTMF, EDC), Vault RIM (regulatory information management), Vault QualityDocs/QMS, Vault Safety | Clinical-development, regulatory-affairs, and quality teams |

**The Vault platform** is the unifying asset: a single content-and-data cloud with built-in compliance (audit trails, e-signatures, validation) that underpins everything from a clinical-trial master file to a marketing-asset approval. Revenue is overwhelmingly **subscription** (high-visibility, multi-year contracts) with a smaller **professional services** component that is run near break-even as an enabler of subscription adoption.

## Competitive Positioning & Peers

Veeva rarely faces a single head-to-head competitor across its whole stack; instead it competes segment-by-segment against horizontal-platform giants and life-sciences specialists. Its structural advantage is that it sells *one* validated, industry-specific cloud where rivals offer either a generic platform that must be configured or a point solution that does not span the lifecycle.

| Competitor | Where it competes | Veeva's relative position |
|---|---|---|
| [[salesforce]] (CRM) | Former platform host; now a direct CRM rival as Veeva migrates to its own Vault CRM | Veeva owns the pharma-specific workflow + reference data; Salesforce is the horizontal CRM incumbent it is decoupling from |
| [[iqvia]] | Pharma data, analytics, and OneKey reference data; also a CRM/orchestration challenger | IQVIA is the data/CRO heavyweight; the two are increasingly direct rivals in data and commercial software |
| [[oracle]] (Health/Cerner, clinical) | Clinical-trial and regulatory systems; broad enterprise stack | Oracle is a generalist with healthcare assets; Veeva is the purpose-built clinical/regulatory cloud |
| Microsoft / generic clouds | Underlying infrastructure and horizontal collaboration tools | Not direct, but a "build it ourselves on Azure/365" alternative for the largest pharmas |

Newer AI-native life-sciences and biotech-software names — [[tempus-ai]], [[schrödinger]], [[recursion-pharmaceuticals]], [[exscientia]] — are adjacent rather than direct competitors; they target drug *discovery*, whereas Veeva owns *operational* commercial and clinical workflows. They trade as a thematic cohort, so sentiment can spill over.

## AI Involvement

Veeva embeds [[artificial-intelligence|AI]] across its platform — the **Veeva AI** suite (AI agents and Shortcuts) automates clinical-data review, regulatory document generation, content management and sales workflows for life-sciences customers, leveraging Veeva's structured industry data as a differentiator. Because Veeva already holds the largest validated, structured corpus of pharma commercial and clinical data, its AI thesis is "data moat → applied AI" rather than building foundation models. AI is positioned as both a productivity feature and a future pricing/upsell lever (see [[ai-healthcare]]).

## 2025-2026 Developments

- **Vault CRM migration:** the multi-year transition of customers from the Salesforce-based CRM to Veeva's own Vault CRM continued, a key revenue-retention and margin driver.
- **Veeva AI launch:** roll-out of native AI agents/applications across Commercial and R&D Cloud, positioning AI as a growth and pricing lever.
- **Durable growth and margins:** Veeva sustained ~30%+ operating margins and high subscription-revenue visibility (multi-billion-dollar annual revenue), with steady double-digit growth guidance.

## Bull vs Bear Case

**Bull case**
- Best-in-class vertical SaaS: high net revenue retention, ~30%+ operating margins, and one of the cleanest [[rule-of-40|Rule-of-40]] profiles in software.
- Vault CRM migration removes the Salesforce dependency, lifting long-run gross margin and giving Veeva full platform control.
- Underpenetrated R&D/Development Cloud TAM (clinical, regulatory, quality, safety) provides a multi-year land-and-expand runway.
- Structural data moat positions Veeva to monetise applied AI without foundation-model capex.

**Bear case**
- CRM migration execution risk: a stumble in moving customers off Salesforce could open the door for [[salesforce]] or [[iqvia]] to win back accounts.
- Concentrated end-market: revenue is tied to global pharma R&D and commercial budgets, which flex with drug-pricing policy, patent cliffs, and biotech funding.
- Premium valuation leaves little room for growth deceleration; high multiple compresses fast in risk-off software regimes.
- Direct competition with both Salesforce and IQVIA intensifies as Veeva broadens into data and analytics.

## Valuation Framework (qualitative)

Veeva is valued as a high-quality compounder, so the framework is quality- and durability-driven rather than turnaround-driven. Key qualitative lenses (no figures asserted beyond what is stated above):

- **Revenue quality:** subscription mix near 80%+ with high net revenue retention; the platform-migration to Vault CRM is the swing factor for gross-margin trajectory.
- **[[rule-of-40|Rule of 40]]:** Veeva typically scores well above 40 (double-digit growth + ~30%+ operating margin), a key reason it commands a premium multiple.
- **FCF margin & SBC:** strong free-cash-flow conversion and comparatively disciplined stock-based-compensation dilution for a software name — a differentiator versus loss-making SaaS peers.
- **Platform-migration optionality:** successful Vault CRM cutover is both a retention insurance policy and a margin tailwind; failure is the largest single re-rating risk.
- **AI/data moat premium:** the market assigns option value to Veeva monetising its structured-data advantage through Veeva AI.

## Capital Return & Dilution

Veeva is notable among SaaS names for its discipline: it is consistently GAAP-profitable, generates strong free cash flow, and has historically run **lower stock-based-compensation dilution** than typical high-growth software peers. Capital allocation has favoured reinvestment and a large net-cash balance sheet over buybacks or dividends; the low-dilution profile is a recurring part of the quality argument.
