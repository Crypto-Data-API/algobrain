**News Corporation** (Nasdaq: NWSA / NWS; ASX: NWS via CDIs) is a global media and information services company and a constituent of both the [[s-and-p-500]] (both share classes) and the [[asx-200]]. This is the canonical company page — share-class-specific fundamentals live at [[news-corp-(class-a)]] (NWSA, non-voting) and [[news-corp-(class-b)]] (NWS, voting). News Corp is a media and information services company with a diversified portfolio spanning digital real estate (REA Group and Realtor.com), financial and business information (Dow Jones / The Wall Street Journal), news media in Australia, the UK and the US, book publishing (HarperCollins), and social media content agency Storyful. Headquartered in New York, News Corp is listed on Nasdaq (NWSA - Class A; NWS - Class B) with CHESS Depositary Interests also trading on the ASX (NWS).

## Business Overview & Segments

News Corp is best understood not as a single media company but as a holding company wrapping five quite different businesses — a high-quality, recurring-revenue information franchise (Dow Jones), a dominant digital-real-estate portal franchise (via its controlling stake in [[rea-group]]), a global trade publisher (HarperCollins), and a collection of legacy print/news mastheads. The first two are the structural growth engines and account for the overwhelming majority of group value; the news-media segment is in secular decline. News Corp reports five operating segments:

| Segment | What it includes | Earnings contribution (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **Digital Real Estate Services** | [[rea-group]] (realestate.com.au, Australia, ~61% owned & separately listed on ASX: REA) and Move/Realtor.com in the United States | Largest earnings contributor and the primary growth engine; high-margin, portal-[[network-effects]] economics |
| **Dow Jones** | The Wall Street Journal, Barron's, MarketWatch, Investor's Business Daily, Factiva, Dow Jones Newswires, Dow Jones Risk & Compliance, Dow Jones Energy, and OPIS | Second growth engine; increasingly a B2B data/compliance business with sticky, recurring subscription revenue |
| **Book Publishing** | HarperCollins Publishers — global general fiction, non-fiction, children's and religious titles across print, digital and audio | Steady cash generator; cyclical with the trade-publishing market and exposed to AI-licensing optionality |
| **News Media** | News Corp Australia (The Australian, Herald Sun, Daily Telegraph, Courier Mail, Advertiser, Sunday Mail, news.com.au); News UK (The Times, The Sunday Times, The Sun, talkSPORT); the New York Post; plus the Storyful social-media content agency | Legacy segment in structural decline; advertising-cyclical, lowest-margin, the principal drag on the multiple |
| **Other** | Corporate activities and other businesses | Corporate/eliminations |

Following the sale of Foxtel to DAZN in April 2025, Subscription Video Services is no longer a core reporting segment; the fiscal 2026 outlook benefits from the repayment of approximately US$380 million of Foxtel shareholder loans. The disposal simplified the portfolio and lifted continuing-operations margins.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

News Corp's [[economic-moat|moat]] is **segment-specific** rather than company-wide — a key reason the consolidated entity trades at a conglomerate/sum-of-the-parts discount:

- **Dow Jones — wide moat.** The Wall Street Journal commands brand and subscription pricing power, but the more durable moat sits in the B2B data franchises — Factiva, Dow Jones Risk & Compliance, and OPIS (energy price benchmarks). These are embedded in customer workflows, carry high [[switching-costs|switching costs]], and generate recurring, contracted revenue that compounds — closer to a data-utility than a newspaper.
- **Digital Real Estate ([[rea-group]]) — wide moat.** REA Group's realestate.com.au is the dominant Australian property portal, protected by classic two-sided [[network-effects]]: agents list where buyers look, buyers look where listings are deepest. Portal dominance confers strong pricing power on listing depth and premiere products.
- **News Media — narrow/eroding moat.** Legacy print mastheads retain brand and political influence but face the secular decline of print advertising and circulation; the moat here is thin and shrinking.

Because the high-moat segments (Dow Jones, REA) are bundled with the low-moat legacy print business inside a Murdoch-controlled dual-class structure, the market frequently applies a **conglomerate discount** and trades the equity on **sum-of-the-parts (SOTP)** logic — the listed [[rea-group]] stake alone backstops a large share of News Corp's market value. Any "simplification" (asset spins, class collapse) is therefore a recurring catalyst.

## Notable History & Milestones

News Corporation was incorporated in 2012 and formally spun off on 28 June 2013 from the original News Corporation (which was renamed 21st Century Fox and retained television and film assets). The split separated the publishing and Australian broadcasting assets (new News Corp) from the broader media/entertainment operations (21st Century Fox — itself later largely sold to [[disney]], with [[fox-corporation-(class-b)]] retaining the remaining broadcast/news assets under continued Murdoch control).

## Recent Developments

For fiscal 2025 (year ended 30 June 2025), News Corp reported its most profitable year on a continuing operations basis, following the Foxtel sale and continued growth in Dow Jones and Digital Real Estate Services.

Q1 FY2026 (quarter ended 30 September 2025):

- Revenue of over US$2.1 billion, up approximately 2% year on year.
- Total segment EBITDA of approximately US$340 million, up approximately 5% year on year.
- Performance led by Dow Jones and Digital Real Estate Services, supported by digital and AI-related revenues (including commercial agreements with OpenAI and other large language model providers).

Q3 FY2026 (quarter ended 31 March 2026; reported 7 May 2026):

- Revenue of US$2.19 billion, up 9% year on year, led by Digital Real Estate Services, Dow Jones and Book Publishing.
- Total Segment EBITDA of US$343 million, up 18%; net income from continuing operations US$121 million, up 13%; adjusted EPS US$0.21 vs US$0.17.
- Dow Jones revenue US$619 million (+8%), with Risk & Compliance +19%, Dow Jones Energy +12% and digital advertising +13%.
- REA Group revenue rose 20% to US$325 million on Australian residential price increases, product mix, and currency tailwinds.

Strategic initiatives: News Corp has leaned into digital, data-led, and AI-adjacent businesses, with Dow Jones (particularly its subscription and data products) and Digital Real Estate Services seen as the group's structural growth engines. The Murdoch family's dual-class share structure (controlling approximately 39% of voting power) continues to be a focus of governance debate.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Dow Jones data + subscriptions** — the structural compounder: Risk & Compliance (+19% in Q3 FY2026), Dow Jones Energy (+12%), digital advertising (+13%), and recurring professional-information revenue.
- **[[rea-group]] growth** — Australian residential price increases, product/mix uplift and premiere-listing penetration; FX-translation tailwind for the AUD reporter.
- **AI content-licensing** — commercial agreements with OpenAI and other LLM providers turn News Corp's archive and live content into a new high-margin revenue line (and a defence against uncompensated scraping).
- **Book publishing** — HarperCollins cyclical recovery plus audio/digital growth and AI back-list licensing optionality.
- **Post-Foxtel margin uplift** — the DAZN disposal simplified the portfolio and lifted continuing-operations profitability; ~US$380M loan repayment aids FY2026.
- **Capital return & simplification** — buybacks, and the recurring catalyst of structural simplification (asset spins, dual-class collapse speculation) that could close the SOTP/conglomerate discount.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- **Hidden value / SOTP** — the ~61% stake in listed [[rea-group]] plus a high-quality Dow Jones data franchise plausibly exceed the whole company's market value, implying the rest is priced near zero; any simplification unlocks it.
- **Dow Jones data compounding** — B2B information (Factiva, Risk & Compliance, OPIS) is sticky, recurring, and growing — a structurally better business than the market's "newspaper" framing.
- **AI-licensing optionality** — content-licensing deals create a new, high-margin revenue stream and reframe News Corp as an AI-data supplier.
- **Simplification path** — pressure to collapse the dual-class structure or spin assets is a live re-rating catalyst.
- **Post-Foxtel quality** — cleaner, higher-margin continuing operations and disciplined capital return.

### Bear case
- **Legacy print decline** — News Media is in secular structural decline, an ongoing drag on growth and multiple.
- **Governance / dual-class discount** — Murdoch family ~39% voting control and a dual-class structure entrench a governance discount and limit minority-holder influence.
- **Conglomerate discount** — bundling high-moat (Dow Jones, REA) and low-moat (print) assets inside one controlled vehicle keeps the SOTP gap from closing absent action.
- **Cyclicality** — advertising and real-estate-transaction cycles drive REA and News Media; a property or ad downturn hits the two most-watched lines.
- **FX translation** — an AUD reporter with large USD-earning assets; AUD/USD swings move reported results independent of operations.

## Key Risks

- **Governance / dual-class control** — Murdoch family (~39% of voting power) can dominate strategic decisions; class-collapse or succession dynamics are a perennial overhang.
- **Secular print decline** — News Media advertising and circulation erosion is structural, not cyclical.
- **Real-estate-cycle exposure** — earnings lean heavily on [[rea-group]]; an Australian housing/transaction slowdown directly hits the largest profit contributor.
- **FX (USD–AUD)** — reporting-currency translation volatility on globally sourced earnings.
- **AI disruption — double-edged** — generative AI both threatens content monetisation (substitution, uncompensated scraping) and creates the licensing-revenue opportunity; the net outcome is uncertain.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

News Corp is most often valued and traded on **sum-of-the-parts** rather than headline multiples, precisely because of the segment-quality dispersion above. Per the preserved snapshot, the consolidated equity trades around a **~19.6x P/E** (P/B ~2.37x, P/S ~2.63x; see the Valuation & Returns table), but the more relevant lens is the SOTP gap between the listed [[rea-group]] stake plus Dow Jones and the group market value. Mechanically:

- **Sum-of-the-parts** — the ~61% stake in separately listed [[rea-group]] (ASX: REA) covers a large share of News Corp's market value, so NWS/REA relative-value (stub) positions are common; the residual implied value of Dow Jones + HarperCollins + News Media is the SOTP debate.
- **Share-class structure** — Class A ([[news-corp-(class-a)|NWSA]], non-voting) and Class B ([[news-corp-(class-b)|NWS]], voting) both sit in the [[s-and-p-500]]; the Murdoch family controls ~39% of Class B votes. NWSA typically trades at a small discount to NWS; the **A/B spread is a long-running pairs-trade candidate** around governance events.
- **ASX access** — CHESS Depositary Interests (NWS, NWSLV) give Australian-hours access for the [[asx-200]] constituent; the AUD-reporting overlay matters for AU investors ([[australian-investing]]).
- **Catalysts** — quarterly earnings (Feb/May/Aug/Nov), Dow Jones subscription and B2B data growth, REA results, AI content-licensing deals (e.g. OpenAI), buybacks, and any structural/simplification news.
- **Dividend / beta** — a modest dividend payer with relatively moderate beta versus high-growth media names; total return leans on SOTP-discount closure and Dow Jones/REA compounding rather than yield.
- **Correlated names** — [[rea-group]], [[fox-corporation-(class-b)]] (shared family control), [[new-york-times]], [[disney]] and the broader [[communication-services|Communication Services]] sector.
