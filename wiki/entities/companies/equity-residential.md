Equity Residential (NYSE: EQR) is one of the largest US apartment REITs, founded by Sam Zell in 1969 and a constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]]. It owns ~80,000 units concentrated in affluent coastal metros (Boston, New York, Washington DC, Seattle, San Francisco, Southern California) with a growing expansion footprint in Atlanta, Dallas, Denver, and Austin — making it a liquid trading vehicle for coastal-rent growth and the supply/demand balance in US multifamily.

## Business Overview

EQR's model is owning and operating high-quality apartment buildings for affluent renters and compounding same-store net operating income (NOI). Led by CEO Mark Parrell, the company has been rebalancing from pure coastal exposure toward Sun Belt expansion markets (including a ~$1B portfolio purchase from Blackstone in 2024) while keeping an investment-grade balance sheet (D/E 0.74x, interest coverage 4.6x per the fundamentals below). Its coastal skew means lower exposure to the Sun Belt supply glut that pressured peers in 2024-2025, but higher exposure to urban regulatory risk (rent control, eviction policy).

### How an Apartment REIT Makes Money

As an [[equity-reit]], EQR earns the spread between rental income and operating costs (same-store NOI), grows it via rent escalation and occupancy, recycles capital by selling lower-growth assets to buy or develop higher-growth ones, and passes the bulk of taxable income to shareholders as dividends (REITs must distribute ≥90% of taxable income to keep their tax pass-through status — see [[reits]]). The two levers that drive the stock are **(1) same-store NOI growth** (a function of market rent growth, occupancy, and expense control) and **(2) the cost of capital** — because the business is capital-intensive and rate-sensitive, EQR's valuation moves inversely with the [[10-year-treasury|10-year Treasury]] yield much like a long-duration bond with growing coupons.

The coastal concentration is the defining bet: those metros have **constrained new supply** (hard to build, expensive land, restrictive zoning), which supports pricing power and protected EQR from the 2024-2025 Sun Belt oversupply that hit MAA and Camden — but it concentrates **regulatory risk** (rent control, eviction moratoria, tenant-protection ballot measures in CA/NY/WA).

## 2025–2026 Developments

- **FY2025 results (reported Feb 2026):** same-store revenue +2.6%, expenses +3.7%, NOI +2.2%; the company achieved the highest full-year resident retention in its history as elevated home-buying costs kept tenants renting.
- **2026 guidance:** same-store revenue growth of 1.2%–3.2%, expense growth 3.0%–4.0%, NOI growth 0.5%–2.5% — a deceleration midpoint reflecting softer rent growth and persistent expense inflation.
- **Portfolio rotation:** continued acquisitions in expansion markets (Atlanta, Dallas, Denver) funded partly by dispositions of older coastal assets.

## Valuation Framework (REIT)

Apartment REITs are **not** valued on GAAP EPS or PE — GAAP net income is distorted by large non-cash depreciation on buildings that often appreciate. The Fundamentals block shows a ~25x PE, but the metrics that actually drive EQR's price are:

- **FFO (Funds From Operations)** — the REIT earnings proxy: net income + real-estate depreciation/amortization − gains on property sales. The market quotes EQR on a **P/FFO multiple** the way it quotes industrials on PE.
- **AFFO / FAD (Adjusted FFO / Funds Available for Distribution)** — FFO minus recurring maintenance capex and straight-line rent adjustments; the truest measure of cash available to fund the dividend. **Dividend safety is judged on the AFFO payout ratio**, not the GAAP payout ratio shown in the Fundamentals block.
- **Implied cap rate** — the market value of EQR's equity + debt, divided by forward NOI, gives the **implied cap rate** the public market is assigning the portfolio. Comparing this to **private-market transaction cap rates** for comparable Class-A apartments tells you whether EQR trades at a premium or discount to its underlying real estate (a NAV premium/discount). When public REITs trade at implied cap rates *above* private cap rates, they screen cheap to NAV and become acquisition/recycling candidates.
- **NAV (Net Asset Value)** — appraise the portfolio at market cap rates, subtract net debt: the per-share NAV anchors long-horizon value. EQR oscillating between premium and discount to NAV is a core mean-reversion signal.

What moves the multiple: **(1) the rate path** (lower 10-year yield expands P/FFO and compresses cap rates, raising NAV), **(2) same-store NOI growth expectations**, and **(3) the spread between EQR's cost of capital and acquisition cap rates** (positive spread enables accretive external growth). See [[funds-from-operations]], [[capitalization-rate|cap rate]], [[net-asset-value]], and [[reits]].

## Bull vs Bear Case

**Bull case**
- Coastal supply scarcity + high homeownership costs = durable pricing power and record tenant retention; affluent renters are sticky and credit-strong.
- Investment-grade balance sheet (D/E 0.74x, coverage 4.6x) gives a cost-of-capital advantage to fund accretive Sun Belt expansion through the cycle.
- Rate-cut cycle is a direct tailwind: lower 10-year yields compress cap rates, lift NAV, and re-rate P/FFO.
- Diversification into Atlanta/Dallas/Denver/Austin reduces single-market regulatory concentration over time.

**Bear case**
- Decelerating fundamentals: FY2026 guidance midpoint implies slower same-store revenue (1.2-3.2%) and NOI (0.5-2.5%) growth with persistent expense inflation (3-4%).
- Rate sensitivity cuts both ways — a higher-for-longer 10-year compresses the multiple and the NAV.
- Coastal regulatory risk: rent control, eviction policy, and tenant-protection ballot measures in CA/NY/WA cap rent growth and raise operating friction.
- Sun Belt expansion buys growth at the moment that cohort faces oversupply; timing/execution risk on capital recycling.

## Capital Return

As a REIT, EQR's dividend is the centerpiece of total return — the pass-through structure forces distribution of ≥90% of taxable income, and the stock is held substantially for yield. The dividend is funded from and should be measured against **AFFO** (not GAAP EPS); the company targets a sustainable AFFO payout that leaves room for maintenance capex and selective development. Secondary capital-return/allocation tools are **capital recycling** (selling older coastal assets to fund higher-growth acquisitions/development) and opportunistic buybacks when the stock trades at a meaningful discount to NAV. Dividend growth tracks same-store NOI growth, so the FY2026 deceleration implies modest near-term dividend increases.

## Risks

- **Interest-rate risk** — the dominant factor; higher 10-year yields compress P/FFO, raise cap rates, and lower NAV.
- **Regulatory risk** — rent control, eviction moratoria, and tenant-protection measures concentrated in EQR's coastal core markets.
- **Supply/demand cyclicality** — local oversupply (esp. in expansion Sun Belt markets) pressures occupancy and rent growth.
- **Expense inflation** — property taxes, insurance, payroll, and turnover costs running above rent growth squeeze NOI.
- **Execution/timing risk** — capital recycling into expansion markets at the wrong point in their cycle.
- **Refinancing risk** — though investment-grade, debt maturities reprice at higher rates if yields stay elevated.
