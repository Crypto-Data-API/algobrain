CyberArk Software Ltd. was the Israeli leader in privileged access management (PAM) and identity security, traded on NASDAQ as CYBR until it was **acquired by [[palo-alto-networks|Palo Alto Networks]] in a ~$25B cash-and-stock deal that closed on 2026-02-11** — the largest acquisition in cybersecurity history. CYBR no longer trades; the page is retained as a historical record and for merger-arbitrage case-study value.

> **M&A case study, not a live ticker.** CYBR was delisted on 11 February 2026. The trading sections below are written in the past tense around the closed [[palo-alto-networks|Palo Alto Networks]] acquisition; the stock cannot be bought or sold. Franchise exposure now runs through PANW.

## Acquisition by Palo Alto Networks (2025–2026)

- **2025-07-30:** Palo Alto Networks announced a definitive agreement to acquire CyberArk for ~$25B — each CYBR share to receive **$45.00 cash + 2.2005 PANW shares**.
- The deal cleared shareholder votes and regulatory approvals in the US, EU, UK and Israel through late 2025 / early 2026.
- **2026-02-11:** acquisition completed; CYBR delisted from NASDAQ. Palo Alto Networks subsequently took a secondary listing on the Tel Aviv Stock Exchange — trading there under the ticker "CYBR" — while remaining PANW on NASDAQ.
- Strategic rationale: adding the CyberArk Identity Security Platform gives PANW an identity pillar covering human, machine and AI-agent ("agentic") identities alongside network and cloud security.
- **Trader takeaway:** the cash+stock structure made CYBR a 2025 merger-arb position whose spread tracked PANW's share price; exposure to the franchise now runs through [[palo-alto-networks]].

## Business (Historical)

Founded 1999 in Petah Tikva, Israel, CyberArk pioneered the privileged access management category — vaulting, rotating and monitoring credentials for admin and machine accounts — and expanded into workforce identity, secrets management (Conjur), and AI-driven identity threat detection. It transitioned successfully from perpetual licenses to SaaS/subscription, with ARR crossing ~$1B in 2024-2025. Rivals included [[okta]], BeyondTrust, Delinea and platform vendors [[crowdstrike]] and Microsoft.

### Business Segments / Products (historical)

| Product area | What it was | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Privileged Access Management (PAM)** | Vault, rotate, and monitor credentials for admin / superuser accounts | The category CyberArk created; core franchise |
| **Workforce & Customer Identity** | SSO, MFA, adaptive access for employees and external users | Expansion beyond PAM into broader IAM/access management |
| **Secrets Management (Conjur)** | Machine-to-machine credentials for apps, pipelines, and cloud workloads | Critical as machine identities outnumbered human ones |
| **Machine / AI-agent Identity** | Securing non-human and "agentic" identities | The forward-looking thesis Palo Alto paid for |
| **Identity Threat Detection & Response** | AI-driven detection of identity-based attacks | Tied the platform into the broader SecOps stack |

The strategic arc: from a single-product PAM leader to a broad **Identity Security Platform** spanning human, machine, and AI-agent identities — precisely the breadth that made it a strategic acquisition target.

## Competitive Positioning / Peers (historical)

CyberArk competed across the identity-security spectrum, from privileged-access specialists to broad IAM platforms and security-platform consolidators. The competitive axis was **best-of-breed PAM depth versus broad identity/security-platform consolidation**.

| **CyberArk** | CYBR (delisted) | — | PAM category leader; broadened into full identity security |

CyberArk's moat was PAM depth, an entrenched enterprise base, and the multi-year shift to subscription/ARR — qualities that ultimately made it the centerpiece of the largest cybersecurity acquisition on record.

## Financial Profile (historical)

- Transitioned from perpetual licenses to SaaS/subscription, with **ARR crossing ~$1B in 2024-2025** (figure as stated in the historical record above).
- Recurring-revenue mix and ARR growth were the headline metrics; the subscription transition initially pressured reported revenue before re-accelerating it.
- The combination of a ~$1B+ ARR identity franchise and the strategic AI-identity angle underpinned the ~$25B valuation Palo Alto paid.

## The Palo Alto Acquisition — M&A Case Study

### Deal structure and strategic fit

- **Cash-and-stock: $45.00 cash + 2.2005 PANW shares per CYBR share**, announced 2025-07-30, ~$25B total — the largest cybersecurity acquisition in history.
- **Strategic rationale:** gives [[palo-alto-networks|Palo Alto]] an **identity pillar** to sit alongside its network and cloud security platforms, covering human, machine, and AI-agent identities — explicitly framed around "securing the AI era."
- **Acquirer fit:** consolidates identity into Palo Alto's platformization strategy, cross-selling PAM/identity into PANW's large enterprise base.

### Merger-arbitrage view (past tense)

Because the consideration was **cash-and-stock**, the merger-arb spread was *not* a simple fixed-cash spread: the stock component (2.2005 PANW shares) meant the deal value floated with PANW's share price, so arbitrageurs typically **shorted PANW** to hedge the stock leg and isolate the spread plus deal-completion risk. Approvals spanned the US, EU, UK and Israel, adding multi-jurisdiction antitrust timing risk through late 2025 / early 2026. The position resolved at the 2026-02-11 close. See [[merger-arbitrage]] for the hedging mechanics of cash-and-stock deals.

### Contrast with an all-cash deal

This cash-and-stock structure stands in contrast to the all-cash [[confluent]]/[[ibm|IBM]] deal that closed weeks later: an all-cash spread is a clean time-to-close trade, whereas CYBR's stock leg required dynamically hedging the acquirer's equity. Both were 2025–2026 strategic-software takeovers, but they demanded different arb playbooks.

## Trading Relevance (historical / routing)

- **No longer tradable** — identity-security exposure now routes through [[palo-alto-networks]] (acquirer) or listed identity/security peers ([[okta]], [[sailpoint]], [[crowdstrike]], [[zscaler]]).
- **Index / ETF note:** when listed, CYBR was a constituent of cybersecurity ETFs such as the [[cibr-first-trust-nasdaq-cybersecurity-etf|First Trust NASDAQ Cybersecurity ETF (CIBR)]] and HACK; those funds rebalanced it out at the February 2026 delisting (PANW remains a constituent).
- **Lesson for the playbook:** entrenched, ARR-scaled security specialists with an "AI-era" angle became prime consolidation targets for platform vendors; the cash-and-stock structure required hedging the acquirer's stock to lock the spread.

## Bull vs Bear Case (as it stood pre-deal)

**Bull case (pre-deal)**
- PAM category leader with deep enterprise entrenchment and high switching costs.
- Successful subscription transition with ARR past ~$1B and re-accelerating growth.
- Strong forward narrative in machine and AI-agent identity — the structural growth in non-human identities.

**Bear case (pre-deal)**
- Bundling pressure from [[microsoft]] Entra and platform consolidators ([[crowdstrike]], [[palo-alto-networks]]) able to fold identity into broader suites.
- Israel-headquartered with geopolitical/operational risk considerations.
- Premium valuation typical of high-growth security software, exposed to multiple compression.

## Capital Return / Dilution (historical)

CyberArk paid no dividend and was a growth-stage security name; like most high-growth software, it carried meaningful **stock-based compensation** dilution. The cash-and-stock acquisition crystallized value for holders (a cash component plus PANW equity), converting CYBR shareholders into partial PANW shareholders rather than returning capital via buyback.

## Risks (resolved by the close)

The pre-deal risks — platform-bundling competition, valuation, and geopolitical exposure — were superseded once the merger was announced; the residual risk was deal completion across four jurisdictions (US, EU, UK, Israel), which cleared by the 2026-02-11 close. The remaining exposure for former holders is now PANW-specific.
