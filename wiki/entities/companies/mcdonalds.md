McDonald's (NYSE: MCD) is the world's largest restaurant company by systemwide sales, a ~95%-franchised royalty-and-rent machine spanning 40,000+ restaurants, and a Dow Jones Industrial Average and [[s-and-p-500]] component. For traders it is a defensive consumer bellwether: its comps are a real-time read on low-income consumer health and value-seeking behavior.

## Business Overview & Segments

McDonald's is fundamentally a **franchise royalty and real-estate company wrapped in a burger brand**. Roughly 95% of its ~43,000 restaurants are operated by franchisees, so the corporate P&L is dominated by two contractually durable streams: a percentage-of-sales **royalty** and **rent** on property McDonald's owns or master-leases. This is why corporate operating margins sit near ~45% — far above any company-operated restaurant peer — and why the cash flows are unusually defensive: franchisees bear the labor, food-cost, and unit-economics risk, while McDonald's collects a top-line-linked toll. Revenue is reported across three geographic segments.

| Segment | What it is | Mix / role (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **U.S.** | Largest, most mature market; the value-war and comps bellwether | Largest profit contributor; the segment traders watch for low-income-consumer health |
| **International Operated Markets (IOM)** | Company-significant wholly/majority operated markets — UK, France, Germany, Canada, Australia | Second growth engine; FX-sensitive |
| **International Developmental Licensed & Corporate (IDL)** | Predominantly licensed markets — Japan, China, Latin America, Middle East — run by local partners | Highest-margin, lowest-capital; pure royalty stream |

A balance-sheet note carried through the fundamentals: **negative book equity** (which makes the headline ROE and debt-to-equity figures look alarming) is deliberate engineering, not distress — decades of debt-funded buybacks against contractually stable franchise cash flows have driven equity below zero. Read interest coverage (~7-8x) and free cash flow, not D/E. McDonald's is a dividend aristocrat that has raised its payout every year since 1976; the dividend-per-share/yield rows in the fundamentals table below carry an obvious API unit anomaly and should be ignored in favor of the real ~2%+ market yield.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

McDonald's holds one of the widest [[economic-moat|economic moats]] in [[consumer-discretionary|consumer discretionary]], resting on reinforcing pillars rather than a single edge:

- **Brand & scale** — one of the most recognized brands on earth, with an advertising and marketing budget no QSR rival can match, spread across a systemwide-sales base measured in the tens of billions.
- **Real-estate ownership** — McDonald's owns or controls the land and buildings under a large share of its restaurants. This converts the franchise relationship into a landlord relationship, deepens [[switching-costs|switching costs]] for franchisees, captures real-estate appreciation, and provides rent that is far stickier than royalties alone.
- **Franchise unit economics** — the ~95%-franchised, asset-light royalty model produces high returns on capital and lets McDonald's grow units with franchisee capital rather than its own.
- **Supply-chain cost advantage** — purchasing scale on beef, potatoes, chicken, packaging, and equipment gives per-unit input costs competitors cannot match, funding the value menu without crushing margins.
- **Network density & convenience** — drive-thru density, mobile-app ordering, loyalty, and delivery integration create a convenience moat that is hard to replicate at scale.

The moat is wide and durable but not frictionless: the brand must continually defend value perception against grocery-at-home substitution and nimbler competitors, and franchisee profitability is the constraint that disciplines how aggressively corporate can push value pricing.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Value platform (McValue)** — the January 2025 formalization of the $5 Meal Deal into a permanent value menu to win back price-sensitive traffic after 2023-24 "too expensive" perception damage; the central lever on comps.
- **Unit growth** — roughly 2,600 new restaurants in 2026 on the path toward a **50,000-restaurant target by end-2027**, contributing ~2.5% to systemwide-sales growth.
- **Digital, delivery & loyalty** — app ordering, the loyalty program, and delivery integration drive frequency, average check, and first-party data.
- **Chicken & beverages** — menu innovation (McCrispy, beverage/CosMc's-style concepts) to broaden occasions beyond the core burger daypart.
- **International developmental licensing** — high-margin, low-capital royalty expansion in emerging markets.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly global and U.S. comps prints, low-income consumer data, beef/commodity cost trends, FX, GLP-1 demand narratives, and value-war headlines versus peers.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Asset-light, ~95%-franchised royalty-and-rent model throws off durable, high-margin, inflation-linked cash flow with low capital intensity.
- Real-estate ownership adds a landlord's stickiness and appreciation on top of the franchise toll.
- Dividend aristocrat (raises since 1976) with relentless buybacks; a quality compounder for income and defensive mandates.
- Value platform plus digital/loyalty drives traffic without crushing franchisee economics; unit growth toward 50,000 adds a steady systemwide-sales tailwind.
- Global scale and supply-chain cost advantage are nearly impossible to replicate.

### Bear case
- Mature U.S. market; growth leans on value pricing that pressures franchisee margins, the system's true constraint.
- Grocery-at-home trade-down and a stretched low-income consumer can stall traffic.
- GLP-1 anti-obesity drugs are a long-dated demand overhang for the QSR category.
- Negative book equity and a debt-funded buyback model leave little balance-sheet cushion if cash flows wobble; rate-sensitive.
- Premium valuation for a low-single-digit-growth business; any comp disappointment de-rates the multiple.

## Key Risks

- **Low-income consumer weakness** — the customer base skews value-seeking; a squeezed low-end consumer hits traffic first.
- **Franchisee margin pressure** — aggressive value pricing plus labor and food-cost inflation can erode franchisee profitability, the lever that disciplines the whole system.
- **Commodity & labor inflation** — beef, chicken, potatoes, packaging, and wage costs flow through to franchisees and value-menu math.
- **Trade-down / substitution** — grocery-at-home competition and fast-casual share gains at the margins.
- **GLP-1 demand shift** — anti-obesity drugs as a structural, long-dated overhang on QSR consumption.
- **FX & balance sheet** — roughly half of operating income is international (FX drag); negative book equity and a leveraged, buyback-funded capital structure add rate sensitivity.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

McDonald's trades as a defensive **quality compounder** — a premium multiple (low-to-mid 20s trailing P/E in the data below) justified by the durability and predictability of the franchise royalty-and-rent stream rather than by growth. Mechanically:

- **Low beta** versus the [[s-and-p-500]] — a classic risk-off rotation beneficiary; it tends to hold up when cyclicals sell off.
- **Dividend aristocrat** — annual increases since 1976; the ~2%+ yield plus steady buybacks is the core total-return story (ignore the API dividend-unit anomaly in the fundamentals table).
- **Index membership** — a Dow Jones Industrial Average and [[s-and-p-500]] component; a top holding of dividend and low-volatility ETFs.
- **Earnings behavior** — the stock trades on global and U.S. comps versus expectations more than on the headline EPS; value-war commentary and franchisee-margin signals drive the reaction.
- **Capital return** — dividends plus buybacks (funded against the franchise cash flows) are the return engine; the negative book equity is a feature of that strategy, not a red flag.

## Notable History & Milestones

| Year | Milestone |
|---|---|
| 1940 | Richard and Maurice McDonald open their original restaurant in San Bernardino, California |
| 1948 | Brothers introduce the "Speedee Service System" — the fast-food assembly-line model |
| 1955 | Ray Kroc opens his first franchise and founds McDonald's Corporation; the franchise era begins |
| 1961 | Kroc buys out the McDonald brothers; Harry Sonneborn builds the **real-estate model** that becomes the profit engine |
| 1965 | IPO on the New York Stock Exchange |
| 1976 | Begins its unbroken streak of annual dividend increases (dividend aristocrat) |
| 1985 | Added to the Dow Jones Industrial Average |
| 2003 | "Plan to Win" turnaround under Jim Cantalupo revives comps after a slump |
| 2015 | All-day breakfast and the "Experience of the Future" refranchising/remodel push |
| 2018-2020 | Heavy refranchising lifts the franchised mix to ~95%; digital, delivery, and mobile app scale up |
| 2025 | Launches the permanent **McValue** platform; restaurant count tops 43,000 en route to a 50,000 target |
| 2025-26 | Value strategy drives U.S. and global comp recovery; stock reaches all-time highs |
