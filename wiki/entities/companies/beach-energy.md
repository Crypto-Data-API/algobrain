**Beach Energy Limited** (ASX: BPT) is an Australian independent [[oil]] and gas exploration and production ([[energy|E&P]]) company listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. It is one of the largest suppliers of natural gas to Australia's structurally short east coast and runs the country's largest onshore oil business on the Cooper Basin's Western Flank. Based in Adelaide, it operates or holds interests across the Cooper/Eromanga, Otway, Bass, Perth and Taranaki (New Zealand) basins, with Kerry Stokes' [[seven-group-holdings|Seven Group Holdings]] as its anchor shareholder. For a trader, Beach is a mid-cap commodity price-taker whose earnings track [[oil|Brent crude]] and east-coast gas prices, partly buffered by contracted gas volumes.

## Business Overview & Segments

Beach's portfolio spans operated and non-operated, onshore and offshore [[oil]] and gas activities across five basins. Gas is the strategic core — Beach is a major supplier into the structurally tight east-coast domestic market and into export tolling via the Perth Basin — while the Cooper Basin Western Flank provides high-margin conventional [[oil]]. Products include natural gas, liquefied natural gas (LNG) via tolling arrangements, liquefied petroleum gas (LPG), condensate and crude oil.

| Basin | Role | Products |
|---|---|---|
| **Cooper / Eromanga** (SA / QLD) | JV partner with [[santos|Santos]] in the Moomba gas project; operates Australia's largest onshore oil production on the Western Flank | Crude oil, gas, condensate, LPG |
| **Otway** (VIC / SA) | Operated and non-operated gas fields (incl. offshore, e.g. Enterprise) supplying eastern Australia | Natural gas, condensate |
| **Bass** (VIC / TAS) | Operated gas and liquids supply to Tasmania and the east coast | Natural gas, liquids |
| **Perth** (WA) | Interest in the Waitsia Gas Project (operated alongside Mitsui) and operated Beharra Springs gas field; gateway to LNG export tolling | Natural gas, LNG (tolling), condensate |
| **Taranaki** (New Zealand) | Operated and non-operated production | Gas, oil, condensate |

The franchise mixes a stable, contracted east-coast gas book with cyclical oil and condensate revenue. The Western Flank conventional oil is low-cost and short-cycle; the east-coast and Perth gas positions tie Beach's economics to domestic gas scarcity and the Waitsia export question rather than purely to global commodity prices.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Like every upstream [[energy|E&P]] producer, Beach is a **price-taker**: it has no control over the [[oil|Brent crude]] price or, ultimately, the east-coast gas price, and therefore no pricing-power [[economic-moat|moat]] in the way a branded or network business does. Its durable advantages are about **asset position and market access**, not pricing:

- **Low-cost conventional acreage** — the Cooper Basin Western Flank is mature, well-understood conventional [[oil]] with short cycle times and competitive lifting costs; this keeps Beach near the lower end of the cost curve and resilient through low-price periods.
- **Infrastructure ownership and JV positions** — equity in the Moomba processing hub (with [[santos|Santos]]) and operated Otway/Bass gas plants and pipelines create entrenched positions that are expensive and slow for newcomers to replicate. Owning the midstream is a structural advantage in a basin with finite processing capacity.
- **Proximity to a structurally short market** — Beach's gas sits next to a southern east-coast market facing chronic supply tightness as legacy fields decline. Being a domestic supplier with existing connections is itself an advantage; transport economics favour incumbent local gas over imported LNG.
- **Contracted gas revenue stability** — term gas contracts smooth a meaningful slice of revenue, dampening the pure [[oil]]-price beta that dominates pure-play crude producers.

The offsets are equally structural: reserves deplete and must be continually replaced, the WA gas-reservation framework can cap the value of the Perth Basin gas, and the moat does nothing to protect against a sustained commodity-price downturn.

## History

Beach was established in 1961 as Beach Petroleum and expanded slowly through the 1960s-1980s in the Cooper Basin. The company was revitalised under Kerry Stokes' [[seven-group-holdings|Seven Group Holdings]] and its late-2000s change of strategy and name: it became Beach Energy Limited in December 2009. Over the following decade Beach grew substantially via acquisitions, most notably the 2018 acquisition of Lattice Energy (ex-Origin Energy E&P business) for A$1.585 billion, which added the Otway, Bass and Perth Basin businesses, and the 2016 acquisition of Drillsearch. [[seven-group-holdings|Seven Group Holdings]] remains Beach's largest shareholder. Beach is a component of the S&P/[[asx-200]] index and was the second-largest Australian [[oil]] producer in 2020.

## Recent Developments

For FY25 (year to 30 June 2025) Beach reported underlying EBITDA up 20% to A$1.1 billion, underlying NPAT up 32% to A$451 million and pre-growth free cash flow of A$657 million, funding a record final fully franked dividend of 6.0 cents per share for total FY25 dividends of 9.0 cents. For H1 FY26 Beach reported underlying EBITDA of A$558 million (down 5%) and underlying NPAT of A$219 million (down 8%). Highlights included first gas from the Waitsia Gas Plant in early December 2025, now in ramp-up with two sales gas compressors commissioned and peak rates of 165 TJ/d; a third compressor was commissioning with a target nameplate capacity of 250 TJ/d. CEO Brett Woods continues to engage with the WA Government on whether Waitsia gas exports will be permitted beyond 2028 under the state's gas reservation framework.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Waitsia ramp-up** — first gas flowed December 2025; the plant is ramping toward a 250 TJ/d nameplate with a third compressor commissioning. The ramp is the single largest near-term production and cash-flow driver.
- **WA gas-reservation / export decision** — a **binary policy catalyst**: whether Waitsia gas exports will be permitted beyond 2028 under WA's domestic gas-reservation framework materially changes the project's value. CEO Brett Woods continues to engage the WA Government.
- **Structurally tight east-coast gas** — chronic southern-market supply scarcity as legacy fields decline supports domestic gas prices and Beach's contracted positions.
- **[[oil|Brent]] price** — the swing factor for Western Flank oil and condensate liquids revenue.
- **Operational cadence** — quarterly production and reserves updates; reserve replacement; cost performance.
- **Capital management** — dividend and buyback decisions, influenced by the [[seven-group-holdings|Seven Group Holdings]] anchor shareholding.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Waitsia ramp and the tight east-coast gas market lift production and cash flow into a structurally short market.
- Contracted gas volumes buffer revenue, giving Beach more stability than a pure [[oil]] producer.
- Underlying FY25 EBITDA of ~A$1.1B and strong free cash flow show the operating business is healthy beneath the non-cash impairments.
- A very low net-debt/balance sheet (D/E ~0.07x) gives resilience and capital-return capacity through the cycle.
- Favourable WA export-policy outcome would unlock additional Waitsia value.

### Bear case
- [[oil]] and gas are cyclical price-takers; a sustained price downturn hits earnings and the dividend directly.
- Recent statutory losses and impairments signal that some assets disappointed; reserve replacement is a continuous capital cost.
- The WA gas-reservation decision could **cap** Waitsia export upside (downside-skewed binary).
- Project-execution risk on the Waitsia ramp to full nameplate.
- [[seven-group-holdings|Seven Group]]'s large stake concentrates key-holder influence over strategy and capital allocation.

## Key Risks

- **Commodity-price cyclicality** — direct exposure to [[oil|Brent]] and east-coast gas prices; no pricing power.
- **Impairment / earnings volatility** — recent non-cash impairments drove statutory losses; statutory EPS understates underlying cash generation.
- **WA reservation policy** — the export-beyond-2028 decision is an unpredictable, value-relevant policy risk.
- **Execution** — delivering the Waitsia ramp to 250 TJ/d nameplate.
- **Reserves & decline** — mature basins require ongoing investment to replace produced reserves.
- **Key-shareholder dynamics** — [[seven-group-holdings|Seven Group Holdings]]' anchor position shapes board and capital decisions.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Beach is a liquid mid-cap [[asx-200]] [[energy]] name with exchange-traded options. Mechanically, it trades as a commodity price-taker:

- **Commodity beta** — the share price tracks [[oil|Brent]] for its liquids and east-coast domestic gas pricing; the contracted gas book damps but does not remove the cyclicality.
- **Earnings optics** — the negative *trailing* P/E and recent losses (see the data and Fundamentals blocks) reflect **non-cash impairments**, not operating distress. Underlying FY25 EBITDA (~A$1.1B), underlying NPAT (~A$451M) and free cash flow are the better read on the business.
- **Dividend** — pays a franked dividend (FY25 total 9.0c, including a record 6.0c final), an income component on top of the commodity beta; the gross-up benefits Australian-resident holders.
- **Balance sheet** — a very low D/E (~0.07x) gives unusual resilience for a cyclical producer and supports capital return through the cycle.
- **Correlated names** — [[santos|Santos]] (Cooper JV partner and gas peer), [[woodside-energy|Woodside Energy]] and [[ampol|Ampol]]; broadly moves with Brent and east-coast gas.
