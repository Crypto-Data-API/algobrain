Amcor plc (NYSE: AMCR, ASX: AMC) is one of the world's largest consumer and healthcare packaging companies, formed in its modern shape by the 2019 Bemis acquisition and the April 2025 US$8.4 billion all-stock merger with Berry Global. It is dual-listed — a constituent of both the [[s-and-p-500]] and the [[asx-200]] — making it a liquid, defensive packaging play with an unusually high dividend yield for the [[materials|Materials]] sector.

## Business Model & Segments

Amcor operates through two reporting segments: Global Flexible Packaging Solutions and Global Rigid Packaging Solutions. Flexible Packaging develops polymer resin, aluminium and fibre-based flexible packaging for food, beverage, medical and pharmaceutical, fresh produce, snack, confectionery and personal care customers, and is a global business with significant scale across all regions. Rigid Packaging manufactures rigid plastic containers, closures, and dispensing and pharmaceutical devices primarily for beverage and food applications and, following Berry, now includes a much larger healthcare-device and engineered-materials business. Amcor sells directly via its own sales force and has identified approximately US$650 million in synergies from the Berry integration.

## History

Amcor's lineage runs from the 1860s paper and paperboard manufacturing operations in Australia (Australian Paper Manufacturers), with the modern Amcor Limited incorporated in 1926. Over the 20th century Amcor expanded into packaging converting, acquired Alcan's global packaging business (2010) and demerged Orora (paper and Australasian packaging) in 2013 to focus on global flexibles and rigids. In 2019 Amcor redomiciled to Jersey and rebranded as Amcor plc after its US$6.8 billion all-stock acquisition of Bemis Company. In November 2024 Amcor announced an US$8.4 billion all-stock merger with Berry Global; the transaction closed on 30 April 2025, creating a global consumer and healthcare packaging leader, with legacy Amcor and Berry shareholders owning roughly 63% and 37% of the combined company respectively.

## Recent Developments

Fiscal 2025 (year ended 30 June 2025) net sales were US$15.01 billion with GAAP net income of US$511 million, adjusted EBIT of US$1.72 billion, adjusted EPS of 71.2 cents and adjusted free cash flow of US$926 million. The first full combined quarter after the Berry close (Q1 FY26, to 30 September 2025) delivered net sales of US$5.75 billion, up 68% year-on-year reflecting the merger. For FY26 Amcor is guiding to adjusted EPS of 80-83 cents (constant-currency growth of 12-17%), including roughly US$260 million of pre-tax Berry synergies and adjusted free cash flow of US$1.8-1.9 billion after approximately US$220 million of integration and transaction costs. Amcor is also trimming its portfolio of non-core assets to focus on higher-margin businesses.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Amcor's [[economic-moat|moat]] sits in the [[materials|Materials]] sector but behaves more like a defensive consumer-supplier franchise. The durable advantages are: (1) **scale** — one of the world's two largest consumer-packaging companies, with global manufacturing footprint that few customers can replicate and that lets Amcor serve multinational FMCG accounts on every continent; (2) **switching costs and customer integration** — packaging is co-engineered into customers' production lines and brand specifications, so qualification, validation and re-tooling create [[switching-costs|switching costs]], especially in regulated **healthcare** and pharmaceutical packaging (the strategic prize of the Berry merger); and (3) **cost pass-through** — resin and raw-material costs are largely passed to customers with a lag, smoothing through-cycle margin. The moat is **narrow-to-medium**: packaging is ultimately a competitive, capital-intensive, commodity-input industry where pricing power is limited and differentiation comes from scale, service and sustainability/recyclability innovation rather than a unique product.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Berry synergies** — ~US$650m of identified synergies (≈US$260m pre-tax captured in FY26) is the dominant near-term earnings lever.
- **Healthcare packaging** — the Berry deal materially expands higher-margin medical-device and pharmaceutical packaging, a defensive, regulated growth pool.
- **Portfolio high-grading** — divesting non-core Berry assets to lift group margin and returns.
- **Sustainability/recyclability** — demand for recyclable and lighter-weight packaging as brand owners chase ESG targets; a differentiation and volume driver.
- **Cost pass-through & deleveraging** — resin-cost recovery plus debt reduction from free cash flow supports EPS and the dividend.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly earnings (June fiscal year-end), Berry synergy and integration updates, resin/raw-material cost commentary, dividend declarations and divestment news.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Berry merger creates a global consumer-and-healthcare packaging leader with ~US$650m synergy runway and a higher-margin healthcare tilt.
- Defensive, consumer-staples-like end-demand (food, beverage, medical) with reliable volumes through the cycle.
- High dividend yield (unusual for [[materials|Materials]]) backed by strong adjusted free cash flow (FY26 guide US$1.8-1.9bn).
- FY26 guidance for 12-17% constant-currency adjusted EPS growth as synergies land and integration costs roll off.

### Bear case
- Post-merger leverage is elevated (D/E ~1.19x, interest coverage ~2.5x), making the stock rate-sensitive and the dividend less cushioned.
- Statutory FY25 results were weak (GAAP net margin negative, ROE near zero) — the bull case rests entirely on adjusted/synergy delivery.
- Packaging is commodity-input, capital-intensive and price-competitive; limited pricing power versus large FMCG customers.
- Integration risk on a US$8.4bn merger; resin-cost spikes can outrun pass-through in the short run.

## Key Risks

- **Leverage / rate sensitivity** — D/E ~1.19x and interest coverage ~2.5x mean higher rates pressure earnings and capital return.
- **Integration risk** — delivering Berry synergies and divestitures on schedule is the whole adjusted-EPS thesis.
- **Input-cost volatility** — resin and aluminium price swings; pass-through lags can compress margin.
- **Customer concentration & power** — large multinational FMCG customers have negotiating leverage.
- **Regulatory / ESG** — plastics regulation, single-use-plastic bans and recyclability mandates reshape demand and require capex.
- **FX translation** — dual listing and global footprint expose reported AUD/USD results to currency moves.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Amcor is a **low-beta, income-oriented defensive** in the [[materials|Materials]] sector that the market values on adjusted EPS and free cash flow / dividend yield rather than the statutory (impairment- and merger-distorted) headline P/E. Mechanically:

- **Dual listing** — [[s-and-p-500]] (AMCR, NYSE — the deeper line) and [[asx-200]] (AMC, settling in AUD via CDIs); index rebalances on either exchange and cross-market flows move the stock.
- **Low beta / defensive** — consumer-staples-like demand makes it a relative-safety holding, but post-merger leverage adds rate sensitivity atypical of a pure defensive.
- **High dividend yield** — unusually high for [[materials|Materials]]; the dividend and FCF coverage are central to the holder base, and the stock supports covered-call income strategies.
- **Catalyst-light, grind-y** — moves on synergy updates, guidance and resin commentary rather than dramatic single events; options are liquid enough for income but far thinner than megacaps.
