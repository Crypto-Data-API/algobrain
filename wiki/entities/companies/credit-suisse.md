**Credit Suisse Group AG** was a Swiss multinational investment bank and financial services company founded in 1856, and for over 160 years one of the two pillars of Swiss banking alongside [[ubs|UBS]]. It collapsed in March 2023 in a crisis of confidence following years of risk-management failures, scandals and losses, and was absorbed by UBS in an emergency, government-brokered rescue — the most significant bank failure since the 2008 financial crisis and a defining case study in how a globally systemic bank can die from reputational and liquidity erosion rather than a single fatal trade. It is referenced in [[itpm-god-like-trader-status|ITPM]] content as a cautionary tale about institutional risk-management failure.

## Business Overview

Founded in 1856 by Alfred Escher to finance the build-out of the Swiss rail network, Credit Suisse grew into a global universal bank organised around wealth management, a large investment bank, the Swiss domestic bank, and asset management. At its peak it was a designated global systemically important bank (G-SIB) with operations across the Americas, Europe and Asia, and was a fixture in prime brokerage, leveraged finance, equities and fixed income. Its private-banking franchise managed money for ultra-high-net-worth clients worldwide, but the investment bank repeatedly generated the outsized risk events that ultimately destroyed the firm.

## The road to collapse (2021-2023)

Credit Suisse's failure was a slow-motion erosion of trust caused by a sequence of self-inflicted disasters:

- **Archegos Capital Management (March 2021).** The implosion of Bill Hwang's family office cost Credit Suisse roughly US$5.5 billion — by far the largest loss of any prime broker exposed to Archegos — exposing catastrophic failures in counterparty risk management and margining. (See [[archegos-capital-management]].)
- **Greensill Capital (2021).** The collapse of supply-chain finance firm Greensill forced Credit Suisse to freeze and wind down ~US$10 billion of client funds, generating litigation and reputational damage.
- **Chronic scandals.** Spying on executives, a money-laundering conviction (Bulgarian cocaine network), the "Suisse Secrets" leak, and repeated regulatory fines compounded the loss of confidence.
- **2022 losses and outflows.** A full-year 2022 net loss of roughly CHF 7.3 billion and accelerating client withdrawals (over CHF 100 billion of net asset outflows in Q4 2022) left the bank structurally weakened.
- **The final run (March 2023).** Amid the US regional-banking panic (Silicon Valley Bank), the Saudi National Bank — Credit Suisse's largest shareholder — publicly said it would not provide more capital. The stock cratered, deposits fled, and despite a CHF 50 billion liquidity backstop from the Swiss National Bank, the run could not be stopped.

## The UBS rescue (March 2023)

On 19 March 2023, the Swiss government, FINMA and the Swiss National Bank brokered an emergency takeover by [[ubs|UBS]] for roughly CHF 3 billion in stock (about CHF 0.76 per share, a fraction of the prior close). The deal's most controversial feature was the write-down to zero of approximately CHF 16 billion of Credit Suisse Additional Tier 1 (AT1) contingent-convertible bonds while equity holders still received some value — inverting the normal creditor hierarchy and triggering lawsuits and lasting repricing of the entire AT1/CoCo market. The merger formally completed in 2024, ending the Credit Suisse brand.

## Trading lessons

- **Liquidity, not solvency, kills banks.** Credit Suisse was nominally solvent and capitalised to regulatory standards yet still failed in days once depositors and counterparties lost faith — the textbook modern [[bank-run]].
- **Reputation is balance-sheet capital.** A drumbeat of scandals raised the firm's funding costs and primed clients to flee at the first shock.
- **Counterparty risk is asymmetric.** The Archegos loss showed how a single under-margined client can dwarf years of prime-brokerage profit.
- **AT1/CoCo subordination is not guaranteed.** The wipe-out repriced bank capital instruments globally and is now standard reading on capital-structure risk.
- **Contagion windows.** The collapse landed inside the March 2023 US regional-bank panic, illustrating how correlated stress propagates across a fragile system ([[too-big-to-fail]]).

## Trading Relevance

Credit Suisse shares (formerly NYSE: CS, SIX: CSGN) no longer trade — the entity is part of UBS. For traders the legacy is analytical: CS is the canonical worked example of bank-failure mechanics and a recurring reference when stress-testing exposure to large financials. Holders of UBS now carry the integration risk and benefit of the combined Swiss banking champion. The episode also permanently raised the risk premium on bank AT1 debt.
