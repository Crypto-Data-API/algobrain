The Australian banking sector is dominated by four major banks — [[commonwealth-bank|Commonwealth Bank (CBA)]], [[westpac|Westpac (WBC)]], [[nab|National Australia Bank (NAB)]], and [[anz|ANZ]] — collectively known as the "Big Four." These four institutions hold a combined market capitalisation exceeding AUD $450 billion, control approximately 80% of the Australian mortgage market, and are among the most profitable banks in the world relative to GDP.

## Overview

Australia's banking system is an oligopoly — four banks dominate lending, deposits, and retail financial services in a country of 26 million people. This concentration makes the sector highly profitable but also heavily regulated by [[apra|APRA]], [[asic|ASIC]], and [[austrac|AUSTRAC]]. The Big Four banks are cornerstones of Australian investment portfolios, paying consistently high, fully [[franking-credits|franked]] dividends that deliver after-tax yields well above global peers.

The banking sector represents roughly 25-30% of the [[asx-200|S&P/ASX 200]] index by market capitalisation, making it impossible to invest in Australia without significant bank exposure — whether you want it or not.

### Four Pillars Policy

The "Four Pillars" policy is an informal but binding Federal Government policy that prevents the Big Four banks from merging with each other. Introduced by Treasurer Peter Costello in 1997, the policy has survived multiple government changes and ensures competitive tension remains between the four major banks. The policy does not prevent mergers with smaller banks — as demonstrated by [[anz|ANZ's]] acquisition of [[suncorp|Suncorp Bank]] in 2024.

### Why Banks Dominate Australian Portfolios

The Big Four banks are the most widely held stocks in Australia for several reinforcing reasons:

1. **Fully franked dividends**: All four pay fully [[franking-credits|franked]] dividends, typically yielding 4-6%. For investors in the 0% tax bracket (retirees, [[smsf|SMSFs]] in pension phase), the franking credit refund pushes effective yields even higher
2. **Perceived safety**: Government-backed deposit guarantee (up to $250,000 per ADI) and "too big to fail" implicit guarantee
3. **Compulsory super**: The $3.9 trillion [[superannuation]] pool creates constant demand for blue-chip, dividend-paying stocks
4. **Oligopoly economics**: High barriers to entry, pricing power, and stable earnings
5. **Dividend reinvestment plans (DRPs)**: All four offer DRPs, enabling compounding without brokerage costs

## How Australian Banks Make Money

Understanding the revenue model is essential to analysing bank shares as investments:

- **Net interest income (NII)** — the dominant earnings driver. Banks borrow cheaply (deposits, wholesale funding) and lend at higher rates (mortgages, business loans, credit cards). The spread is the **net interest margin (NIM)**, typically ~1.8-2.1% for the majors.
- **Mortgages** — home lending is the core of the Australian banks; the Big Four control ~80% of the market. This makes their earnings highly levered to the housing cycle, household debt, and the cash rate set by the [[reserve-bank-of-australia|RBA]].
- **Fee and commission income** — account fees, transaction fees, card interchange, and (historically) wealth-management fees, much of which was cut after the Royal Commission.
- **Business and institutional lending** — [[nab|NAB]] is the #1 business bank; [[macquarie-group|Macquarie]] skews to institutional, infrastructure, and markets income.
- **Trading and markets** — smaller for the retail-focused Big Four, larger for Macquarie.

Because earnings are dominated by NII on a vast mortgage book, the single most important macro variable for bank profits is the **interest-rate environment**: rising rates can widen NIM (until competition and funding costs catch up), while a deteriorating housing market raises **bad-debt provisions** that hit profit.

## Key Metrics for Analysing Bank Stocks

Banks are valued differently from industrials or tech. The metrics that matter most:

| Metric | What it tells you |
|--------|-------------------|
| **Net interest margin (NIM)** | Profitability of the lending spread; the headline earnings lever |
| **Return on equity (ROE)** | Australian majors are among the highest-ROE banks globally (low double digits) |
| **Cost-to-income ratio** | Operating efficiency; lower is better, ~40-50% for the majors |
| **CET1 capital ratio** | Balance-sheet strength under [[apra\|APRA]] rules; APRA's "unquestionably strong" benchmark is ~10.5%+ |
| **Bad and doubtful debts (BDD) / provisions** | Credit-cycle health; provisions spike in downturns and crush earnings |
| **Price-to-book (P/B)** | Primary valuation multiple for banks (vs P/E for most stocks) |
| **Dividend yield & franking** | The reason most investors hold them — typically 4-6% fully [[franking-credits\|franked]] |
| **Loan growth & deposit mix** | Volume driver and funding quality |

## Royal Commission Impact

The 2018-2019 Royal Commission into Misconduct in the Banking, Superannuation and Financial Services Industry (the "Hayne Royal Commission") fundamentally reshaped Australian banking. Key outcomes for investors:

- **Banks exited financial advice**: CBA sold Colonial First State, NAB sold MLC Wealth, Westpac scaled back BT. Massive adviser exodus — from ~28,000 advisers to ~15,000
- **Fee-for-no-service scandal**: Banks charged clients for advice never provided — billions in remediation
- **Stricter lending standards**: "Responsible lending" obligations tightened, initially slowing credit growth
- **AUSTRAC enforcement**: [[commonwealth-bank|CBA]] ($700M fine) and [[westpac|Westpac]] ($1.3B fine) for AML/CTF failures. See [[austrac]]
- **Cultural change**: Performance-based remuneration restructured, compliance spending increased

## Current Trends

### Digital Transformation
All four banks are investing heavily in digital banking — mobile-first experiences, API integrations, real-time payments via the New Payments Platform (NPP). CBA is widely regarded as having the best consumer app.

### Neo-Bank Competition
While neo-banks have captured attention, they have failed to meaningfully erode Big Four market share. [[ing-australia|ING Australia]] remains the most successful challenger with ~2 million customers, but this is a fraction of CBA's 16 million.

### Mortgage Competition
Intense competition in home lending has compressed net interest margins. Cashback offers, fixed-rate competition, and broker channel dominance (>70% of new mortgages originated via mortgage brokers) have pressured bank profitability.

### Shrinking Advice Footprint
Post-Royal Commission, banks largely exited financial advice. This creates an opportunity for independent advisory firms and self-directed investors using platforms like [[commsec]], [[nabtrade]], and [[selfwealth]].

## How to Invest in the Banking Sector

There are several ways to gain Australian bank exposure, each with different diversification and income characteristics:

| Approach | Vehicle | Character |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| **Direct shares** | Individual Big Four ([[commonwealth-bank\|CBA]], [[westpac\|WBC]], [[nab\|NAB]], [[anz\|ANZ]]) plus [[macquarie-group\|MQG]] | Maximum franked income and control; concentration risk |
| **Sector ETF** | Betashares MVB (ASX 200 Financials) and similar | Spreads exposure across the majors and second tier in one trade |
| **Broad index** | VAS / A200 (see [[australian-investing]]) | Banks are already ~25-30% of the [[asx-200\|ASX 200]] — you own them whether you want to or not |
| **High-yield ETF** | VHY and similar franked-income ETFs | Tilts toward banks for their fully franked yield |
| **DRP / compounding** | Dividend reinvestment plans on direct holdings | Brokerage-free compounding, popular for long-term [[smsf\|SMSF]] holders |

For income-focused investors, the canonical Australian play is **owning the Big Four directly for fully franked dividends**, held inside [[superannuation]] or an [[smsf]] where the [[franking-credits|franking credit]] is refunded in pension phase. The sector trades on **price-to-book and dividend yield**, and tends to rotate in favour when interest rates rise (margin expansion) and out of favour when housing/credit stress looms.

## Sector Risks

| Risk | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Housing/mortgage cycle** | Earnings are levered to a heavily indebted household sector and floating-rate mortgages; a property downturn raises bad-debt provisions |
| **Net-interest-margin compression** | Intense mortgage competition, cashback wars, and broker-channel dominance (>70% of new loans) squeeze NIM |
| **Concentration** | Holding the [[asx-200]] means ~25-30% in four correlated names — a sector shock hits the whole index |
| **Regulatory & political** | [[apra\|APRA]] capital rules, [[asic\|ASIC]] conduct enforcement, [[austrac\|AUSTRAC]] AML penalties (CBA $700M, Westpac $1.3B), and bank-levy/franking-policy risk |
| **Funding & rates** | Reliance on wholesale funding exposes banks to global credit conditions; the [[reserve-bank-of-australia\|RBA]] cash-rate path drives both margins and loan demand |
| **Dividend sustainability** | High payout ratios mean dividends can be cut in a downturn (as in 2020), removing the core reason many hold the stocks |
| **Disruption (slow)** | Neo-banks and fintech have not dented Big Four dominance yet, but digital competition pressures fees over time |

## Why Banks Matter to Investors

Australian banks matter to investors in two distinct ways:

1. **As direct investments**: Buying bank shares for [[dividend]] income and [[franking-credits|franking credits]]. The Big Four are among the highest-yielding major bank stocks globally and are cornerstone holdings in virtually every Australian income portfolio
2. **As service providers**: Banks provide the infrastructure investors use — brokerage platforms ([[commsec]], [[nabtrade]]), [[superannuation]] products, margin lending, wealth management, and cash management accounts

Understanding the Australian banking landscape is therefore essential for both stock selection and choosing the right investment platform.
