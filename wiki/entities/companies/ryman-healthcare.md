**Ryman Healthcare Limited** (ASX: RYM; NZX: RYM) is a dual-listed owner, developer and operator of integrated retirement villages and aged-care facilities across New Zealand and Australia, and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index following its October 2025 ASX dual listing. For traders it is a leveraged, capital-intensive [[healthcare]] turnaround story with heavy [[real-estate]]/REIT-adjacent characteristics: a once-premium compounder that diluted heavily through a NZ$1.0 billion 2025 equity raise and is now being repriced on free-cash-flow recovery evidence. The company is headquartered at 92d Russley Road, Christchurch, New Zealand and has a market capitalisation of approximately A$1.78 billion.

## Business Overview & Segments

Ryman owns and operates approximately 49 integrated retirement villages across New Zealand and Victoria, Australia. Its defining feature is the **continuum-of-care village**: a single community offering everything from independent living to high-acuity aged care on one site, so residents need not move as their needs change. The economics run on the **Occupation Right Agreement (ORA)** model — residents buy a licence to occupy (not freehold title); Ryman collects a deferred management fee (DMF) on the way out, recycles the unit to a new resident at the prevailing price, and books resale gains. This makes Ryman as much a property-development-and-recycling business as a care operator.

| Segment / care line | What it is | Role in the model |
|---|---|---|
| **Independent living** | Apartments and townhouses for active residents | Largest unit base; the ORA + DMF + resale-gain engine |
| **Assisted living** | Serviced apartments for residents needing more support | Bridges independent living and full care |
| **Aged care** | Rest home, hospital-level, specialist dementia and respite care | Government-subsidised care fees; staffing-cost intensive |
| **Development pipeline** | New villages and brownfield expansions (NZ + Victoria) | Capital-intensive growth — and the source of the debt and dilution debate |

The **ORA model** is the heart of the investment case: it is a quasi-property business where reported earnings swing on fair-value movements of the village portfolio, while the real cash story is DMF capture, resales, and development margin. In Australia, Ryman has villages and sites across Greater Melbourne (Wheelers Hill, Brandon Park, Burwood East, Coburg, Mount Eliza, Mount Martha, Aberfeldie, Ocean Grove and Geelong).

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Ryman's [[economic-moat|moat]] derives from scale, brand, and the structural demographics of an ageing population:

- **Vertically integrated village model** — owning land, developing, operating care, and recycling units in-house is hard to replicate and creates a recognisable, premium brand among NZ retirees.
- **Demographic tailwind** — the over-75 and over-85 cohorts grow structurally for decades in both NZ and Australia, underpinning long-run demand for villages and care beds.
- **ORA float economics** — interest-free resident loans (the occupation-right capital) fund a large share of the asset base, a structural funding advantage when the model is working.
- **Switching costs / stickiness** — once a resident moves in, relocation is highly disruptive, so occupancy is sticky and the resale flywheel is durable in a normal property market.

The moat has clear limits, exposed in the recent downturn: the model is **capital-intensive and cyclically fragile**. Resident purchases are funded by selling the buyer's own home, so a soft NZ/Australian housing market stalls inflows, lengthens sell-down of new stock, and chokes the cash flywheel. Aggressive development plus high debt turned a structural-growth story into a balance-sheet repair story — culminating in the 2025 dilution. Competitively, [[summerset-group-holdings]] (Summerset) is the closest peer and has at times executed the development-and-recycling model more conservatively.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Free-cash-flow recovery** — the central re-rating evidence; 1H FY26 delivered the first positive half-year free cash flow in over a decade (NZ$56.2m).
- **Cost-out programme** — annualised savings of ~NZ$40m achieved, with the FY26 target lifted to NZ$50–60m.
- **Sales/occupancy momentum** — Q4 sales up ~10% YoY; occupancy and new-sales rates are the operational pulse.
- **Demographic demand** — long-run ageing-population tailwind across NZ and Victoria.
- **Balance-sheet repair** — the NZ$1.0B 2025 raise and the November 2025 refinancing of NZ$2.0B facilities (five-year average tenor) reset solvency.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year/full-year results (March balance date), quarterly sales updates, the strategy/capital-management review (investor day 3 Feb 2026), NZ/AU house-price data, and NZ interest-rate moves.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- First positive free cash flow in a decade (1H FY26) signals the operational turnaround is real.
- Long-dated demographic tailwind; a recognised premium brand and vertically integrated, hard-to-replicate village model.
- Balance sheet reset via the 2025 raise and 2026 refinancing removes the near-term solvency overhang.
- Trades below book (P/B ~0.4x) — a deep-discount entry if the recovery holds and house prices stabilise.

### Bear case
- Heavy dilution: the NZ$1.0B raise roughly lifted the share count, permanently impairing per-share metrics.
- The ORA cash flywheel depends on a healthy NZ/AU housing market — a weak property cycle stalls inflows and sell-down.
- Reported losses driven by negative fair-value movements; net loss after tax persisted into 1H FY26.
- High capital intensity and development commitments against NZ$2.0B of debt leave limited margin for error.
- Sensitive to NZ interest rates and house prices despite the "healthcare" label.

## Key Risks

- **Property-cycle dependence** — resident purchases funded by home sales; a soft housing market is the single biggest risk to the cash flywheel.
- **Leverage** — NZ$2.0B facilities and a development pipeline; covenant headroom matters in a downturn.
- **Dilution/equity-markets dependence** — the model has needed capital-markets support; further raises would compound dilution.
- **Fair-value volatility** — reported earnings swing on portfolio revaluations, obscuring the cash picture.
- **Execution** — delivering the cost-out and sell-down of unsold stock at acceptable margins.
- **Regulatory** — aged-care funding, occupation-right consumer protections, and care-staffing rules in both NZ and Australia.
- **FX** — NZD-functional results reported and ASX-listed in AUD.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Ryman trades as a **deep-value, leveraged turnaround** rather than the premium compounder it once was — a property-cycle-sensitive [[healthcare]] name repriced on balance-sheet and cash-flow evidence.

- **Book-value anchor** — trades below net asset value (P/B ~0.4x as pulled), so the debate is whether portfolio fair values and the recovery are credible, not earnings multiples (statutory earnings are loss-making).
- **Property/rates beta** — behaves more like a [[real-estate]]/REIT-adjacent stock than a defensive healthcare name; sensitive to NZ interest rates and NZ/AU house prices.
- **Dilution overhang** — the 2025 raise reset per-share metrics; the market is re-underwriting the diluted base.
- **Liquidity** — split across NZX and the newer ASX line; ASX liquidity is still building.
- **Catalyst-driven** — free-cash-flow prints, sales/occupancy updates, and the capital-management review move the stock more than headline revenue.

## Recent Developments

For the half year to 30 September 2025 (1H FY26), Ryman's financial performance strengthened significantly. Total revenue grew 13%, underpinned by an increasing resident base and strong fee growth. The company achieved its first positive free cash flow in more than a decade, reaching NZ$56.2 million for the half. However, Ryman posted a net loss after tax of NZ$45.2 million for the period, reflecting lower fair value movements and a higher share count post equity raise.

Annualised cost savings achieved to date total NZ$40 million, with the FY26 target lifted to NZ$50-60 million. A strategy and capital management review progressed during the period, with an update provided at an investor day on 3 February 2026. The fourth quarter sales performance showed a 10% increase on the prior year, supporting management's confidence in the company's operational turnaround. Correlated names include retirement-village peer [[summerset-group-holdings]] (SUM.NZ), Oceania Healthcare, and REIT-adjacent property names like [[scentre-group]] given Ryman's property-cycle sensitivity.

## Leadership

Ryman is led by Chief Executive Officer Naomi James. The Board and executive team comprise leaders with deep experience in retirement living, aged care, property development and corporate finance. Founder Kevin Hickman retired from the Board some years ago; fellow founder John Ryder was inducted into the NZ Business Hall of Fame in 2020 in recognition of his long contribution to New Zealand business and the retirement living sector.
