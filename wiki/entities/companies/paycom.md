Paycom Software (NYSE: PAYC) is a cloud HCM (human capital management) company offering payroll, HR, time, and talent management on a single-database platform, founded in 1998 by CEO Chad Richison. For traders it is a former hyper-growth SaaS darling that has matured into a high-margin, GAAP-profitable compounder — the fundamentals feed below shows a ~15x PE that contrasts sharply with the 80-100x multiples PAYC commanded in 2020-2021, making it a case study in growth-to-value de-rating. It is a constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]] index and competes head-to-head with [[paychex]] and [[automatic-data-processing|ADP]].

## Business Overview & Segments

Paycom reports a single operating segment — its cloud HCM platform — monetized through high-margin recurring SaaS fees plus interest on client payroll float. The defining architectural choice is a single database for the entire employee lifecycle, which is the source of both its product differentiation and its software-grade margins. The lines below are product/revenue drivers within that one segment rather than reported segments.

| Product / line | What it is | Role in the model |
|---|---|---|
| **Single-database platform** | One application, one login, one database for recruiting-through-payroll, versus legacy acquired-and-stitched suites | The core moat and the >80% gross-margin engine |
| **Beti** | Employee-driven, self-service payroll (launched 2021) — employees verify their own payroll before submission | Improves accuracy/retention but cannibalized billable correction services — the trigger for the late-2023 guidance reset |
| **GONE / automation & AI** | Automated time-off decisioning and AI-driven modules | The current upsell engine driving margin expansion |
| **Float income** | Interest earned on funds held for clients between collection and disbursement | A rate-sensitive, capital-light profit line |
| **Recurring SaaS fees** | Per-employee-per-period pricing; ~98% recurring; in-house direct salesforce targeting US firms ~50-10,000 employees | The dominant revenue base |

The [[technology|technology]] differentiator is the single database — one normalized data model means a change entered once (a new hire, a pay-rate edit) propagates across payroll, time, benefits and talent without the reconciliation overhead of stitched-together suites. That architecture is both Paycom's selling point and the foundation of its software-grade economics.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Paycom's [[economic-moat|economic moat]] is narrower than the [[paychex]]/[[automatic-data-processing|ADP]] incumbents but real, resting on switching costs and product architecture:

- **High [[switching-costs]]** — payroll/HR is mission-critical and error-intolerant; once a mid-market client runs payroll, time, benefits and talent on Paycom's single database, re-platforming is costly and risky, producing ~98% recurring revenue and high retention.
- **Single-database architecture** — a genuine technical [[economic-moat|differentiator]]: legacy competitors grew by acquisition and run stitched-together products with reconciliation seams; Paycom's one-database design is hard to retrofit and underpins both UX and >80% gross margins.
- **Direct salesforce & usability** — an in-house, consultative salesforce plus a product reputation for usability drives land-and-expand within target mid-market accounts.
- **Float economics** — like every payroll processor, Paycom earns interest on client funds — a capital-light profit pool scaling with client count and rates.
- **Founder alignment** — founder-CEO Chad Richison's large voting stake aligns long-term incentives (though it is also a governance/key-man risk).

The moat is contested: the mid-market is the most competitive lane in HCM, with [[paychex]] (via Paycor), Paylocity, Dayforce and [[workday]] all pushing modern single-platform offerings. Paycom's bear case is saturation in its core 50-10,000-employee niche and slowing new-logo growth.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **New-logo wins & seat expansion** — the core driver; the direct salesforce lands mid-market accounts and expands within them.
- **Beti & GONE / AI automation** — automation modules reduce service costs (margin expansion) and serve as upsell; the post-Beti revenue model is the swing factor on growth re-acceleration.
- **Revenue retention** — ~98% recurring; retention and client-count disclosures are closely watched.
- **Float income** — interest on funds held for clients is geared to short-term rates; Fed easing is a headwind.
- **International / TAM expansion** — early international moves and up-market reach extend the runway.
- **Capital return** — ongoing buybacks plus a quarterly dividend (~$1.50/year, initiated 2023) — unusual for a SaaS name and a marker of its cash-cow transition.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly earnings (Feb/May/Aug/Nov), revenue/client-count prints, SMB/mid-market employment data, Fed policy (float), and buyback pace.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- A rare profitable SaaS compounder: 30%+ ROE, 40%+ EBITDA margins, >80% gross margins, and a de-rated ~15x multiple — quality at a reasonable price.
- Genuine single-database product differentiation and high [[switching-costs]] underpin ~98% recurring revenue.
- Beti/GONE automation expands margins by cutting service cost while improving retention.
- Founder-led with aligned incentives; buyback plus dividend signal cash-cow discipline.
- Optionality in AI modules, up-market reach, and international expansion not fully in numbers.

### Bear case
- Growth deceleration below double digits is the core fear — the market trades PAYC on the second derivative of revenue growth; any inflection moves the stock hard.
- Beti's cannibalization of billable correction services structurally reset the revenue model (the Nov 2023 guidance cut that halved the stock).
- The mid-market is the most contested HCM lane — [[paychex]]/Paycor, Paylocity, Dayforce, [[workday]] all pressing.
- Saturation risk in the core 50-10,000-employee niche.
- Float income falls as the Fed eases.
- Key-man/governance risk: founder Richison controls a large voting stake.

## Key Risks

- **Growth deceleration** — the dominant risk; sub-double-digit growth de-rates the multiple sharply.
- **Competitive/pricing pressure** — intensifying mid-market competition and pricing from cloud-native challengers and incumbents.
- **Float/rate sensitivity** — client-fund interest income compresses as short-term rates fall.
- **SMB/mid-market employment** — client headcounts and new logos track the labor cycle.
- **Product-transition risk** — automation (Beti/GONE) that cannibalizes legacy billable services must be net-accretive.
- **Governance/key-man** — concentrated founder voting control and key-person dependence on Richison.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Paycom is a **growth-to-value transition story**: at ~15x trailing earnings with 30%+ ROE and 40%+ EBITDA margins it screens as a quality compounder, but the market still trades it on growth-deceleration fear rather than on its profitability. Mechanically:

- **Trades on the growth second derivative** — revenue-growth inflections (re-acceleration or deceleration) move the stock hard in both directions; the multiple, not the margin, is what re-rates.
- **High single-stock volatility around earnings** — a history of >20% one-day moves (e.g. the Nov 2023 Beti guidance cut that halved the stock); options are liquid enough for earnings structures.
- **Float income as a rate driver** — like its payroll peers, Paycom earns interest on client funds; the float line is geared to the Fed path and feeds the rate-sensitivity read.
- **De-rating case study** — the ~15x PE versus the 80-100x multiples of 2020-2021 is the headline growth-to-value compression; the stock is a textbook example of multiple normalization as a SaaS name matures.
- **Capital return** — buybacks plus a small dividend; total return is now part capital-return, not pure growth.
- **Trades as a cohort** — moves with [[paychex]], [[automatic-data-processing|ADP]], Dayforce, [[workday]] and Paylocity — the HCM basket — and with mid-cap software sentiment.
