Larva Labs is a two-person software studio founded by Matt Hall and John Watkinson in 2005 that became one of the most influential entities in NFT history. The studio created [[cryptopunks|CryptoPunks]] (2017), [[autoglyphs|Autoglyphs]] (2019), and [[meebits|Meebits]] (2021) — three landmark NFT collections that helped define the entire non-fungible token movement. In March 2022, Larva Labs sold the intellectual property rights to all three collections to [[yuga-labs|Yuga Labs]].

## History

Matt Hall and John Watkinson founded Larva Labs in 2005 as a general-purpose software development studio. The pair worked on various projects including mobile apps and other software before turning their attention to blockchain experimentation.

In June 2017, Larva Labs created [[cryptopunks|CryptoPunks]] — a collection of 10,000 algorithmically generated 24x24 pixel art characters on the [[ethereum|Ethereum]] blockchain. The Punks were given away for free to anyone with an Ethereum wallet. CryptoPunks predated the ERC-721 token standard and are widely considered the project that inspired the standard itself, as well as the entire profile picture (PFP) NFT movement.

In April 2019, the studio launched [[autoglyphs|Autoglyphs]], a collection of 512 generative art pieces created entirely on-chain. Autoglyphs were the first "on-chain generative art" project — the algorithm that generates each piece exists within the Ethereum smart contract itself, making the art fully decentralized and immutable.

In May 2021, during the height of the NFT boom, Larva Labs released [[meebits|Meebits]] — a collection of 20,000 3D voxel characters intended as metaverse-ready avatars.

Despite the cultural and financial success of their creations (CryptoPunks collectively became worth billions of dollars), Larva Labs faced community criticism. Punk holders expressed frustration over unclear intellectual property rights — it was never definitively resolved whether owning a Punk granted commercial usage rights. The two-person team also maintained limited public engagement, which contrasted with the community-driven ethos other NFT projects embraced.

In March 2022, Larva Labs sold the IP for CryptoPunks, Meebits, and Autoglyphs to [[yuga-labs|Yuga Labs]] for an undisclosed sum (reportedly in the $200-400 million range). [[yuga-labs|Yuga Labs]] subsequently granted full commercial IP rights to holders of these collections. After the sale, Hall and Watkinson largely stepped back from the NFT space.

## Significance

Larva Labs is arguably the most historically significant creator in the NFT space. CryptoPunks inspired the ERC-721 standard that underpins virtually all NFTs on Ethereum, and the PFP (profile picture) format that CryptoPunks pioneered became the dominant paradigm for NFT collections — spawning [[bored-ape-yacht-club|Bored Ape Yacht Club]], Azuki, Doodles, and thousands of other projects.

Autoglyphs established the concept of fully on-chain generative art, which became a major artistic movement within the NFT space through platforms like Art Blocks.

The studio's decision to give CryptoPunks away for free — only for the collection to become worth billions — remains one of the most remarkable value-creation stories in digital asset history. The trajectory from free mint to multi-billion-dollar cultural artifact demonstrated the potential for digital scarcity to create lasting value.

Larva Labs' eventual sale to [[yuga-labs|Yuga Labs]] also highlighted the challenge of maintaining and managing large-scale NFT communities as a small team, and the tension between creator control and community expectations in the Web3 space.
