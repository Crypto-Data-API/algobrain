Nucor (NYSE: NUE) is the largest US steel producer and most diversified steelmaker in North America, operating low-cost electric-arc-furnace (EAF) mini-mills. It is a constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]] index and the classic liquid large-cap vehicle for trading the US steel cycle, tariffs, and infrastructure/construction demand. A [[materials]] sector heavyweight, Nucor is the textbook example of a low-cost commodity producer: a price-taker on [[steel]] that wins through cost position and balance-sheet strength across the cycle rather than pricing power.

## Business Overview & Segments

Nucor is the largest and most diversified North American steelmaker, built entirely on the electric-arc-furnace (EAF) "mini-mill" model — melting recycled scrap rather than smelting iron ore in blast furnaces. That gives it a structurally lower and far more flexible cost base than integrated blast-furnace peers, plus the ability to flex output up and down with demand. It is famed for a decentralized, no-layoff, pay-for-performance culture and a long record of counter-cyclical capex (building capacity in downturns when steel is cheap).

| Segment | What it is | Role in the model |
|---|---|---|
| **Steel mills** | Sheet, bar, structural, plate — EAF/scrap-based | Largest segment; the core commodity-steel earnings engine and the most price-sensitive line |
| **Steel products** | Joists, decking, rebar fabrication, racking, towers, buildings, data-center/utility products | Higher value-add and more stable margins; pushes Nucor downstream toward end customers |
| **Raw materials** | DJJ scrap brokerage/processing, DRI (direct-reduced iron) plants | Vertical integration into the scrap and metallics feedstock that sets the cost base |

Earnings are driven by US sheet/plate steel prices (HRC — hot-rolled coil), **metal margin** (the spread of steel selling price over scrap input cost), and construction/manufacturing demand. Section 232 tariffs and trade policy materially shape its pricing power by curbing import competition.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Nucor's [[economic-moat|economic moat]] is a **cost-advantage** moat, not a brand or switching-cost moat — steel is a near-commodity and Nucor is fundamentally a **price-taker**. Its durable edge is being the **low-cost producer**:

- **EAF cost structure** — scrap-melting mini-mills carry lower fixed costs, lower energy intensity, and far more operating flexibility than legacy integrated mills, so Nucor stays profitable deeper into price troughs where higher-cost competitors lose money.
- **Scale + diversification** — the broadest product range and geographic footprint in North American steel smooths the cycle relative to single-product mills.
- **Vertical integration** — DJJ scrap and DRI capacity hedge feedstock cost and supply.
- **Balance-sheet strength & culture** — low leverage and counter-cyclical capex let Nucor invest through downturns; the variable-pay culture flexes labor cost with the cycle.

The moat is real but cyclical: in a steel-price upcycle Nucor earns supernormal returns; in a trough returns can fall below the cost of capital for the whole industry. The edge is *relative* — being lowest-cost when everyone is squeezed — rather than absolute pricing power.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Section 232 tariffs / trade protection** — import curbs lift domestic prices and Nucor's pricing power; the single biggest policy swing factor.
- **Infrastructure & re-shoring** — IIJA infrastructure spend, data-center buildouts (Nucor sells utility/data-center steel products), and US manufacturing re-shoring underpin volume.
- **Apple Grove (West Virginia) sheet mill** — flagship growth capex adding ~3Mtpa of sheet capacity; a multi-year volume catalyst as it ramps.
- **Counter-cyclical capacity** — new mills and product expansions add earnings power into the next upcycle.
- **Mid-quarter guidance** — Nucor pre-announces each quarter, creating a recurring, market-moving catalyst.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Lowest-cost, most diversified North American steelmaker — stays profitable through troughs that bankrupt higher-cost peers.
- Trade protection (Section 232) plus infrastructure/re-shoring/data-center demand structurally supports US steel prices and volumes.
- Fortress balance sheet and counter-cyclical capex compound capacity and earnings power across the cycle; a 50+ year dividend aristocrat.
- Apple Grove and downstream steel-products expansion add through-cycle earnings.

### Bear case
- A commodity price-taker — earnings are a leveraged bet on the HRC steel price, which Nucor does not control.
- Cyclical: net margins and ROE collapse at the trough (see the optically extreme trailing P/E on trough EPS in the Fundamentals below).
- Trade policy is double-edged — tariff rollbacks or import surges compress metal margins fast.
- Heavy capex (Apple Grove) at the wrong point in the cycle pressures free cash flow; demand is tied to construction and the broader economy.

## Key Risks

- **Steel-price cyclicality** — HRC and plate prices, and the metal margin over scrap, drive earnings; a price downturn hits revenue and margin simultaneously.
- **Trade policy reversal** — removal/weakening of Section 232 tariffs or an import surge erodes domestic pricing power.
- **Demand cyclicality** — construction, automotive, and manufacturing demand are economy-sensitive.
- **Input costs** — scrap and energy price swings compress metal margin if steel prices don't follow.
- **Execution / capex timing** — large projects (Apple Grove) carry ramp and cost-overrun risk.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

NUE is a **cyclical commodity stock**, and the single most important thing about valuing it is that conventional multiples invert across the cycle. The trailing P/E looks *extreme at the trough* (low earnings push the multiple up — see the optically extreme reading in the Fundamentals below) and *cheap at the peak* (high cyclical earnings push it down). Experienced cyclical traders therefore value Nucor on **through-cycle/normalized earnings**, mid-cycle EV/EBITDA, and price-to-book against tangible plant — not on a static forward P/E.

- **Cyclical/commodity trade:** NUE trades on HRC futures direction, scrap spreads, the **metal margin**, tariff headlines, and construction/PMI data — not on static valuation multiples. It tends to bottom on price *before* earnings trough and to lead the steel cycle.
- **Beta / behaviour:** a higher-beta [[materials]] cyclical; rallies hard in reflation/infrastructure regimes and sells off in recession/deflation scares. Often paired against [[steel-dynamics]] for relative-value.
- **Dividend:** a **dividend aristocrat** with 50+ consecutive years of increases — the dividend is the steady through-cycle anchor even when earnings swing violently (note the "API dividend yield" figures below are not the market yield; see the source page).
- **Catalysts:** mid-quarter guidance (Nucor pre-announces each quarter), HRC price moves, trade-policy actions (Section 232/tariff changes), infrastructure spending bills, West Virginia (Apple Grove) mill ramp milestones.
- **Correlated names:** [[steel-dynamics]] (closest EAF comp), Cleveland-Cliffs, U.S. Steel, [[commercial-metals]]; inputs from scrap markets.
- **Options:** liquid chain; IV spikes around tariff news and quarterly guidance updates.
