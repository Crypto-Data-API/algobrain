**Dalrymple Bay Infrastructure Limited** (ASX: DBI) is a regulated-[[infrastructure]] owner listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. DBI owns the 99-year lease and operating rights (to 2051, with a 50-year renewal option) to the Dalrymple Bay Terminal (DBT) in Queensland – a major metallurgical [[coal]] export terminal located at the Port of Hay Point, near Mackay. DBT is one of the largest metallurgical (coking) coal export terminals in the world, handling exports produced principally in Queensland's Bowen Basin and bound for steelmakers across Asia, Europe and the Americas. For traders the key insight is that DBI is a **regulated-monopoly yield security, not a coal-price play**: it earns take-or-pay tariffs regulated by the Queensland Competition Authority, so its equity behaves like a long-duration bond proxy whose dominant drivers are the AU long-end yield curve and distribution guidance — not the price of coking coal.

## Business Model & Segments

DBI is a pure-play infrastructure owner. It does not own coal reserves or operate mines; instead, it earns regulated revenue from Bowen Basin coal producers who use the terminal under long-dated user agreements. The principal revenue stream is the **Terminal Infrastructure Charge (TIC)** – a take-or-pay charge regulated under a framework overseen by the Queensland Competition Authority (QCA) – supplemented by handling charges and ancillary revenue. The terminal is operated under contract by an operator (Dalrymple Bay Coal Terminal Pty Ltd), with DBI retaining economic ownership.

The business model is characterised by:

- Highly contracted, CPI-linked revenue under long-term user agreements
- Low direct commodity price exposure (users, not DBI, bear coal price risk)
- Regulated tariff framework providing visibility over Terminal Infrastructure Charges
- A capital allowance mechanism funding expansions (e.g. Northern Expansion)

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

DBI has one of the cleanest **regulated-monopoly / essential-infrastructure [[economic-moat|moats]]** on the ASX:

- **Natural monopoly on the asset** — DBT is *the* export gateway for a large share of Bowen Basin coking coal. There is no competing terminal at the location, and a rival could not be built without enormous capital, land, port access and regulatory approval. Producers must use it to reach seaborne markets.
- **Long-dated exclusive concession** — the 99-year lease to 2051 (plus a 50-year renewal option) legally locks in the operating rights.
- **Take-or-pay contracting** — users commit to pay for capacity whether or not they ship, so revenue is contracted and largely volume-insensitive; producers (not DBI) bear coal-price and demand risk.
- **Regulated tariff floor** — the QCA framework sets the TIC against a regulated asset base and allowed return, giving cash-flow visibility and a regulated return on expansion capex.

This is a **concession/regulated-asset moat** — fundamentally different from the trophy-real-estate moat of [[scentre-group]] or [[gpt-group]]. The trade-off: the same regulator that guarantees the floor also *caps* the upside (allowed returns), and the asset is exposed to a single, structurally declining commodity (seaborne metallurgical coal) over a 2051+ horizon. The moat protects cash flow through the cycle but cannot protect the terminal value against the energy transition — which is why the market embeds an ESG/terminal-value discount.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **CPI-linked TIC escalation** — inflation flows directly into the regulated tariff, growing revenue with little volume risk.
- **Expansion capital allowance** — Northern Expansion (8X) and other capacity projects earn a regulated return on incremental capex.
- **Distribution growth** — management lifted 2026 distribution guidance 7.7% to 26.375 cps; the income-growth track is the core thesis.
- **Refinancing / rate path** — the A$1.07B late-2025 refinancing extended maturities; falling rates would ease the swap roll-off (effective interest guided toward ~6.5% from mid-2026).
- **Regulatory clarity** — QCA decisions on the TIC framework that confirm the allowed return and asset base.
- **Recurring catalysts** — Feb/Aug results, annual distribution guidance, QCA rulings, expansion milestones, refinancing terms.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Regulated-monopoly, take-or-pay cash flows — among the most defensive, predictable revenue on the ASX.
- CPI-linked tariff is an inflation hedge; distribution growing (26.375 cps for 2026, +7.7%).
- Essential infrastructure with a concession to 2051 plus a 50-year renewal option.
- Attractive, growing, partly franked yield — a bond-proxy carry instrument for income portfolios.
- Falling rates would re-rate the security (lower discount rate on a long-duration cash-flow stream).

### Bear case
- Single-asset, single-commodity concentration tied to seaborne *metallurgical coal* — energy-transition terminal-value risk.
- High leverage (D/E ~2.1x) — normal for regulated infra but sensitive to rates; swap roll-off lifts effective interest toward ~6.5% from mid-2026.
- Bond-proxy duration cuts both ways — rising long-end yields de-rate the equity hard.
- Regulated returns cap the upside; QCA decisions can compress the allowed tariff.
- Statutory profit is thin and falling (FY25 NPAT A$29.3M) on higher depreciation/financing — read FFO, not net profit.

## Key Risks

- **Interest-rate / bond-proxy risk** — the dominant driver; rising AU long-end [[interest-rates]] de-rate a long-duration yield instrument, and the swap roll-off raises effective interest costs toward ~6.5%.
- **Structural coal decline** — seaborne metallurgical [[coal]] demand beyond 2051 is the terminal-value question; the ESG discount is already partly embedded.
- **Leverage & refinancing** — D/E ~2.1x means cash flow is sensitive to refinancing terms and rate moves.
- **Single-asset concentration** — one terminal, one location; an operational outage or port disruption hits all revenue.
- **Counterparty risk** — if Bowen Basin producers fail, take-or-pay contracts could be tested.
- **Regulatory risk** — QCA tariff resets that lower the allowed return compress earnings.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

DBI is a **regulated-infrastructure yield instrument** valued like a long-duration bond proxy rather than on a P/E (statutory profit is thin and noisy):

- **Distribution yield & growth** — the practical anchor. Distribution per security (2026 guide: 26.375 cps, +7.7%) and its sustainability (covered by FFO, not statutory profit) drive the income thesis.
- **FFO, not net profit** — management's preferred measure (FY25 FFO A$173.3M, +10.6%) is the right earnings lens; statutory NPAT (A$29.3M) is depressed by depreciation and financing tied to expansion capex.
- **Regulated asset base (RAB) & allowed return** — the regulated framework values the terminal on its asset base and QCA-allowed return; this sets the floor under cash flows and the tariff trajectory.
- **Duration / rate sensitivity** — the security trades inversely to AU long-end yields; falling yields lift it (lower discount rate on long-dated cash flows), rising yields and the swap roll-off (effective interest → ~6.5%) weigh on it.
- **Leverage** — D/E ~2.1x is normal for regulated infrastructure but above Fred's generic bar; refinancing terms materially affect distributable cash.
- **Franking & liquidity** — partly franked distributions lift after-tax yield for AU residents; a limited options market — an income/carry name, not a momentum vehicle.

## History

The Dalrymple Bay Terminal was originally commissioned in 1983. DBI in its current form was established in 2020 to acquire the long-term lease and associated economic rights from Brookfield Infrastructure Partners, which in turn had owned the asset since its acquisition of Prime Infrastructure in 2010. DBI listed on the ASX in December 2020 via an initial public offering, one of Australia's largest IPOs of that year. Since listing, DBI has negotiated new user agreements, progressed the Northern Expansion Capital Allowance projects, and transitioned to a refreshed regulatory framework.

## Recent Developments

For the full year ended 31 December 2025 (released February 2026), DBI reported revenue of A$849.81 million (up 10.6%), driven by CPI-linked Terminal Infrastructure Charge increases and higher ancillary revenue. EBITDA grew 5.2% to A$294.3 million, and Funds from Operations – management's preferred measure for distribution sustainability – rose 10.6% to A$173.3 million (excluding one-offs). Statutory net profit fell to A$29.26 million, reflecting higher depreciation and financing charges tied to the Northern Expansion Capital Allowance projects. Management lifted 2026 distribution guidance by 7.7% to 26.375 cents per security. DBI completed an A$1.07 billion refinancing in late 2025, reducing interest costs and extending debt maturity; however, the roll-off of lower-cost interest rate swaps from mid-2026 is expected to lift effective interest costs to around 6.5%.

The most recent filing (annual) was lodged on 2023-06-30. See [[#Financial Reports]] below.
