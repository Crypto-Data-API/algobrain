ChargePoint Holdings Inc. (NYSE: CHPT) operates one of the largest electric-vehicle charging networks in North America and Europe, selling networked charging hardware, software subscriptions, and services to commercial, fleet, and residential customers. For traders it is a small-cap, high-beta EV-infrastructure name that has lost the vast majority of its SPAC-era value — a vehicle for speculative options flow tied to the EV adoption narrative and to its own survival/balance-sheet story.

## Business Overview

ChargePoint's model is "asset-light": it sells charging stations to site hosts (workplaces, retailers, fleets, multifamily) rather than owning chargers itself, then layers on recurring cloud-software subscriptions (network management, billing, driver app) and assure warranty services. Revenue splits across networked charging systems (hardware, the larger but lower-margin piece), subscriptions, and other services. It competes with [[tesla|Tesla]]'s Supercharger/NACS ecosystem, EVgo, Blink, ABB, and increasingly with utilities and oil majors entering charging.

The company went public via SPAC merger in March 2021 at a ~$24 billion peak valuation; by June 2026 its market capitalisation was roughly $187 million — one of the most severe de-ratings of the SPAC class of 2021.

## Business Segments / Products

ChargePoint reports revenue across three lines, with the strategic shift toward higher-margin recurring software:

| Line | What it is | Margin profile | Strategic role |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Networked charging systems** | Level 2 (AC) and DC fast-charging hardware sold to site hosts | Lower-margin, hardware-cyclical | The largest line; sells the installed base |
| **Subscriptions** | ChargePoint Cloud software (network management, billing, driver app) + Assure warranty/maintenance | Higher-margin, recurring | The recurring-revenue lever the bull case rests on |
| **Other / services** | Professional services, installation support | Mixed | Attach revenue around deployments |

The structural thesis is that every charger sold seeds a long-tail software subscription, so an installed-base flywheel eventually drives gross-margin expansion and operating leverage. The bear counter is that hardware demand is slow and lumpy while the subscription base is not yet large enough to cover the cost structure — hence the ongoing restructuring and cash burn.

## Competitive Positioning / Peers

EV charging is a fragmented, capital-hungry market with very different business models competing for the same drivers.

| Player | Model | Network position | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **ChargePoint (CHPT)** | Asset-light — sells hardware + software to site hosts | Largest L2 network in NA by ports | Software/subscription flywheel thesis |
| **[[blink-charging\|Blink]] (BLNK)** | Owner-operator + host-owned hybrid | Smaller, micro/small-cap | Also burning cash; listing-compliance risk |
| **[[evgo\|EVgo]] (EVGO)** | Owner-operator DC fast-charging | Public DC fast network | Charging-as-a-service; capital intensive |
| **[[tesla\|Tesla]] / Supercharger (NACS)** | Owns & operates; opened NACS to other OEMs | Dominant DC fast-charging | The standard-setter; existential competitive force |
| **Utilities / oil majors** | Charging as adjacency to energy retail | Growing | Deep-pocketed late entrants |

The pivotal industry shift is **NACS standardization**: [[tesla]] opening its connector standard pulled most OEMs onto NACS, reshaping where drivers charge and pressuring independent networks. ChargePoint's differentiation is breadth of installed base and software, but it competes against a deep-pocketed Tesla, cash-burning peers ([[blink-charging]], [[evgo]]), and energy incumbents. The whole basket — ChargePoint, [[blink-charging]], [[evgo]], plus EV makers [[rivian-automotive]], [[lucid-group]], [[nio]] — trades on EV-adoption sentiment and rate/risk appetite rather than on idiosyncratic fundamentals.

## Bull vs Bear Case

**Bull case**
- Largest L2 charging network in North America — a scaled installed base that seeds recurring software.
- Subscription/software mix shift drives gross-margin expansion and operating leverage over time.
- Balance-sheet relief: cut debt by $172M (>50%) in Nov 2025, easing near-term solvency pressure.
- Leverage to a multi-decade EV-adoption secular trend and to any rebound in EV sales / charging-infrastructure policy funding.
- Asset-light model avoids the heavy owner-operator capex that burdens DC-fast peers.

**Bear case**
- Persistent cash burn against a sub-$100M cash pile; the path to profitability is unproven.
- Severe de-rating already (~$24B SPAC peak → ~$187M) and a 1-for-20 reverse split needed to hold its NYSE listing — both signs of structural distress.
- Dilution risk: a cash-burning company funds itself through equity/debt markets.
- Competition from [[tesla]] NACS standardization and deep-pocketed utilities/oil majors.
- Demand is highly sensitive to slower-than-hoped US EV adoption and to US EV tax-credit/policy changes.

## Valuation Framework

ChargePoint is a survival-and-inflection story, valued on network economics and the burn:

- **Network / installed base** — ports and managed chargers; the asset that seeds software.
- **Utilization** — throughput per charger; drives both hardware reorders and the value of subscriptions.
- **Subscription mix & gross margin** — the margin-expansion lever; watch the shift from low-margin hardware to recurring software.
- **Cash burn & runway** — quarterly operating cash use against the cash pile ($96.2M at the Apr-2026 quarter end) is the gating solvency metric.
- **Dilution / share count** — equity issuance to fund the burn directly offsets per-share value; the Nov-2025 debt reduction (>50%) eased but did not eliminate the funding question.
- **Reverse-split mechanics** — the 1-for-20 split (effective 2025-07-28) restruck the share base; compare pre/post-split charts carefully.
