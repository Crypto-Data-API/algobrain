National Australia Bank (ASX: NAB) is Australia's third-largest bank by market capitalisation (~AUD $110 billion+) and the country's leading business and institutional bank. Founded in 1858 as the National Bank of Australasia, NAB has historically differentiated itself from the other Big Four banks through its strength in business lending — it is the number one business bank in Australia by market share.

## Overview

NAB operates across retail banking, business banking, institutional banking, and wealth management. Its retail brokerage arm [[nabtrade]] provides self-directed share trading, while JBWere delivers premium wealth management for high-net-worth clients. NAB acquired the neo-bank 86 400 in 2021, merging it into its UBank digital brand to compete with challengers like [[ing-australia|ING Australia]] on savings rates.

NAB's deliberate focus on business banking makes it particularly relevant for investors who are also business owners — trading companies, family trusts, and [[smsf|SMSFs]] established as corporate structures often bank with NAB for the integration between business accounts and investment platforms.

## History

- **1858**: Founded as the National Bank of Australasia in Melbourne
- **1981**: Merged with Commercial Banking Company of Sydney to form National Australia Bank
- **1990s-2000s**: Aggressive international expansion — acquired banks in the UK (Clydesdale, Yorkshire), US, New Zealand, and Asia
- **2004**: FX trading scandal — NAB traders lost AUD $360 million through unauthorised foreign exchange trades. Four traders convicted.
- **2009**: Sold struggling UK banks to Virgin Money (eventually) and began retreating from international operations
- **2018-2019**: Royal Commission — CEO Andrew Thorburn and Chairman Ken Henry both resigned in February 2019 after the Commissioner criticised NAB's leadership
- **2021**: Acquired 86 400 neo-bank, merged into UBank. Sold MLC Wealth to IOOF/Insignia Financial for ~$1.4 billion — exited wealth management
- **2024**: Under CEO Ross McEwan, focused on business banking dominance, digital transformation, and simplified group structure

## Investment Services

### nabtrade

[[nabtrade]] is NAB's online brokerage platform, the second-largest bank-owned broker after [[commsec]]:

- **ASX shares**: Full access to all ASX-listed equities, [[chess-sponsorship|CHESS-sponsored]]
- **International shares**: 10+ markets including NYSE, NASDAQ, LSE, HKEX, Tokyo, Frankfurt
- **Options**: ASX Exchange Traded Options — puts, calls, and multi-leg strategies
- **mFunds**: Managed fund settlement via ASX mFund platform
- **Managed funds**: Additional managed fund options beyond mFunds
- **Brokerage fees**: $14.95 flat (up to $5,000), $19.95 ($5,001-$20,000), 0.11% ($20,001+)
- **iress ViewPoint**: Advanced trading terminal available at additional cost

### NAB Equity Lending (Margin Lending)

NAB offers margin lending — borrowing against a share portfolio to invest. The facility allows investors to leverage their existing portfolio to buy additional shares, ETFs, or managed funds. Standard margin call and [[risk-management]] considerations apply.

### JBWere

NAB's premium wealth management brand for high-net-worth (HNW) clients with investable assets typically exceeding $500,000:

- **Portfolio management**: Actively managed investment portfolios
- **Estate planning**: Structuring wealth for intergenerational transfer
- **Philanthropy advice**: Structured giving and philanthropic foundations
- **Private banking**: Dedicated relationship manager, bespoke lending, exclusive investment opportunities
- JBWere traces its history back to 1840, making it one of Australia's oldest advisory firms

### MLC Wealth (Divested)

NAB sold MLC Wealth to IOOF/Insignia Financial in 2021 as part of post-Royal Commission simplification. MLC was one of Australia's largest [[superannuation]] and wealth management platforms. The sale significantly reduced NAB's direct super and wealth offering, with the bank now focusing on [[nabtrade]] for self-directed investors and JBWere for HNW clients.

### UBank

NAB's digital-only banking brand — acquired when NAB bought neo-bank 86 400 in 2021 and merged it into UBank. Offers competitive savings rates and home loans with no branches. Relevant for investors as a high-interest savings vehicle for the cash component of investment portfolios.

### Business Banking

NAB is the #1 business bank in Australia — directly relevant for traders and investors who operate through business structures. Whether setting up a trading company, a family trust for investment, or a corporate trustee for an [[smsf]], NAB's business banking franchise offers integrated solutions across business accounts, lending, and investment (via [[nabtrade]]).

NAB pays fully [[franking-credits|franked]] semi-annual dividends. Like all Big Four banks, NAB cut its dividend during COVID-19 (2020) but has since recovered. NAB's dividend yield typically sits between [[commonwealth-bank|CBA]] (lower yield, higher price) and [[westpac|Westpac]] (higher yield, lower price).

## Regulatory and Legal

### FX Trading Scandal (2004)

Four NAB traders were convicted of manipulating AUD/NZD foreign exchange rates, resulting in AUD $360 million in losses. The scandal exposed fundamental failures in NAB's risk management and trading oversight. It remains one of the most significant trading misconduct cases in Australian financial history.

### Royal Commission (2019)

NAB's leadership bore the brunt of the Royal Commission's criticism. Commissioner Kenneth Hayne singled out NAB, saying he was "not as confident as I would wish to be" that NAB's leadership understood the gravity of the issues. Both CEO Andrew Thorburn and Chairman Ken Henry resigned within days of the final report's release.

## For Investors and Traders

NAB sits in the middle of the Big Four spectrum — neither the premium valuation of [[commonwealth-bank|CBA]] nor the deep value of [[westpac|Westpac]].

**Bull case**: Number one business bank in Australia — this is a structural competitive advantage as business banking is stickier and higher-margin than retail. Strong earnings recovery under Ross McEwan. Fully [[franking-credits|franked]] dividends at ~4-4.5% yield. Simplified group structure post-MLC sale reduces complexity and risk.

**Bear case**: Lost wealth management scale when MLC was sold. [[nabtrade]] has lower market share than [[commsec]]. FX scandal history raises governance questions. Business banking concentration means more exposure to SME credit risk during economic downturns.

**For business-owner investors**: NAB is uniquely positioned for investors who also run businesses. The integration between NAB business banking, [[nabtrade]] brokerage, and JBWere wealth management creates a unified ecosystem for entrepreneurs managing both business cash flows and investment portfolios.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

NAB shares the wide collective [[economic-moat|economic moat]] of the Australian banking oligopoly, but its specific edge is concentrated in business banking:

- **#1 business bank** — NAB has the largest share of Australian business and SME lending. Business banking relationships are stickier and higher-margin than retail mortgages: they bundle transaction banking, payments, merchant services, equipment finance and deposits, raising [[switching-costs]] and supporting a wider spread.
- **Deposit franchise & funding cost** — like its peers, NAB funds the bulk of its book with low-cost transaction and savings deposits, the foundational moat in banking.
- **Regulatory oligopoly** — the [[apra|APRA]] capital regime and the "four pillars" policy protect the Big Four collectively; NAB is one of the four protected incumbents.
- **Specialised verticals** — deep franchises in agribusiness, health, professional services and government lending give NAB sector expertise that sub-scale challengers struggle to replicate.

The moat is **wide but business-cyclical**: business banking is more exposed to the economic cycle than residential mortgages, so NAB's earnings carry more credit-quality torque (both upside and downside) than a retail-skewed peer like [[commonwealth-bank|CBA]].

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Business credit growth** — NAB's signature lever; SME and corporate borrowing tracks business confidence, capex and the broader economy.
- **Net interest margin (NIM)** — repricing of loans and deposits as the [[reserve-bank-of-australia|RBA]] moves the cash rate; the dominant earnings swing factor.
- **Proprietary home-loan origination** — CEO Andrew Irvine's push to grow direct-channel mortgages (reducing broker dependence) to protect margin.
- **Customer-deposit growth** — a stated strategic priority that lowers funding cost.
- **Cost discipline & digital** — UBank/digital migration and automation to improve the cost-to-income ratio.
- **Capital management** — surplus [[apra|APRA]] CET1 capital funds buybacks; recurring catalyst.
- **Recurring events** — full-year results (Nov, fiscal year ends 30 Sep), half-year (May), quarterly trading updates, dividend declarations, and [[reserve-bank-of-australia|RBA]] decisions.

## Trading Relevance

- **Index weight** — one of the largest [[asx-200]] constituents; trades as part of the "bank basket" alongside [[commonwealth-bank|CBA]], [[westpac]] and [[anz]].
- **Business-cycle proxy** — because of its SME tilt, NAB is often read as a barometer of Australian business conditions; its impairment commentary moves the sector.
- **Rate sensitivity** — trades off [[reserve-bank-of-australia|RBA]] expectations and bond yields like all the majors.
- **Dividend dynamics** — large ex-dividend gaps around the May/Nov cycle; franking drives domestic income demand.
- **Beta** — typically a moderate, income-style large-cap; slightly more credit-cyclical than retail-heavy CBA.

## Key Risks

- **Business-credit cyclicality** — NAB's SME/business concentration means a recession or sharp rise in unemployment would lift impairments faster than for a retail-skewed peer; FY2025 already saw loan-impairment charges rise ~14%.
- **Australian housing exposure** — NAB still carries a large residential-mortgage book; a house-price downturn would hit it like the other majors.
- **Net interest margin compression** — broker-driven mortgage competition and deposit competition squeeze NIM.
- **Regulatory / [[apra|APRA]] capital** — capital-ratio increases, macroprudential limits and responsible-lending rules constrain returns and payouts.
- **Conduct & governance history** — the 2004 FX trading scandal and the Royal Commission criticism are reminders of operational/conduct tail risk.
- **Execution** — delivering proprietary home-loan growth without sacrificing margin, and integrating digital (UBank) at scale, are not guaranteed.

- **Capital return** — surplus CET1 capital is returned through buybacks; capital management is a recurring catalyst.

## Business Model & Segments

NAB reports five segments:

- **Business and Private Banking:** Serves small, medium, and private banking clients, including specialised agriculture, health, government, education, and community services. This is NAB's signature franchise and the largest earnings contributor.
- **Personal Banking:** Home loans, transaction and savings accounts, personal loans, credit and debit cards, and digital banking. Also includes specialised accounts (foreign currency, farm management, business interest, cash maximiser, trust, and project bank accounts).
- **Corporate and Institutional Banking:** Operates in Australia and via branches in the UK, Asia, and the US. Services include debt capital markets, transactional banking, specialised capital, financial markets, and alternative investments for corporate and institutional clients.
- **New Zealand Banking:** Trades under the Bank of New Zealand (BNZ) brand, serving personal, business, corporate, and agribusiness customers across New Zealand.
- **Corporate Functions and Other:** Group functions including treasury, enterprise technology, and risk.

Product offerings span transaction/savings accounts, term deposits, home loans, personal loans, business loans and overdrafts, trade and invoice finance, vehicle and equipment finance, insurance (home/contents/landlord/travel/car/life/business), SMSF services, cash management, payments and merchant services, and industry-specific banking solutions.

## Recent Developments

For FY2025 (year ended 30 September 2025), NAB reported:

- Cash earnings of approximately A$7.091 billion - essentially flat on FY2024 and slightly below the consensus of A$7.18 billion.
- Loan impairment charges of approximately A$833 million, up 14%, driven by missed payments, a small number of individual business banking exposures, and some unsecured retail stress.
- Final dividend of 85 cents per share, bringing the full-year DPS to 170 cents, up 1 cent year-on-year, fully franked.
- Deposit balances up 7% over the year; new transaction account openings up 16% across Business & Private and Personal Banking.

Q1 FY2026 (quarter ended 31 December 2025):

- Unaudited cash earnings of approximately A$2.02 billion, up 15% on the 2H FY25 quarterly average.
- Underlying profit up 12% versus the 2H FY25 average, with growth across customer-facing divisions.

CEO Andrew Irvine has articulated three strategic growth priorities:

- **Business and private banking** (NAB's leading franchise).
- **Customer deposits.**
- **Proprietary home loans** (direct channel mortgage origination).

The most recent filing (annual) was lodged on 2023-06-30. See [[#Financial Reports]] below.
