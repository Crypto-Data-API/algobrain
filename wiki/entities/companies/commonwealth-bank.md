Commonwealth Bank of Australia (ASX: CBA) is Australia's largest company by market capitalisation (~AUD $230 billion as of 2024) and the country's biggest bank by deposits, home loans, and retail customer numbers. With approximately 16 million customers — more than half the Australian population — CBA is the dominant force in Australian retail banking and the parent company of [[commsec]], Australia's largest retail stockbroker.

## Overview

Originally established as a government-owned bank in 1911 under the Commonwealth Bank Act, CBA was privatised in three tranches between 1991 and 1996 — one of the most successful privatisations in Australian history. Today it operates across retail banking, business banking, institutional banking, and wealth management, with the most widely used banking app in Australia and the largest retail brokerage platform via [[commsec]].

CBA consistently trades at a premium valuation compared to the other Big Four banks ([[westpac]], [[nab]], [[anz]]), reflecting its dominant retail franchise, superior technology platform, and strong earnings growth. Its price-to-earnings ratio typically sits 20-30% above peers.

## History

- **1911**: Founded by the Australian Government under the Commonwealth Bank Act
- **1912**: Opened first branch in Melbourne
- **1920**: Given central banking functions (note issuance, monetary policy)
- **1959**: Central banking functions transferred to the Reserve Bank of Australia (RBA) — CBA became purely a commercial bank
- **1991**: First tranche of privatisation (30% sold at $5.40 per share)
- **1993**: Second tranche (another 19.4% sold at $9.50 per share)
- **1996**: Final tranche — CBA fully privatised
- **2000**: Acquired Colonial Limited (wealth management) for $9.4 billion — brought Colonial First State into the group
- **2008**: Acquired BankWest from HBOS during the GFC for ~$2.1 billion — a bargain acquisition
- **2018**: Paid $700 million fine to [[austrac|AUSTRAC]] for 53,750 AML/CTF breaches — largest corporate fine in Australian history at the time
- **2019**: APRA Prudential Inquiry found "widespread complacency" in governance and risk management
- **2020**: Matt Comyn era — strategic simplification, sold CommInsure, began Colonial First State divestment
- **2021**: Sold Colonial First State (CFS) to KKR for ~$1.7 billion, retaining distribution agreements
- **2024**: Market cap surpassed AUD $230 billion — most valuable company in Australia by a significant margin

## Investment Services

### CommSec

[[commsec|CommSec (Commonwealth Securities)]] is Australia's largest retail stockbroker with over 3 million accounts. It offers:

- **ASX shares**: Full access to all ASX-listed equities, [[chess-sponsorship|CHESS-sponsored]]
- **International shares**: 20+ markets including NYSE, NASDAQ, LSE, HKEX
- **Options**: ASX Exchange Traded Options (puts, calls, complex strategies)
- **mFunds**: Managed fund settlement via ASX mFund platform
- **Margin lending**: Borrow against share portfolio to invest — secured lending facility
- **Brokerage fees**: $10 (up to $1,000), $19.95 (up to $10,000), $29.95 (up to $25,000), 0.12% above $25,000

### CommSec Pocket

Micro-investing app for beginners — invest from $50 into curated themed ETFs ("Aussie Top 200", "Tech Savvy", "Sustainability Leaders"). Brokerage: $2 for trades up to $1,000, 0.20% above. Designed as an onramp into investing for younger Australians.

### Colonial First State (CFS)

CBA's former wealth management and [[superannuation]] platform. Sold to KKR consortium in 2021 as part of post-Royal Commission simplification, but CBA retains distribution agreements. CFS manages ~$150 billion in retirement and investment assets.

### SMSF Services

CBA offers integrated banking and brokerage for [[smsf|self-managed super funds]] — CBA transaction account + [[commsec]] brokerage with CHESS-sponsored HIN, [[franking-credits|franking credit]] tracking, and pension phase portfolio management.

### Term Deposits and Savings

CBA offers competitive term deposits and savings accounts that integrate with [[commsec]] investment accounts — enabling easy movement of funds between cash holdings and the share market. The NetBank Saver and GoalSaver products are widely used as cash management tools within investment portfolios.

### Dividend History

CBA has paid dividends every year since listing on the ASX. Dividends are consistently fully [[franking-credits|franked]], making CBA a cornerstone holding for income investors. The bank pays semi-annual dividends (interim in February, final in August). CBA's dividend payout ratio sits around 70%, balancing income distribution with capital retention for growth and regulatory buffers.

## Regulatory and Legal

### AUSTRAC Fine (2018)

CBA was fined $700 million by [[austrac|AUSTRAC]] in 2018 for 53,750 breaches of the Anti-Money Laundering and Counter-Terrorism Financing Act. The breaches related to CBA's Intelligent Deposit Machines (IDMs) — the bank failed to submit threshold transaction reports for $625 million in cash deposits, many linked to money laundering syndicates. This was the largest corporate fine in Australian history at the time (subsequently surpassed by [[westpac|Westpac's]] $1.3 billion fine in 2020). CEO Ian Narev departed in the aftermath.

### APRA Prudential Inquiry (2018)

[[apra|APRA]] commissioned an independent inquiry into CBA's governance, culture, and accountability. The inquiry found "widespread complacency" and a "reactive" approach to risk management. CBA was required to hold an additional $1 billion in capital as a penalty until remediation was completed.

## For Investors and Traders

CBA is the most widely held stock in Australia. It is included in virtually every Australian income portfolio and [[superannuation]] fund — either directly or through index funds tracking the [[asx-200]].

**Bull case**: Dominant retail franchise with no realistic competitive threat. Best-in-class digital banking platform. Consistent earnings growth. Fully franked dividend with ~70% payout ratio provides income with growth optionality. [[apra|APRA]] capital requirements ensure balance sheet strength.

**Bear case**: Trades at a significant premium to peers (P/E ratio 20-30% above other Big Four banks). Concentrated in Australian housing market — vulnerable to property downturn. Interest rate cycle sensitivity. Limited growth opportunities in a mature, heavily regulated market. Revenue growth constrained by competition in home lending via mortgage brokers.

**Key consideration for [[smsf|SMSF]] investors**: CBA's fully franked dividends are particularly valuable for SMSFs in pension phase, where the 0% tax rate means the full franking credit is refunded as cash. A $70 CBA dividend carries a $30 franking credit — in pension phase, that is $100 of pre-tax value for $0 tax.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

CBA holds one of the widest [[economic-moat|economic moats]] in Australian [[financials|financials]], built on reinforcing layers rather than a single advantage:

- **Deposit franchise & funding-cost advantage** — ~16 million customers and the largest pool of low-cost transaction deposits in the country give CBA the cheapest funding base of the Big Four. Cheap, sticky deposits are the foundational moat in retail banking: they translate directly into a structurally wider net interest margin (NIM) than peers can sustain.
- **Switching costs & inertia** — Australians change banks rarely; salary credits, direct debits, mortgages and the CommBank app create high [[switching-costs]]. The main everyday-banking relationship is exceptionally sticky.
- **Scale & technology** — CBA's app is widely regarded as the best digital banking platform in Australia, and its scale lets it amortise multi-billion-dollar technology spend across the largest customer base. This is a self-reinforcing data and distribution advantage.
- **Regulatory oligopoly** — [[apra|APRA]] capital rules, the "four pillars" policy (which blocks mergers among the Big Four), and high barriers to entry protect the incumbents collectively. CBA is the prime beneficiary as the largest pillar.
- **Distribution reach** — the largest branch/ATM footprint plus [[commsec]] (Australia's largest retail broker) deepen the ecosystem and cross-sell.

The moat is **wide but mature**: the same forces that protect CBA also cap its growth. Mortgage-broker origination (now ~70% of new home loans industry-wide) commoditises the lending side and erodes pricing power, while digital challengers and [[macquarie-group|Macquarie]] chip at the edges of the mortgage book.

## Competitors / Peer Set

CBA's core peer set is the rest of the Big Four plus the "fifth pillar" Macquarie. CBA is the premium-valued leader; the others trade at lower multiples and higher yields.

CBA commands premium economics (highest ROE and the richest P/E) versus every domestic peer; the watch items are mortgage-broker commoditisation and any narrowing of its technology lead.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Australian credit growth** — system-wide home-loan and business-credit growth, itself a function of population growth, immigration, and house prices.
- **Net interest margin (NIM)** — the single most important earnings lever; NIM expands as deposit pricing lags rate moves and contracts under mortgage-pricing competition.
- **[[reserve-bank-of-australia|RBA]] cash-rate path** — rate cuts/hikes reprice the entire book; the rate cycle is the dominant macro catalyst for all the banks.
- **Cost discipline & technology** — continued automation and the migration of customers to digital channels lower the cost-to-income ratio.
- **Capital management** — surplus [[apra|APRA]] CET1 capital funds buybacks and special dividends, a recurring catalyst.
- **Recurring events** — half-year (Feb) and full-year (Aug) results, interim/final dividend declarations, RBA decisions, and quarterly trading updates.

## Trading Relevance

- **Index torque** — CBA is the single largest weight in the [[asx-200]]; its results-day moves shift the whole index, and it anchors essentially every Australian index fund and [[superannuation]] portfolio.
- **Rate sensitivity** — the stock trades heavily off [[reserve-bank-of-australia|RBA]] expectations and bond yields; bank-sector rotation accelerates around rate-decision days.
- **Dividend dynamics** — large, predictable ex-dividend gaps in February and August; franking makes the grossed-up yield a key driver of demand from domestic income investors.
- **Correlated names** — moves with [[westpac]], [[nab]], [[anz]] as a "bank basket"; broadly inverse to housing-stress and credit-quality scares.
- **Beta** — typically below 1.0 versus the broad market (a defensive, income-style large-cap), but high sensitivity to domestic macro and housing data specifically.

## Key Risks

- **Australian housing concentration** — the majority of CBA's loan book is residential mortgages; a material house-price downturn, a sharp rise in unemployment, or a mortgage-arrears spike would hit impairments and capital simultaneously. This is the defining risk for all the majors.
- **Net interest margin compression** — intense mortgage-broker-driven price competition and deposit competition can squeeze NIM even in a benign rate environment.
- **Regulatory / [[apra|APRA]] capital** — APRA can raise CET1 requirements, impose capital overlays (as it did post-2018), or tighten serviceability/macroprudential rules, directly affecting returns and payouts.
- **Conduct & compliance** — the [[austrac|AUSTRAC]] history and Royal Commission show the tail risk of large fines and remediation; ongoing AML/CTF and responsible-lending obligations are costly.
- **Valuation / de-rating** — CBA's 20-30% multiple premium to peers prices in continued execution; any earnings disappointment or housing shock can de-rate a crowded, heavily index-weighted name hard.
- **Macro & funding** — reliance on wholesale funding for the portion not covered by deposits exposes CBA to global credit-spread shocks; recession and rising unemployment are the classic bank-cycle risks.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

CBA consistently trades at the richest valuation of the Big Four — its price-to-earnings ratio typically sits 20-30% above peers (see the data blocks above for the figures as pulled) — reflecting its dominant retail franchise, lowest funding cost, highest ROE, and best technology platform. Many analysts regard it as expensive on absolute and relative metrics versus global banks; the bull retort is that quality and scale justify a premium and that domestic index/super demand provides a structural bid. Mechanically:

- **Dividend & franking profile** — fully [[franking-credits|franked]] semi-annual dividends (interim Feb, final Aug) at a ~70% payout ratio; the cash yield is the lowest of the Big Four (because the price is highest), but the grossed-up (franked) yield is what domestic income investors actually compare. See [[dividend]].
- **Beta** — a relatively low-beta, defensive income large-cap versus the broad market, but with high specific sensitivity to [[reserve-bank-of-australia|RBA]] policy and Australian housing.
- **Index weight** — the heaviest stock in the [[asx-200]]; price moves transmit directly to the index and to passive flows.
- **Capital return** — surplus CET1 capital is returned via buybacks and occasional special dividends; capital management is a recurring share-price catalyst.

## Business Model & Segments

CBA reports four main segments:
- Retail Banking Services - home loans, deposits, transaction accounts, personal lending, credit cards, insurance and digital banking (CommBank app, NetBank) for Australian retail consumers, plus the Bankwest brand in Western Australia.
- Business Banking - SME, commercial, agribusiness and private banking, including transaction banking, payments, merchant services (including Smart EFTPOS and BPay), lending and deposits.
- Institutional Banking and Markets - corporate banking, debt capital markets, markets (FX, rates, credit, commodities), global transaction banking, and specialised finance for large corporates, financial institutions and government clients.
- New Zealand - ASB Bank, one of New Zealand's major banks, offering the full suite of retail, business and institutional banking services.
CBA also operates CommSec (retail stockbroking) and CommInsure (insurance), plus the now largely divested Colonial First State wealth business.

## Recent Developments

For FY25 (year ended 30 June 2025, reported August 2025) CBA delivered cash NPAT of A$10.3 billion, EPS of A$6.12, ROE of 13.5% and a full-year dividend of A$4.85 fully franked. Profit growth was supported by volume growth in core lending, stable underlying net interest margin and lower loan impairment expense, partly offset by cost inflation and accelerated investment. CEO Matt Comyn added ~2,000 IT engineers in 2025 (about a third offshore) and lifted annual investment spend by A$300 million to A$2.3 billion. For H1 FY26 (six months to 31 December 2025) CBA reported cash NPAT up 6% and statutory NPAT up 5% to A$5.41 billion, with EPS up 19 cents; the Board declared an interim fully franked dividend of A$2.35 per share, up 10 cents on the prior half.

The most recent filing (half_year) was lodged on 2026-06-30. See [[#Financial Reports]] below.
