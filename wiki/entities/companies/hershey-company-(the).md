The Hershey Company (NYSE: HSY) is the largest US chocolate and confectionery maker, owner of Hershey's, Reese's, Kisses, Jolly Rancher and the US licence for Kit Kat, plus a growing salty-snacks arm (SkinnyPop, Dot's Homestyle Pretzels). A [[s-and-p-500]] constituent, it trades primarily as a defensive [[consumer-staples|Consumer Staples]] compounder whose earnings are heavily geared to cocoa input costs and US confectionery pricing power.

## Business Overview & Segments

Hershey is built on a dominant US confectionery franchise, with a deliberate diversification into salty snacks and a small international tail. Seasonal sell-in (Halloween, Easter, Valentine's) drives the quarterly cadence, and the cocoa-cost cycle is the single largest variable on margins.

| Segment | Key brands | Role in the model |
|---|---|---|
| **North America Confectionery** | Reese's, Hershey's, Kisses, Jolly Rancher, Kit Kat (US licence) | Dominant — the profit engine; Reese's alone is one of the best-selling US candy franchises |
| **North America Salty Snacks** | SkinnyPop, Dot's Homestyle Pretzels, Pirate's Booty, LesserEvil | The diversification leg, extended via the 2025 LesserEvil acquisition |
| **International** | Hershey brands abroad | Small remainder; far less penetrated than peers globally |

Governance is distinctive: the **Hershey Trust** retains supervoting control, which has historically blocked takeover approaches (it rebuffed [[mondelez]] in 2016). Kirk Tanner (ex-Wendy's) became CEO in August 2025.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Hershey has a strong, US-centric brand [[economic-moat|economic moat]]:

- **Iconic brands / shelf dominance** — Reese's, Hershey's, and Kisses command leading share and shelf space in US confectionery, with pricing power and loyalty private label cannot easily erode.
- **Scale in US chocolate** — the largest US chocolate manufacturer benefits from manufacturing, distribution, and procurement scale at home.
- **Seasonal merchandising expertise** — entrenched retailer relationships around Halloween/Easter/Valentine's seasons are a recurring, defensible advantage.
- **Trust governance** — the Hershey Trust's control structure deters takeovers and enforces a long-term operating horizon (a double-edged feature for outside shareholders).

The moat's limitation is **cocoa exposure and geographic concentration**: Hershey is the purest cocoa-cost play among staples and far more US-concentrated than [[mondelez]] or Nestlé, so it lacks the emerging-market growth cushion and is fully exposed to the cocoa cycle.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Pricing realization** — net price increases (~9% in late 2025) to recover cocoa inflation; 2026 guided net-sales growth of 4-5%.
- **Salty-snacks expansion** — SkinnyPop/Dot's/LesserEvil extending beyond confectionery (acquisitions adding ~150 bps to growth).
- **Cocoa-cost easing** — the central 2026 bull case: input costs fall while pricing sticks, re-expanding gross margin.
- **Seasonal execution** — strong Halloween/Easter sell-through.
- **Innovation / better-for-you** — premium and health-positioned line extensions.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly earnings (Feb/May/Jul/Oct-Nov), ICE cocoa futures, holiday sell-through data, and any Hershey Trust governance headlines.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Dominant, high-margin US confectionery franchise with durable pricing power (Reese's, Hershey's).
- Cocoa-cost normalization is a clear margin-recovery catalyst if pricing holds.
- Salty-snacks diversification reduces single-category dependence over time.
- Defensive, dividend-growing compounder with a fortress brand moat at home.
- High returns on capital (ROE/ROA well above sector bars — see [[#Fundamentals]]).

### Bear case
- **Purest cocoa-cost exposure** in staples — record cocoa prices crushed gross margin and forced double-digit pricing.
- Price elasticity — volume loss after aggressive list-price increases is the key watch item.
- US-concentrated — little emerging-market growth cushion versus [[mondelez]].
- GLP-1 / health trends pressure indulgent-category demand long-term.
- Trust governance limits takeover optionality and outside-shareholder influence.

## Key Risks

- **Cocoa cost cycle** — the dominant margin driver; HSY is the cleanest large-cap cocoa proxy.
- **Price elasticity** — consumer trade-down to private label after big price increases.
- **Geographic concentration** — heavy US reliance with limited international diversification.
- **Consumer-health shift** — GLP-1 adoption and better-for-you trends as a structural demand question.
- **Governance** — Hershey Trust supervoting control limits M&A optionality.
- **Commodity inputs beyond cocoa** — sugar, dairy, packaging, and energy as secondary cost swings.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Hershey trades as a quality [[consumer-staples]] compounder; the multiple compresses when cocoa crushes margins and re-rates on the prospect of cocoa easing while pricing sticks (see the [[#Fundamentals]] block for the as-reported figures and high returns on capital). Mechanically:

- **Dividend** — a long-standing, regularly raised dividend; HSY is a classic dividend-growth staple where capital return (dividend + buybacks) is core to the thesis.
- **Beta** — low, defensive; it typically outperforms in risk-off and rate-cut regimes, but the cocoa cycle can override the defensive character.
- **Cocoa torque** — the purest large-cap cocoa-cost play in staples; the stock reads off ICE cocoa futures, and inverse sensitivity to cocoa makes HSY a popular pair against soft-commodity exposure.
- **Seasonality** — quarterly cadence tracks Halloween/Easter/Valentine's sell-in; holiday sell-through data is a soft catalyst.
- **Liquidity** — liquid options (monthly + weeklies); a core staples-sector-fund holding.
