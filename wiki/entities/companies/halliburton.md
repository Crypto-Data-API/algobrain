Halliburton (NYSE: HAL) is the world's second-largest oilfield services company (behind SLB), providing drilling, completions, and production services to oil and gas producers worldwide. It is a constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]] index and the most North America-levered of the big three service names, making it a high-beta proxy for US shale activity and the oil-services capex cycle.

## Business Overview & Segments

Halliburton sells equipment, technology, and field services to oil and gas producers — it does not own the oil. Its revenue is a derivative of producers' drilling and completion spend, so the franchise lives and dies on the [[energy|energy]]-sector capex cycle and on US rig and frac-spread counts. The company reports two operating divisions:

| Segment | What it is | Revenue / skew |
|---|---|---|
| **Completion and Production** | Hydraulic fracturing/pressure pumping (the **ZEUS** electric-frac fleet franchise), cementing, completion tools, artificial lift, and production chemicals | ~55–60% of revenue; the company's outsized exposure to **North American shale completions** — most NA-levered of the big three |
| **Drilling and Evaluation** | Drill bits (Sperry/Security DBS), drilling fluids (Baroid), wireline, logging-while-drilling, testing, and project management | ~40–45% of revenue; more **international / offshore-skewed**, with steadier, contract-led demand |

Roughly 40% of revenue is North American; the remainder spans the Middle East, Latin America, and other international markets, where Halliburton has been growing unconventional and lift work (notably **Saudi Arabia** and **Argentina's Vaca Muerta**). The Completion and Production mix is what makes HAL the highest-beta of the big three service names to the US onshore cycle — a strength in upcycles and the source of its current North American softness.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Honest framing for an oilfield-services name: Halliburton has **no durable commodity moat**. It is a cyclical, capital-intensive **price-taker on its own services** — pricing tracks the oil-services capex cycle, and revenue follows rig and frac-spread counts rather than a defensible recurring franchise. Through a downturn, pricing and utilization can collapse, as the 2020 COVID crash showed. There is no [[economic-moat|economic moat]] resembling a software or consumer-brand business; the [[risk-management|risk]] here is structural cyclicality, not idiosyncratic.

The real — but **narrow** — competitive edges are:

- **Scale and route density in North American pressure pumping** — fleet logistics, sand/water supply, and sub-basin density lower unit costs the share leader can defend.
- **Proprietary technology** — the **ZEUS** electric-frac platform (lower emissions, fuel cost, and maintenance), Sperry drilling/geosteering, and Security DBS drill bits earn pricing premiums when activity is firm.
- **Integrated service bundles** — selling completions + drilling + production as a package raises switching friction versus single-service pure-plays.
- **Customer relationships and installed equipment base** — long-standing producer accounts and a deployed fleet create incumbency on the next program.

Against peers, Halliburton's tilt is North American and completions-heavy, contrasting with [[slb|SLB]]'s international and digital/reservoir bias and [[baker-hughes]]'s diversification into LNG and industrial/energy-technology (which dampens its oilfield cyclicality). HAL is the most pure-play, most NA-cyclical expression of the trio.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **International momentum** — Middle East unconventional (notably Saudi Arabia), Argentina's **Vaca Muerta**, and artificial lift/intervention work are the growth engines while North America stalls.
- **ZEUS e-frac adoption** — the electric-frac fleet is the technology-led pricing/share lever in completions.
- **North American recovery optionality** — management frames NA as "first to respond when macro fundamentals improve," giving HAL the most torque to a US onshore upturn.
- **Capital return** — free cash flow funds the dividend and buybacks through the cycle.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly earnings (NA vs international margin split), US rig/frac-spread counts, OPEC+ decisions, WTI/Henry Hub moves, and E&P capex budgets (the Jan–Feb guidance season).

### 2025–2026 Developments

- **FY2025 revenue of US$22.2 billion**, down from US$22.9 billion in 2024, as weak North American activity (low rig and frac fleet counts, E&P consolidation and capital discipline) outweighed international resilience.
- **Q4 2025 (reported 21 January 2026):** revenue US$5.7B (vs US$5.6B in Q3), net income US$0.70 per diluted share, ~13% operating margin. **North America revenue US$2.2B, down 7% sequentially; international revenue US$3.5B, up 7% sequentially** — the clearest snapshot of the bifurcated cycle.
- **Strategy:** management's "Maximize Value" stance in North America (hold pricing/returns rather than chase share) and growth engines internationally (unconventionals, artificial lift, intervention); CEO Jeff Miller framed North America as "first to respond when macro fundamentals improve."

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- International momentum (Middle East, Vaca Muerta) and artificial-lift/intervention growth offset North American weakness.
- **ZEUS e-frac** is a genuine technology edge — lower emissions and fuel cost win share and defend pricing.
- The most NA-levered of the trio means the most **torque on any US onshore upcycle** — "first to respond."
- Solid free cash flow funds a steady dividend and buybacks even mid-cycle.

### Bear case
- A **price-taker** with no durable moat; North American pricing pressure compresses the highest-margin business.
- Earnings depend wholly on the producer **capex cycle** — a synchronized E&P pullback hits revenue and margins together.
- **E&P consolidation** shrinks the customer set and hands buyers more pricing power.
- The **deepest cyclicality of the big three** — least diversified away from oilfield activity, so drawdowns are sharpest.

## Key Risks

- **Oil-price / capex cycle** — the dominant driver; a downturn cuts drilling and completion budgets, collapsing utilization and pricing.
- **North American pricing pressure** — low rig/frac-spread counts and capital discipline squeeze the Completion and Production margin.
- **Customer consolidation** — fewer, larger E&Ps with more negotiating leverage and disciplined spend.
- **International political / contract risk** — concentration in Middle East and frontier basins carries sovereign, FX, and contract-renewal exposure.
- **Equipment-cycle capital intensity** — fleet reinvestment (including e-frac) is costly; idle capacity in a downturn is a fixed-cost drag.

## Trading Relevance & How the Stock Trades

Halliburton trades as a **high-beta proxy for US shale activity and the oil-services capex cycle**. Point-in-time multiples mislead — through-cycle framing matters far more than a single-quarter P/E for a name whose margins swing from deeply negative (2020) to mid-cycle stall (2025). Mechanically:

- **Index/options:** [[s-and-p-500]] constituent; deep, liquid [[options]] chains; a standard vehicle for expressing oil-services views alongside the **OIH/XES** ETFs.
- **Drivers:** moves with WTI/Henry Hub, US rig and frac-spread counts, OPEC+ decisions, and E&P capex guidance — not with HAL-specific fundamentals alone.
- **Relative value vs [[slb|SLB]]:** HAL typically **outperforms SLB when US onshore activity inflects upward** and underperforms in international/offshore-led upcycles; the pair trade is a clean expression of the NA-vs-international cycle.
- **Correlated names:** [[slb]] (Schlumberger), [[baker-hughes]], and the producer complex it serves — [[apa]], [[conocophillips]], [[chevron]].
- **Capital return:** dividend plus buybacks; capital-return durability through the cycle is part of the equity story.
- **Cycle note:** the Fundamentals section's strong 2021–2024 margin trend reflects the post-COVID recovery; 2025's NA softness shows the mid-cycle stall.
