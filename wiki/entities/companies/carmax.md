CarMax (NYSE: KMX) is the largest US used-car retailer, selling vehicles through ~250 superstores plus a national online/omnichannel platform, with an in-house lender — CarMax Auto Finance (CAF) — that funds a large share of its sales. A [[consumer-discretionary|Consumer Discretionary]] constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]], CarMax is the incumbent half of the used-car "duopoly" narrative against the online challenger [[carvana]]. Spun out of Circuit City and IPO'd in 2002, it pioneered the no-haggle, fixed-price used-car model; through fiscal 2026 it has been a turnaround and CEO-transition story after market-share losses, an affordability-driven demand slump, and a sharp earnings decline.

## Business Overview & Segments

CarMax buys, reconditions, sells, and finances used vehicles at national scale. The economics rest on a relatively stable **gross profit per unit (GPU)** on retail sales (historically in the ~$2,200–2,400 range), high used-unit throughput, and the finance spread CAF earns on the loans it originates. Founded in 1993 as a Circuit City pilot store in Richmond, Virginia (CIK 0001170010), the company spun off and listed on the NYSE in 2002. It reports on a February fiscal year-end.

| Segment / line | What it is | Mix (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **Retail used-vehicle sales** | Core engine: no-haggle fixed pricing, nationwide reconditioning and inter-store logistics, increasingly sold omnichannel (in-store, online, or hybrid). GPU × units is the profit driver | The largest revenue line and the headline volume metric (used-unit comps) |
| **Wholesale auctions** | Vehicles bought from consumers that don't meet retail standards are sold to dealers at CarMax's wholesale auctions — a high-velocity, lower-margin channel | Meaningful, steadier profit contributor; a function of appraisal/buy volume |
| **CarMax Auto Finance (CAF)** | Captive lender originating loans on a large share of retail sales, earning a net interest margin/spread and securitizing receivables (non-recourse) | Large, high-margin profit center; a relative bright spot through the FY2026 slump |
| **Extended protection plans (EPP) & other** | Extended service plans and GAP insurance sold at point of sale (third-party underwritten), plus other income | Small but high-margin, attached to retail unit sales |

The **2.99x debt-to-equity** in the Fundamentals section below is mostly **non-recourse CAF securitization debt** tied to the auto-loan receivables CAF originates — it is operating funding for the finance book, not distressed corporate leverage. Reading KMX's balance sheet like an unlevered retailer's overstates its risk.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

CarMax's [[economic-moat|moat]] is built on scale and process rather than a single defensible asset, and it is genuinely contested by Carvana's online model:

- **Scale & reconditioning/logistics network** — ~250 superstores plus centralized reconditioning and an inter-store transfer network let CarMax move the right inventory to the right market, a cost and selection advantage smaller dealers cannot replicate.
- **Brand & no-haggle trust** — decades as the category's trusted, transparent, fixed-price brand in a famously distrusted industry; a real source of repeat and referral demand.
- **CAF captive finance** — originating and securitizing its own loans captures the finance spread (rather than ceding it to banks) and smooths profit through the retail cycle.
- **Inventory & pricing data** — millions of appraisals and transactions feed proprietary pricing and reconditioning-cost models that underpin GPU discipline.

The bear's counter is that **the online model erodes the value of physical scale**: [[carvana]] proved buyers will purchase cars sight-unseen with home delivery, turning CarMax's superstore footprint into a cost base rather than a moat. CarMax has responded by building its own omnichannel capability, but it is defending share rather than extending a lead.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Used-unit recovery** — reversing the comps decline is the central thesis; even modest unit reacceleration leverages a fixed reconditioning/store cost base.
- **Omnichannel build-out** — closing the convenience gap with Carvana (online buying, delivery, instant appraisal) to defend and recapture share.
- **CAF spread & penetration** — finance-margin expansion as funding costs normalize, and higher CAF penetration of retail sales, are direct EPS levers.
- **Used-vehicle affordability cycle** — as new-car prices and rates stay elevated, used demand can substitute in; a normalization of used prices (Manheim) toward affordability helps unit volumes.
- **GPU discipline** — holding gross profit per unit while regaining volume is the bull's "have-it-both-ways" outcome.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly earnings (roughly Apr/Jun/Sep/Dec on the Feb fiscal year), monthly Manheim/used-price prints, auto-loan-rate and subprime-delinquency data, and new-CEO strategy milestones through 2026.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Incumbent scale, the strongest brand in used cars, and a captive finance arm — durable advantages if volume normalizes.
- FY2026 earnings are cyclically/transition-depressed; a unit recovery plus stable GPU produces large operating leverage off the fixed cost base.
- CAF is a high-quality, growing profit center that the market under-credits when focused on retail comps.
- New CEO (Keith Barr) plus price/marketing reset is a credible self-help catalyst; the stock is cheap on normalized earnings.
- Elevated short interest sets up a squeeze if comps inflect.

### Bear case
- [[carvana]] has structurally better online convenience and is taking share; the physical footprint is a cost, not a moat.
- Used-car affordability (high prices + high rates) is a multi-year demand headwind; comps could stay negative.
- The price/marketing reset compresses GPU and margins precisely when volumes are weak.
- CAF carries credit risk if subprime delinquencies rise through a consumer downturn.
- Execution risk under a new CEO turning the ship mid-cycle.

## Key Risks

- **Share loss to [[carvana]]** — the most-watched risk; the online model continues to pressure CarMax's unit volumes and growth narrative.
- **Used-car price cycle** — the **Manheim Used Vehicle Value Index** drives appraisal spreads, inventory mark risk, and consumer affordability; falling used prices can squeeze GPU and trade-in equity.
- **CAF / subprime credit** — CarMax Auto Finance is exposed to rising auto-loan delinquencies and loss rates in a downturn; provisioning swings hit earnings.
- **Affordability & macro** — elevated rates and prices suppress used demand; KMX is a cyclical consumer-discretionary name sensitive to the labor market and credit conditions.
- **Execution under new leadership** — turning around comps while resetting price/marketing is unproven under the new CEO; the strategy reset could compress margins without restoring volume.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

KMX trades as a cyclical turnaround rather than a stable compounder; the figures in the data/Fundamentals blocks are as pulled and shown qualitatively here. Mechanically:

- **GPU and used-unit comps are the stock** — gross profit per unit and comparable used-unit sales are the metrics that move the shares on earnings day, more than the headline revenue line.
- **Squeeze-prone** — elevated short interest during the FY2026 drawdown makes KMX prone to sharp short-covering rallies when comps or GPU surprise to the upside.
- **The Carvana pair** — the dominant relative-value expression is long/short KMX vs [[carvana]] (CVNA); the two trade as the two sides of the used-car share battle.
- **Manheim sensitivity** — the stock reacts to monthly used-vehicle price data (Manheim index) and to auto-loan-rate/subprime-delinquency prints that bear on CAF.
- **No dividend** — capital return has historically been via buybacks, not a dividend; this is a price-appreciation/turnaround story, not an income name.
- **Cyclical beta** — moves with consumer-discretionary risk appetite and the credit cycle; it is a higher-beta, sentiment-driven name versus defensive staples.

## 2025–2026 Developments

- **Demand slump:** Q3 FY2026 (quarter ended Nov 2025, reported 2025-12-18): net sales -6.9%, retail used units -8.0% (169,557), comps -9.0%, EPS -50.4% — share losses to Carvana and affordability pressure.
- **Leadership shake-up:** CEO Bill Nash stepped down 2025-11-06 after nine years; board member David McCreight became interim CEO with former CEO Tom Folliard returning as interim executive chair; **Keith Barr appointed permanent President & CEO effective 2026-03-16**.
- **Strategy reset:** cutting prices/margins and lifting marketing spend in 2026 to reverse unit declines; CAF earnings have remained a relative bright spot.

## Trading Relevance

- S&P 500 constituent; liquid options; elevated short interest during the FY2026 drawdown made it squeeze-prone on earnings.
- Primary pair/relative-value trade: long/short vs [[carvana]] (CVNA) — the market-share battle is the dominant single-name narrative; also correlated with [[autonation]], [[penske-automotive-group]], [[autozone]] (inverse-ish on the repair-vs-replace cycle).
- Macro inputs: Manheim Used Vehicle Value Index, auto-loan rates and subprime delinquencies (CAF credit risk), tariff effects on new-car prices (used demand substitution).
