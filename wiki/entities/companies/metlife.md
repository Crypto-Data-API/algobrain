MetLife (NYSE: MET) is one of the world's largest life insurers, a Dow-era financial founded in 1868 and a constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]] index. It writes group benefits, retirement and income solutions, and life insurance across the US, Asia, Latin America, and EMEA, and runs a fast-growing third-party asset manager (MetLife Investment Management). For traders it is a rate-sensitive, capital-return-heavy insurance name whose earnings track spread income, mortality/morbidity trends, and equity-market levels.

## Business Overview & Segments

MetLife reports across several segments: **Group Benefits** (US dental, disability, life — its largest and most stable franchise), **Retirement and Income Solutions (RIS)** (pension risk transfer, stable value, annuities), **Asia** (Japan and other markets), **Latin America**, **EMEA**, and **MetLife Holdings** (legacy run-off). A core growth pillar is **MetLife Investment Management (MIM)**, the company's institutional asset-management arm. Insurance earnings are driven by net investment spread (the gap between portfolio yields and crediting rates), underwriting margins, and fee income — making the company a textbook beneficiary of higher-for-longer interest rates.

A life insurer's economics differ from a P&C insurer's: the dominant engine is **net investment spread** — the gap between what MetLife earns on its huge fixed-income portfolio (the deployed [[float|float]] from policy reserves) and what it credits to policyholders — layered with **underwriting margin** (mortality/morbidity on protection products) and **fee income** (group benefits and asset management). This makes the stock a leveraged play on long-duration interest rates and credit spreads.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

MetLife's [[economic-moat|economic moat]] is moderate — life insurance is competitive and partly commoditized — but it has real scale and distribution advantages plus a growing capital-light fee engine:

- **Group Benefits scale & distribution** — a leading US group-benefits franchise with deep employer relationships and [[switching-costs|switching costs]] (employers rarely re-bid whole benefits programs); recurring, sticky revenue.
- **Brand & longevity** — a 150-plus-year brand (the "Snoopy" mark for decades) supporting trust in a confidence-dependent product.
- **Float compounding** — a very large, long-duration investment portfolio funded by policy reserves; spread income compounds as yields rise (the [[float|float]] dynamic).
- **MIM fee engine** — third-party asset management (scaled by the PineBridge acquisition) adds capital-light, fee-based earnings that the market values more highly than spread income.
- **Global diversification** — Asia, LatAm, and EMEA smooth US-specific cycles and add growth optionality.

The moat is narrower than a P&C disciplined underwriter's: life products are price-competitive, spread income depends on capital markets, and the business is balance-sheet-heavy with low ROA.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Higher-for-longer rates** — wider net investment spread on the large fixed-income portfolio; the primary earnings tailwind.
- **MIM / asset-management expansion** — PineBridge lifts MIM to a top-tier diversified manager (~$736B AUM); fee growth is the strategic priority and a multiple-friendly mix shift.
- **Pension risk transfer (PRT)** — corporate plan de-risking drives RIS volumes.
- **Group Benefits growth** — employment levels and product penetration drive the stable core.
- **Asia & LatAm** — demographic and middle-class growth, partly FX-driven.
- **Capital return** — aggressive buybacks (~$4.4B returned in FY2025) plus dividend growth.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly earnings (spread income, group ratio, variable investment income), Fed rate path, equity-market levels, and capital-return announcements.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- A textbook beneficiary of higher-for-longer rates via wider net investment spread.
- The "New Frontier" strategy and PineBridge tilt the mix toward capital-light, higher-multiple asset-management fees.
- A leading, stable Group Benefits franchise anchors recurring earnings.
- Aggressive capital return (~$4.4B in FY2025) on a ~16% adjusted ROE.
- Global diversification across Asia, LatAm, and EMEA.

### Bear case
- Rate cuts compress net investment spread — the core earnings driver works in reverse.
- Variable investment income (private equity/alternatives marks) is volatile and has disappointed across the industry.
- Credit losses in the investment portfolio in a downturn (the balance sheet is enormous and leveraged).
- Equity-market drawdowns hit variable-annuity and AUM-linked fees.
- Low ROA and heavy balance-sheet leverage are structural; integration risk on PineBridge.

## Key Risks

- **Interest-rate sensitivity** — falling long rates compress spread income; rate volatility complicates ALM and reserves.
- **Credit / investment risk** — a large fixed-income and alternatives portfolio carries credit, default, and mark-to-market risk in a downturn.
- **Variable investment income volatility** — alternatives/private-equity returns swing quarterly results.
- **Equity-market exposure** — variable annuities and fee AUM fall with markets.
- **Mortality / morbidity / longevity** — adverse claims experience (and longevity risk on PRT/annuities) hits underwriting.
- **Regulatory / capital** — statutory capital regimes, RBC requirements, and international (esp. Japan) regulation; FX translation risk.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Life insurers are valued on **operating EPS and adjusted ROE** (MetLife runs ~16% adjusted ROE) against **book value per share**, with the market increasingly rewarding the **capital-light asset-management (MIM) mix shift** over balance-sheet-heavy spread income. The stock is a rate-sensitive, capital-return-heavy insurance name. Mechanically (see the Fundamentals block below for figures as pulled — note the GAAP margins are distorted by insurance accounting and should be read with care):

- **Long-duration rate trade** — MET tends to rise with long-end yields (wider spread) and fall when rates drop.
- **Spread + fee mix** — the bull case is a re-rating as MIM's capital-light fees grow as a share of earnings.
- **Equity-market beta** — variable-annuity and AUM fees link earnings to market levels.
- **Catalysts** — quarterly spread income and group ratios, variable investment income, the Fed rate path, and capital-return updates.
- **Capital return** — a steady, growing [[dividend|dividend]] (yield typically above the [[s-and-p-500]]) plus large buybacks; income plus repurchases drive total return more than multiple expansion.
