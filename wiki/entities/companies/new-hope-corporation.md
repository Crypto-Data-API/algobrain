**New Hope Corporation Limited** (ASX: NHC) is an Australian thermal coal producer listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. Trading as New Hope Group, it operates the Bengalla (NSW) and New Acland (QLD) mines plus port, agriculture and investment assets, and is majority-owned by Washington H. Soul Pattinson (ASX: SOL). For traders it is a high-dividend, low-cost leveraged play on the Newcastle thermal coal price — earnings and the franked dividend swing hard with the coal cycle.

## Business Model & Segments

New Hope reports three operating segments:

- **Coal Mining in Queensland:** Includes the New Acland Mine, an open-cut thermal coal mine located approximately 27 km north-west of Oakey in south-east Queensland. Stage 3 of New Acland is projected to produce approximately 5 million tonnes per annum (Mtpa) over a mine life of around 12-15 years.
- **Coal Mining in New South Wales:** Includes the Bengalla Mine, an open-cut thermal coal mine approximately 4 km south-west of Muswellbrook. Bengalla is operated as a joint venture with New Hope holding 80% and Taipower holding 20%. It produces around 10-11 Mtpa of thermal coal.
- **Other:** Includes Queensland Bulk Handling (Port of Brisbane coal export facility, approximately 12 Mtpa capacity), agriculture businesses (Bengalla Agricultural Company, Acland Pastoral Company), oil and gas interests, and coal investments.

Other coal interests include the West Muswellbrook assessment lease, undeveloped coal assets (North Surat Coal project and Bee Creek tenement in Queensland), and an equity stake in Malabar Resources (~25.97%) - a privately held metallurgical coal developer operating the Maxwell Mine in NSW.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

New Hope sells a globally fungible commodity — [[coal|thermal coal]] — priced off the Newcastle (NEWC) benchmark. It is a pure **price-taker**: it has essentially **no [[economic-moat|economic moat]]** in the durable-pricing-power sense that applies to a software platform or a branded consumer franchise. No producer of an undifferentiated bulk commodity can set price; the market sets it, and every tonne of NEWC-spec coal is interchangeable. The honest framing is that New Hope's competitive position rests not on a moat but on **where it sits on the industry cost curve**.

That position is its real and only durable edge. New Hope's open-cut Bengalla and New Acland assets sit toward the **bottom of the global thermal-coal cost curve** — low strip ratios, established infrastructure, and modest unit cash costs. The cost-curve dynamic is the whole game in a cyclical commodity:

- **At high coal prices**, every producer makes money, but a low-cost producer converts the spread between price and its low unit cost into outsized cash flow and franked dividends — this is the "torque" income investors buy NHC for.
- **At low coal prices** that push high-cost mines below breakeven and force them to curtail or close, a bottom-of-the-curve producer keeps operating and keeps generating cash. **Low-cost producers are the ones that endure** in a structurally declining commodity — they outlast marginal supply, and their longevity is itself a scarce asset as the thermal-coal industry consolidates and capital exits.

Two secondary structural advantages reinforce this:

- **Owned export infrastructure** — Queensland Bulk Handling (the Port of Brisbane coal export terminal, ~12 Mtpa capacity) gives New Hope control of a logistics chokepoint rather than renting capacity from third parties, lowering cost and de-risking offtake.
- **A debt-light balance sheet** — low gearing (see the [[#Fundamentals]] block) means New Hope does not need high coal prices just to service debt. That solvency-through-the-cycle is what lets it survive troughs *and* fund counter-cyclical [[mergers-and-acquisitions|M&A]] when distressed assets come up cheap.

The honest verdict: NHC is a **low-cost survivor in a no-moat, price-taking, structurally challenged industry**. The investment case is cost-curve position and capital discipline, not pricing power.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

For a price-taking commodity producer, "growth" is dominated by price, with volume and M&A as secondary levers:

- **Newcastle thermal coal price** — by far the primary driver of revenue, earnings, and the dividend. Everything else is second-order to the NEWC benchmark.
- **New Acland Stage 3 ramp** — the Queensland mine restarted after a multi-year approvals saga and is ramping toward ~5 Mtpa; incremental volume at low marginal cost.
- **Bengalla volumes** — the NSW workhorse (~10-11 Mtpa, 80%-owned); production optimisation and recovery from any disrupted periods add saleable tonnes.
- **M&A appetite** — management has explicitly flagged appetite for **east-coast coal acquisitions** of high-quality, low-cost, cash-generative assets without large rehabilitation liabilities; a debt-light balance sheet is the dry powder.
- **Metallurgical-coal diversification via Malabar** — the ~26% stake in Malabar Resources (Maxwell Mine) adds exposure to the steel-linked coking-coal cycle, partially diversifying away from thermal/power demand.
- **Asian import demand** — Chinese and Indian thermal-coal import appetite (power generation, policy on domestic vs seaborne sourcing) is the demand-side swing factor on NEWC.
- **Results & dividend cadence** — half-year (March) and full-year (September) results, quarterly production/sales updates, and dividend declarations are the recurring share-price catalysts for an income-driven holder base.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- **Low-cost torque** — bottom-of-the-cost-curve assets turn high coal prices into outsized cash flow; few ASX names offer this gearing to a single price.
- **Very high fully franked yield** — among the highest on the ASX; for Australian-resident investors the franking gross-up sweetens an already large headline yield.
- **Cash generation & balance-sheet strength** — low gearing means cash is returned or redeployed, not consumed by debt service; survives troughs that close marginal competitors.
- **M&A optionality** — a war chest and a willing acquirer (management actively hunting cheap east-coast coal assets) at a point in the cycle when capital is fleeing the sector.
- **Demand stickier than consensus** — thermal coal demand, especially in Asia, has repeatedly outrun "imminent decline" forecasts; a slower-than-feared transition supports prices for longer than the bears model.

### Bear case
- **Structural ESG / thermal-coal decline** — the secular thesis is that thermal coal demand erodes as power decarbonises; New Hope is levered to a sunset commodity.
- **Narrowing buyer & financing base** — ESG-driven divestment shrinks the pool of equity holders, and lenders/insurers increasingly avoid thermal coal, raising capital costs and capping multiple expansion.
- **Price cyclicality** — extreme operating leverage cuts both ways; a NEWC slump compresses earnings and the dividend violently (see Key Risks).
- **Approval / permitting risk** — expansions face long, contested approval processes (New Acland Stage 3's court history), adding execution and timing risk to volume growth.

## Key Risks

- **Newcastle coal price (extreme operating leverage)** — the dominant risk. Because cash costs are relatively fixed, profit swings far more than price: in **1H FY2026, NPAT fell ~84% to A$54.3 million on roughly a 21% year-on-year decline in benchmark Newcastle thermal coal prices** (Source: [[stockmarketapi-fundamentals-2026-05-10]]) — a textbook illustration of the gearing. The dividend rides the same lever.
- **China / India import policy** — demand-side policy (Chinese restrictions or quotas favouring domestic coal, Indian import substitution, power-mix shifts) can move NEWC independent of supply.
- **ESG-driven divestment & financing access** — index/fund exclusions narrow the buyer base; banks and insurers withdrawing from thermal coal raise the cost and availability of capital, an overhang on the multiple regardless of earnings.
- **Expansion approval / permitting risk** — New Acland Stage 3 endured years of legal and regulatory challenge before restarting; future approvals (and rehabilitation/closure obligations) carry similar timing and cost risk.
- **Long-run thermal demand decline** — the secular energy transition is a structural headwind to terminal volumes and supports a permanently discounted valuation.
- **Weather & logistics** — Hunter Valley rail/port disruption and La Niña-driven wet weather periodically curtail production and export tonnes.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

New Hope trades as a **coal-price proxy with low-cost-producer torque** rather than as a growth or quality compounder. Mechanically:

- **Commodity-cycle valuation** — the share price tracks the NEWC benchmark; earnings (and the dividend) gear up and down with it, so static point-in-time multiples can mislead — current-period margins flatter or understate the through-cycle reality depending on where the cycle sits (see the [[#Fundamentals]] Fred-framework note).
- **High fully franked income profile** — the stock is held predominantly for income; the trailing yield sits **among the highest on the ASX**, and the fully franked status adds a gross-up benefit for Australian-resident holders. Dividends scale with the cycle, so the yield is large but variable, not a bond-like coupon.
- **Low PE reflecting terminal-decline discount** — the market assigns a structurally low earnings multiple (a "value trap"-style discount common to thermal coal) to price in declining long-run demand, a shrinking buyer base, and ESG financing pressure — not a sign of cheapness in the quality sense.
- **Soul Pattinson control & free float** — [[washington-h-soul-pattinson|Washington H. Soul Pattinson]] holds roughly **39%**, so the effective free float and on-market liquidity are smaller than the headline market cap implies; the controlling stake also anchors a conservative, dividend-friendly capital-allocation posture.

## History

New Hope Corporation was founded in 1952 in Ipswich, Queensland, initially as a small coal mining operation. The company became a listed ASX entity in 2003. Over subsequent decades it expanded through organic development and acquisitions:

- **Queensland Bulk Handling:** Acquired and developed as a wholly owned coal export subsidiary at the Port of Brisbane.
- **New Acland mine:** Operated since the 1990s; faced extended regulatory and approvals processes for Stage 3 expansion, with production restarting after approvals.
- **Bengalla:** Acquired its initial 40% interest in Bengalla from Rio Tinto in 2016 for approximately A$865 million; subsequently increased to 80% ownership, with Taipower retaining 20%.
- **Parent company:** New Hope is a majority-owned subsidiary of Washington H. Soul Pattinson & Company Limited (ASX:SOL), which has held a controlling stake since the mid-20th century.
- **Malabar Resources:** Progressively accumulated equity stake in the Maxwell Mine owner, reaching approximately 25.97% (from earlier 22.97% stake).

## Recent Developments

For the half year ended 31 January 2025 (1H FY2025), New Hope reported:

- Net profit after tax of approximately A$340.3 million (up from A$251.7 million in 1H FY2024), beating consensus of A$302.2 million.
- Total coal sales for the period of approximately 5.4 million tonnes (up 44%), driven primarily by the restart and ramp-up of New Acland (production rose tenfold).
- Maintained dividend distribution to shareholders.

CEO Rob Bishop has outlined a strategy focused on:

- Pursuing opportunistic acquisitions - particularly of high-quality, low-cost, cash-generative coal assets in eastern Australia without large rehabilitation liabilities.
- Increasing exposure to metallurgical coal via the Malabar Resources stake.
- Managing Bengalla mine production to optimise margins based on thermal coal pricing cycles.

Subsequent quarterly updates into 2026 have pointed to continued EBITDA strength despite coal price volatility. New Hope's improved balance sheet and investor receptivity to its debt have supported its M&A ambitions.

**1H FY2026 (half year ended 31 January 2026, reported 17 March 2026):**

- NPAT fell ~84% to A$54.3 million as benchmark Newcastle thermal coal prices declined approximately 21% year on year — a clean illustration of the company's coal-price operating leverage.
- Saleable coal production of 5.5Mt, supported by the continued ramp-up of New Acland; Bengalla was in a recovery period during the half.
- Fully franked interim dividend of 10.0 cents per share maintained despite the profit fall, reflecting the low-cost asset base and balance sheet strength.
- Q3 FY2026 updates indicated EBITDA improvement as Newcastle coal prices rallied off early-2026 lows.

The most recent filing (annual) was lodged on 2023-06-30. See [[#Financial Reports]] below.
