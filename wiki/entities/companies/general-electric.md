GE Aerospace (NYSE: GE) is the pure-play jet-engine and aftermarket-services company that remains after the historic General Electric conglomerate completed its three-way split (GE HealthCare spun January 2023; [[ge-vernova]] spun April 2024). A constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]] index and one of the largest [[industrials]] in the market (~$300B+ market cap, approximate, as of mid-2026), it is structurally one of the highest-quality industrial franchises in existence. The core asset is an installed base of ~45,000 commercial engines — anchored by the **CFM LEAP** built through the 50/50 CFM International joint venture with [[safran]] — that generates decades of high-margin spare-parts and shop-visit revenue. The investment story is not "selling more engines" so much as "the world keeps flying the engines GE already sold," which is the [[economic-moat|moat]] in one sentence.

## Business Overview & Segments

GE Aerospace designs, manufactures and services jet engines for commercial and military aircraft. The economic shape of the business is **razor-and-blades**: original-equipment (OE) engines are frequently sold near (or below) cost to win a platform, and the return is harvested over a 20-30 year service life through spare parts and shop visits. Services/aftermarket is ~70% of revenue and carries the overwhelming majority of the margin. The company that traders still quote as "GE" bears little resemblance to the 2000s conglomerate: finance (GE Capital), healthcare ([[ge-healthcare]]) and power/renewables ([[ge-vernova]]) are all gone.

| Segment | What it is | Qualitative weight |

The franchise is not just the metal: it is the **certified, sole-source position** on engine programs, the CFM JV scale with [[safran]], and the multi-decade service contracts that lock the installed base to GE shops. ## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

GE Aerospace holds one of the widest [[economic-moat|economic moats]] in [[industrials]] — and, critically, it is an **honest industrials moat**: it does not rest on being a low-cost commodity producer or on transient pricing, but on installed-base aftermarket economics, sole-source specification, and certification barriers. Several reinforcing layers:

- **Installed-base aftermarket (razor-and-blades)** — the deepest source of durable cash flow. With ~45,000 commercial engines in service, GE captures decades of spare-parts and shop-visit revenue per engine. Engines are sold cheap to win the platform; the ~70%-of-revenue services stream then compounds over 20-30 year service lives. This annuity is far more valuable, and far more predictable, than the one-time OE sale.
- **Sole-source / spec-in positions** — once an engine is certified onto an airframe and "specced in," it is effectively the only engine that platform will ever fly. The airframe-engine pairing is locked for the life of the aircraft type, giving GE a captive, multi-decade parts monopoly on each program rather than a contestable market share.
- **CFM JV scale** — LEAP is built through CFM International, the 50/50 joint venture with [[safran]], the best-selling engine franchise on narrowbodies. The JV's volume and shared R&D base are a scale advantage no new entrant can replicate.
- **Certification & regulatory barriers** — a clean-sheet jet engine takes a decade-plus and billions of dollars to design, certify (FAA/EASA), and field. The regulatory moat alone deters entry; combined with the installed-base flywheel it makes the competitive set effectively fixed.
- **Switching costs & IP** — re-engining or qualifying an alternative supplier carries enormous [[switching-costs]] (recertification, retraining, parts-pool risk), and decades of metallurgy, hot-section and digital-twin IP underpin performance-per-fuel leadership.

The moat is **wide and durable**, but not riskless: the same installed base that drives the annuity also concentrates exposure to engine-durability problems (a recall or premature-wear issue can invert the razor-and-blades math), and OE volumes ultimately gate the future aftermarket pool, tying GE to [[boeing]]/[[airbus]] build rates.

## Competitors / Peer Set

| Company | Primary battleground | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| [[safran]] | CFM partner (not rival) | 50/50 JV on CFM/LEAP — GE's most important relationship; aligned economics, the inverse of a competitor |
| Rolls-Royce | Widebody engines (Trent) | The main widebody rival; GE/GEnx and GE9X compete head-to-head on long-haul platforms |
| [[rtx]] (Pratt & Whitney) | Narrowbody + military engines | GTF competes with LEAP on the A320neo; the **Pratt GTF durability saga** is the cautionary template for GE's own aftermarket risk — see also [[raytheon]] |
| [[honeywell]] | Aerospace systems, APUs, avionics | Adjacent aerospace supplier; overlaps in systems and services rather than large commercial engines |
| [[heico]] / [[transdigm]] | Aftermarket parts & PMA | Compete for aftermarket dollars (PMA parts, repairs); the swing factor on how much of the spares pool GE keeps versus third parties |

GE commands premium, sole-source economics on the programs it owns; the watch items are widebody share versus Rolls-Royce, narrowbody share versus Pratt's GTF, and aftermarket leakage to [[heico]]/[[transdigm]]-style PMA suppliers.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **LEAP ramp + supply-chain heal** — LEAP deliveries up ~60% (Q1 2026) as supply chains recover; every OE engine shipped today seeds 20-30 years of future aftermarket.
- **Aftermarket spares super-cycle** — airlines are flying older fleets longer amid Boeing/Airbus delivery delays, structurally lifting shop-visit and spare-parts demand; the ~$170B commercial-services backlog underpins this.
- **777X / GE9X certification** — GE9X is sole-source on the [[boeing|777X]]; certification and entry-into-service unlock a new widebody OE-plus-aftermarket leg.
- **Defense awards** — T901 (Army ITEP), F414 derivatives and adaptive-cycle (XA102) programs provide a less-cyclical, IP-rich growth book.
- **Air-traffic (RPK) growth** — global revenue-passenger-kilometer growth directly drives engine flight-hours, which drives shop visits — the cleanest top-line lever.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly orders/backlog and LEAP delivery counts, shop-visit volume commentary, air-traffic data, Boeing/Airbus production-rate announcements, 777X certification timing, and defense engine awards.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Best-in-class installed-base aftermarket: ~45,000 engines throwing off decades of high-margin, recurring spares and shop-visit cash — an annuity, not a cyclical OE business.
- Aftermarket tailwind is structural: older fleets flying longer (Boeing/Airbus delays) lifts shop-visit demand; ~$170B commercial-services backlog gives visibility.
- LEAP output recovering (+~60% deliveries) as supply chains heal — each engine seeds future aftermarket.
- Sole-source/spec-in positions plus certification barriers make the competitive set effectively fixed; one of the great large-cap turnarounds under Larry Culp is now a clean, focused franchise.
- Q4'25 orders $27.0B (+74% y/y), record backlog, 2026 adjusted EPS guide ~$6.00-6.20 — momentum into a multi-year up-cycle.

### Bear case
- Rich multiple (~40x trailing PE) prices in continued aftermarket margin expansion; any stumble de-rates hard.
- Engine-durability/recall risk is the asymmetric threat — the [[rtx]] Pratt GTF saga shows how a hot-section problem can invert razor-and-blades economics and force costly fleet inspections.
- OE volumes (and thus the future aftermarket pool) are gated by [[boeing]]/[[airbus]] build rates — a production cut compounds downstream.
- Air-travel demand is cyclical and shock-prone (macro, geopolitics, pandemics); flight-hours drive the aftermarket directly.
- Supply-chain slips can cap LEAP output and shop-visit throughput, stranding margin.

## Key Risks

- **Valuation / positioning** — ~40x trailing PE leaves little room for execution error; a crowded quality-industrial trade can de-rate sharply on any guidance miss.
- **Engine durability / recall** — a LEAP or GEnx hot-section/durability issue could trigger inspections, warranty costs, and aftermarket disruption, mirroring the Pratt [[rtx]] GTF template.
- **Air-travel demand shocks** — recessions, fuel spikes, or travel disruptions cut flight-hours and shop visits, hitting the aftermarket engine directly.
- **Supply-chain constraints** — castings, forgings, and parts bottlenecks can cap LEAP deliveries and shop-visit capacity.
- **OEM build rates** — [[boeing]]/[[airbus]] production cuts or certification delays (e.g. 777X timing) reduce OE volumes and the future aftermarket pool.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

GE Aerospace trades at a premium multiple (~40x trailing PE per the Fundamentals snapshot below) that reflects the quality and durability of the aftermarket annuity rather than a cheap valuation; the debate is whether services-margin expansion and LEAP-driven aftermarket growth keep compounding fast enough to justify it. Mechanically:

- **What moves the stock** — the market re-rates GE on the **aftermarket margin trajectory**, services backlog (~$170B commercial services), shop-visit volumes, and LEAP delivery counts far more than on headline OE volumes. Quarterly orders/backlog and air-traffic (RPK) prints are the high-leverage datapoints.
- **Turnaround re-rate** — much of the post-2021 move is a re-rating story: the Larry Culp breakup turned an opaque, distressed conglomerate into a focused, cash-generative pure-play, and the multiple expanded as the franchise quality became visible. That re-rate is now largely in the price, raising the bar for further multiple expansion.
- **Mega-cap liquidity** — one of the largest [[industrials]] in the [[s-and-p-500]] with deep options liquidity; a core institutional aerospace holding.
- **Cyclicality vs annuity** — OE is cyclical (build rates), but the ~70%-of-revenue aftermarket dampens the cycle; the stock trades with more annuity-like quality than a typical capital-goods industrial, which is part of the premium-multiple justification.
- **Capital return** — post-breakup the company has reinstated a dividend and buybacks funded by strong aftermarket cash flow; capital return is a growing but secondary part of the story versus reinvestment in the engine roadmap.
