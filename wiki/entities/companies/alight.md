Alight Inc. (NYSE: ALIT) is a Chicago-based employee benefits administration and HR technology company whose Alight Worklife platform serves a large share of Fortune 500 employers (health, wealth/401(k) and leaves administration). For traders it is a small/mid-cap turnaround story marked by activist involvement (Starboard), serial CEO changes and persistent underperformance since its 2021 SPAC listing. It trades on the NYSE in the [[technology]]-adjacent HR-SaaS space.

## Business Overview

Alight (spun out of Aon's benefits-outsourcing arm in 2017, public via Bill Foley's SPAC in July 2021) runs benefits administration at enterprise scale — recurring, contracted revenue (>90% of each year's revenue typically under contract entering the year). It completed the ~$1.2B sale of its Payroll & Professional Services segment to an H.I.G. Capital affiliate in 2024, refocusing on the core benefits platform. Revenue now runs at roughly $2.2-2.3B annually with the equity in the low-single-digit billions — as of June 2026 (approximate).

## Business Segments / Products (detail)

After the 2024 divestiture of Payroll & Professional Services, Alight is a focused **benefits-administration platform** business centered on Alight Worklife:

| Offering | What it is | Strategic role |
|---|---|---|
| **Health (benefits administration)** | Enrollment, eligibility, and ongoing administration of employer health plans | The core; annual-enrollment season (Q4-weighted) is the key operating event |
| **Wealth / DC & 401(k) administration** | Defined-contribution / retirement plan recordkeeping and administration | Sticky, recurring; high switching costs |
| **Leaves administration** | Leave-of-absence and absence management | Add-on cross-sell into the installed base |
| **Alight Worklife (platform)** | Unified employee-experience / navigation platform layered over the above | The differentiator and BPaaS upsell vehicle |
| **BPaaS bookings** | Business-Process-as-a-Service: bundled platform + outsourced administration on a recurring contract | The growth/transformation metric management guides to |

The business model is **recurring, contracted revenue** — over 90% of a year's revenue is typically under contract entering the year — with high client switching costs once an employer's benefits stack is integrated. The strategic pitch is migrating clients to higher-value, recurring **BPaaS** arrangements. See [[technology]] and [[recurring-revenue]].

## Bull vs Bear Case

**Bull case**
- Highly recurring, contracted revenue (>90% under contract entering the year) with sticky enterprise clients and high switching costs.
- Post-divestiture focus on the higher-margin core benefits platform; payroll-sale proceeds fund debt paydown and buybacks.
- BPaaS migration is a margin- and revenue-quality upgrade story.
- Persistent PE/strategic interest in benefits-administration assets supports take-private optionality.
- New CEO (2026) strategy reset could re-energize execution.

**Bear case**
- Flat-to-declining top line and revenue attrition from client losses.
- Activist floor removed — Starboard fell below 5% in late 2025, taking away a support catalyst.
- SPAC-era leverage; the equity is small and has persistently underperformed since the 2021 listing.
- Serial CEO churn signals execution instability.
- Competition from [[workday]]/[[automatic-data-processing]]/Fidelity in benefits outsourcing.

## Valuation Framework (qualitative)

Alight is a recurring-revenue / margin-and-leverage turnaround, valued more on cash flow, recurring-revenue quality, and deleveraging than on top-line growth:

- **BPaaS bookings** — the forward growth indicator; the migration from legacy contracts to recurring BPaaS is management's central transformation metric.
- **Recurring revenue & retention** — over 90% of revenue is contracted entering the year; client retention and net revenue retention drive the durability of the model and the multiple.
- **Margin** — operating leverage and adjusted-EBITDA margin improvement post-divestiture are the operational proof points; the story is "margin/retention execution rather than growth."
- **Leverage / deleveraging** — SPAC-era debt is a key overhang; payroll-sale proceeds funding debt paydown and buybacks is the de-risking lever.
- **Free cash flow & capital return** — buybacks and debt reduction are the capital-allocation priorities.
- **Take-private optionality** — benefits-administration peers have repeatedly attracted PE interest; a buyout premium is a recurring valuation backstop.

## Capital Structure & Dilution Risk

- **SPAC-era leverage**: the July 2021 SPAC listing (via Bill Foley's vehicle) left Alight with meaningful debt that remains a key overhang.
- **Divestiture proceeds**: the ~$1.2B sale of Payroll & Professional Services to an H.I.G. Capital affiliate in 2024 funds debt paydown and buybacks — the central deleveraging lever.
- **Buybacks**: management has used buybacks alongside debt reduction; share-count dynamics are driven by repurchases and SPAC-legacy share structures rather than ongoing primary dilution.
- For traders, the deleveraging path and buyback support partly offset the SPAC-era balance-sheet concerns, but leverage remains a material part of the risk profile.

## 2025-2026 Developments

- **2025 quarterly trajectory**: Q1 revenue $548M, Q2 $528M, Q3 $533M — flat-to-declining top line; by Q3, $2.25B of 2025 revenue was under contract. The story remained margin/retention execution rather than growth.
- **CEO churn**: Dave Guilmette (CEO from August 2024, after Stephan Scholl's exit under Starboard pressure) stepped down 2025-12-31; **Rohit Verma became CEO effective 2026-01-01** and joined the board.
- **Starboard Value exited**: after a February 2025 cooperation agreement (board nominations withdrawn), Starboard fell below 5% ownership as of 2025-12-03 — removing the activist floor under the stock.
- Continued buybacks and debt paydown funded by the payroll-sale proceeds.
