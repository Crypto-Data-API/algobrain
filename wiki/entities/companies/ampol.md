**Ampol Limited** (ASX: ALD) is an oil & gas refining & marketing company listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. Ampol Limited is an integrated Australian transport fuels company, supplying petrol, diesel, jet fuel, lubricants and related products across Australia and New Zealand. It owns the Lytton refinery in Brisbane, a nationwide branded retail network, a fuels-and-infrastructure wholesale business, and New Zealand fuel retailer Z Energy.

## Business Overview & Segments

Ampol is an integrated [[energy]] company spanning the full transport-fuels value chain: it refines crude (Lytton), imports and trades product, owns terminal/pipeline infrastructure, and sells through a nationwide branded retail and wholesale network across Australia and New Zealand. The economics are a barbell — a volatile refining-margin cyclical bolted onto a stable, high-throughput fuel-retailing and convenience annuity. Ampol reports three segments:

| Segment | What it is | Profile |
|---|---|---|
| **Fuels & Infrastructure** | Lytton refinery (Brisbane), crude/product trading and shipping, terminals, pipelines, import infrastructure; supplies defence, mining, transport, marine, agriculture and aviation | The cyclical engine — earnings swing with the Lytton Refiner Margin (a function of regional Singapore product cracks) and import economics |
| **Convenience Retail** | Ampol-branded service stations and convenience stores across Australia; AmpCharge EV charging and home-electricity rollout | The stable annuity — fuel volume plus growing non-fuel (shop) margin; defensive cash flow |
| **New Zealand (Z Energy)** | NZ's largest branded fuel retailer (Z, Caltex and Challenge! brands), acquired 2022 | A second retail/wholesale annuity; adds NZD exposure and trans-Tasman scale |

Products are distributed through depots, terminals, pipelines and service-station sites. The "fuel security" backstop matters: the federal Fuel Security Services Payment underwrites Lytton's downside, capping (but not eliminating) refining-loss risk in weak-margin years.

## Economic Moat

Ampol's durable advantage is **infrastructure and distribution scale**, not a price-setting franchise — at the refining end it is a textbook [[economic-moat|price-taker]]. Margins on refined product are set by global crude and regional Singapore cracks; Lytton sits on the cost curve and is a swing asset whose profitability is dictated by the cycle, with the Fuel Security Services Payment providing a regulatory floor rather than a moat. The genuine moat sits downstream:

- **Terminal, pipeline and import infrastructure** — hard-to-replicate logistics assets at key Australian ports give Ampol a structural cost and reliability edge in supplying the domestic market, whether product is refined locally or imported.
- **Brand + site network** — a nationwide, well-located retail footprint with high switching inertia for fuel buyers; convenience/shop margin is a higher-quality, less cyclical earnings stream than fuel itself.
- **Integration** — owning trading, infrastructure and retail lets Ampol capture margin along the chain and optimise between refining and importing as economics shift.

Honest framing: the moat protects *volume and distribution economics*, not the *commodity margin*. The refining segment is a cyclical price-taker; the retail/infrastructure segments are the moat.

## Notable History & Milestones

Ampol traces its roots to The Australasian Petroleum Company, founded in 1900, with the modern lineage running through Caltex Australia.

## Recent Developments

For the full year 2025, reported in late February 2026, Ampol delivered RCOP EBITDA of A$1.4 billion, EBIT of A$947 million (up more than 30% year-on-year) and NPAT of A$429 million (up more than 80%), excluding significant items. The board declared a final fully franked dividend of 60 cents per share, taking full-year FY25 dividends to A$1.00 per share, up from 65 cents in FY24. CEO Matt Halliday pointed to broad-based growth in Convenience Retail, improved Lytton performance and continued demand across Australia and New Zealand. Looking to 2026, priorities include completing the EG transaction, executing retail segmentation, commissioning the Lytton Ultra Low Sulfur Fuels upgrade in Q2 2026, and a further A$50 million of nominal cost reductions across 2026-27. Ampol guided to net capex of around A$600 million in 2026.

The most recent filing (annual) was lodged on 2023-06-30. See [[#Financial Reports]] below.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Convenience Retail growth** — non-fuel (shop) sales, network refresh and retail segmentation lift the quality and stability of earnings.
- **Lytton Ultra Low Sulfur Fuels upgrade** — commissioning in Q2 2026 positions the refinery for tightening fuel standards.
- **EG Group Australia transaction** — completion reshapes the domestic convenience footprint and adds scale.
- **Energy transition optionality** — AmpCharge EV charging, home electricity, and emerging low-carbon fuels (SAF, renewable diesel) as long-dated demand pools as petrol/diesel volumes eventually fade.
- **Z Energy / NZ scale** — trans-Tasman synergies and a second retail annuity.
- **Cost-out** — a further A$50M of nominal cost reductions across 2026-27.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year/full-year results (Feb/Aug), monthly/quarterly Lytton Refiner Margin disclosures, crude and AUD moves, fuel-security policy, and fully franked dividend declarations.

## Bull vs Bear

**Bull case**
- The retail/convenience and infrastructure annuity is durable, cash-generative and growing — a defensive base under a cyclical top.
- Integration lets Ampol optimise between refining and importing as economics shift; the Fuel Security Services Payment caps refining downside.
- Strong fully franked dividend stream appeals to Australian income investors; capital discipline (capex guided ~A$600M for 2026) and cost-out support returns.
- FY25 showed the operating leverage on the way up — EBIT up >30% and NPAT up >80% (ex-significant items) as conditions improved.

**Bear case**
- Refining is a structural price-taker; a weak-crack environment can swing the segment to a loss and crater group EPS (why the headline PE screens extreme at troughs).
- Long-run energy transition erodes petrol/diesel volumes — the core demand base is in secular decline.
- Capital intensity and execution risk at Lytton; an unplanned outage hits a high-fixed-cost asset hard.
- Net income has fallen sharply across FY24-FY25 (A$122M → A$82M reported), showing how thin through-cycle margins can get.

## Key Risks

- **Refining-margin cyclicality** — the dominant swing factor; regional Singapore cracks and crude moves can flip Lytton from profit to loss.
- **Crude / AUD** — input cost and translation risk; a weak AUD raises landed crude costs.
- **Energy transition** — secular decline in transport-fuel demand; stranded-asset risk on refining over the long horizon.
- **Operational** — refinery outages, supply-chain disruption, single-asset concentration at Lytton.
- **Regulatory / policy** — changes to fuel-security arrangements, fuel standards, or competition rulings (e.g. on the EG transaction).

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Ampol is best read as a **refining-margin cyclical wrapped in a retail/infrastructure annuity** — which is why static multiples mislead. At cycle troughs depressed EPS makes the trailing PE screen extreme (see the data block above), while at the peak the same metric looks cheap; experienced holders value it on through-cycle earnings, replacement value of the network, and the dividend rather than spot PE. Mechanically:

- **Commodity beta** — earnings track the Lytton Refiner Margin (Singapore cracks) and crude; the shares carry meaningful [[energy]]-cycle beta and AUD sensitivity, partly damped by the retail annuity.
- **Dividend / yield** — fully franked payout is a core part of the total-return case for Australian-resident investors (franking gross-up materially lifts the after-tax yield — see the fundamentals block); the dividend flexes with the cycle.
- **Index torque** — an [[asx-200]] (and ASX 100) large-cap; also NZX-listed (same ALD ticker) since the Z Energy deal.
- **Liquidity / options** — liquid in the shares; ASX-listed single-name options exist but are thin, so most positioning is in the underlying.
- **Correlated names** — [[viva-energy]] (closest comp), with broader AU [[energy]] beta to [[woodside-energy]] and [[santos]] and regional refiners.
