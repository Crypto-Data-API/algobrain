Affirm Holdings Inc. (NASDAQ: AFRM) is an American financial technology company founded in 2012 by PayPal co-founder Max Levchin and headquartered in San Francisco, California. Affirm is the largest US-based buy-now-pay-later (BNPL) provider, offering point-of-sale installment loans through partnerships with major retailers including [[amazon|Amazon]], [[shopify|Shopify]] merchants, and Apple Pay. The stock carries high [[implied-volatility]] and is a major mover on macroeconomic and consumer credit news, making it popular for credit spreads and directional options plays. In fiscal Q3 2026 Affirm reached **GAAP operating profitability for the first time as a public company**, marking the transition from cash-burning growth story to profitable lender. It is a [[nasdaq]]-listed name in the consumer-finance corner of the [[financials]] sector.

## Business Overview

Affirm underwrites each transaction individually (no revolving credit, no late fees) and monetises through merchant discount fees, interest income on interest-bearing loans, the Affirm Card (debit+pay-later hybrid), and loan sales/securitisations to funding partners. Key distribution: Amazon, Shopify (Shop Pay Installments), Apple Pay integration, and a growing 0% APR promotional mix funded by merchants. Competitive set: Klarna (listed 2025), Afterpay ([[block]]), PayPal Pay Later, and increasingly banks' instalment products.

## Business Segments / Economics

Affirm is best understood as a three-engine machine: a **GMV flywheel**, a **monetization (take-rate) layer**, and a **funding/credit balance-sheet engine**. The interaction of these three determines whether growth is profitable.

| Pillar | What it measures | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **GMV (gross merchandise volume)** | Total dollar value of transactions processed | The top-of-funnel growth metric; scale of the platform and merchant distribution |
| **Revenue / take-rate** | Revenue as a % of GMV (merchant fees + interest income + card + servicing) | Monetization efficiency; mix-shift between 0% APR promos, interest-bearing loans, and the Affirm Card moves it |
| **Funding & credit (RLTC)** | Revenue Less Transaction Costs — revenue minus funding cost, loss provisions, processing | The true unit-economics line; the closest thing to a "gross profit" for a lender |

Key revenue streams:

- **Merchant network revenue** — discount fees merchants pay (especially on 0% APR promotional offers, where the merchant subsidizes the financing).
- **Interest income** — on interest-bearing installment loans held on balance sheet or via partners.
- **Affirm Card** — a debit-style card with pay-later optionality that drives repeat, app-led engagement and higher lifetime value per consumer.
- **Servicing / gain on sale** — fees and gains from selling loans into forward-flow arrangements and securitisations.

The **funding model** is central and distinctive: Affirm originates loans, then sells most of them to a capital-light network of funding partners (forward-flow buyers, warehouse facilities, and securitisation investors). Originating-to-distribute lowers balance-sheet capital intensity, but exposes Affirm to **funding cost** (sensitive to interest rates and credit spreads) and **funding availability** (a credit-market freeze is an existential risk for any non-bank lender). Take-rate, RLTC margin, funding cost, and credit losses (delinquencies/charge-offs) are the four numbers that determine whether GMV growth is value-creating.

## 2025-2026 Developments

- **Walmart loss (March 2025)**: Klarna — backed by Walmart's OnePay fintech — replaced Affirm as exclusive BNPL provider at Walmart. The partnership had generated ~5% of GMV and ~2% of adjusted operating income (H2 2024); the stock sold off hard on the news but the volume impact proved manageable.
- **FQ3 2026 (March quarter, reported May 2026)**: GMV **$11.6B, +35% YoY**; first-ever GAAP operating profit (**$88.4M, 8.5% margin**); net income **$102.9M**.
- **FY2026 guidance**: GMV **$49.3-49.6B**; revenue **$4.18-4.21B**; GAAP operating margin 8.9-9.4%; adjusted operating margin 28.2-28.8%.
- **Funding environment**: continued forward-flow and securitisation capacity expansion; credit performance (30+ day delinquencies) remained the key watch item as US consumer stress data oscillated through 2025-2026.

## Competitive Positioning / Peers

BNPL is a crowded, low-switching-cost market. Affirm's differentiation is transparent, no-late-fee underwriting, deep merchant integrations (Amazon, Shopify, Apple Pay), and a real underwriting/data engine rather than a fixed pay-in-4 product.

Affirm trades and is sentiment-linked with the broader fintech/consumer-credit basket — see [[sofi]], [[nu-holdings]], [[block]], [[paypal]].

## Bull vs. Bear Case

**Bull case**

- Reached GAAP operating profitability (FQ3 2026), validating the model and shifting the narrative from "will it ever make money" to "how fast does it compound."
- Strong distribution moat: Amazon, Shopify, Apple Pay, plus the Affirm Card driving repeat engagement and offline/everyday spend.
- BNPL secular growth and share-taking from legacy credit; underwriting-data advantage improves loss control over time.
- Capital-light funding model scales GMV without proportional balance-sheet capital.

**Bear case**

- Consumer-credit cyclicality: a recession/labor-market deterioration drives delinquencies and charge-offs, hitting RLTC directly.
- Funding-cost and funding-availability risk: higher rates/credit spreads or a frozen securitisation market squeeze margins or originations.
- Intense competition (Klarna, PayPal, Block, banks) and low switching costs pressure take-rate and merchant economics.
- Regulatory overhang: CFPB and other regulators scrutinize BNPL (disclosure, credit-bureau reporting, dispute rights).
- Customer concentration in a few large merchants (Amazon/Shopify) — a partner loss is material (cf. Walmart).

## Valuation Framework

Affirm is valued as a **growth lender**, which blends a software-style growth multiple with the credit-risk and funding constraints of a finance company. Qualitative levers:

- **GMV growth vs. credit losses**: the core tension. GMV growth is the bull thesis; the *quality* of that growth depends on whether loss rates (delinquencies/charge-offs) stay contained as the book scales. Profitable growth requires losses to grow slower than revenue.
- **Take-rate / RLTC margin trajectory**: mix-shift toward interest-bearing loans, the Affirm Card, and higher-margin merchant deals lifts monetization; a shift toward 0% promotional GMV can dilute take-rate even as volume grows.
- **Funding cost**: as a non-bank originate-to-distribute lender, Affirm's profitability is geared to interest rates and credit spreads. Falling rates are a tailwind to both funding cost and consumer demand.
- **Path of GAAP profitability**: now that operating profit is positive, the market re-rates on the *durability and slope* of margin expansion (operating-margin guidance) rather than on revenue growth alone.
- **Operating leverage**: incremental GMV over a fixed technology and underwriting cost base drives margin once scale is reached.

## Trading & Options Playbook

- **Catalysts**: quarterly earnings (GMV growth and credit metrics), US retail-sales and consumer-credit data (delinquencies move AFRM more than most fintechs), CFPB/regulatory headlines on BNPL, partner wins/losses (Amazon, Shopify, Apple), rate moves (funding-cost sensitivity).
- **Index/ETF membership**: not in the [[s-and-p-500]]; [[nasdaq]]-listed and held across fintech/innovation thematic ETFs. A potential future S&P inclusion (sustained GAAP profitability is a gating criterion) would be a passive-flow catalyst.
- **High IV / retail behavior**: beta well above 2 historically; earnings moves of 15-25% are routine, so defined-risk structures (verticals, iron condors) dominate. IV rank tends to peak pre-earnings and around macro prints. Heavy retail and short-interest dynamics can amplify squeezes and air-pockets.
- **Correlated names**: Klarna (post-2025 listing, the direct comp), [[block]], [[paypal]], [[sofi]], [[nu-holdings]]; trades with the unprofitable-fintech basket on risk-on/risk-off days despite now being GAAP-profitable.

## Capital Return / Dilution

- No dividend or buyback program; Affirm reinvests for growth and prioritizes funding capacity and balance-sheet strength.
- **Stock-based compensation** is a material, ongoing dilution factor (typical of high-growth fintech) and a key item in per-share economics.
- Convertible-note financing has been used historically; convertible structures and warrant arrangements (e.g., merchant equity deals) can affect share count.

## Risks

- **Consumer-credit cycle**: rising unemployment/consumer stress lifts delinquencies and charge-offs, directly hitting RLTC.
- **Funding risk**: higher rates/credit spreads or impaired access to forward-flow/securitisation markets squeeze margins or constrain originations.
- **Competition**: Klarna, PayPal, Block/Afterpay, and banks compress take-rate; low switching costs.
- **Regulatory**: CFPB/BNPL scrutiny on disclosures, credit reporting, and dispute handling.
- **Partner concentration**: dependence on Amazon, Shopify, Apple; a major partner loss is material (cf. Walmart 2025).
- **Valuation**: growth-multiple sensitivity; earnings disappointment or credit deterioration de-rates quickly.
