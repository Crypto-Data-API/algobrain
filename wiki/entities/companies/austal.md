**Austal Limited** (ASX: ASB) is an aerospace & defense company listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. Austal is Australia's largest defence exporter and first ASX-listed shipbuilder, designing and building military and commercial vessels for customers worldwide. It operates major yards at Henderson (Western Australia), Mobile (Alabama, USA) and Balamban (Philippines), plus smaller support and refit facilities in the United States, Middle East, Asia-Pacific and Vietnam.

## Business Model & Segments

Austal reports four segments:
- USA Shipbuilding - design and construction of advanced naval vessels at Austal USA's Mobile, Alabama yard for the U.S. Navy, Coast Guard and allies, including Independence-class Littoral Combat Ships (LCS), Expeditionary Fast Transports (EPF), Navajo-class Towing, Salvage and Rescue ships (T-ATS) and submarine components.
- USA Support - vessel sustainment, repair, refit and logistics for U.S. defence and commercial customers.
- Australasia Shipbuilding - Henderson (WA) and Balamban (Philippines) builds of defence vessels (Cape-class patrol boats, Guardian-class patrol boats for Pacific nations, Evolved Cape, Landing Craft) and high-speed commercial ferries (vehicle and passenger) and offshore windfarm support vessels.
- Australasia Support - in-service support and sustainment, integrated logistics, crew training, systems support and refit for Australian, New Zealand and other regional operators.
Austal also develops MARINELINK vessel control and information-management systems and motion-control interceptors, and provides aluminium vessel brokerage, consulting and parts.

The strategic shift over the past decade has been **from commercial ferries to defence**, and within defence toward higher-value steel hulls and submarine-module capability tied to the AUKUS pathway. A near-A$18bn order book gives multi-year revenue visibility; the central debate is conversion margin, not demand.

## History

Austal was founded in 1987 by John Rothwell and is headquartered at Henderson, south of Perth. Initially a builder of high-speed aluminium catamarans for the commercial ferry market, it expanded into defence via ferries for government clients. Austal USA was established in 1999 as a 70:30 JV with Bender Shipbuilding, which Austal bought out in 2006. In October 2005 Austal (with General Dynamics) won a U.S. Navy contract to build its trimaran Littoral Combat Ship design, with the USS Independence (LCS-2) keel laid in January 2006. Austal severed its GD partnership in 2010 and won direct U.S. Navy LCS contracts. Today Austal USA also builds steel vessels, including T-ATS, and has invested heavily in submarine-module capability to support the AUKUS pathway.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Austal's [[economic-moat|moat]] derives from **strategic positioning in [[defense|defence]] procurement** rather than commercial advantage:

- **Sovereign/strategic shipyards** — the Mobile (Alabama) and Henderson (WA) yards are accredited, security-cleared facilities embedded in US and Australian naval supply chains; building or qualifying a rival yard takes years and government backing (a very high [[switching-costs|switching/qualification cost]] for buyers).
- **Incumbency & program lock-in** — once a class (LCS, EPF, T-ATS, Cape-class, LCH) is in production with Austal, the design knowledge, tooling and sustainment relationship make it the default for follow-on hulls and decades of in-service support.
- **National-security protection** — defence shipbuilding is deliberately shielded by FIRB/CFIUS-style controls and "build-at-home" policy; this both protects incumbents and is why the Hanwha stake required strict government conditions.
- **AUKUS optionality** — submarine-module capability positions Austal in a multi-decade, government-funded program.

The moat's weakness is **execution, not demand**: aluminium/steel shipbuilding is a fixed-price, fixed-program business where cost overruns (Austal USA's history of margin blowouts and a US DOJ/ASIC accounting saga) can erase contract economics. Returns on capital have been low and volatile (see Fundamentals); the franchise is strategic rather than high-return.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Defence-spending up-cycle** — rising US and Australian naval budgets and the [[defense|defence]] theme broadly.
- **AUKUS** — submarine-module work as a multi-decade, government-funded demand pool.
- **Order-book conversion** — turning the ~A$17.7bn backlog (incl. the A$4bn LCH award) into delivered revenue at improving margins.
- **Hanwha relationship** — capital, shipbuilding know-how and takeover optionality.
- **US sustainment growth** — higher-margin support/repair revenue.
- **Catalysts** — M&A/strategic-stake news (the dominant driver), contract awards, US Navy budget decisions, H1 (Feb) and FY (Aug) results, and Austal USA margin/execution updates.

## Bull vs Bear

**Bull case**
- Record ~A$17.7bn order book gives years of revenue visibility; demand is not the question.
- Structural defence-spending tailwind plus AUKUS submarine-module optionality.
- Hanwha's approved move to 19.9% keeps takeover optionality live against Tattarang's ~19.3% blocking stake — a scarce strategic asset.
- Improving margins (FY25 record revenue A$1.8bn, EBIT A$113m; FY26 EBIT guided A$135m).

**Bear case**
- Long history of cost overruns and execution problems at Austal USA; fixed-price contracts amplify margin risk.
- Low, volatile returns on capital; net income has swung from losses to profit across recent years.
- Customer concentration on US/Australian government budgets and program timing.
- Strategic-ownership uncertainty (Hanwha vs Tattarang, FIRB/CFIUS constraints) is a two-sided overhang.

## Key Risks

- **Execution / cost overruns** — fixed-price defence contracts; past US margin blowouts make cost headlines high-impact.
- **Customer concentration** — dependence on US Navy/Coast Guard and ADF program funding and timing.
- **Regulatory / national-security** — FIRB, CFIUS-equivalent controls on foreign ownership and sensitive information (central to the Hanwha situation).
- **Strategic-stake uncertainty** — the Hanwha/Tattarang standoff can move the stock sharply in either direction.
- **Legacy legal/accounting overhang** — the prior US DOJ/ASIC accounting saga.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Austal is one of the few listed **pure-play [[defense|defence]]** exposures on the ASX, so it captures flows whenever the defence-spending theme runs — but **M&A/strategic-stake news is the dominant price driver**, not earnings multiples. Mechanically:

- **Valuation lens** — the trailing PE (~17x) and P/B (~1.5x) look undemanding for a defence name, reflecting low/volatile historic margins; the market is really pricing order-book conversion and takeover optionality rather than steady-state earnings.
- **Dividend** — modest/intermittent; capital is largely reinvested in capacity (Mobile, submarine modules), so this is a capital-appreciation/strategic-value story rather than an income one.
- **Beta / behaviour** — small/mid-cap (~A$2bn) industrial; the stock gaps on contract awards, Hanwha/Tattarang stake moves, AUKUS headlines and any Austal-USA cost-overrun news.
- **Liquidity/options** — small/mid-cap ASX liquidity; no liquid single-name options — shares only.
- **Correlated names** — Hanwha Ocean/Aerospace, Huntington Ingalls, General Dynamics, and ASX defence-adjacent names (Codan, EOS); AUKUS news moves the whole cluster.

## Recent Developments

For FY25 Austal reported record revenue of A$1.8 billion and EBIT of A$113 million, with FY26 EBIT guidance of A$135 million. For H1 FY26 (six months to 31 December 2025) Austal reported revenue of A$1.1 billion and EBIT of A$60.3 million, up 41.3% year-on-year. In early 2026 Austal was awarded a ~A$4 billion (US$2.8 billion) contract for eight Landing Craft Heavy (LCH) vessels for the Australian Defence Force, its largest-ever Australian defence contract, taking its order book to approximately A$17.7 billion. Korean defence conglomerate Hanwha Aerospace acquired an initial 9.9% stake in March 2025; a full takeover approach lapsed amid regulatory concerns, but on 12 December 2025 the Treasurer approved Hanwha increasing its stake to 19.9%, making it Austal's largest shareholder (ahead of Andrew Forrest's Tattarang at ~19.28%), with strict conditions on board nominees and access to sensitive information.

The most recent filing (annual) was lodged on 2023-06-30. See [[#Financial Reports]] below.
