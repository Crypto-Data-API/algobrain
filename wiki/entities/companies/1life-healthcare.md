This page is written in past tense and retained as a historical case study in pandemic-era telehealth IPOs and [[merger-arbitrage]]. Nothing here implies the stock still trades.

1Life Healthcare, Inc. (formerly NASDAQ: ONEM), operating as **One Medical**, was a membership-based US primary-care and telehealth platform. It no longer trades: [[amazon|Amazon]] acquired the company for approximately **$3.9 billion ($18.00 per share in cash)** — announced July 2022, closed February 2023 — and ONEM was delisted from NASDAQ. The page is retained for historical reference and as a case study in pandemic-era healthcare IPOs and [[merger-arbitrage]].

## What the Business Was

One Medical built a **membership-based, tech-enabled primary-care model**: members paid an annual fee for an enhanced primary-care experience — same-day appointments, a polished app for booking/messaging/virtual visits, and modern clinics — layered on top of conventional insurance billing. It deliberately blended in-person care (a national footprint of branded offices) with [[artificial-intelligence|telehealth]], positioning itself as a consumer-grade front door to healthcare.

### Business segments

| Segment | What it was | Notes |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| **Consumer / commercial membership** | Annual-fee primary care for individuals and via employers | The original brand and growth engine; benefited from the pandemic telehealth surge |
| **At-risk / Medicare (Iora Health)** | Value-based, capitated senior care acquired in 2021 (~$2.1B all-stock) | Pushed One Medical into at-risk reimbursement; widened losses materially |
| **Enterprise / employer health** | B2B contracts to provide One Medical access as a benefit | Distribution channel and recurring revenue base |

The structural tension: the membership/consumer model and the capitated senior-care model had very different economics, and bolting on Iora widened losses just as the market turned against unprofitable digital health.

## Company History

- **2007** — founded in San Francisco as a tech-enabled, membership-fee primary-care network (One Medical brand).
- **January 2020** — IPO on NASDAQ at $14 per share, riding the telehealth/digital-health wave; the stock peaked above $50 during the 2020-2021 pandemic telehealth boom.
- **2021** — acquired Iora Health (~$2.1B all-stock) to expand into Medicare-focused senior care; losses widened and the stock de-rated sharply with the rest of unprofitable digital health.
- **July 2022** — Amazon agreed to acquire 1Life Healthcare for $18.00/share cash (~$3.9B), a ~77% premium to the pre-announcement price.
- **February 2023** — deal closed despite FTC scrutiny of Amazon's healthcare ambitions; ONEM shares ceased trading.

## AI Involvement

One Medical marketed an [[artificial-intelligence|AI]]-enhanced primary-care delivery platform (triage, scheduling, and clinical-workflow automation). Post-acquisition, the technology was folded into Amazon Health Services alongside Amazon Pharmacy and Prime-integrated One Medical memberships.

## Competitive Positioning at the Time

One Medical sat at the intersection of bricks-and-mortar primary care and the pandemic-era telehealth boom. Its edge was brand, UX, and an integrated in-person + virtual model; its weakness was capital intensity (real clinics, clinicians) and persistent losses, especially after layering on at-risk Medicare economics.

| Peer | Positioning vs. One Medical | Contrast |
|------|------------------------------|----------|
| [[teladoc]] (TDOC) | Pure-play virtual care at scale; no clinic footprint | Asset-light vs. One Medical's hybrid clinics; also de-rated hard post-2021, but remained independent |
| Amwell (AMWL) | Telehealth platform/infrastructure | Software-platform tilt vs. One Medical's owned-care delivery |
| Oscar Health, Bright Health (insurtech) | Insurance-led digital health | Payer model vs. provider model |
| Traditional primary care / payers' clinics (CVS/Aetna, UnitedHealth Optum, Walgreens VillageMD) | Incumbent scale, captive payer distribution | The deep-pocketed strategics that ultimately set the M&A backdrop for primary-care roll-ups |

One Medical's competitive arc rhymed with [[teladoc]]'s: both were repriced violently when the market shifted from "growth at any cost" to "path to profit." The difference in outcome was that One Medical found a strategic acquirer ([[amazon]]) at a large premium, while Teladoc remained a (battered) standalone.

## The Deal: Amazon Acquisition

Amazon agreed to acquire 1Life Healthcare in July 2022 at **$18.00/share in cash (~$3.9B)**, a ~77% premium to the pre-announcement price, and closed in February 2023.

- **Consideration:** all-cash — no exchange-ratio risk for ONEM holders, only timing and antitrust/break risk.
- **Strategic rationale:** Amazon sought a physical + virtual primary-care delivery network to anchor Amazon Health Services, integrate with Amazon Pharmacy, and bundle One Medical memberships into Prime — a "front door" to healthcare for its consumer base.
- **Regulatory backdrop:** the deal closed despite FTC scrutiny of Amazon's broader healthcare ambitions; the FTC did not block it but flagged ongoing concerns.

## Merger-Arb / Trading Postmortem

A clean all-cash deal whose spread was driven almost entirely by **antitrust headline risk**, not exchange-ratio or financing risk:

- **The spread.** The ~7-month gap between announcement (Jul 2022) and close (Feb 2023) left ONEM trading below the $18 cash price. That discount *was* the [[merger-arbitrage]] spread — compensation for the probability the FTC blocked or materially delayed the deal.
- **What moved it.** Because Amazon was already under intense antitrust scrutiny, the spread widened on adverse FTC/regulatory headlines and tightened as clearance looked more likely. Arb desks were effectively pricing the probability of an FTC challenge.
- **Outcome.** The deal closed at terms ($18 cash), so holders who bought the discount and held to close earned the full spread. Risk that *could* have materialized (an FTC block) did not.
- **Contrast with collapse cases.** Unlike [[nikola]] / [[view-inc]], ONEM holders were taken out at a large premium in cash — the difference between a company that found a strategic buyer and one that ran out of money.

## Lessons / What Defined the Outcome

- **Telehealth boom/bust cycle**: ONEM rose ~250% from IPO to peak, then fell ~80% as rates rose and unprofitable growth de-rated — a textbook example of theme-driven multiple expansion and collapse (2020-2022).
- **A premium exit rescued a de-rated story.** The strategic value to [[amazon]] (distribution, Prime, vertical integration) exceeded what public markets would pay for a loss-making provider — the acquirer, not the market, set the re-rating.
- **Merger-arb case**: the 7-month spread between announcement (Jul 2022) and close (Feb 2023) traded wide at times on FTC antitrust risk around Amazon; the deal ultimately closed at terms.
- **Read-through name**: today, exposure to One Medical economics comes only via [[amazon]] (healthcare segment disclosures, Prime membership integration).

## Risks That Materialized (for an independent One Medical thesis)

- **Profitability / cash-burn risk:** chronic losses (widened by the Iora acquisition) repriced the equity ~80% off its peak before the bid.
- **Rate-sensitivity:** as a long-duration, unprofitable growth name, ONEM de-rated sharply as rates rose in 2022.
- **Loss of independence:** no investable standalone vehicle remains; exposure exists only inside [[amazon]].
