S&P Global (NYSE: SPGI) is one of the world's dominant financial-data and benchmarks franchises: it owns the S&P credit-ratings agency (a duopoly with [[moodys|Moody's]]), the [[s-and-p-500]] index family via S&P Dow Jones Indices, the [[sp-platts|Platts]] commodity benchmarks, and the Market Intelligence data platform. It is itself a constituent of the index it administers — and one of the highest-weighted [[financials]] names in the [[s-and-p-500]] — making it a classic wide-moat compounder whose earnings are geared to debt-issuance volumes, index/[[etf|ETF]] asset growth, and recurring subscription data. The business sits at the intersection of three oligopolies (credit ratings, equity indices, commodity price assessment), each with deep [[switching-costs]] and regulatory or network-effect barriers to entry.

## Business Overview & Segments

S&P Global operates a portfolio of recurring-revenue and transaction-geared franchises. As of 2025 it reports across five segments, with the Mobility unit slated to separate (see below). Roughly two-thirds of company revenue is recurring (subscriptions, index licensing, surveillance fees), and the balance is transaction-linked (new-issue ratings) — a mix that gives the model both defensiveness and upside torque to the credit cycle.

| Segment | What it does | Revenue character |
|---|---|---|
| **Ratings** | Credit ratings on corporate/sovereign/structured debt — the largest profit pool | Half transaction (new-issue), half recurring (surveillance); geared to [[bonds]] issuance cycles |
| **Market Intelligence** | Desktop/data feeds (Capital IQ), enterprise data, credit & risk analytics | Sticky subscription revenue — low churn, high retention |
| **Commodity Insights** | [[sp-platts|Platts]] price assessments (Dated Brent, JKM, iron ore 62% Fe) and energy/commodity analytics | Subscription + benchmark licensing |
| **Indices** | S&P Dow Jones Indices — licensing fees on ~trillions of indexed/[[etf|ETF]] AUM (SPY, index futures/options) | Asset-linked licensing + data; scales with passive AUM |
| **Mobility** | CARFAX, automotive data and analytics — being spun off in 2026 | Subscription; to become standalone |

The 2022 merger with IHS Markit (~$44B all-stock) created the current portfolio and roughly doubled the non-ratings, subscription-heavy base — the temporary balance-sheet distortions of that deal explain the unusual ROE/D-E swings in the fundamentals trend below. The strategic logic was to diversify away from the cyclical Ratings franchise toward recurring data and analytics, deepening cross-sell across the Market Intelligence and Commodity Insights platforms.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

S&P Global holds one of the widest [[economic-moat|economic moats]] in financial services, resting on three reinforcing oligopolies rather than a single advantage:

- **Ratings duopoly** — S&P and [[moodys|Moody's]] together rate the overwhelming majority of rated global debt, with Fitch a distant third. The franchise is protected by NRSRO (Nationally Recognized Statistical Rating Organization) regulatory recognition, decades of issuer relationships, and the simple fact that bond covenants, regulatory capital rules, and index-inclusion criteria are written around S&P/Moody's ratings — a self-reinforcing standard that is extraordinarily hard to dislodge.
- **Index licensing oligopoly** — the [[s-and-p-500]] is the single most-tracked benchmark on earth. Once an index becomes the reference for trillions in [[etf|ETF]] and derivative assets (SPY, E-mini futures, index options), the benchmark itself is a near-irreplaceable standard. S&P Dow Jones Indices, [[msci]], and FTSE Russell ([[london-stock-exchange-group|LSEG]]) form a tight oligopoly; competing on price is nearly impossible because the value is in the brand and incumbency, not the calculation.
- **Recurring subscription data** — Capital IQ, Platts assessments, and Market Intelligence feeds embed into client workflows, creating high [[switching-costs]]: re-papering an entire research, risk, or pricing process onto a rival platform imposes cost and operational risk most clients refuse to bear.

The cumulative result is high pricing power, ~90%+ retention in subscription lines, and operating margins that sit among the highest in the [[financials]] sector. The moat is wide but not frictionless — ratings regulation, index fee compression, and the rise of cheaper data alternatives are the long-term watch items (see Key Risks).

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Debt-issuance super-cycle** — Ratings revenue is a direct derivative of global [[bonds]] issuance; refinancing waves, M&A-driven leveraged finance, and falling-rate refi windows are tailwinds.
- **Passive / ETF AUM growth** — index licensing scales with the secular shift to passive investing; every dollar that flows into S&P-benchmarked [[etf|ETFs]] and index derivatives lifts asset-linked fees.
- **Subscription data expansion** — Market Intelligence and Commodity Insights grow with seat counts, price increases, and cross-sell across the merged IHS Markit base.
- **Private-markets & alternatives data** — a fast-growing demand pool (private credit, fund analytics) where S&P is building out coverage.
- **AI / analytics layering** — generative-AI tooling on top of proprietary datasets (Kensho, Capital IQ Pro) as a monetization and retention lever.
- **Recurring catalysts** — monthly/quarterly global issuance volumes, [[etf|ETF]] flow data, quarterly earnings, the Mobility spin timeline, and index-rebalance events.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Three oligopoly franchises with pricing power, ~90%+ subscription retention, and sector-leading margins — a genuine wide-moat compounder.
- Secular tailwinds: passive/[[etf|ETF]] growth lifts index licensing; structural debt-issuance growth lifts Ratings over a cycle.
- Recurring-revenue mix (~two-thirds) dampens the cyclicality of the legacy Ratings business and supports a premium multiple.
- The Mobility spin sharpens the portfolio around the highest-quality data/benchmarks core and can surface sum-of-the-parts value.
- Strong free cash flow funds a long dividend-raise streak and consistent buybacks.

### Bear case
- Premium valuation (see Fundamentals) leaves the stock exposed to multiple compression in risk-off regimes — the Q4 2025 EPS-miss sell-off is a live example.
- Ratings revenue is cyclical: an issuance freeze (rate shock, credit-market stress) hits the highest-margin segment first and hardest.
- Index fee compression as [[msci]]/FTSE and low-cost benchmark providers compete on price.
- Regulatory and litigation overhang on the ratings franchise (a recurring post-2008 theme).
- Integration and execution risk around portfolio reshaping (IHS Markit absorption, Mobility separation).

## Key Risks

- **Issuance cyclicality** — Ratings, the largest profit pool, swings with global [[bonds]] issuance; a synchronized issuance pause compresses revenue and the multiple at once.
- **Ratings regulation & litigation** — NRSRO oversight, conflict-of-interest scrutiny, and periodic litigation are structural risks for any rating agency.
- **Valuation / multiple compression** — a premium compounder multiple means SPGI de-rates hard on any growth wobble (the early-2026 drawdown).
- **Index fee compression** — competitive pressure on licensing economics from [[msci]], FTSE Russell ([[london-stock-exchange-group|LSEG]]) and cheaper benchmark alternatives.
- **Integration / spin execution** — risk around the Mobility separation and continued IHS Markit synergy capture.
- **Data substitution** — long-run threat from lower-cost or AI-native data platforms eroding subscription pricing power.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

S&P Global trades at a premium compounder multiple that reflects its wide moat, recurring-revenue mix, and pricing power rather than a cheap valuation — the debate is whether the franchise can keep compounding fast enough to justify the multiple (see the Fundamentals block below for the figures as pulled). Mechanically:

- **Premium multiple** — among the higher-rated names in [[financials]]; quality and durability are priced in, so the stock de-rates sharply on any growth or guidance disappointment (the Q4 2025 GAAP EPS miss triggered a roughly quarter-off-the-highs sell-off into early 2026).
- **Capital return** — a long, aristocrat-style multi-decade dividend-raise history plus consistent buybacks; the dividend yield is modest, with total return driven by compounding earnings and repurchases rather than yield.
- **Beta** — moderate; lower-octane than high-beta tech megacaps but more cyclical than a pure staples name because of the Ratings issuance gearing.
- **Index torque** — a meaningful [[financials]] and [[s-and-p-500]] constituent; it is, notably, a member of the very index it administers.
- **Options** — liquid single-stock options around earnings and the spin timeline; not a zero-DTE / leveraged-ETF battleground like the megacaps.
- **Issuance-cycle proxy** — Ratings revenue makes SPGI a liquid macro expression of credit-issuance volumes; it rallies with refinancing waves and falls when issuance windows shut.
- **Duopoly pair** — a classic pairs candidate against [[moodys]] (MCO); both trade on issuance and recurring-data multiples, so relative-value trades key off divergences in segment mix and valuation.
- **Spin-off event** — the mid-2026 Mobility separation is a live special-situations catalyst (index-rebalancing flows, when-issued trading, potential spin-off undervaluation).
