DoorDash (NYSE: DASH) is the dominant US food-delivery marketplace (roughly two-thirds US share) and, after acquiring Deliveroo in 2025, a global local-commerce platform spanning restaurants, grocery, retail, and advertising. A [[consumer-discretionary|Consumer Discretionary]] constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]] (added March 2025), it is a high-multiple growth name whose stock trades on order growth, take-rate, and the path from thin GAAP profits to its large adjusted-EBITDA stream.

## Business Overview

DoorDash monetizes its marketplace through commissions on Marketplace GOV, consumer fees, the DashPass subscription, a fast-growing advertising business, and white-label logistics (Drive). New-vertical expansion (grocery, convenience, retail) and international scale via Wolt (acquired 2022) and Deliveroo are the growth pillars. The Fundamentals section's FY2024 figures capture the first full GAAP-profitable year after a decade of losses; profitability has since scaled. Main competitors: Uber Eats ([[uber]]), Instacart, and in Europe Just Eat Takeaway and Delivery Hero.

## 2025–2026 Developments

- **2025-10-02:** completed the ~£2.9B acquisition of Deliveroo plc, consolidating the UK and parts of Europe; Deliveroo expected to contribute ~US$200M to 2026 adjusted EBITDA.
- **2025:** closed the ~US$1.2B acquisition of SevenRooms (restaurant reservations/CRM); six months post-close it was adding venues ~50% faster than pre-acquisition.
- **Q1 2026 (reported May 2026):** revenue +33% YoY to US$4.04B (+21% ex-Deliveroo); total orders +27% to 933M; Marketplace GOV +37% to US$31.6B; adjusted EBITDA US$754M; GAAP net income US$184M (-5% YoY on integration costs); net revenue margin 12.8%.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

DoorDash holds a **moderate-to-wide [[economic-moat|moat]]** in its home market built primarily on **[[network-effects]] and local liquidity density**: more consumers attract more restaurants and more couriers (Dashers), which shortens delivery times and lowers costs, which attracts still more consumers — a self-reinforcing flywheel that is hardest to replicate in the dense markets DoorDash already leads. Reinforcing layers: (1) **scale economics** — fixed logistics, ad, and engineering costs amortized over the largest US order base, giving a structural unit-cost edge over sub-scale rivals; (2) **DashPass [[switching-costs]]** — subscribers concentrate spend on DoorDash; (3) **two-sided data** — routing, demand-prediction, and ad-targeting data that improve with volume; and (4) **merchant integrations** (POS, SevenRooms reservations/CRM) that embed DoorDash into restaurant operations. The moat is local and contestable rather than absolute — it is strong where DoorDash is #1 by density but thinner in markets where [[uber]] Eats leads, and the whole category historically struggled to convert share into durable profit. The 2024-25 swing to GAAP profitability is the evidence the flywheel finally produces operating leverage at scale.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **New verticals** — grocery, convenience, and retail extend the same logistics network across a far larger TAM; the central domestic growth story.
- **Advertising** — a high-incremental-margin business scaling on top of marketplace traffic; the key margin-expansion lever.
- **International integration** — Wolt and Deliveroo add scale and density; synergy realization (Deliveroo ~$200M to 2026 adjusted EBITDA) is a watched catalyst.
- **DashPass penetration** — subscription growth lifts order frequency and retention.
- **Merchant SaaS (SevenRooms, Drive)** — diversifies revenue into rate-insensitive, recurring tooling.
- **Autonomous / drone delivery** — long-dated cost-structure optionality.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly order/GOV growth, take-rate and ads disclosures, integration synergies, gig-labor regulation headlines, and S&P 500-flow effects.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Dominant US share with a genuine local-density [[network-effects]] flywheel now throwing off real operating leverage.
- Multiple growth vectors stacked: new verticals, advertising, international, DashPass — a long runway off a proven core.
- Inflection from a decade of losses to scaling GAAP profitability and a large adjusted-EBITDA stream.
- Advertising and merchant SaaS add high-margin, more durable revenue mix.

### Bear case
- **Premium valuation** — an extreme trailing P/E (see Fundamentals) leaves no room for growth or margin misses.
- **Gig-labor regulation** — minimum-pay laws (NYC, Seattle), the EU platform-work directive, and reclassification risk raise the cost base.
- **Take-rate compression** — competition with [[uber]] Eats and merchant pushback on commissions can cap monetization.
- **Cyclicality** — discretionary delivery spend is consumer-sensitive; a downturn pressures order growth.
- **Integration risk** — Deliveroo/Wolt/SevenRooms must deliver promised synergies.

## Key Risks

- **Gig-economy regulation** — the defining structural risk; minimum-pay and worker-classification rules directly raise unit costs (NYC/Seattle minimum-pay laws, EU platform-work directive).
- **Valuation / positioning** — a very high multiple (see PE in Fundamentals) makes the stock a long-duration, rate-sensitive growth name prone to sharp de-rates on any miss.
- **Competition** — [[uber]] Eats overlap and Instacart in grocery pressure share and take-rates.
- **Integration execution** — realizing Deliveroo/Wolt/SevenRooms synergies without margin leakage.
- **Consumer cyclicality** — discretionary, fee-laden orders soften when the consumer pulls back.
- **Thin GAAP margins** — profitability, while inflecting, is still slim relative to the adjusted-EBITDA headline; the gap (stock-based comp, integration) is a watch item.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

DoorDash trades as a **high-multiple consumer-internet growth stock** valued on order/GOV growth, take-rate, and the path from thin GAAP profit to its large adjusted-EBITDA stream rather than on a normalized P/E (the trailing multiple is extreme — see Fundamentals). Mechanically:

- **Long-duration growth name** — rate-sensitive; de-rates hard when discount rates rise or growth decelerates.
- **EV/revenue and unit-economics lens** — given the slim GAAP base, the market leans on EV/revenue, contribution margin per order, and adjusted EBITDA more than earnings multiples.
- **Index torque** — added to the [[s-and-p-500]] in March 2025 (the inclusion itself was a squeeze catalyst); now subject to index-flow effects.
- **Options** — very liquid; high single-digit implied earnings moves; IV elevated into prints.
- **Correlated names** — [[uber]] (closest comp), Instacart, Delivery Hero, and high-beta consumer-internet baskets.
- **No dividend** — total return is price-only; capital is reinvested into growth.
