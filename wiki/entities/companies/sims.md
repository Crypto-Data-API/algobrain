**Sims Limited** (ASX: SGM) is a global metal-recycling and circular-economy company listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. For traders it is a leveraged play on ferrous/non-ferrous scrap spreads, Chinese steel-export pressure and — increasingly — AI data-centre demand via its IT-asset recycling arm Sims Lifecycle Services. The company is headquartered at 189 O'Riordan Street, Mascot, New South Wales, and employs approximately 3,920 people.

## Business Model & Segments

Sims reports across five operating segments:

- North America Metal - collection and processing of ferrous and non-ferrous scrap metals across North America, via legacy Sims Metal operations and partnerships.
- Australia/New Zealand Metal - scrap metal collection, processing and trading across Australia and New Zealand.
- Investment in SA Recycling - 50% joint venture interest in SA Recycling, one of the largest metal recyclers in the US, operating more than 130 facilities across 15 states alongside JV partner Adams Steel.
- Sims Lifecycle Services - repurposing and recycling of IT assets and electronic equipment for commercial and public-sector customers, including data destruction, asset recovery and parts harvesting.
- Global Trading Operations - secondary processing, stevedoring and trading activities.

Core product lines include recycled iron and steel; non-ferrous metal alloys and residues, principally aluminium, lead, copper, zinc and nickel-bearing materials; and a growing suite of IT asset lifecycle services.

## History

Sims was founded in 1917 in Sydney by Albert Sims, a recycled metals dealer. Sims had migrated to Australia from England in 1913, pawning his wife's wedding ring to start a scrap metals collection business. His bicycle was eventually upgraded to a horse and cart, and in 1917 he formally started his scrap collection business. The business was incorporated as Albert G. Sims Limited in 1928 and was renamed Simsmetal Limited in November 1968.

In November 1970, Sims merged with Consolidated Metal Products Limited and the merged ASX-listed company was renamed Sims Consolidated Limited. In February 1988, Sims entered the US scrap recycling market with the acquisition of LMC Corporation in California. In February 1995, Sims acquired a 51% stake in Sims Bird Limited in the UK, with the remaining 49% acquired in May 1998.

A transformational acquisition came in March 2008, when Sims acquired Metal Management Inc. (MMI), creating what was then the world's leading publicly traded recycling company. In November 2008, shareholders approved the corporate name change to Sims Metal Management Limited. The company later simplified its name to Sims Limited.

Over subsequent years Sims has focused on adding higher-margin non-ferrous capacity, investing in technology-driven shredder and sorting infrastructure, and expanding Sims Lifecycle Services (formerly Sims Recycling Solutions) as a global e-waste and IT asset disposal business. The 50% interest in SA Recycling has grown into one of the group's most significant earnings contributors.

## Recent Developments

For FY25 (year ended 30 June 2025), Sims reported sales revenue of A$7,494.0 million, up 4.1% on FY24 (A$7,195.4 million). The revenue increase was assisted by a 4.0% rise in metal average sales prices, supported by a higher non-ferrous component, partially offset by a 3.8% reduction in overall proprietary metal sales volumes. Sims Lifecycle Services revenue was 22.0% higher in FY25 than in FY24.

FY25 underlying EBIT rose 198.0% to A$174.9 million (versus A$58.7 million in FY24), reflecting better pricing, higher-margin product mix and operating leverage across the recycling platform.

Sims continues to position itself as a key enabler of the circular economy, with sustainability and decarbonisation strategies aimed at growing low-carbon metal recycling, expanding IT asset lifecycle services and leveraging its 50% stake in SA Recycling in the growing US recycling market.

### H1 FY26 (reported 17 February 2026)

- Underlying EBIT of A$121.1 million for the half-year ended 31 December 2025 — described by management as "a good solid result in a difficult market".
- Sims Lifecycle Services underlying EBIT jumped 247.5%, driven by global demand for used DDR4 memory chips as hyperscale and AI data-centre builds accelerate — SLS has become the swing growth driver of the group.
- North America Metal and SA Recycling both delivered higher trading margins and improved profitability despite lower processed-scrap sales volumes.
- ANZ saw softer ferrous markets on high Chinese steel exports, partially offset by stronger non-ferrous trading.
- The interim dividend was increased and FY26 underlying EBIT guidance was set at **A$350–400 million** — roughly double FY25's A$174.9 million; the stock rallied on the guidance upgrade.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Metal recycling has no pricing power: Sims buys scrap and sells recovered metal, and earns the **spread** between the two. Like a commodity price-taker in [[materials]], it cannot set the price of either side — ferrous and non-ferrous metal prices are set in global markets, and the realised margin is whatever the spread allows after collection, processing and freight costs. There is no classic [[economic-moat]] of brand, switching costs or pricing power here. What competitive advantage exists is structural and operational:

- **Scale and density of collection/processing infrastructure** — recycling economics reward dense networks of feeder yards, shredders and ports. Sims' North American and ANZ footprints, plus deep-water export capability, lower unit cost and secure scrap flow in a fragmented industry.
- **The 50% SA Recycling joint venture** — one of the largest metal recyclers in the US (130+ facilities across 15 states with JV partner Adams Steel). It is one of the group's most significant earnings contributors and gives Sims scale in the world's largest scrap market without consolidating the full cost base.
- **Shredder and sorting technology** — investment in advanced separation lifts recovery of higher-margin **non-ferrous** metals (aluminium, copper, zinc, lead, nickel-bearing residues) from the shredder stream, where most of the value sits versus low-margin ferrous.
- **Sims Lifecycle Services (SLS)** — the structurally growing, higher-margin, lower-cyclicality layer: IT-asset disposition, data destruction, asset recovery and parts harvesting. SLS has become an **AI-data-centre play** — hyperscale/AI build-outs are driving demand for used DDR4 memory and decommissioned IT hardware (SLS underlying EBIT jumped 247.5% in H1 FY26).

Net: Sims is a **deep cyclical with a circular-economy / AI optionality layer**. The scrap business is a spread-taker; the genuine edge is scale plus the SLS growth annuity sitting on top of it.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Ferrous scrap spreads** — HMS 80:20 pricing versus collection cost; the bulk-volume earnings lever.
- **Non-ferrous mix** — higher-margin aluminium/copper/zinc recovery; a richer mix lifts blended margin even at flat volumes.
- **Chinese steel-export volumes** — heavy Chinese exports pressure ANZ ferrous markets (a recurring drag flagged in recent results); easing exports is a tailwind.
- **US scrap spreads & SA Recycling earnings** — the 50% JV is a major swing factor on group profit.
- **AI-driven DDR4 / IT-asset boom** — hyperscale and AI data-centre build-outs lifting demand for used memory and decommissioned hardware; the SLS swing driver (EBIT +247.5% in H1 FY26).
- **Circular-economy / decarbonisation tailwind** — long-run structural demand for recycled (low-carbon) metal.
- **Calendar catalysts** — half-year (February) and full-year (August) results and EBIT-guidance revisions; the market re-rates hard on guidance (the FY26 A$350–400m upgrade drove a rally).

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Cyclical recovery in scrap spreads compounding with a richer non-ferrous mix lifts margins off a trough.
- **SLS / AI growth** is doubling group EBIT guidance (FY26 A$350–400m vs FY25 A$174.9m) and adds a secular, less-cyclical earnings leg.
- Circular-economy and decarbonisation create a structural tailwind for recycled metal demand.
- Conservative balance sheet (low D/E ~0.2x) gives room to invest through the cycle and fund the dividend.
- The 50% SA Recycling JV provides scale exposure to the recovering US scrap market.

### Bear case
- **Deep cyclicality** — earnings swing from large profits to losses on scrap-spread moves (negative net income in FY24–FY25).
- Chinese steel-export flooding structurally pressures ANZ ferrous markets.
- Scrap-spread and volume volatility make earnings hard to forecast; trough/negative headline P/E.
- **No meaningful listed-options market** — limited ways to hedge or express views beyond the underlying.
- SLS upside is itself tied to the AI/data-centre capex cycle, which can cool.

## Key Risks

- **Scrap-spread compression** — the core earnings driver; spreads narrow fast in soft markets.
- **Chinese steel-export flooding** — depresses regional ferrous prices and ANZ volumes.
- **Volume softness** — lower processed-scrap volumes erode operating leverage even when prices hold.
- **Non-ferrous price reversal** — a pullback in aluminium/copper hits the higher-margin mix.
- **JV-earnings dependence** — a large share of profit rides on the 50% SA Recycling JV, outside full operational control.
- **SLS demand tied to the AI/data-centre cycle** — the fastest-growing leg is exposed to a capex cycle that can turn.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Sims is a **classic deep cyclical**, and its valuation must be read as one:

- **Headline P/E is meaningless at the trough** — earnings were negative in FY24–FY25, making the reported P/E (deeply negative) uninformative. Through-cycle normalised earnings, EV/EBITDA and price-to-book are more useful frames than a static P/E.
- **It trades on scrap-spread momentum and EBIT-guidance revisions** — the market re-rates on the direction of spreads and management's EBIT outlook far more than on trailing earnings (the stock rallied on the FY26 A$350–400m guidance upgrade, ~double FY25).
- **High beta to global steel and aluminium** — loosely tracks steel/aluminium prices and the broader [[materials]] cycle; correlated (not directly) with [[bhp]], [[south32]] and [[bluescope-steel]].
- **No meaningful listed-options market** — views are expressed in the shares or via ASX [[materials]] baskets, not single-stock options.
- **Modest fully-franked dividend** — a payout-ratio story that flexes with the cycle, carrying [[franking-credits]] valuable to Australian residents; not a growth-dividend.
