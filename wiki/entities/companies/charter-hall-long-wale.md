**Charter Hall Long WALE REIT** (ASX: CLW) is a diversified Australasian net-lease property trust listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index, managed by [[charter-hall|Charter Hall Group]]. A [[real-estate|real estate]] [[reits|REIT]] designed to deliver long-dated, inflation-linked income, its portfolio is deliberately weighted toward assets leased to investment-grade corporate and government tenants on very long leases (a long weighted-average lease expiry, or "long WALE"), with a strong bias to CPI-linked rental escalation. Because its cash flows are long, contracted and inflation-linked, CLW trades as a classic **bond proxy** — its NTA and unit price are highly sensitive to long-bond yields and cap rates.

## Business Overview & Segments

CLW targets assets characterised by long lease terms (typically 10 years or more at acquisition), high-quality counterparties, and embedded rent growth. The portfolio spans multiple property sectors, including industrial and logistics, office, retail (including convenience and long-WALE large format), hospitality (pubs), agri-logistics and social infrastructure. Key tenants have historically included Endeavour Group (ALH pubs), Coles, BP, Australian government agencies, Inghams, and Telstra. Revenue is generated from recurring rental income, with growth coming from contracted rent reviews, asset-level value-add initiatives, and selective acquisitions and divestments.

| Portfolio segment | What it is | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Industrial & logistics** | Warehouses, distribution, agri-logistics (e.g. Inghams) | Growth tilt; tight-vacancy metro logistics |
| **Long-WALE retail** | Convenience and large-format net-lease retail; BP service stations | Defensive, long-dated, often CPI-linked |
| **Hospitality (pubs)** | ALH/Endeavour pub portfolio (JV with CQR/Hostplus) | Very long leases to a single strong counterparty |
| **Office** | Government- and corporate-leased office | Long leases but the most cyclical/structurally challenged segment |
| **Social infrastructure / other** | Government-leased and specialised assets | Sticky, investment-grade counterparties |

The unifying thread is **lease duration and tenant quality**: ~99.9% occupancy, a ~9-year WALE and ~52% of income on CPI-linked reviews make the income stream look like an inflation-linked bond secured against property.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

CLW does not have a wide operating moat in the franchise sense — net-lease REITs are largely undifferentiated rent collectors. Its durable advantages are **portfolio-construction and sponsor-driven**:

- **Lease duration & tenant credit** — a ~9-year WALE to investment-grade and government tenants makes the cash flow exceptionally predictable and the income defensive.
- **Inflation linkage** — ~52% CPI-linked rent reviews provide structural protection that pure fixed-rate net-lease portfolios lack.
- **[[charter-hall|Charter Hall]] sponsorship** — access to the manager's origination, leasing and development pipeline and balance-sheet co-investment; mandates and deals flow through the platform.
- **Investment-grade balance sheet** — a Moody's Baa1 rating lowers cost of debt versus smaller, unrated peers — a real cost-of-capital edge in a capital-intensive business.

The competitive position is therefore about **cost of capital and asset quality**, not pricing power: CLW competes for long-lease assets against other yield buyers, and its edge is cheaper funding plus the Charter Hall pipeline.

## History

Charter Hall Long WALE REIT was established and listed on the ASX in November 2016 via an initial public offering, seeded with a portfolio of long-leased assets assembled by Charter Hall Group. Since listing, CLW has grown materially through several major acquisitions, including the 2020 purchase of a 49% interest in the BP Australia service station portfolio, the 2021 acquisition of the ALH hotels portfolio (in joint venture with Charter Hall Retail REIT and Hostplus), and various industrial and government-leased office acquisitions.

## Recent Developments

CLW released its half-year results for the period to 31 December 2025 (HY26) on 12 February 2026. Net tangible assets (NTA) per security rose to A$4.68, a 2% increase from June 2025. The trust reported operating earnings of 12.75 cents per security, up 2% on the prior corresponding period. Portfolio occupancy was 99.9% and weighted average lease expiry was 9.2 years, with approximately 52% of portfolio income subject to CPI-linked rent reviews. Like-for-like net property income growth was 3%. Moody's reaffirmed CLW's Baa1 investment-grade credit rating during the period. For FY25, the REIT had previously reported a small net decline of A$9 million (0.1%) across its A$5.5 billion portfolio, with NTA per security moving from A$4.66 to A$4.59.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Falling long-bond yields / cap-rate compression** — the primary upside driver; lower rates lift NTA and re-rate the unit price (CLW de-rated heavily as rates rose 2022–24, and recovers as they ease).
- **Contracted rent reviews** — CPI-linked and fixed escalations deliver organic income growth without new capital.
- **Accretive acquisitions & developments** — sourced via the Charter Hall pipeline when cost of capital allows.
- **Asset recycling** — selling tightly-priced assets to fund higher-yielding or longer-WALE opportunities and to reduce gearing.
- **Distribution growth & guidance** — operating-EPS and distribution guidance upgrades.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year results (operating EPS, NTA, gearing, occupancy, WALE), revaluations, refinancing terms, and Moody's rating actions.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Defensive, contracted, inflation-linked income with ~99.9% occupancy and a ~9-year WALE — among the most resilient cash flows on the ASX.
- Levered to falling rates: NTA and unit price recover sharply as long yields ease and cap rates compress.
- Investment-grade (Baa1) balance sheet lowers funding cost and supports the distribution.
- Charter Hall sponsorship provides deal flow, leasing and development optionality.
- Often trades below NTA, offering a discount-to-asset-value entry for income investors.

### Bear case
- Pure bond proxy — rising or higher-for-longer rates compress both NTA and the unit price.
- Office exposure is structurally challenged and a drag on revaluations.
- Tenant concentration (Endeavour/ALH pubs, government) means a single large counterparty matters.
- Gearing and covenant headroom are pressured in a falling-asset-value environment.
- Limited organic growth beyond contracted reviews; external growth needs cheap capital.

## Key Risks

- **Interest-rate / cap-rate sensitivity** — the dominant risk; long-duration cash flows magnify the impact of rate moves on NTA.
- **Gearing & covenants** — falling asset values can push gearing toward covenant limits and force divestments.
- **Tenant concentration** — reliance on a few large counterparties (Endeavour/ALH, government).
- **Office structural decline** — weak demand and rising vacancy in the office sleeve.
- **Refinancing risk** — rolling debt at higher rates compresses distributable income.
- **Inflation surprise** — while CPI-linkage helps income, a stagflationary mix (high rates, weak growth) is the worst case for the multiple.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

CLW is valued like an **inflation-linked bond secured against property**, so the relevant frame is yield, NTA and cap rates rather than earnings multiples:

- **NTA & discount/premium** — the unit price is benchmarked against net tangible assets per security (HY26 NTA ~A$4.68); CLW frequently trades at a discount to NTA, the perennial A-REIT debate.
- **Cap rates & bond yields** — portfolio value moves inversely with cap rates, which track long-bond yields; this is why CLW is a textbook bond proxy and why it de-rated hard through the 2022–24 hiking cycle.
- **Distribution yield** — the headline attraction; distributions are funded from operating earnings (HY26 operating EPS 12.75 cps) and underpinned by CPI-linked rent reviews. Yield is high relative to the broad market, reflecting rate risk.
- **Gearing & rating** — the Moody's Baa1 investment-grade rating and gearing level are central to the cost of debt and the sustainability of distributions.
- **Beta / behaviour** — low operating beta but high rate-duration: the unit price behaves like a long-duration income instrument, rallying on rate-cut expectations and falling on hawkish surprises. See the data block above for point-in-time figures as pulled.
