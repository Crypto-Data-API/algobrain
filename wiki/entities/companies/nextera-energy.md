# NextEra Energy (NEE)

NextEra Energy (NYSE: NEE) is the largest US utility by market value, combining Florida Power & Light (the biggest US regulated electric utility by retail load) with NextEra Energy Resources, the world's largest generator of wind and solar power. A [[s-and-p-500]] heavyweight and the dominant [[utilities|Utilities]]-sector weight, NEE has become a core vehicle for the "power the AI data centres" trade alongside its traditional rate-sensitive utility profile. The result is a hybrid stock: part regulated-utility bond proxy, part clean-energy growth platform levered to surging US electricity demand.

## Business Overview & Segments

NextEra is run as two complementary engines — a regulated monopoly that delivers stable, rate-base-driven earnings, and a competitive development arm that is the largest renewables and storage builder in the world. The combination gives the stock a hybrid character: **bond-proxy stability from FPL plus growth optionality from NEER's renewables and nuclear backlog.** Leverage is high in absolute terms (D/E 1.44x in the [[#Fundamentals]] below) but normal for the capital-intensive, regulated-utility model, where long-duration infrastructure debt is matched against regulated, recurring cash flows.

| Engine | What it is | Earnings character |
|---|---|---|
| **Florida Power & Light (FPL)** | The largest US regulated electric utility by retail load, serving ~12 million Floridians; vertically integrated generation, transmission and distribution under the Florida Public Service Commission (PSC) | Regulated, low-risk; earnings grow with **rate-base investment** and Florida population/load growth; functions as the bond-proxy anchor |
| **NextEra Energy Resources (NEER)** | The world's largest generator of wind and solar power, plus battery storage and a growing nuclear fleet; sells output under long-term contracts (PPAs), increasingly to data-centre and technology customers | Competitive/contracted; the growth engine — backlog/origination drives the multi-year earnings algorithm |

FPL benefits from a constructive Florida regulatory compact, a low-cost generation fleet, and one of the fastest-growing service territories in the country. NEER monetises NextEra's scale as a developer: a vast project pipeline, privileged interconnection-queue positions, and the financing/tax-equity capacity that only the largest renewables player can amortise. Across the group, management runs to a long-stated adjusted-EPS growth algorithm underpinned by rate-base growth at FPL and contracted origination at NEER.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

NextEra holds a wide but regulation-bounded [[economic-moat|economic moat]], layered across both engines:

- **Regulated-monopoly franchise at FPL** — a legal service-territory monopoly with a constructive regulatory compact. Returns are earned on an ever-growing rate base; capital deployed into the grid and generation translates into authorised returns, and a low-cost, efficient fleet in a high-growth state supports favourable rate cases.
- **Scale as the world's largest renewables developer (NEER)** — the deepest project pipeline in the industry, best-in-class development expertise, advantageous interconnection-queue positions, and the **tax-equity and financing scale** that no smaller developer can match. Size compounds: more projects → more financing efficiency and supplier leverage → lower-cost megawatts → more contracted backlog.
- **Nuclear fleet scarcity value** — operating and restartable nuclear assets are scarce, hard to permit, and uniquely able to supply 24/7 carbon-free baseload — exactly what hyperscalers want for data centres.

The honest counterweights: FPL's returns are **capped by rate regulation** — the PSC sets allowed ROEs, so upside is bounded and rate cases are a recurring negotiation. Both engines are intensely **capital-hungry**, leaving the company sensitive to financing costs and interest rates, and NEER's growth depends on policy (tax credits) and the pace of US electricity-demand growth.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **FPL rate-base growth** — sustained capital investment in generation, grid hardening and transmission in a high-population-growth state drives regulated earnings.
- **NEER renewables backlog & origination** — record origination (~13.5GW added to backlog in 2025, including a record ~3.6GW in Q4, taking total backlog to roughly 30GW) converts into multi-year contracted earnings.
- **Nuclear restarts & data-centre PPAs (AI electricity demand)** — the 25-year [[alphabet|Google]] partnership to restart the Duane Arnold Energy Center (Iowa) for 24/7 carbon-free nuclear power, plus advanced discussions on ~9GW of additional nuclear capacity for data centres (some online as soon as 2028, per CEO John Ketchum), put NEE at the centre of the AI-power scarcity theme.
- **Battery storage** — storage attach to wind/solar firms intermittent output and adds contracted, high-margin capacity.
- **Dividend-growth algorithm** — management targets ~10%/yr dividend growth through 2026 (off the 2024 base), then ~6%/yr from end-2026 through 2028 — a structural total-return driver for income investors.
- **Policy tailwinds (and risks)** — Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) and renewables tax credits underwrite NEER project economics; their durability is both a driver and a risk (see below).

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- US electricity demand is inflecting higher after two decades of flat load, driven by AI data centres, electrification and reshoring — NextEra is the best-positioned beneficiary across regulated and competitive supply.
- NEER's scale as the largest renewables/storage developer is a compounding advantage in pipeline, financing and tax equity that rivals cannot match.
- Nuclear restarts and 24/7 carbon-free PPAs are scarce, premium-priced, and hard to replicate — a NextEra-specific moat into the data-centre buildout.
- FPL provides a regulated, growing earnings anchor; the visible dividend-growth algorithm (~10% then ~6%) supports total return and attracts income capital.
- If long-bond yields fall, the bond-proxy leg re-rates higher even before the growth story plays out.

### Bear case
- Rising long-bond yields de-rate the bond-proxy leg; utilities are among the most rate-sensitive sectors and NEE often *is* the utilities trade.
- High leverage and relentless capital intensity make the equity sensitive to financing costs; a higher-for-longer rate regime pressures both the multiple and project returns.
- IRA/renewables tax-credit reversal or curtailment would hit NEER project economics and the growth algorithm directly.
- Regulatory risk at FPL — an adverse Florida PSC rate case caps the regulated-earnings engine.
- Florida hurricane/storm exposure can drive restoration costs and headline volatility.
- Execution risk on nuclear restarts (Duane Arnold) and the scale-up of data-centre PPAs; any slip undercuts the AI-power narrative the recent re-rating depends on.

## Key Risks

- **Interest-rate sensitivity / bond-proxy de-rating** — utilities sell off when Treasury yields rise; NEE's high index weight means it leads the sector lower in rate-up regimes.
- **High leverage & capital intensity** — continuous large capex and a leveraged balance sheet (D/E 1.44x) make equity value sensitive to financing costs and credit conditions.
- **Regulatory / rate-case risk at FPL** — allowed ROEs and cost recovery are set by the Florida PSC; an unfavourable outcome caps regulated earnings growth.
- **IRA / tax-credit policy reversal** — NEER economics lean on renewables tax credits; curtailment or repeal would impair project returns and backlog conversion.
- **Hurricane / storm exposure** — Florida concentration brings storm-restoration costs and operational/headline risk.
- **Execution on nuclear restarts and data-centre PPAs** — the Duane Arnold restart and ~9GW nuclear pipeline carry permitting, schedule and cost risk.
- **Financing costs** — as one of the market's largest issuers of utility debt and tax equity, NextEra is exposed to spread widening and the cost/availability of capital.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

NextEra trades as a premium utility whose multiple reflects both regulated stability and clean-energy growth optionality; the debate is which leg the market is paying for at any given moment. Mechanically:

- **Index torque within utilities** — NEE is the largest weight in **XLU** and most utilities ETFs, so it often *is* the utilities-sector trade; sector flows move it and it moves the sector.
- **Two regimes** — historically a **rate-sensitive bond proxy** (sells off when Treasury yields rise); since 2024–2025 it also trades as an **AI-electricity-demand beneficiary**, which can dominate on data-centre headline days. The key read is *which regime is in control* on a given day — yields versus power-demand narrative.
- **Beta** — low beta relative to the [[s-and-p-500]], typical of a regulated utility; it dampens broad-market moves but amplifies rate moves.
- **Inverse sensitivity to long-bond yields** — the dominant macro driver outside company-specific catalysts; the stock tends to move opposite the long end.
- **Options** — liquid with weeklies; modest implied volatility outside earnings; popular for covered calls and dividend-growth overlays rather than high-octane directional bets.
- **Capital return** — the **dividend-growth algorithm** is the core capital-return story: ~10%/yr growth through 2026 (off the 2024 base), then ~6%/yr from end-2026 through 2028 — see the [[#Fundamentals]] block and Sources for the figures as disclosed.
- **Typical catalysts** — quarterly results and adjusted-EPS guidance reaffirmations (January/April/July/October), backlog/origination numbers, nuclear-restart and data-centre PPA announcements, Florida PSC rate decisions, IRA/renewables policy shifts, hurricane season, and FOMC/yield moves.
