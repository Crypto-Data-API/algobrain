**QBE Insurance Group Limited** (ASX: QBE) is one of the world's largest general insurance and reinsurance groups, operating across ~27 countries, listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. Headquartered in Sydney, it is the most globally diversified of the major ASX insurers, with large North American, International (London/Lloyd's) and Australia Pacific divisions. For traders it is a USD-reporting global P&C insurer whose earnings turn on the underwriting (combined-ratio) cycle, catastrophe experience and investment income — a more international, harder-market-leveraged alternative to the domestically focused [[insurance-australia-group]] and [[suncorp]].

## Business Model & Segments

QBE writes a broad book of general insurance and reinsurance across commercial and domestic lines, including property, public and product liability, motor, professional indemnity, workers' compensation, accident and health, financial and credit, marine, energy, aviation, agriculture and specialty. The group also operates a significant presence in the Lloyd's syndicates market and provides investment management services in support of its insurance operations.

QBE organises its business across three geographic divisions:

- Australia Pacific - headquartered in Sydney, covering Australia, New Zealand, Papua New Guinea and various Pacific Islands.
- North America - headquartered in New York, providing general insurance through Specialty & Commercial, Alternative Markets and Crop business units.
- International - headquartered in London, covering European and Asian operations (Hong Kong, Singapore, Vietnam, Malaysia), and including a leading position in the Lloyd's market as a commercial property, specialty and multinational insurance provider.

The group structure also includes Equator Re, a captive reinsurer headquartered in Bermuda, and a shared services centre in the Philippines, alongside corporate functions at the Sydney head office.

### How a P&C insurer makes money

QBE earns from two distinct profit engines, and traders should track both. First, **underwriting profit** — premiums collected less claims and expenses, summarised by the [[combined-ratio|combined operating ratio (COR)]]: below 100% means the book is profitable before investment income; above 100% means underwriting loses money. Second, **investment income** on the "float" — the large pool of policyholder premiums QBE holds before claims are paid, invested largely in bonds. Higher bond yields lift investment income directly, which is why QBE is a structural beneficiary of a higher-rate world. See [[insurance-float]] and [[interest-rates]].

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

QBE's [[economic-moat|economic moat]] is **narrow-to-moderate** — general insurance is a commoditised, capital-intensive business where the dominant long-run driver of returns is underwriting discipline, not brand or [[switching-costs]]. QBE's durable advantages are:

- **Scale and global diversification** — operating across ~27 countries spreads catastrophe and pricing-cycle risk no single-market peer can match; the Lloyd's platform gives access to specialty risks that are hard to replicate.
- **Underwriting data and franchise** — 130+ years of loss data, established broker relationships, and a recognised name in commercial/specialty lines.
- **Capital efficiency via reinsurance** — Equator Re and external reinsurance let QBE write more premium per dollar of equity through the [[insurance-float|float]] mechanism.

The moat's limits are real: pricing is cyclical and competitors can chase share when capital is abundant, compressing margins. QBE's history of US reserve problems shows that scale without discipline destroys value. The competitive position has **improved markedly** under the current management's remediation, with the COR trending toward the low-90s.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Hard-market premium rates** — the multi-year commercial-insurance rate-hardening cycle lifts gross written premium and margins; the key swing factor is how long it persists.
- **Higher-for-longer bond yields** — directly boost investment income on the float (see [[interest-rates]]).
- **Underwriting remediation** — continued portfolio pruning (exiting unprofitable lines) lifting the combined ratio toward the low-90s.
- **Capital management** — the on-market buyback launched in 2025 and rising dividends as earnings normalise.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year (June) and full-year (December) results (the COR vs. ~92.5% guidance and GWP growth are the headline prints), catastrophe-claims experience, reserve releases/strengthening, and global reinsurance pricing at the January renewal season.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Hard pricing plus higher bond yields drive a structurally higher level of through-cycle return on equity (15%+ target).
- Remediation has de-risked the historically troublesome North American and reserve positions; COR now consistently in the low-90s.
- A genuinely global, diversified franchise trading at a discount to global peers like [[chubb]] — re-rating optionality if discipline holds.
- Buyback plus rising fully franked-equivalent dividends return capital while the float compounds.

### Bear case
- Insurance is cyclical: a softening rate market as capital floods in compresses margins, and QBE is leveraged to that cycle.
- Catastrophe experience is lumpy and worsening with climate change; a bad cat year can wipe out a year's underwriting profit.
- Long history of adverse prior-year reserve development — the risk that today's profits are tomorrow's reserve top-ups.
- USD-reporting global insurer listed in AUD: FX translation adds noise; investment-market drawdowns hit both income and capital.

## Key Risks

- **Catastrophe losses** — large natural-catastrophe events (the cat budget was ~4.1% of net insurance revenue in FY25) are the single biggest earnings swing factor.
- **Reserve adequacy** — adverse prior-year claims development, historically a recurring QBE problem, especially in long-tail US liability lines.
- **Pricing-cycle reversal** — a softening hard market reduces both premium growth and margins.
- **Claims inflation** — social and economic inflation pushing claim costs above premium-rate increases.
- **Investment & FX risk** — bond-market volatility hits the float; AUD/USD translation moves the reported result.
- **Regulatory** — APRA capital requirements and conduct regulation across multiple jurisdictions.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

QBE trades like a **cyclical, capital-intensive financial** rather than a growth compounder; the market values it primarily on through-cycle return on equity, the combined operating ratio, and book value, not on an earnings multiple alone. Mechanically:

- **Valuation anchors** — price-to-book and the COR are the metrics that matter most. A sustained sub-93% COR and 15%+ ROE supports a re-rating toward (but still below) the price-to-book multiples of global quality peers like [[chubb]]; the P/E (~16x) is secondary and distorted by reserve and cat noise.
- **Rate sensitivity** — unusually for a "financial," QBE benefits from higher bond yields (investment income on the float), so it can trade *with* rising rates rather than against them — a different profile from the rate-sensitive A-REITs.
- **Earnings behaviour** — the stock moves on the COR-versus-guidance print and the catastrophe-claims line at the half-year (June) and full-year (December) results far more than on headline GWP.
- **Capital return** — partially franked dividends (lower franking than domestic peers because much profit is earned offshore) plus the on-market buyback are the capital-return story.
- **Beta & flows** — an ASX-200 financials-sector constituent held in broad Australian-equity and financials ETFs; options liquidity is modest by global standards, so most positioning is via the ordinary shares.

## History

QBE was founded in 1886 as the North Queensland Insurance Co in Townsville, by Scottish migrants James Burns and Robert Philp - founders of shipping company Burns Philp - with the initial purpose of insuring their ships. By 1890, the business had established more than 35 agencies, extending its reach to international hubs such as London, Hong Kong and Singapore, signalling an early global perspective.

A pivotal moment came in 1921 with the founding of the Bankers' and Traders' Insurance Company (B&T) by James Burns. The modern QBE Insurance Group was listed on the Australian Securities Exchange in 1973 via the merger of three companies whose names contributed the letters of the combined group: Queensland Insurance (Q), Bankers' and Traders' Insurance Company (B), and Equitable Life and General Insurance Co. (E). J. D. O. Burns was QBE's founding chairman.

Over the following decades QBE expanded aggressively through bolt-on acquisitions globally, including the purchase of Mexican insurer Seguros Cumbre SA de CV and American insurer General Casualty Insurance in 2007 and Balboa Insurance of California in 2011. These and many other acquisitions significantly enhanced QBE's global footprint and product diversity and established it as one of the few truly global general insurers headquartered in Australia.

## Recent Developments

For FY25 (year to 31 December 2025), QBE reported net profit after tax of US$2,157 million, up from US$1,779 million in FY24, with an improved combined operating ratio (COR) of 91.9%, comfortably ahead of QBE's approximately 92.5% guidance. On an adjusted basis, net profit after tax was US$2,132 million, delivering an adjusted return on equity of 19.8%.

Gross written premiums rose 7% (or 8% excluding exited portfolios) to approximately US$23.9 billion, exceeding QBE's own guidance for mid-single-digit growth. A material contributor to the improved result was a reduction in catastrophe claims costs to US$751 million (4.1% of net insurance revenue), down from US$1,048 million (5.9%) in the prior year.

For FY26, QBE has reaffirmed guidance of an approximately 92.5% combined operating ratio and mid-single-digit constant currency GWP growth. Medium-term targets include a 15%+ adjusted return on equity and continued mid-single-digit GWP growth. During 2025, QBE also launched an on-market share buyback to support capital management.

The most recent filing (annual) was lodged on 2023-06-30. See [[#Financial Reports]] below.

- Options liquidity is modest by global standards; most exposure is via the ordinary shares. Volatility concentrates around half-year/full-year results and major catastrophe events.
- **Catalysts:** half-year (June) and full-year (December) results — the combined operating ratio vs. ~92.5% guidance and GWP growth are the key prints — catastrophe-claims experience, reserve releases/strengthening, investment-income (bond-yield) trends, buyback updates, and global reinsurance pricing.
- **Correlated names:** [[insurance-australia-group]] and [[suncorp]] (domestic ASX P&C peers), [[aub-group]] (insurance broking); globally correlated with [[chubb]], Allstate and Travelers and the catastrophe cycle.
- **Risks:** large catastrophe losses, adverse prior-year reserve development, a softening pricing cycle as the hard market fades, claims inflation, investment-market volatility, and FX translation (USD results into an AUD-listed share).
