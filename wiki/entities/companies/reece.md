**Reece Limited** (ASX: REH) is Australia's largest supplier of plumbing and bathroom products and a leading distributor of HVAC-R and waterworks products across Australia, New Zealand and the United States. It is listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and is a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. For traders, REH is a Wilson-family-controlled, high-multiple compounder whose earnings are geared to the residential and commercial construction/renovation cycle on two continents — making it a leveraged play on US Sun Belt housing and mortgage-rate conditions as much as on Australian building activity. It is headquartered in Cremorne, Victoria, and employs approximately 9,000 people.

## Business Model & Segments

Reece distributes plumbing, waterworks, HVAC-R, bathroom, irrigation and pools, and fire protection products to commercial and residential customers. Its product range spans tapware and accessories, vanities and mirrors, showers, toilets, basins, baths and spas, pipe and fittings, sinks and troughs, hot water systems, bathroom and kitchen appliances, heating and cooling equipment, irrigation and pools, pumps and water filtration, tools, and valves.

Reece serves trade, retail, commercial and infrastructure markets, and is organised around brand-led vertical specialisations. In Australasia it operates under the Reece brand with category-specialised branch networks across plumbing, bathrooms and heating/ventilation. In the United States, the company operates under the Reece USA brand (formerly MORSCO), with leading positions across plumbing, waterworks and HVAC distribution, concentrated in the US Sun Belt.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Reece's [[economic-moat]] is built on **scale plus branch density** rather than any single product advantage:

- **Dense, category-specialised branch network** — a deep mat of branches positioned close to trade customers (plumbers, builders, contractors) lets Reece fulfil fast and hold the breadth of stock that smaller, single-location merchants cannot match. In distribution, the merchant nearest the job site with the part on the shelf usually wins; density compounds that advantage and is expensive for a sub-scale rival to replicate.
- **Trade relationships and vertical specialisation** — brand-led, category-specialised formats (plumbing, bathrooms, HVAC-R, waterworks) plus local branch service, technical advice and trade credit create modest [[switching-costs]] for tradies who organise their workflow around a familiar local branch. The relationship, not just the price, is the stickiness.
- **Scale buying power** — national (and now bi-national) volume gives Reece supplier terms, private-label leverage and inventory economics that fragmented independents struggle to access.
- **US replication (Reece USA, ex-MORSCO)** — the same density playbook is being run in a large, fragmented US Sun Belt market, where consolidation runway is greater but incumbents (notably Ferguson) are far larger.

The franchise also carries the hallmarks of a **Wilson-family-controlled quality compounder**: long-term capital allocation, a premium rating, and limited free float. The moat is real but **cyclical** — it is geared to construction, renovation and repair activity on two continents — and **US competition is intensifying**, with Reece USA facing same-branch sales and market-share pressure (see [[industrials]]).

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **US Sun Belt branch rollout** — continued greenfield and bolt-on branch additions in a fragmented, structurally growing region; the central long-run growth lever.
- **Residential renovation / repair (R&R) demand** — repair-and-remodel activity is the bread-and-butter end market and less volatile than new construction over a cycle.
- **Commercial & infrastructure waterworks** — municipal water, civil and commercial projects diversify away from pure residential housing.
- **Category expansion** — deepening HVAC-R, irrigation and pools, and fire-protection ranges widens the addressable spend per branch.
- **Digital and branch-format investment** — ongoing investment in branch formats, omni-channel ordering and trade tools to defend and extend the moat.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year (February) and full-year (August) results, FY EBIT guidance, Reece USA same-branch sales and market-share commentary, US housing and mortgage-rate data (mortgage-rate lock-in suppressing existing-home turnover is a live drag), and branch-count updates.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Durable scale + branch-density distribution moat that is hard and slow for rivals to replicate.
- Two-continent runway: a mature, dominant Australasian core funding a long US Sun Belt growth option.
- US market fragmentation is a multi-decade consolidation and share-gain opportunity.
- Wilson-family stewardship aligns long-term, through-cycle capital allocation rather than quarter-to-quarter optimisation.
- Branch-density advantage strengthens with scale — a compounding flywheel in distribution.

### Bear case
- Deeply **cyclical** — earnings ride construction and renovation activity on two continents.
- Management has flagged **FY26 as likely one of the most challenging years in the company's history**.
- **Intensifying US competition** and visible market-share pressure at Reece USA.
- **Mortgage-rate lock-in** suppresses existing-home turnover and the R&R that follows.
- **Rich, premium valuation** (~29x) is vulnerable to de-rating if growth stalls.
- **Limited free float** from majority family ownership reduces liquidity and the influence of outside holders.

## Key Risks

- **Cyclical construction downturn** — a synchronized AU + US building/renovation slowdown hits revenue and operating leverage simultaneously.
- **Mortgage-rate lock-in** — homeowners sitting on low fixed mortgages don't move, suppressing existing-home turnover and renovation spend (a live FY26 US drag).
- **US competitive intensity** — Ferguson and specialist distributors pressure Reece USA same-branch sales and share.
- **Margin compression** — competition and soft volumes squeeze gross and EBIT margins (FY26 flagged accordingly).
- **Valuation de-rating** — a premium ~29x multiple leaves little room for disappointment.
- **FX translation** — a large and growing US earnings base means AUD/USD swings move reported results.
- **Key-person / family control** — concentrated Wilson-family ownership and leadership creates governance and succession concentration.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Reece has long traded as a **premium-multiple quality compounder** rather than a cheap cyclical — the page's ~29x earnings multiple reflects the market paying up for the distribution moat and growth runway, not depressed expectations. Mechanically:

- **Premium rating supported by control** — the Wilson family's majority stake **limits free float** and underpins the premium multiple; the stock trades like a scarce, closely held compounder.
- **Reinvestment-led, modest franked dividends** — Reece pays a comparatively low, franked dividend (lighter yield than ASX retail peers); the capital-return story is reinvestment into US growth, not yield.
- **Liquidity vs options** — a large-cap [[asx-200]] name with reasonable equity liquidity, but **thin single-name options**, so positioning is almost entirely outright equity.
- **What it actually trades on** — increasingly a **leveraged play on US Sun Belt housing and mortgage-rate conditions** as much as on Australian building activity; US same-branch and housing data move the stock alongside domestic results.

## History

Harold Joseph Reece began selling plumbing products from the back of his truck in 1920, later opening the first HJ Reece store in Caulfield, Victoria. The business grew through the middle of the 20th century as a Melbourne-based plumbing merchant.

In 1958, Les Wilson was offered a seat on the board and gradually began acquiring shares. By 1969 the Wilson family was approaching a controlling stake, and on 20 July 1969 regulators approved the Wilson family becoming majority shareholders of HJ Reece. The business remained under Wilson family stewardship through subsequent decades as it expanded nationally, modernised its branch network and added new product categories.

The company was formerly known as Reece Australia Limited and changed its name to Reece Limited in November 2015 to reflect its growing international ambitions. In May 2018, Reece announced the acquisition of US plumbing and HVAC distributor MORSCO for approximately US$1.9 billion, establishing a significant US footprint in the Sun Belt region. In early 2022, following integration of the businesses, the MORSCO brand was retired and rebranded as Reece USA.

## Recent Developments

For FY25 (year to 30 June 2025), Reece reported sales revenue down 1% to A$8.98 billion, EBITDA down 11% to A$901 million and EBIT down 20% to A$548 million, reflecting softening demand in both Australasia and the United States and intensifying competition in US plumbing and HVAC distribution. CEO Peter Wilson described FY26 as likely to be one of the most challenging years in the company's history.

For H1 FY26, Reece reported a 6% increase in group sales revenue to A$4.6 billion but a 6% decline in EBITDA, reflecting challenging market conditions in both Australasia and the US. Despite adding 24 stores to its branch network, Reece USA experienced market share pressure, with revenue down 5% and EBITDA down 23%. US plumbing and waterworks divisions are in negative year-on-year territory in FY26, impacted by a sluggish residential new construction market and the effect of mortgage rate lock-in among homeowners. Management has guided FY26 Group EBIT in the range of A$520-540 million.

## Leadership

Reece is led by Chief Executive Officer Peter Wilson, son of co-founder and long-serving chair Alan Wilson.
