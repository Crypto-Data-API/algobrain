**Yancoal Australia Ltd** (ASX: YAN; dual-listed HKEX: 3668) is one of Australia's largest listed pure-play coal producers, with open-cut and underground operations across New South Wales, Queensland and Western Australia. It is a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index and is majority-owned by Yankuang Energy Group, a large Chinese state-linked energy and coal group. For traders it is a high-beta, high-yield play on export thermal (and some metallurgical) coal prices and on Chinese energy demand, distinguished by a low cost base, a strong balance sheet and large, sometimes lumpy, capital returns — with a free float constrained by Yankuang's controlling stake.

## Business Model & Segments

Yancoal Australia operates a portfolio of open-cut and underground coal mines, including:

- **Moolarben** (98.75% interest) — large thermal coal mine in the Western Coalfields of NSW.
- **Mount Thorley Warkworth** (approximately 83.6%) — open-cut coking and thermal coal in the Hunter Valley, NSW.
- **Hunter Valley Operations (HVO)** (51%) — large Hunter Valley open-cut thermal and coking coal mine, jointly owned with Glencore.
- **Ashton** — underground and open-cut operations in the Hunter Valley, NSW.
- **Stratford Duralie / Donaldson** — NSW operations (various statuses).
- **Yarrabee** (100%) — coking/PCI coal mine in the Bowen Basin, QLD.
- **Cameby Downs** (50%) — thermal coal mine in the Surat Basin, QLD.
- **Middlemount** (50%) — coking coal mine in the Bowen Basin, QLD.
- **Austar** (100%) — NSW underground operation.
- **Premier Coal** — thermal coal mine at Collie, Western Australia supplying the domestic power market.

The company also holds exploration tenements and growth options in coal and adjacent sectors.

## History

- **2004**: Yancoal Australia Pty Ltd incorporated as Yanzhou Coal Mining Company's (now Yankuang Energy) Australian platform.
- **2009**: Acquired Felix Resources, gaining the Moolarben, Ashton, Yarrabee and Minerva coal mines.
- **2012**: Listed on the Australian Securities Exchange via a reverse takeover of Gloucester Coal.
- **2017**: Acquired Coal & Allied from Rio Tinto for US$2.45 billion, adding Mount Thorley Warkworth and the HVO joint-venture interest — a defining transaction that roughly doubled production.
- **2018**: Dual-listed on the Hong Kong Stock Exchange (HKEX: 3668).
- **2020–2023**: Weathered cyclical coal price volatility; maintained low cost base and prioritised capital returns to shareholders.
- **September 2024**: Admitted to the S&P/ASX 200 and S&P/ASX 300 indices.

## Recent Developments

Yancoal reported record operational performance in calendar 2025 despite weaker realised coal prices:

- **CY2025 operational**: Record ROM coal production of 67.0 Mt (100% basis), up 7% on 2024. Attributable saleable production of 38.6 Mt, up 5% and in the top quartile of guidance.
- **Revenue and earnings**: Revenue of approximately A$5.95 billion, down approximately 13% on 2024, primarily reflecting a 17% decline in realised average coal price to A$146 per tonne. Operating EBITDA of approximately A$1,437 million at a 24% margin. Reported net profit of approximately A$440 million, down significantly on 2024 given the coal price decline.
- **Unit costs**: Cash operating costs (excluding government royalties) of A$92 per tonne, down A$1 on 2024 and below the midpoint of guidance.
- **2026 guidance**: Attributable saleable production of 36.5–40.5 Mt. Cash operating costs of A$90–98 per tonne. Attributable capital expenditure of A$750–900 million (including some deferred 2025 spend).
- **Capital management**: Maintained shareholder distributions through a combination of dividends and balance-sheet discipline.

## Business Segments

Yancoal is best framed by **coal type** and **market**, because the two products have very different price dynamics:

| Segment | Product | Key assets | Demand driver |
|---------|---------|-----------|---------------|
| **Thermal (export)** | Energy coal sold into Asia, benchmarked to [[thermal-coal\|Newcastle thermal coal]] | Moolarben, HVO, Ashton, parts of Mount Thorley Warkworth | Asian (esp. Chinese, Japanese, Korean) power generation |
| **Metallurgical / PCI** | Coking and pulverised-coal-injection coal for steelmaking | Yarrabee, Middlemount, portions of HVO/MTW | Global steel production |
| **Thermal (domestic)** | Energy coal to the WA power market | Premier Coal (Collie, WA) | Domestic WA electricity demand |

The bulk of group revenue and earnings comes from **export thermal coal**, which is why Yancoal trades primarily as a high-beta play on the Newcastle benchmark and Chinese energy demand, with metallurgical coal a secondary, steel-cycle-linked overlay.

## Competitive Positioning & Peers

Yancoal is one of a small cohort of large ASX-listed pure-play coal producers. The natural comparison set is the other listed Australian coal miners; the key differentiators are coal-mix (thermal vs met), cost position, balance sheet, and ownership structure.

| Company | Ticker | Coal mix | Distinctive feature |
|---------|--------|----------|---------------------|
| **Yancoal** | YAL/YAN | Mostly export **thermal**, some met/PCI | Majority-owned by Yankuang Energy (constrained free float); low cost base; large lumpy capital returns |

Relative to peers, Yancoal's signature traits are (1) a **controlling majority shareholder** (Yankuang Energy) that limits free float and shapes distribution policy, (2) a **dual listing** on the HKEX (3668) alongside the [[asx-200]], and (3) a heavily **thermal-weighted** book that makes it more of a direct Newcastle-price and China-energy proxy than the increasingly met-tilted [[whitehaven-coal]].

## Valuation Framework

Yancoal is a **cyclical commodity producer**, so valuation is dominated by price and cost beta rather than steady-state multiples:

- **Realised coal price beta** — the single biggest driver. Earnings are highly geared to the [[thermal-coal|Newcastle thermal-coal benchmark]] (and, secondarily, met/PCI prices). A given % move in realised price flows through to EBITDA with high operating leverage because cash costs are relatively fixed.
- **AUD/USD** — coal is priced in **USD** while a large share of costs are in **AUD**; a weaker Australian dollar widens AUD-denominated margins (a tailwind shared with the [[asx-gold-sector|gold miners]]).
- **Unit cost position (cash costs / AISC)** — Yancoal's low cost base (cash operating costs ~A$92/t in CY2025) is the margin-of-safety: low-cost producers stay cash-generative deeper into a price downcycle.
- **China demand & import policy** — Chinese power demand and import quotas/tariffs swing seaborne thermal-coal volumes and prices.
- **Capital returns / dividend yield** — in strong-price years the stock is valued substantially on its (high) free-cash-flow and dividend yield; the durability of those returns is the swing factor.
- **Terminal value / ESG discount** — the market applies a structural discount for energy-transition risk and constrained financing access, capping the multiple even in good years.

Because earnings are cyclical, trailing P/E is a poor guide near price extremes; mid-cycle earnings power, free-cash-flow yield, and cost-curve position are more useful anchors.

## Capital Return

Yancoal is run for **cash return**: in strong-price years it has prioritised large dividends and one-off special distributions over aggressive growth capex, supported by a strong balance sheet. Key features for income-oriented holders:

- **High, variable dividends** — payouts scale with the coal cycle, so the yield is large but lumpy and not reliable through the cycle.
- **Franking** — Australian dividends can carry [[franking-credits|franking credits]], relevant to domestic income and [[smsf|SMSF]] investors (subject to the company's franking position in a given year).
- **Majority-shareholder influence** — Yankuang Energy's controlling stake shapes the distribution policy and special-distribution decisions; minority shareholders are price-takers on capital-return timing.
- **Balance-sheet discipline** — periods of debt paydown have alternated with large returns, the company favouring low gearing as a buffer against price volatility.

## Trading Relevance

- **Index membership:** [[asx-200]] / S&P/ASX 300 (admitted September 2024); also dual-listed on HKEX (3668).
- **Free float / ownership:** Yankuang Energy's controlling stake limits free float and can constrain index weight and trading liquidity; capital-return policy is shaped by the majority shareholder's preferences.
- **Options liquidity:** Thin ASX single-stock options; expression is overwhelmingly via the underlying. Very high price beta to the Newcastle benchmark thermal-coal price.
- **Typical catalysts:** Newcastle thermal-coal price moves, quarterly production/cost reports vs guidance, dividend/special-distribution announcements, China energy-demand and import-policy signals, weather/logistics (rail/port) disruptions, and ESG/financing-access pressure on the coal sector.
- **Correlated names:** Australian coal peers [[whitehaven-coal]], [[new-hope-corporation]], Coronado; broadly correlated with the energy-commodity complex and Chinese industrial demand.

## Risks

- **Commodity-price risk** — the dominant risk; thermal- (and met-) coal prices are volatile and earnings are highly geared to them, as the CY2025 price-driven profit decline showed.
- **Energy-transition / structural demand risk** — long-run decarbonisation pressures thermal-coal demand and weighs on the terminal multiple.
- **FX risk** — USD-priced revenue vs AUD costs means a stronger AUD compresses margins.
- **China / policy concentration** — heavy reliance on Asian (notably Chinese) demand and import policy; tariff or quota changes can swing volumes and prices.
- **Ownership / governance** — Yankuang Energy's controlling stake constrains free float, can limit index weight and liquidity, and gives the majority holder outsized influence over capital-return policy; related-party considerations apply.
- **ESG / financing access** — coal producers face rising costs and reduced availability of insurance, bank financing and equity capital.
- **Operational / logistics** — weather, rail and port disruptions, and regulatory/royalty changes (NSW/QLD coal royalties) affect output and unit costs.
- **Capacity-return variability** — dividends are large but cyclical and shaped by the majority shareholder; not a stable income stream.
