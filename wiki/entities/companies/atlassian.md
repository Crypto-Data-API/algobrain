Atlassian Corporation (NASDAQ: TEAM) is the maker of Jira, Confluence and a suite of collaboration and software-development tools, founded in 2002 in Sydney by Mike Cannon-Brookes and Scott Farquhar. A large-cap enterprise-SaaS company now mid-way through a cloud and AI transition, it trades on cloud-migration momentum, enterprise seat expansion, and the monetization of its Rovo AI assistant. The two co-founders retain dual-class voting control.

## Business Overview

Atlassian sells software for teamwork and DevOps — Jira (project/issue tracking), Confluence (knowledge), Jira Service Management (ITSM), Bitbucket, Loom, Trello and more — sold primarily as cloud subscriptions after sunsetting its legacy Server product. Growth is driven by cloud migration of its huge installed base, upsell to enterprise editions, and AI features (Rovo) layered across the portfolio. The model is land-and-expand with low-touch distribution plus a growing enterprise sales motion.

## Business Segments / Products

Atlassian does not report by product line but by deployment (Cloud, Data Center, Marketplace/other). Economically the portfolio is a flywheel: a low-cost, self-serve entry product (Jira/Confluence/Trello) that lands inside teams, expands seat-by-seat, and upgrades to enterprise editions and adjacent products.

| Product | Category | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Jira** | Project / issue tracking, agile, DevOps | The flagship land product; deepest moat in software-team workflows |
| **Confluence** | Knowledge / wiki / collaboration | Attaches to Jira; the natural surface for [[artificial-intelligence|AI]] knowledge retrieval |
| **Jira Service Management (JSM)** | ITSM | Enterprise-grade, competes with ServiceNow down-market |
| **Bitbucket** | Source control / CI | DevOps completeness; overlaps [[gitlab]] and GitHub |
| **Loom** | Async video | Acquired; collaboration breadth |
| **Trello** | Lightweight task boards | Top-of-funnel land for non-technical teams |
| **Rovo** | AI search / agents / Studio | Cross-portfolio AI assistant; the monetisation narrative |
| **Dia** | AI-native browser (via The Browser Company) | Enterprise knowledge-worker browser bet |

Reporting buckets: **Cloud** (the growth engine, now the majority of revenue), **Data Center** (self-managed enterprise, the migration source), and **Marketplace & other** (third-party apps, a high-margin ecosystem annuity). The forward metric the market watches is Cloud revenue growth plus **RPO** (remaining performance obligations) as a proxy for enterprise commitment.

## Competitive Positioning / Peers

Atlassian's moat is workflow entrenchment in software-development teams (Jira's bottoms-up adoption is extremely hard to displace) plus a large third-party Marketplace ecosystem. The competitive frontier is enterprise collaboration breadth, ITSM, and AI-assisted knowledge work, where larger platforms compete.

## Bull vs Bear Case

**Bull case.** Cloud migration of the entrenched Data Center base is a multi-year, high-visibility growth and margin tailwind; RPO acceleration signals deepening enterprise commitment. Rovo turns Atlassian's vast workflow and knowledge data into an AI monetisation surface (AI customers expand ARR faster), and seat expansion plus edition upsell compound the land-and-expand engine. The Marketplace is a sticky, high-margin annuity. Founder-led with long-term capital allocation discipline and strong free cash flow.

**Bear case.** Cloud growth deceleration is the core fear; the stock punishes any guide that implies the migration tailwind is fading. [[microsoft]] (GitHub Copilot, Teams, Azure DevOps) is a structural competitive overhang, and AI agents could deflate seat-based pricing across collaboration SaaS. Dual-class governance limits shareholder influence. The Browser Company / Dia acquisition adds integration and strategic risk. Valuation still embeds durable high-growth + margin expansion.

## Valuation Framework

Atlassian is a Rule-of-40 SaaS compounder; qualitatively the levers are:

- **Cloud revenue growth + RPO** — the leading indicators; RPO growth (notably outpacing revenue) signals enterprise backlog building.
- **Net retention / seat expansion** — land-and-expand health; the engine of efficient growth.
- **Rule-of-40** — Atlassian typically screens well combining growth and FCF margin, the basis for its premium multiple.
- **FCF margin and conversion** — the cash-generation story management emphasises over GAAP profit.
- **Stock-based compensation / dilution** — like most US SaaS, SBC is meaningful; the bear watch is whether FCF-per-share grows net of dilution.
- **AI attach (Rovo)** — whether AI converts to incremental ARR rather than just retention.

## Capital Return / Dilution

Atlassian generates strong free cash flow and has used buybacks opportunistically rather than paying a dividend, framing capital allocation around organic investment, tuck-in M&A (e.g. Loom, The Browser Company) and offsetting dilution. The key shareholder watch-item is FCF-per-share growth net of stock-based compensation; dual-class shares concentrate voting control with the two co-founders.

## 2025–2026 Developments

- **Q2 FY2026 (Dec quarter):** revenue +23% y/y to $1.6B; Cloud revenue +26% to $1.1B — its first ever $1B+ cloud quarter. RPO +44% y/y to $3.8B, signaling accelerating enterprise commitments. Stock dipped post-print despite the beat.
- **Rovo AI:** surpassed 5 million monthly active users; Rovo customers grow ARR roughly 2x faster than non-Rovo customers, with AI credit usage up >20% month-over-month — the core AI monetization narrative.
- **The Browser Company acquisition:** announced Sept 4, 2025 and completed Oct 21, 2025 for $610M cash, bringing the AI-native **Dia** browser (and Arc team). Atlassian's bet is an enterprise/knowledge-worker browser with AI skills and admin/security controls baked in.
- Stock had fallen sharply from its peak, making valuation a debate even as cloud/AI metrics accelerated.
