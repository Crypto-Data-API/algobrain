Nikola Corporation (formerly NASDAQ: NKLA) was a US zero-emission truck maker (battery-electric and hydrogen fuel-cell Class 8 trucks) that filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy on 19 February 2025 and was liquidated, with all common stock cancelled in December 2025. It survives in the wiki as a case study: one of the defining SPAC-mania blowups of the 2020-2025 cycle and a textbook short thesis (the Hindenburg Research fraud report) playing out to a zero.

## Status: Defunct (Liquidated)

- **19 Feb 2025** — Filed Chapter 11 in the US Bankruptcy Court for the District of Delaware; Nasdaq issued a delisting notice the same day.
- **26 Feb 2025** — Trading suspended on Nasdaq; shares moved to OTC as **NKLAQ**. Deregistration of common stock took effect 14 April 2025.
- **11 Apr 2025** — [[lucid-group|Lucid Group]] agreed to acquire select Arizona facilities and assets for roughly US$10 million plus ~US$7 million tied to truck-retrofit obligations. The deal excluded Nikola's core business, customer contracts, and hydrogen fuel-cell technology.
- **12 Dec 2025** — Liquidating plan went effective after a 363 sale of substantially all assets; control passed to the Nikola Liquidating Trust. **All common stock and equity securities were cancelled and extinguished — equity holders received no distribution.**

## What the Business Was

Founded in 2014 by Trevor Milton, Nikola went public in June 2020 via SPAC merger (VectoIQ) and briefly reached a market capitalisation above US$30 billion — at one point exceeding Ford — without meaningful revenue. In September 2020 Hindenburg Research published "Nikola: How to Parlay An Ocean of Lies Into a Partnership With the Largest Auto OEM in America," alleging the company staged a promotional video of a truck rolling downhill. Milton resigned days later, was convicted of securities and wire fraud in October 2022, and was pardoned in March 2025. The company produced small numbers of Tre BEV and FCEV trucks but burned cash faster than it could raise it, with repeated dilutive equity raises and a 1-for-30 reverse split before the bankruptcy.

### Business segments / products

Nikola positioned itself as a zero-emission heavy-trucking and energy-infrastructure company. Its stated business spanned two product families plus an infrastructure ambition:

| Segment | What it was | Reality at the end |
|---------|-------------|--------------------|
| **Tre BEV** (battery-electric Class 8) | Battery-electric day-cab/regional tractor | Reached limited series production; small delivered volumes, recalls |
| **Tre FCEV** (hydrogen fuel-cell Class 8) | Hydrogen fuel-cell long-haul tractor | The flagship "story" product; minimal commercial deployment |
| **HYLA hydrogen infrastructure** | Hydrogen production/fueling network to support FCEV adoption | Aspirational; capital-intensive chicken-and-egg with truck sales |

The core thesis — that Nikola could simultaneously build trucks *and* the hydrogen-refueling network to fuel them — required enormous, sustained capital while generating almost no revenue, which is the structural reason the equity went to zero.

## Competitive Positioning at the Time

Nikola was the most notorious of the 2020-2021 EV/clean-mobility SPAC cohort, but it competed (at least narratively) against both established truck OEMs and other EV startups. Its differentiator was the hydrogen fuel-cell long-haul angle; its fatal weakness was that it had neither the manufacturing scale of incumbents nor the cash discipline of the better-funded startups.

| Peer | Positioning vs. Nikola | Outcome |
|------|------------------------|---------|
| [[tesla]] (TSLA) | Vastly larger, self-funding, Semi program with real deliveries and battery/charging scale | Survived and dominant; the benchmark Nikola was implicitly valued against at its SPAC peak |
| [[rivian-automotive]] (RIVN) | Far better capitalized EV startup (Amazon/Ford backing), real consumer + commercial vehicles | Survived with large cash buffer; illustrates that funding depth, not narrative, separated survivors |
| Traditional Class 8 OEMs (Daimler Truck, Paccar, Volvo) | Incumbent manufacturing scale, dealer networks, financing | Pursued BEV/FCEV at lower risk inside profitable franchises |
| [[lucid-group]] (LCID) | Premium-EV SPAC peer; later acquired Nikola's Arizona facilities out of bankruptcy | Survived (heavily funded); see The Failure below |

The takeaway: within its peer set, Nikola was distinguished almost entirely by promotional intensity and the hydrogen narrative rather than by product, scale, or balance sheet — precisely the profile a [[short-selling|short]] thesis targets.

## The Failure: Cash-Burn, Dilution, and the Path to Zero

Nikola was not acquired as a going concern at a premium — it failed. The terminal path followed the canonical pre-revenue SPAC blowup sequence:

1. **Story-stock peak (June-Sept 2020).** SPAC listing → euphoria → >US$30B market cap on renders, announcements, and an OEM partnership, with no meaningful revenue to anchor valuation.
2. **The catalyst short report (Sept 2020).** Hindenburg Research's fraud allegations punctured the narrative; Milton resigned within days. The fall was years long, not instant — the equity bled lower across 2021-2024 as fundamentals never caught up to the SPAC-era story.
3. **Chronic cash burn vs. minimal revenue.** The company produced only small numbers of Tre BEV/FCEV trucks while burning cash faster than it could raise it. Each truck delivered cost far more than it earned; the hydrogen-infrastructure ambition compounded the capital need.
4. **The dilution spiral.** To stay alive Nikola ran serial dilutive equity raises (ATM offerings, convertible notes), steadily expanding share count and crushing per-share value — the classic mechanism by which a distressed equity grinds toward zero even before formal bankruptcy.
5. **The reverse split.** A **1-for-30 reverse split** (a late-stage cosmetic move to maintain the Nasdaq minimum bid price) is itself a distress tell; it resets the optics without fixing the burn.
6. **Chapter 11 and equity cancellation.** The filing (19 Feb 2025), 363 asset sale, and liquidating trust left common holders with **$0** (see Status section above for exact dates). [[lucid-group|Lucid]] bought select Arizona facilities — but explicitly *not* the core business — confirming the operating company itself had no going-concern value.

## Merger-Arb / Trading Postmortem

Nikola is the inverse of a clean merger-arb name like [[splunk]] — there was no acquirer bidding for the equity at a premium; the trade was the collapse, the squeezes, and the dilution.

- **Short thesis with a public catalyst.** The [[short-selling|short]] case was unusually well-telegraphed: a named research firm published the thesis (Sept 2020) years before the zero. The hard part was not the thesis but the *timing and carry* — borrow costs and recurring [[short-squeeze|short squeezes]] punished early shorts.
- **Short-squeeze dynamics.** As a high-short-interest, high-retail, high-[[implied-volatility]] name, NKLA repeatedly spiked on partnership headlines, order announcements, and meme-driven flow — see [[short-squeeze]]. Shorting the obvious zero still required surviving violent counter-rallies; many right-thesis shorts were stopped out.
- **Bankruptcy-equity bid.** NKLAQ kept trading OTC for months after filing despite a near-certain zero recovery — a recurring pattern (Hertz, Bed Bath & Beyond) where retail flow keeps worthless claims bid. Trading the "lottery ticket" post-filing is negative-EV.
- **Options endgame.** [[implied-volatility]] stayed extreme into the decline; selling premium into a delisting carries assignment/settlement complications, and the OCC adjusted/settled NKLA option classes following the delisting (see Options Trading Profile above). Long calls expired worthless as the equity was cancelled.

## Lessons / What Killed It

- **SPAC-mania archetype:** pre-revenue story stock valued on renders and announcements; the [[short-selling|short]] thesis (Hindenburg, Sept 2020) was publicly available years before the terminal decline.
- **Funding depth separated survivors from zeros.** Better-capitalized peers ([[tesla]], [[rivian-automotive]], [[lucid-group]]) survived; Nikola's combination of minimal revenue, huge capital needs (trucks *and* hydrogen infrastructure), and reputational damage made each raise more dilutive than the last.
- **Bankruptcy equities:** NKLAQ continued to trade OTC for months after the filing despite a near-certain zero recovery — a recurring pattern (see Hertz, Bed Bath & Beyond) where retail flow keeps worthless claims bid.
- **Dilution spiral:** serial capital raises plus a 1-for-30 reverse split are classic late-stage distress markers; [[implied-volatility]] stayed extreme but selling premium into a delisting carries assignment/settlement complications.

## Risks That Materialized

- **Fraud / governance risk** — founder convicted of securities and wire fraud (Oct 2022); reputational damage permanently impaired fundraising.
- **Cash-runway risk** — burn rate vs. revenue was the knowable kill metric; it was never closed.
- **Dilution risk** — serial equity issuance and a 1-for-30 reverse split destroyed per-share value ahead of the wipeout.
- **Going-concern / bankruptcy risk** — Chapter 11, 363 sale, liquidating trust; **equity = $0**.
