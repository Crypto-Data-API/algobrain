**Metrics Master Income Trust** (ASX: MXT) is an ASX-listed investment trust (LIT) in the [[financials]] sector — a vehicle for [[private-credit]] that gives retail and wholesale investors access to a diversified portfolio of Australian corporate loans, assets traditionally held on bank balance sheets. It is a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index and is managed by Metrics Credit Partners, an Australian private-markets specialist focused on corporate lending, real estate debt and structured credit. Crucially, MXT is **a fund, not an operating company**: it is bought for monthly income and trades close to its net asset value (NAV), so the right lens is NAV, distribution yield and the credit quality of the underlying loan book — not earnings multiples or growth.

## Business Overview & Segments

MXT is a fund, not an operating company — a listed wrapper that channels investor capital into Metrics' wholesale private-credit funds. Its key features:

- **Objective:** Deliver a total return of the **RBA cash rate plus 3.25% p.a.**, paid as monthly distributions, while managing the risk of principal loss.
- **Structure:** MXT invests in the MCP Wholesale Investment Trust, which in turn allocates across three Metrics-managed wholesale funds.

| Underlying fund | Strategy | Approx. allocation |
|---|---|---|
| **Diversified Australian Senior Loan Fund (DASLF)** | Senior secured corporate lending | ~60% |
| **MCP Secured Private Debt Fund II (SPDF II)** | Secured private corporate debt | ~20% |
| **MCP Real Estate Debt Fund (REDF)** | [[real-estate]] (commercial property) debt | ~20% |

- **Portfolio:** Diversified across approximately 350 loan positions spanning corporate lending, real estate debt, infrastructure and acquisition finance.
- **Credit quality:** Predominantly investment-grade and senior secured, with short interest-rate and credit duration — designed for low mark-to-market volatility relative to equities or long-duration bonds.
- **Rate profile:** Predominantly **floating-rate** — coupons reset with the RBA cash rate, so income rises in a hiking cycle and falls when the cash rate is cut.
- **Listing:** ASX: MXT; market capitalisation approximately A$2.4 billion; trades close to NAV (~A$1.93–1.94 per unit as at June 2026).

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

As a fund, MXT does not have a corporate [[economic-moat|moat]] in the operating-business sense; its "moat" is the franchise and origination capability of its manager, **Metrics Credit Partners**:

- **Origination scale & relationships** — Metrics manages in excess of A$20bn in private-markets debt and equity, giving it preferential access to deal flow and the ability to set terms, pricing and covenants that a smaller lender cannot. Borrower relationships and a multi-year lending track record are genuinely scarce.
- **Bank-disintermediation tailwind** — post-GFC regulatory capital reforms pushed Australian banks out of segments of mid-market and leveraged corporate lending, creating a structural opening that non-bank lenders like Metrics fill at attractive spreads.
- **Diversification & seniority** — ~350 positions, predominantly senior secured and short-duration, limit single-name loss and mark-to-market swings; this is a structural risk-management advantage over concentrated credit funds.
- **First-mover scale in listed private credit** — MXT was among the first major listed private-debt trusts in Australia (2017) and has built a large, sticky retail and adviser income-investor base.

The flip side: the "moat" is manager-dependent, fees are a permanent drag, and the strategy is fundamentally exposed to the credit cycle — none of these advantages protect principal in a severe corporate-default event.

## History

Metrics Credit Partners was founded around 2011-2013 by Andrew Lockhart and his partners to provide investors with access to the Australian corporate loan market, in response to bank retreat from segments of the market following post-GFC regulatory capital reforms. MXT itself was launched on the ASX in October 2017 as one of the first major listed private-debt trusts in Australia, and the platform has since scaled materially (see the milestones table below). Metrics now manages in excess of A$20 billion in private-markets debt and equity assets.

## Competitors / Peer Set

MXT competes for income-investor capital across listed private-credit trusts, fixed-income alternatives and the broader yield complex. It is best understood as an income alternative to bank hybrids and term deposits rather than to growth equities.

| Vehicle / alternative | Type | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| MCP Income Opportunities Trust (ASX: MOT) | Sister Metrics LIT | Higher-yield, higher-risk private-credit sibling — more subordinated/opportunistic exposure |
| Metrics Real Estate Multi-Strategy Fund (ASX: MRE) | Sister Metrics LIT | Real-estate debt and equity focused sibling |
| Other listed credit LITs (e.g. Qualitas, KKR-style vehicles) | Listed private credit | Direct competitors for the same adviser/retail income flows |
| Bank hybrids | Listed fixed income | The most direct income substitute — APRA's hybrid phase-out has pushed capital toward private-credit LITs |
| Term deposits / cash | Bank deposits | The floor: MXT targets a meaningful spread (RBA + 3.25%) above the cash rate |
| Bond ETFs / long-duration credit | Fixed income | Compete on yield but carry far more duration (rate) risk than MXT's floating-rate book |

MXT's edge versus these is the combination of Metrics' origination scale, a floating-rate (low-duration) profile, and a long listed track record at low mark-to-market volatility.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Higher-for-longer cash rates** — floating-rate coupons reset upward with the RBA cash rate, so a higher cash rate directly lifts distributions.
- **Bank disintermediation** — continued bank retreat from mid-market and leveraged lending expands the addressable private-credit market Metrics lends into.
- **Hybrid phase-out** — APRA's wind-down of bank hybrid securities redirects income-seeking capital toward listed private-credit trusts like MXT.
- **Unit issuance / scale** — institutional placements and entitlement offers grow the asset base and Metrics' deal capacity (shares out grew from ~879M in FY2022 to ~1.22bn in FY2025).
- **Premium/discount to NAV** — strong demand can push units to a premium, enabling accretive raisings; trading at NAV is the normal state.
- **Credit-quality reassurance** — low arrears and stable NAV through a softening cycle would reinforce the "defensive income" reputation.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- High, dependable monthly income at a defined spread over the cash rate (target RBA + 3.25%; ~8.05% 12-month net return to Feb 2026).
- Floating-rate, short-duration book means very low mark-to-market volatility versus equities and long bonds — a genuine portfolio diversifier.
- Predominantly senior secured, investment-grade-tilted and diversified across ~350 positions, limiting single-name loss.
- Backed by a large, experienced manager (Metrics, >A$20bn AUM) with strong origination and a long track record.
- Structural tailwinds: bank disintermediation and hybrid phase-out funnel capital into the asset class.

### Bear case
- Pure credit-cycle exposure — a recession lifting corporate default and arrears rates is the principal threat to NAV and principal.
- Rate-cut sensitivity — falling cash rates lower floating-rate coupons and compress the distribution.
- Spread compression in a benign, competitive market erodes returns.
- Private-credit valuations are model-marked, not market-traded — NAV may understate stress until losses crystallise (a sector-wide concern).
- Manager- and fee-dependent; no capital growth — total return is essentially the income.

## Key Risks

- **Credit risk** — borrower defaults and rising arrears in the underlying corporate-loan book; the dominant risk to principal, concentrated in a recession.
- **Cash-rate cuts** — floating-rate coupons reset lower, reducing the distribution.
- **Spread compression** — a benign, capital-flooded credit market narrows lending spreads and lowers returns.
- **Valuation opacity** — private loans are model-marked; NAV may lag a deterioration in true credit quality.
- **Liquidity** — underlying loans are illiquid; in stress the listed units can trade at a discount to NAV despite stable marks.
- **Manager / key-person** — performance depends on Metrics' origination discipline and underwriting; concentration in one manager's process.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

MXT is a **listed private-credit fund and should be valued on NAV, yield and credit quality — not on a P/E** (the reported P/E is an artefact). The framework:

- **NAV / premium-discount** — units trade close to NAV (~A$1.93–1.94 in June 2026). The premium or discount to NAV is the key value gauge; persistent discounts signal credit or liquidity concern, premiums enable accretive raisings.
- **Distribution yield** — the headline metric: a ~8% trailing net return (12 months to Feb 2026) at a target of RBA cash rate + 3.25% p.a., paid **monthly**. Income, not capital growth, is the entire return.
- **Credit quality** — the durability of that yield depends on the loan book: investment-grade tilt, senior-secured share, arrears/default trends and sector diversification. Watch arrears and any NAV mark-down as the leading risk signal.
- **Floating-rate / low duration** — coupons reset with the RBA cash rate, so income rises in a hiking cycle and falls on cuts; duration risk is far lower than long-bond funds, giving low mark-to-market volatility.
- **Low beta / bond-like** — MXT behaves like a high-yielding cash-plus instrument, not an equity; it is held to dampen portfolio volatility and is an alternative to bank hybrids and term deposits.
- **Catalysts** — RBA cash-rate decisions, credit-spread moves, monthly distribution announcements, and any sign of credit-quality deterioration or rising arrears.
