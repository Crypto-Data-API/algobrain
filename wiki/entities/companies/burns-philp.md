Burns Philp & Company Limited was a historic Australian conglomerate, founded in Sydney in 1883 by James Burns and Robert Philp, with extensive operations in Papua New Guinea and the Pacific Islands — shipping, island trading, plantations, and later consumer goods. After a century as a blue-chip name, it nearly destroyed itself in the 1990s through a disastrous diversification into the global herbs-and-spices business, was rescued and ultimately controlled by New Zealand billionaire Graeme Hart's Rank Group, and was delisted from the ASX in 2006 following Rank's full takeover. It is now studied chiefly as a capital-destruction-and-distressed-rescue case in [[australian-investing]].

## History

- **1883–1960s — Pacific trader**: Burns Philp built a dominant position in South Pacific shipping, copra plantations, island retailing and merchant trading, becoming one of Australia's best-known companies and a fixture of PNG commerce.
- **1970s–1980s — diversification**: as island trading declined, the company pushed into food ingredients — becoming one of the world's largest producers of bakers' yeast — plus hardware retailing (BBC Hardware) and financial services.
- **1990s — the spice debacle**: an aggressive 1992–96 push to become a global number-two in herbs and spices (buying brands and paying large "slotting fees" for US supermarket shelf space against the entrenched leader McCormick) collapsed. In 1997 Burns Philp wrote off roughly A$700 million, breached covenants and traded within sight of receivership; the share price fell from several dollars to a few cents — one of the great Australian blue-chip wipeouts.
- **1997–2003 — Hart's rescue**: Graeme Hart's Rank Group bought into the distressed company (ultimately majority control), recapitalized it, exited spices and hardware, and refocused on yeast and bakery ingredients.
- **2003 — Goodman Fielder takeover**: Burns Philp launched a hostile ~A$2 billion-plus bid for Goodman Fielder, Australia's largest food company, completing the acquisition in 2003 — a leveraged bet that paid off handsomely when assets were later refloated and sold.
- **2006 — endgame**: after selling its major food businesses (Goodman Fielder was re-listed in late 2005; the yeast/ingredients business was sold to Associated British Foods in 2004), Rank Group acquired the remaining minorities and Burns Philp was delisted from the ASX in 2006. Hart's stake, built for a few hundred million dollars in the late 1990s, ultimately returned billions — the foundation of his later leveraged-packaging empire.

## Trading and Investing Lessons

1. **Diworsification kills blue chips** — a century-old franchise was nearly destroyed in five years by entering a category (US spices) where an entrenched competitor (McCormick) controlled the shelf. Competitive position beats brand heritage.
2. **Watch capitalized intangibles** — Burns Philp capitalized supermarket slotting fees as assets; when the strategy failed, the write-offs were sudden and fatal to the balance sheet. Aggressive accounting often precedes collapse.
3. **Distressed control investing** — Hart's playbook (buy control cheaply in crisis, fix the capital structure, sell assets into strength) is one of the canonical examples of contrarian distressed equity generating extreme returns.
4. **Equity is an option near insolvency** — shareholders who bought at cents in 1997-98 were effectively buying a call option on survival; most such options expire worthless, this one did not. Position sizing, not conviction, is what makes that trade survivable.
