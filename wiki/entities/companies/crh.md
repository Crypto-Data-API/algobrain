**CRH plc** (NYSE: CRH) is the largest provider of building materials and construction solutions in North America and one of the largest in the world, supplying aggregates, cement, asphalt, ready-mixed concrete, paving and construction services. For traders it is the bellwether liquid play on North American infrastructure and non-residential construction spending, and following its September 2023 move to a primary New York listing and December 2025 addition to the [[s-and-p-500]], it now trades as a US large-cap [[materials|Materials]] name rather than a European industrial.

## Business Overview

CRH was formed in 1970 from the merger of Cement Limited and Roadstone, and built itself over five decades into a global building-materials champion through hundreds of bolt-on acquisitions. It operates a vertically integrated, locally dense model: it owns aggregates reserves (sand, gravel, crushed stone) close to demand, and converts them into cement, asphalt and ready-mix concrete sold into infrastructure, non-residential and residential construction. The company employs roughly 78,500 people across approximately 3,390 operating locations in 28 countries, but the overwhelming majority of profit now comes from North America, where it is the #1 building-materials supplier. Its competitive moat is the local-monopoly economics of heavy, low-value-to-weight materials (aggregates cannot economically be shipped far) plus disciplined pricing and a relentless acquisition machine.

## Business Segments and the Vertical-Integration Model

CRH's edge is **vertical integration around aggregates**: it mines the rock, then captures margin again by converting it into higher-value products and selling integrated solutions (materials + paving/construction services) to the same project. This bundling and local density is what differentiates it from pure-play aggregates peers.

| Product / segment | What it is | Economic role |
|---|---|---|
| **Aggregates** | Crushed stone, sand, gravel from owned reserves | The moat: heavy, low value-to-weight (cannot ship far), reserve-backed local pricing power |
| **Cement** | Binding material for concrete | Capital-intensive; CRH balances cement production with downstream demand |
| **Ready-mixed concrete** | On-demand concrete delivered to site | Downstream conversion of aggregates + cement |
| **Asphalt & paving** | Road-surfacing materials and paving services | Direct leverage to highway/infrastructure (IIJA) spending |
| **Building/construction solutions** | Outdoor living, infrastructure products, construction services | Higher-margin, integrated "solutions" layer beyond raw materials |

**Why the moat holds:** aggregates have a low value-to-weight ratio, so transport cost makes each quarry a regional near-monopoly within ~30-50 miles. Owning permitted reserves (which are increasingly hard to permit) is a durable, scarce asset. Layering cement, ready-mix, asphalt and paving services on top captures margin at every downstream stage of the same project. CRH's serial **bolt-on M&A roll-up** continually adds local density and reserves.

## Competitive Positioning and Peers

CRH is the scaled, integrated, North-America-weighted leader. US aggregates pure-plays are higher-margin but narrower; the global cement majors are larger but more cyclical and emerging-market-exposed.

Its pitch is "best of both": the integrated, services-bundled, North-America-led model with the breadth of a global champion — now wrapped in a US large-cap [[s-and-p-500]] listing that has narrowed its historical valuation discount to the US peers.

## 2025-2026 developments

- **S&P 500 inclusion (22 December 2025).** CRH was added to the [[s-and-p-500]] index, a milestone that brought forced index-fund buying and cemented its status as a US large-cap.
- **US primary listing (September 2023).** CRH completed its transition to a primary NYSE listing and delisted from London, repositioning its investor base toward US capital. It remains incorporated in Ireland with headquarters in Dublin.
- **FY2025 results.** CRH reported full-year 2025 revenue of approximately US$37.4 billion (up ~5%) and adjusted EBITDA of approximately US$7.7 billion (up ~11%), with margin expansion driven by pricing discipline and favourable end-market demand.
- **Capital deployment.** During 2025 CRH deployed roughly US$5.8 billion in growth investment (acquisitions and capex) while returning about US$2.2 billion to shareholders via dividends and buybacks.
- **FY2026 guidance.** Management guided to net income of approximately US$3.9-4.1 billion and adjusted EBITDA of approximately US$8.1-8.5 billion, implying continued growth.

## Bull vs Bear Case

**Bull case**
- Direct, scaled leverage to a US infrastructure super-cycle — federal highway/[[infrastructure|IIJA]] funding, reshoring-driven non-residential construction, and data-centre build-out all flow through aggregates, asphalt and paving.
- Aggregates pricing power is durable: reserves are scarce/hard to permit, transport economics create local near-monopolies, and CRH has consistently pushed price above cost inflation.
- The M&A roll-up engine compounds value via disciplined bolt-ons that add local density and reserves.
- The US primary listing and [[s-and-p-500]] inclusion broadened the investor base and have narrowed the historical valuation discount to [[vulcan-materials]]/[[martin-marietta-materials]].
- FY2025 delivered revenue and EBITDA growth with margin expansion; FY2026 guidance implies continued growth.

**Bear case**
- High macro/rate sensitivity — a construction-cycle downturn or higher-for-longer rates hits volumes, especially residential and rate-sensitive non-residential.
- Infrastructure-funding cadence is policy-dependent and lumpy; IIJA tailwinds can plateau.
- A serial-acquirer model carries integration and capital-allocation risk and can mask organic softness.
- Cement is capital-intensive and exposed to energy costs and carbon/regulatory pressure.

## Valuation Framework (qualitative)

CRH is valued as a US infrastructure-materials compounder. The key qualitative drivers:

- **US-infrastructure / IIJA exposure:** the volume tailwind from federally funded highway and infrastructure work, plus reshoring and data-centre non-residential construction.
- **Aggregates pricing:** the ability to raise aggregates prices ahead of cost inflation is the single most important margin lever (reserve scarcity + local-monopoly economics).
- **M&A roll-up:** value created by disciplined bolt-on acquisitions that add reserves and local density — and the returns on that deployed capital.
- **Margin trajectory:** pricing discipline and mix (more integrated "solutions") driving EBITDA-margin expansion.
- **Re-rating dynamic:** the move to a US primary listing and [[s-and-p-500]] membership has been narrowing CRH's historical discount toward the US-aggregates peer multiples — a structural re-rating lever.

## Capital Return

CRH balances heavy **growth investment** (acquisitions and capex) with **shareholder returns via dividends and buybacks**. Per FY2025 disclosure, CRH deployed roughly US$5.8 billion in growth investment while returning about US$2.2 billion to shareholders — a framework that prioritizes reinvesting in reserves and bolt-ons but maintains a meaningful, growing dividend and ongoing repurchases. The split between growth deployment and returns is itself a key signal watched by investors.

## Risks

- **Construction-cycle and interest-rate sensitivity** — volumes fall in downturns / higher-rate regimes.
- **Policy dependence** — US infrastructure-funding cadence (IIJA) is lumpy and politically contingent.
- **M&A integration / capital-allocation risk** from the serial-acquirer model.
- **Cement cost & carbon exposure** — energy-intensive and subject to climate/regulatory pressure.
- **Cyclical end-market mix** — residential and non-residential demand swings.
