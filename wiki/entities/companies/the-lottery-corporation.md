**The Lottery Corporation Limited** (ASX: TLC) is an gambling company listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. The Lottery Corporation Limited (ASX: TLC) is Australia's largest lottery and keno operator, and one of the highest-performing lottery businesses in the world. Headquartered at 180 Ann Street in Brisbane, the group operates the iconic Australian draw-based lottery games — Powerball, Oz Lotto, TattsLotto (and its state variants), Set for Life — and the Instant Scratch-Its range under The Lott brand, as well as Keno in more than 3,400 licensed venues.

## Business Model & Segments

The Lottery Corporation operates two principal product segments:

- **Lotteries (The Lott)** — Australia's national lottery portfolio: Powerball, Oz Lotto, TattsLotto / Saturday Lotto, Monday and Wednesday Lotto, Super 66, Lucky Lotteries, Set for Life and Instant Scratch-Its. Distributed through approximately 3,800 franchised retailers (newsagents, pharmacies, convenience stores, service stations) as well as via The Lott's rapidly growing digital channel (website and app).
- **Keno** — a high-frequency number draw game sold in approximately 3,400 licensed clubs, pubs and hotels in Queensland, NSW, Victoria and the ACT, along with a growing digital channel.

Revenue is driven by ticket and entry sales, with a regulated share of turnover paid to state/territory governments and the remainder applied to prize pools, retailer commissions, operating costs and shareholder returns. Jackpot activity in Powerball and Oz Lotto is a key driver of period-to-period variability.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

The Lottery Corporation possesses one of the widest [[economic-moat|economic moats]] on the [[asx-200]], and it is a textbook example of a **licence / regulatory moat** — the strongest moat type, because the advantage is government-granted exclusivity rather than something a rival can replicate by spending money or building a better product:

- **Exclusive, long-dated lottery licences** — TLC holds the exclusive right to operate official draw-based lotteries in its jurisdictions, with key licences extending out to **~2068**. There is no legal direct competitor for products like Powerball, Oz Lotto and TattsLotto in its states. This is a fundamentally different and more durable advantage than [[switching-costs]] or network effects, which can erode; the moat here is statutory and multi-decade.
- **Iconic brands** — Powerball, Oz Lotto, TattsLotto, The Lott (umbrella brand) and Keno carry deep national recognition built over more than a century, reinforcing habitual, low-ticket participation.
- **Distribution scale** — a large franchised retail network (~3,800 outlets: newsagents, pharmacies, convenience stores, service stations) combined with a fast-growing, **higher-margin digital channel** (The Lott website and app), plus Keno across ~3,400 licensed venues. The dual retail-plus-digital footprint is itself a barrier to entry.

The defining feature is **durability**: the multi-decade licence horizon means the moat is unusually visible and long-lived versus a typical consumer-discretionary name. The principal long-term threats are not competitive but **regulatory** — licence renewal terms, tax/levy changes, and responsible-gambling rules — addressed in [[#Key Risks]] below.

## Competitors / Peer Set

In its core market TLC effectively has **no direct competitor**: it is a legal monopoly in licensed draw-based lotteries within its jurisdictions. The real contest is for share of the broader **gambling and discretionary-leisure wallet** rather than for lottery customers specifically.

## History

- **1881**: Lottery heritage traces to George Adams organising the first Tattersalls public sweep on the Sydney Cup.
- **1896**: Tattersalls Sweeps began selling tickets across Australia and to New Zealand by post.
- **1954**: Tattersalls acquired the Victorian public lottery licence.
- **2007–2014**: Tatts Group acquired state lottery businesses — Golden Casket (Queensland) in 2007, NSW Lotteries in 2010 and SA Lotteries in 2012 — consolidating Australia's lotteries market.
- **December 2017**: Tatts Group combined with Tabcorp Holdings under the Tabcorp brand, bringing the lotteries and keno businesses together with wagering and media.
- **July 2021**: Tabcorp announced the proposed demerger of its lotteries and keno business.
- **23 May 2022**: Demerger implemented; the Lottery Corporation began trading on the ASX on 24 May 2022 (initially on a deferred settlement basis).

Post-demerger, TLC has focused on product innovation, digital channel growth, retail network optimisation and selective price increases to sustain long-term contribution.

## Recent Developments

FY25 reflected a challenging jackpot cycle offset by price changes and digital growth; FY26 has seen a leadership transition:

- **FY25 (year to 30 June 2025)**: Revenue of approximately A$3.75 billion (down 6.2% on FY24), EBITDA of approximately A$749 million, statutory net profit of approximately A$365 million. Strong retention of Saturday Lotto following a price increase; Powerball entry price increase planned for November 2025.
- **Community contribution**: Approximately A$2.4 billion returned to governments, retailers and venue partners in FY25.
- **Powerball repricing (November 2025)**: Entry price increase implemented to support long-term prize pool growth.
- **CEO transition**: Long-serving CEO Sue van der Merwe announced her retirement in March 2025 following nearly 35 years in the lotteries industry. Wayne Pickup was appointed as incoming CEO, commencing in November 2025 subject to regulatory approvals.
- **Capital management**: TLC maintains a high dividend payout ratio underpinned by its cash-generative licensed lottery model.
- **1H FY26 result**: Management described "resilient underlying performance" despite the least favourable half for jackpot outcomes since the demerger, with an estimated ~A$400m turnover impact from weak jackpots. Interim dividend held at **8.0 cps** (~103% payout ratio). Digital share of lottery turnover rose to **41.2%** (+80 bps), the Saturday Lotto game change delivered ~103% price retention, and Keno continued strong growth. ## Trading Relevance

- **Index membership**: [[asx-200]] constituent; a defensive, high-yield, franking-credit-generating name favoured by Australian income investors.
- **Defensive profile**: Lottery turnover is relatively recession-resistant (low-ticket, habitual spend), making TLC a low-beta holding versus cyclical [[consumer-discretionary]] peers — though it sits in the discretionary GICS sector.
- **Key swing factor**: Quarterly/half-year results are dominated by the **jackpot cycle** — the number and size of large Powerball/Oz Lotto runs drives turnover variability far more than economic conditions. This is the single biggest catalyst/risk for the stock.
- **Other catalysts**: Digital-channel mix shift (rising ~150-200 bps/year), Keno growth, price increases (e.g. the November 2025 Powerball repricing), regulatory/licence renewals, and tax/levy changes.
- **Correlated names**: Other ASX gaming/wagering names ([[tabcorp]], [[aristocrat-leisure]]); broader Australian defensives.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Digital-channel mix shift** — the highest-quality growth lever: digital's share of lottery turnover has been rising ~150-200 bps/year (toward ~41% in 1H FY26), and online sales carry better economics than retail.
- **Selective price increases** — pricing power from the monopoly position allows periodic repricing (e.g. the **November 2025 Powerball entry-price increase**) with high retention, supporting long-term prize-pool and contribution growth.
- **Game changes & innovation** — e.g. the Saturday Lotto game change, which delivered ~103% price retention; ongoing product refresh sustains engagement.
- **Keno growth** — continued expansion across licensed venues plus a growing Keno digital channel.
- **Retail-network optimisation** — refining the ~3,800-outlet footprint and integrating it with digital.
- **Recurring catalysts** — above all the **jackpot cycle** (the number and size of large Powerball/Oz Lotto runs drives turnover variability far more than the economy); plus half-year/full-year results (February/August), regulatory and **licence renewals**, and **tax/levy changes**.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- A **monopoly licence moat out to ~2068** — visibility and durability of cash flows that almost no other [[asx-200]] business can match.
- **Recession-resistant demand** — low-ticket, habitual spend makes lottery turnover relatively insensitive to the economic cycle.
- **Strong cash generation and a high franked-dividend payout** — attractive to Australian income investors via the franking gross-up.
- **Digital margin uplift** — the structural mix shift to online lifts margins over time.
- **Pricing power** — the ability to reprice games (e.g. Powerball) with high customer retention.

### Bear case
- **Jackpot-cycle earnings variability** — weak jackpot runs can dent turnover materially (1H FY26 saw an estimated ~A$400m turnover impact from unfavourable jackpots).
- **High payout ratio** leaves little reinvestment buffer and limits balance-sheet flexibility.
- **Regulatory / tax risk** — the entire model rests on the licensed framework; renewal terms, levy increases or responsible-gambling rules could compress economics.
- **Mature, low-growth turnover** — organic volume growth is modest; growth leans on price, digital mix and Keno rather than new customers.
- **Rate sensitivity** — despite its GICS [[consumer-discretionary]] classification, TLC trades like a bond-proxy defensive, so its multiple is sensitive to rising yields.

## Key Risks

- **Jackpot-cycle variability** — the dominant near-term swing factor; a stretch of small jackpots suppresses turnover regardless of the economy.
- **Licence / regulatory risk** — licence renewal terms, tax/levy increases, or changes to the exclusive framework are the most material long-term threats to the moat.
- **Responsible-gambling regulation** — tighter advertising, spend-limit or harm-minimisation rules could constrain participation.
- **High payout ratio / thin equity base** — the elevated debt-to-equity shown in [[#Fundamentals]] reflects a licence-heavy, capital-light balance sheet; little retained-earnings cushion.
- **Rate sensitivity** — as a bond-proxy income name, the valuation de-rates if long yields rise.
- **Mature turnover growth** — limited organic volume upside beyond price and digital mix.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

The Lottery Corporation trades on a **premium defensive multiple** (the page shows a trailing P/E in the low-30s) that reflects the unusual durability of its monopoly licence moat and its appeal as a bond-proxy income holding rather than a growth story. Mechanically:

- **Income appeal / franking** — a high dividend payout, with franking credits providing a gross-up benefit for Australian-resident investors, makes TLC a core holding for income-tilted portfolios.
- **Low beta / defensive** — despite sitting in the GICS [[consumer-discretionary]] bucket, lottery turnover is habitual and low-ticket, so the stock behaves defensively and low-beta versus cyclical discretionary peers.
- **Liquidity** — a large-cap [[asx-200]] constituent with deep liquidity; positioning is predominantly outright equity.
- **What moves the stock** — results are dominated by the **jackpot cycle** and digital-mix progression far more than by macroeconomic conditions; bond yields drive the multiple.
