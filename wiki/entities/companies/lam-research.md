Lam Research (NASDAQ: LRCX) is one of the world's three dominant wafer-fabrication equipment (WFE) makers, specializing in etch, deposition, and surface-preparation tools sold to memory and foundry chipmakers. Alongside [[applied-materials]], [[asml]], [[kla-corporation]] and Tokyo Electron, it is a core [[technology|Information Technology]] "picks and shovels" play on the AI semiconductor capex cycle, and a constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]] index.

## Business Overview & Segments

Lam sells capital-equipment systems plus a large installed-base/services business that provides recurring revenue through cycles. Its competitive position is defined by leadership in two of the most critical front-end process steps — **etch** and **deposition** — where it sits in an oligopoly with [[applied-materials]] and Tokyo Electron.

| Business line | What it is | Role / mix (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **Etch systems** | Selectively removes material to form 3D transistor and memory structures; critical for 3D NAND and advanced logic | Core franchise; Lam is a market leader, increasingly important as structures go vertical |
| **Deposition systems** | Lays down thin films (CVD/ALD/ECD); enables advanced logic and memory stacks | Second core franchise; etch + deposition are Lam's twin pillars |
| **Customer Support Business Group (CSBG)** | Spares, upgrades, services and refurbished tools tied to the installed base | The recurring, counter-cyclical revenue cushion that smooths the WFE cycle |
| **Advanced packaging** | Tools for chiplets/heterogeneous integration and HBM | Fast-growing demand driver — management expects >40% growth in 2026 |

Demand drivers as of 2026: AI-driven foundry/logic spending at leading edge, a DRAM (HBM) buildout, a NAND upgrade cycle, and advanced packaging. Management said foundry rose to ~59% of systems revenue and projects calendar-2026 industry WFE spending around US$135B. China remains a large revenue region (expected below 30% of revenue in 2026) and the key geopolitical swing factor.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Lam's [[economic-moat]] is built on process-step specialization, deep customer co-development, and an installed-base annuity:

- **Etch/deposition technology leadership** — these are among the hardest, most-iterated steps in chipmaking; as transistors and memory go 3D (3D NAND, gate-all-around logic), etch and deposition intensity rises, and Lam's process know-how is extremely hard to replicate.
- **High [[switching-costs]] and qualification lock-in** — chipmakers co-develop recipes with Lam on each node and qualify tools over years; re-qualifying a competitor's tool mid-node is slow, risky and costly, so design-ins persist for the life of a process.
- **Installed-base services (CSBG)** — a large fleet of deployed tools generates recurring spares/upgrades/services revenue that is far less cyclical than new-system sales.
- **Scale R&D and oligopoly structure** — the front-end WFE market is effectively a handful of players ([[applied-materials]], Tokyo Electron, Lam in etch/deposition; [[asml]] in lithography; [[kla-corporation]] in inspection), each dominant in its niche with limited head-to-head overlap.

The moat is wide but the business is deeply cyclical: the same equipment intensity that drives upside in an upcycle reverses hard in memory downturns.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **AI capex super-cycle** — leading-edge foundry/logic spending tied to AI accelerators ([[nvidia]] and the hyperscalers' buildouts).
- **HBM / DRAM buildout** — high-bandwidth-memory demand lifts deposition and etch intensity per wafer.
- **NAND upgrade cycle** — the move to ever-taller 3D NAND stacks is etch-intensive — directly in Lam's wheelhouse.
- **Advanced packaging** — chiplets and heterogeneous integration (>40% growth expected in 2026) expand the served market.
- **Installed-base/CSBG growth** — a larger deployed fleet compounds recurring service revenue.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly earnings and WFE outlook revisions, NAND/DRAM capex announcements from Samsung/SK Hynix/[[micron-technology]], [[tsmc]] capex guidance, and U.S.-China export-control news (the dominant gap risk).

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- AI is driving a structural WFE super-cycle; record 2025 results show the demand is real, not a blip.
- Rising etch/deposition intensity per wafer (3D NAND, GAA logic, HBM, advanced packaging) expands Lam's served market faster than wafer volumes.
- The CSBG installed-base annuity dampens cyclicality and supports through-cycle cash generation.
- Quality fundamentals (8/8 on Fred's bars) plus buybacks make per-share compounding durable.

### Bear case
- WFE is deeply cyclical — the stock has historically drawn down 40%+ in memory downcycles despite quality fundamentals.
- China is a large revenue region being managed down under export controls; further restrictions cap shipments.
- Customer concentration among a few mega-cap chipmakers means any capex pause hits revenue hard.
- A rich trailing multiple (see Fundamentals) prices in continued AI-capex strength.

## Key Risks

- **Memory/WFE cyclicality** — sharp drawdowns in memory downturns are the historical norm.
- **U.S.-China export controls** — the dominant geopolitical swing factor; China revenue is being managed below 30% for 2026.
- **Customer concentration** — a handful of foundry/memory customers drive most systems revenue.
- **Valuation** — a high trailing P/E demands sustained AI-capex growth.
- **Technology transitions** — must keep winning etch/deposition design-ins at each new node against [[applied-materials]] and Tokyo Electron.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Lam trades as a high-quality but deeply cyclical semicap name — the market prices it on the WFE cycle and AI-capex tape rather than on stable earnings, which is why the trailing multiple swings with where in the cycle estimates sit (see the Fundamentals block below for figures as pulled). Mechanically:

- **Modest dividend + heavy buybacks** — Lam pays a small, growing dividend and returns most cash via repurchases; total capital return matters more than yield. A 10-for-1 split (October 2024) lowered the share price and broadened retail participation.
- **Semicap-basket beta** — trades tightly with [[applied-materials]], [[kla-corporation]], [[asml]] and Tokyo Electron; high beta to [[nvidia]]/AI-capex headlines and memory-pricing data points.
- **Options** — very liquid options market; the post-split lower share price broadened retail options activity.
- **Catalysts** — quarterly earnings and WFE outlook revisions, NAND/DRAM capex announcements, [[tsmc]] capex guidance, and export-control news.
- **Cyclicality** — expect 40%+ peak-to-trough drawdowns in memory downcycles despite quality fundamentals.
