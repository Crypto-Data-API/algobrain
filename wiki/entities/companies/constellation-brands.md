Constellation Brands (NYSE: STZ) is the exclusive US importer and brewer of Mexican beer brands Modelo Especial, Corona and Pacifico — the dominant profit engine after it divested most of its mainstream wine portfolio in 2025 — in the [[consumer-staples|Consumer Staples]] sector and a constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]]. It matters to traders as a fallen large-cap staple: Hispanic-consumer demand weakness, aluminum tariffs and beer volume declines have compressed the multiple, while a Berkshire Hathaway stake anchors the value case.

## Business Overview & Segments

Constellation is, in economic substance, a Mexican-beer company with a residual premium wine/spirits attachment. The franchise rests on the **perpetual, exclusive US rights to import and brew the Modelo and Corona portfolios** — rights Constellation acquired in 2013 as the antitrust remedy when [[anheuser-busch-inbev]] bought the rest of Grupo Modelo. It brews those brands in large, purpose-built facilities in Nuevo León and Veracruz, Mexico, and ships into the US. After the 2025 divestiture of the mainstream ("Woodbridge-tier") wine portfolio to The Wine Group, beer dominates the P&L and the equity story; what remains in [[consumer-staples|Consumer Staples]] terms is a high-margin, single-country-sourced beer machine with a small luxury-wine tail.

| Segment | What it is | Revenue / role |
|---|---|---|
| **Beer** | Exclusive US Modelo Especial, Corona Extra, Pacifico, Victoria and Modelo Chelada — brewed in Mexico, sold in the US; the #1 dollar-share gainer in US tracked beer channels | ~91% of net sales; **$8,315.2M in FY2026** — essentially the entire profit engine and the reason to own the stock |
| **Wine & Spirits** | A smaller, higher-end portfolio (premium/luxury wine plus craft spirits) left after the 2025 sale of mainstream wine | **$823.8M** in FY2026 (down from $1,668.9M pre-divestiture) — a minority business deliberately repositioned upmarket |

The durable asset is the beer rights, not the breweries: the Modelo/Corona US licenses are perpetual and exclusive, so the brands cannot be re-bid away by a competitor. Two accounting caveats matter for anyone reading the [[#Fundamentals|fundamentals]] block below. First, the negative FY2025 net margin and −5.5% ROE reflect **large non-cash impairments of wine goodwill and assets**, not the cash economics of beer, which remain strongly profitable. Second, Constellation carries a legacy equity stake from its **Canopy Growth cannabis investment** (made ~2017–18), which drove a string of heavy historical write-downs and distorts several prior-year API margin averages; treat the FY2021–FY2024 operating/EBITDA-margin figures in the source series as segment-restatement artifacts rather than real margins.

## 2025–2026 Developments

- **Berkshire Hathaway stake:** disclosed a ~$1.24B position in February 2025 (Q4 2024 13F) and increased it during 2025 — a prominent value endorsement that put a floor under sentiment.
- **Wine divestiture (2025):** sold the bulk of the mainstream wine business to The Wine Group, repositioning Wine & Spirits around higher-end brands.
- **FY2026 results (year ended 28 Feb 2026, reported April 2026):** net sales $9,139.0M vs $10,208.7M (decline mostly divestiture-driven); comparable EPS **$11.82, down 14%**. Beer depletions fell for Modelo Especial (~-3%), Corona Extra (~-7%) and Modelo Chelada (~-1%), but grew for Pacifico (+15%) and Victoria (~+16%). Beer operating margin slipped 170bp to **38.0%** on lower shipments and aluminum-tariff-driven COGS. Q4 FY2026 headline figures missed consensus.
- **FY2027 guidance:** comparable EPS **$11.20–$11.90** (flat-to-down vs $11.82); beer operating margin 37–38%; enterprise comparable operating margin 32–33%; ~$1.8B free cash flow. The company **withdrew its prior FY2028 outlook**, citing the demand environment.
- Persistent headwinds: weak Hispanic-consumer spending (an outsized share of Modelo/Corona volume), GLP-1/moderation trends in alcohol, and tariffs on aluminum cans.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Constellation's [[economic-moat|economic moat]] is unusual for a beverage company because it is **regulatory/contractual, not just brand-based**:

- **Perpetual exclusive US rights to Modelo and Corona** — the single most important asset. These rights, carved out of the 2013 [[anheuser-busch-inbev]]–Grupo Modelo antitrust remedy, are perpetual and exclusive; a rival cannot license or out-bid Constellation for the same brands. This is an irreplaceable, legally protected position — closer to a franchise concession than an ordinary trademark.
- **Brand strength and share momentum** — Modelo Especial has been a structural share-taker in US beer, and the group has been the **#1 dollar-share gainer in US tracked channels**, with Pacifico and Victoria now growing fast enough to partially offset softness in the flagships.
- **Distribution scale and Mexican brewing capacity** — large, dedicated capacity in Nuevo León and Veracruz plus an entrenched US distributor network give cost and reach advantages a new entrant could not quickly replicate.

The moat is **wide but narrowing at the margin**. The protected rights do not insulate the company from *demand* erosion: a large share of Modelo/Corona volume comes from Hispanic-American consumers whose spending softened in 2025–26, and the secular GLP-1 / alcohol-moderation trend pressures the whole category. So the franchise is irreplaceable on the supply side but exposed on the demand side — the bull/bear debate is almost entirely about volume, not about whether someone can take the brands away.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Newer brand growth offsetting flagship decline** — Pacifico (~+15%) and Victoria (~+16%) depletions are growing fast enough to cushion declines in Modelo Especial, Corona Extra and Modelo Chelada; the mix is the key volume swing factor.
- **Premiumization** — both the upmarket repositioning of the residual Wine & Spirits book and continued trade-up within beer support price/mix even as volumes soften.
- **Capital return** — substantial free cash flow (~$1.8B guided for FY2027) funds buybacks and a dividend; capital-allocation moves typically land alongside guidance.
- **Margin recovery** — beer operating margin (38.0% in FY2026, guided 37–38%) recovering as aluminum-tariff and shipment headwinds normalize would be a direct earnings lever.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly earnings on an off-cycle February fiscal year, monthly Circana/Nielsen beer scanner/depletion data, Berkshire Hathaway 13F filings, and tariff/immigration-policy headlines that bear on the core consumer.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- **Irreplaceable franchise:** perpetual exclusive US rights to Modelo and Corona — a legally protected, share-gaining beer business that a competitor cannot take away.
- **Berkshire floor:** [[berkshire-hathaway]]'s disclosed and increasing stake is a high-profile value endorsement that has anchored sentiment.
- **Cash generation:** ~$1.8B FCF guided, high beer margins, and ongoing buybacks; the multiple has already de-rated to value territory.
- **Mix optionality:** Pacifico/Victoria momentum and premiumization can stabilize the top line even with flagship softness.

### Bear case
- **Demand erosion:** Hispanic-consumer spending weakness hits an outsized share of Modelo/Corona volume; flagship depletions are already declining.
- **Secular moderation:** GLP-1 drugs and the broader alcohol-moderation trend pressure the entire beer category structurally, not cyclically.
- **Cost/policy drag:** aluminum-can tariffs and immigration/tariff-policy headlines weigh on COGS and on the core consumer simultaneously.
- **Guidance credibility:** management **withdrew its prior FY2028 outlook** citing the demand environment, and Q4 FY2026 missed consensus — a signal that visibility has deteriorated.
- **Leverage:** D/E of 1.67x and sub-1 quick ratio leave less cushion than peers if cash flow disappoints.

## Key Risks

- **Hispanic-consumer demand** — a disproportionate share of Modelo/Corona volume rides on Hispanic-American spending; softness there transmits directly to the profit engine.
- **GLP-1 / alcohol-moderation** — a secular, not cyclical, headwind to US beer consumption; the most important long-term demand risk.
- **Tariff & immigration policy** — aluminum-can tariffs raise beer COGS, and immigration-policy headlines can dent the core consumer; both are unpredictable, externally driven swing factors.
- **Leverage** — D/E 1.67x with a 0.56x quick ratio (see [[#Fundamentals|fundamentals]]) gives a thinner balance-sheet cushion than the staples average if FCF undershoots.
- **Further wine impairments** — the residual Wine & Spirits book and any remaining Canopy/cannabis legacy carry continued non-cash write-down risk that can distort GAAP results.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Constellation has re-rated from a **premium-multiple growth staple into a value/turnaround name**: the de-rating reflects beer-volume worries and the demand headwinds above rather than any threat to the brand rights. Mechanically:

- **Sentiment tracks scanner data** — the stock swings on **monthly Circana/Nielsen beer scanner and depletion prints** more than on almost anything else; depletion trends for Modelo, Corona, Pacifico and Victoria are the real-time tape for the name.
- **Off-cycle fiscal year** — Constellation runs a **February fiscal year**, so it reports roughly Apr/Jul/Oct/Jan, off the calendar-quarter cadence of most [[consumer-staples|staples]] peers; earnings land when few other large-caps report.
- **Berkshire as a floor** — [[berkshire-hathaway]]'s disclosed and rising stake is widely read as a sentiment floor; **13F filings are themselves catalysts**.
- **Earnings anchors** — FY2026 comparable EPS came in at **$11.82** (down 14%), with FY2027 guided to **$11.20–$11.90** (flat-to-down); the withdrawn FY2028 outlook is the overhang. (No current price is asserted here — see the [[#Fundamentals|fundamentals]] block for the figures as pulled.)
- **Capital return** — a real dividend plus buybacks funded by ~$1.8B of FCF; it screens as a [[s-and-p-500]] value/income name, held in consumer-staples (XLP) and dividend-growth baskets, with a liquid options chain.
