**Worley Limited** (ASX: WOR) is an oil & gas equipment & services company listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. Worley Limited (ASX: WOR) is a global professional services company providing engineering, procurement, construction management, operations and asset performance, and technical and sustainability consulting to the world's energy, chemicals and resources industries. Headquartered at 420 George Street in Sydney, Worley employs approximately 38,000 people across 44 countries and has positioned itself as one of the leading partners for customers navigating the energy transition.

## Business Model & Segments

Worley provides a full lifecycle service offering, structured around four core propositions:

- **Project delivery** — engineering, procurement and supply-chain management; construction and fabrication management; installation and commissioning.
- **Asset performance** — operations and maintenance, workflow optimisation, inspection and integrity.
- **Specialist and technology solutions** — including digital twins, advanced analytics and proprietary technology solutions.
- **Professional and consulting services** — planning and investment, capital project support, digital solutions, environment and sustainability consulting, optimisation, repurposing and decommissioning.

Customer sectors include:

- **Chemicals and Fuels** — plastic recycling, low-carbon fuels, refining, petrochemicals and carbon capture, utilisation and storage (CCUS).
- **Conventional Energy** — midstream infrastructure, decommissioning, integrated gas/LNG and water.
- **Low-Carbon Energy** — hydrogen, energy storage, nuclear power, power networks, renewable energy, biogas and renewable natural gas.
- **Resources** — battery materials, copper and energy transition materials, aluminium / bauxite / alumina, precious metals, iron ore and mined fertilisers.

Sustainable and transitional work represents approximately 69% of Worley's backlog — up from around 32% only a few years earlier — reflecting its strategic re-positioning.

## History

- **1971**: John Grill co-founded Wholohan Grill and Partners, a small Australian engineering consultancy that became the foundation of today's Worley. (Grill served as CEO from 1975 to 2012.)
- **1987**: Acquired the Australian operations of US-based Worley engineering firm; adopted the Worley name.
- **2002**: Listed on the Australian Securities Exchange.
- **2004**: Merged with US-based Parsons E&C to form WorleyParsons Limited, substantially expanding its US and Middle East presence and process engineering capability.
- **2004–2019**: Operated as WorleyParsons; expanded globally through numerous bolt-on acquisitions.
- **October 2018**: Announced acquisition of Jacobs Engineering Group's Energy, Chemicals and Resources (ECR) business for approximately US$3.3 billion.
- **April 2019**: Jacobs ECR acquisition completed, adding approximately 20,000 staff and North American scale.
- **October 2019**: Rebranded from WorleyParsons to Worley Limited.

## Recent Developments

Under CEO Chris Ashton (CEO since early 2020), Worley has focused on the energy transition, disciplined margin improvement and backlog growth:

- **FY25 full-year results (year to 30 June 2025)**: Consistent revenue, earnings and margin growth; underlying EBITA margin rose to 6.8% (from 6.5% in FY24). Backlog reached A$16.9 billion (up 22% on 30 June 2024) following the addition of the Venture Global CP2 project.
- **Sustainability mix**: Sustainable and transitional work represented approximately 69% of backlog at FY25, tracking the company's long-stated target of 75% of revenue coming from sustainability projects.
- **1H FY26 results (six months to 31 December 2025)**: Aggregated revenue of A$6.3 billion, up 5.4% on pcp; underlying EBITA of A$377 million (steady). Backlog of approximately A$6.7 billion (factored) providing revenue visibility into 2H FY26 and 2027.
- **Bookings**: Record bookings of A$9.8 billion for the half, supported by wins across LNG, carbon capture, mining and energy infrastructure.
- **Market commentary**: CEO Chris Ashton noted a "tone shift" entering calendar 2026 compared with tariff-related uncertainty in late 2025.
- **Strategic execution**: Continued focus on higher-margin, lower-risk reimbursable contracting and selective M&A targeting sustainability capability.
- **HY26 bookings (six months to 31 Dec 2025)**: Record bookings of approximately A$9.8 billion (up ~63% on the prior half); period-end aggregated backlog of approximately A$16.7 billion (vs A$16.9 billion at FY25). New contract wins since 1 January 2026 carry potential value of A$3.5 billion+ (Source: Verified via Perplexity, sonar, 2026-06-10).
- **FY25 summary (per external reporting)**: aggregated revenue ~A$12.05 billion, underlying EBITA ~A$823 million (+10%), NPATA ~A$475 million (+29%); on-market buyback of up to A$500 million; management guided to moderate growth in FY26.

> Note: the A$6.3 billion / A$6.7 billion HY26 figures above reflect half-year aggregated revenue and a factored half-year backlog measure, whereas the A$16.7 billion is the total aggregated backlog at 31 December 2025; both are consistent measures of different bases.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Worley is **not** a commodity price-taker like an [[iron-ore]] or [[coal]] producer — it is an asset-light, capital-light professional-services firm whose "product" is engineering hours, project management and embedded technical knowledge. Its [[economic-moat|economic moat]] is therefore of the services/integration kind rather than the cost-curve kind, built on several reinforcing layers:

- **Scale and global delivery footprint** — roughly 38,000 staff across 44 countries lets Worley staff the world's largest [[energy]], chemicals and resources projects and move work to lower-cost delivery hubs. Few rivals can field comparable global capacity, which is itself a barrier to entry on mega-projects.
- **Embedded asset knowledge and switching costs** — once Worley is engaged across the lifecycle of a client's asset (design → build → operate → maintain → decommission), it accumulates proprietary knowledge of that facility. Replacing the incumbent engineer mid-lifecycle is costly and risky for the client, producing recurring, sticky [[energy]]-sector relationships.
- **Accumulated energy-transition capability** — a deep, hard-to-replicate bench in hydrogen, CCUS, low-carbon fuels, [[lng]] and battery/transition materials. With sustainable and transitional work at roughly **69% of backlog** (up from ~32% a few years earlier), Worley has front-run peers in re-tooling for the [[energy-transition]].
- **Backlog as revenue visibility** — a large multi-year backlog (~A$16.7B at end-2025) converts to forward revenue, smoothing the lumpiness of project work and giving the franchise more durability than a single-project contractor.
- **Mix shift toward reimbursable contracting** — the deliberate move to higher-margin, lower-risk reimbursable (cost-plus) work over fixed-price/lump-sum exposure is a quality lever that improves both margin and risk profile.

The moat is **real but moderate**: engineering services is competitive and absolute margins are thin (single-digit EBITA), so the edge is scale, relationships and transition positioning rather than pricing power. The business does not own the underlying [[commodities]] — it sells the capability to build and run the infrastructure that produces them.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Energy-transition capex super-cycle** — the structural growth driver: global decarbonisation, electrification and energy-security spending creates a multi-decade pipeline of design-and-build work in hydrogen, CCUS, low-carbon fuels, power networks and transition materials. See [[energy-transition]].
- **Sustainability revenue-mix shift** — progress toward the long-stated target of **75% of revenue** from sustainability projects (vs ~69% of backlog today) lifts both growth and the quality of earnings.
- **Large contract awards** — wins in [[lng]], carbon capture (CCUS), hydrogen and mining drive bookings and backlog; the addition of projects like Venture Global CP2 illustrates the lumpy-but-large nature of catalysts.
- **Backlog and bookings momentum** — record HY26 bookings (~A$9.8B) and a large aggregated backlog (~A$16.7B) underpin forward revenue visibility into FY26–FY27.
- **Margin expansion** — the mix shift toward reimbursable contracting and operating leverage lifts underlying EBITA margin (6.5% → 6.8% across FY24–FY25), a key re-rating lever.
- **Selective M&A** — bolt-on acquisitions targeting sustainability and digital capability, funded by strong free cash flow.
- **Capital return** — an on-market buyback (up to A$500m announced) plus dividends signal balance-sheet confidence.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Structural, multi-decade [[energy-transition]] tailwind with Worley positioned ahead of peers on sustainability mix.
- Record bookings and a large multi-year backlog give unusual revenue visibility for a services firm.
- Margin uplift from the reimbursable-contracting shift and operating leverage; underlying EBITA margin trending up.
- Asset-light/capital-light model converts earnings to strong free cash flow, funding dividends and a buyback.
- Optionality across hydrogen, CCUS, [[lng]] and transition materials not fully reflected in a mid-teens multiple.

### Bear case
- Absolute margins are thin (single-digit EBITA/net margin), so small cost overruns or pricing pressure hit profit hard.
- Revenue is geared to the energy-capex cycle — project deferrals or a capex downturn would shrink backlog conversion.
- Execution/contract risk on large projects, especially any residual fixed-price/lump-sum exposure (a legacy industry pain point).
- Still meaningful exposure to conventional oil & gas clients during a transition whose pace and economics are uncertain.
- Competitive, fragmented services market can compress pricing; tariff/macro uncertainty can pause client final-investment-decisions.

## Key Risks

- **Energy-capex cyclicality** — backlog and bookings depend on clients' willingness to sanction capital projects; a [[energy]]-capex downturn or wave of project deferrals directly slows revenue.
- **Contract execution risk** — fixed-price/lump-sum work can generate cost overruns and write-downs; the industry's history of project losses is the cautionary backdrop, even as Worley shifts toward reimbursable contracts.
- **Thin-margin amplification** — single-digit margins mean modest cost slippage or utilisation drops have an outsized earnings effect.
- **Client/sector concentration** — heavy exposure to oil & gas and resources clients during a transition of uncertain pace; a slower-than-expected transition (or a faster collapse in conventional spend) cuts both ways.
- **Currency** — the bulk of revenue is earned outside Australia, so AUD strength translates to lower reported AUD earnings.
- **M&A integration** — a growth-by-acquisition history (notably the 2019 Jacobs ECR deal) carries integration and goodwill risk.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Worley does **not** trade on a commodity price — it trades on **backlog, bookings and margin**. The market re-rates the stock as the sustainability revenue mix climbs and underlying EBITA margin expands, and de-rates it when bookings slow or project deferrals threaten backlog conversion. Mechanically:

- **Key reads** — half-year/full-year results where the focus is the **backlog** number, **bookings** momentum and the **EBITA margin** trajectory, plus the sustainability-mix progress toward 75%.
- **Cyclicality, lower beta than a producer** — earnings track the broad [[energy]]-capex cycle, but the asset-light model and recurring services revenue make the stock less violently cyclical than a pure commodity producer; it is a derivative of clients' capital spending rather than of a spot price.
- **Options/liquidity** — ASX single-stock options are thinner than US large caps; most expression is via the underlying. The stock is moderately volatile around half-year results and major contract awards.
- **Capital return** — a dividend (partly franked) plus an on-market buyback; [[franking-credits]] add to the after-tax yield for Australian residents.
- **Multiple** — a mid-teens P/E that can re-rate higher if the transition-mix and margin story compounds, or compress if the energy-capex cycle turns; price-to-sales is low, reflecting the thin-margin, high-turnover services model.
