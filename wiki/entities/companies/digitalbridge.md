DigitalBridge Group Inc. (NYSE: DBRG) is an alternative asset manager dedicated to digital infrastructure — data centers, cell towers, fiber, and edge — managing roughly **$108 billion of AUM** across funds such as the DigitalBridge Partners flagship series, credit, and InfraBridge. As of mid-2026 the stock is primarily a **merger-arbitrage situation**: [[softbank|SoftBank Group]] agreed on 29 December 2025 to acquire DigitalBridge for **$16.00 per share in cash** (~$4 billion) as part of its AI-infrastructure push, and the deal is pending closing in H2 2026.

## Business Overview

DigitalBridge evolved from Colony Capital (a diversified real estate manager under Tom Barrack) into a pure-play digital-infrastructure investment manager under CEO Marc Ganzi, completing the rotation in 2021-2022. It earns management fees on **fee-earning equity under management (FEEUM)** — $40.8 billion as of Q1 2026, up 9% year over year — plus carried interest, with balance-sheet GP stakes in portfolio companies spanning data centers (e.g. Vantage, Switch, DataBank), towers (Vertical Bridge), and fiber (Zayo). Q1 2026: fee revenue $87.5 million, fee-related earnings $24.0 million (27% FRE margin), distributable earnings $13.4 million.

## Business Segments / Products

DigitalBridge is an *asset manager* (GP) for digital infrastructure, not a direct operator — it raises third-party capital into funds and earns fees plus carry, with selective GP/balance-sheet co-investments. The model spans the four pillars of digital infrastructure:

| Pillar | Representative portfolio companies | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Data centers** | Vantage Data Centers, Switch, DataBank | The AI-capex epicentre; hyperscaler/AI demand is the core growth narrative |
| **Cell towers** | Vertical Bridge | Stable, contracted, inflation-linked cash flows |
| **Fiber** | Zayo | Long-haul/metro connectivity backbone |
| **Edge / small cells / connectivity** | various | 5G densification and edge-compute build-out |

Fund families and fee engine:

| Vehicle | Role |
|---|---|
| **DigitalBridge Partners (DBP I/II/III)** | Flagship equity drawdown funds — the primary FEEUM source |
| **Credit** | Digital-infrastructure debt strategies |
| **InfraBridge** | Mid-market global infrastructure (acquired team) |
| **Co-investment / GP stakes** | Balance-sheet capital alongside LPs |

Economics: management fees on **FEEUM** ($40.8B, Q1 2026) drive **fee-related earnings (FRE)**, supplemented by **carried interest** on fund performance. FRE margin (~27% in Q1 2026) is the profitability metric the market scaled the equity on pre-deal (Source: [[stockmarketapi-options-stocks-2026-04-13]]).

## Competitive Positioning / Peers

DigitalBridge is unusual as a *listed pure-play digital-infrastructure asset manager* — most digital-infra exposure is either an operating REIT (owns the assets) or a diversified alternatives manager (digital is one sleeve). DBRG sits between the two.

| Company | Ticker | What it is | Relationship to DigitalBridge |
|---|---|---|---|
| **DigitalBridge** | DBRG | Pure-play digital-infra GP/asset manager | Subject — now a SoftBank merger-arb situation |
| [[equinix]] | EQIX | Data-center REIT (operator, retail colo + interconnection) | Asset-owner comp; what DBRG's data-center holdings resemble |
| [[digital-realty]] | DLR | Data-center REIT (operator, wholesale/hyperscale) | Asset-owner comp; the other public DC REIT |
| [[blackstone]] | BX | Diversified alternatives manager with large digital-infra/DC bets (QTS) | Fee-model comp and a much larger competitor for infra capital |
| Brookfield (BAM/BIP) | BAM/BIP | Global infrastructure alternatives manager | Direct competitor for digital-infra fundraising |
| [[american-tower]] | AMT | Tower REIT (operator) | Asset-owner comp for the towers sleeve |
| Blue Owl | OWL | Alternatives manager (GP-stakes/credit) | Fee-model comp |

Versus operating REITs [[equinix]]/[[digital-realty]], DigitalBridge offers *asset-light, fee-based* exposure to the same demand (data-center capacity for AI) without owning the depreciating real estate directly. Versus [[blackstone]]/Brookfield it is the focused pure-play but far smaller and more capital-constrained — which is precisely why a slow flagship fundraise (DBP III) made the SoftBank cash exit attractive.

## 2025–2026 Developments

- **29 Dec 2025:** SoftBank Group agreed to acquire all outstanding DBRG common stock for **$16.00/share cash** — a 15% premium to the prior close and ~50% premium to the unaffected 52-week average — to expand SoftBank's AI data-center and connectivity capacity alongside its Stargate ambitions.
- **23 Apr 2026:** DigitalBridge stockholders approved the acquisition with **~96% of votes cast in favor**; closing expected **H2 2026** subject to regulatory approvals.
- **Q1 2026 results:** FEEUM $40.8B (+9%); FRE $24.0M; distributable earnings $13.4M; results also included a one-off gain and a favorable jury verdict noted in filings.
- Pre-deal 2025 had been a fundraising grind (flagship DBP III slower than hoped), which is part of why the board took the cash exit.

## Bull vs Bear Case

Because DBRG is a pending acquisition, the "bull/bear" frame is really *deal-closes vs deal-breaks* overlaid on the standalone thesis:

**Bull case (deal certainty + standalone)**
- SoftBank cash deal at **$16.00/share** is shareholder-approved (~96% in favor) — the dominant, near-term outcome with a defined arb return to an H2 2026 close.
- Standalone, DigitalBridge owns scarce digital-infrastructure exposure (data centers, towers, fiber) at the centre of the AI-capex super-cycle.
- Asset-light, fee-based model with growing FEEUM and ~27% FRE margin would compound if fundraising re-accelerated.

**Bear case (deal-break risk)**
- A regulatory or financing break would re-rate the stock to **standalone fundamentals well below $16** — the asymmetric downside.
- Standalone fundraising had been a grind (DBP III slower than hoped) — part of why the board chose the cash exit.
- SoftBank balance-sheet stress or financing-market deterioration could threaten the deal.
- Capital constraints versus mega-managers ([[blackstone]], Brookfield) competing for the same infra capital.

## Valuation Framework

DBRG straddles two valuation regimes:

**As a merger-arb situation (current):** value the **arb spread** — the annualised return from the current price to the $16.00 cash terms, weighted by the probability and timing of an H2 2026 close versus the downside in a break scenario. This dominates the equity today.

**As a standalone asset manager (the break-scenario floor and the model the deal price was struck against):**
- **FEEUM growth** — fee-earning equity under management ($40.8B, +9%) is the top-line driver; new fund closes expand it.
- **Fee-related earnings (FRE) and FRE margin** — the recurring, capitalizable profit stream (~27% margin) the market multiples; the cleanest quality metric for an alt manager.
- **Carried interest / distributable earnings** — the variable, performance-linked upside on fund realizations.
- **Data-center / AI-infrastructure demand** — the secular tailwind underpinning FEEUM growth and the strategic rationale for the SoftBank bid.
- **Sum-of-parts (fee business + GP stakes + net cash/debt)** — the framework for the standalone re-rate if the deal breaks.

These are qualitative inputs; do not infer a specific target beyond the stated $16.00 deal price.

## Capital Return / Dilution

Pre-deal, DigitalBridge paid a nominal common dividend and carried preferred equity; capital was prioritized toward GP co-investments and balance-sheet stakes rather than large buybacks. The completed Colony-to-digital transformation involved significant asset sales and share-count history. With the **SoftBank cash acquisition pending**, capital-return policy is effectively frozen — the $16.00/share cash consideration is the terminal return to common holders if the deal closes, superseding ongoing dividend/buyback considerations.
