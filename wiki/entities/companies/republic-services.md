Republic Services (NYSE: RSG) is the second-largest US **waste-collection, recycling and environmental-services** company, behind Waste Management. It is an S&P 500 constituent and a classic defensive compounder: contracted, recurring waste-collection revenue, strong pricing power (often CPI-linked or above), high barriers to entry (landfills, routes, permits) and steady free-cash-flow growth make it a low-beta, "all-weather" industrial that traders use for defensive positioning and as a bond-proxy-adjacent name.

## Business Overview & Segments

Republic provides collection, transfer, recycling and disposal services to residential, commercial and industrial customers, plus a growing **Environmental Solutions** segment (hazardous-waste and field services). Its [[economic-moat|moat]] is structural: a dense network of collection routes, transfer stations and landfills that is extremely difficult to replicate, paired with long-term municipal/commercial contracts and consistent above-inflation pricing. Earnings quality is high — recurring revenue, disciplined pricing, fleet/route efficiency, landfill-gas-to-energy and sustainability investments, and bolt-on M&A funded by robust free cash flow.

The business is best understood through its vertically integrated waste value chain plus the higher-margin environmental arm:

| Segment / line | What it is | Role in the model (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **Collection** | Residential, commercial and industrial waste pickup under long-term contracts | The recurring revenue base and customer relationship — the densest, stickiest part of the model |
| **Transfer** | Transfer stations that consolidate waste for efficient long-haul to landfills | The logistics link that makes route density and landfill ownership pay off |
| **Disposal (landfills)** | Owned landfills — the scarce, permitted, hard-to-replicate asset | The structural choke point and highest barrier to entry; "internalisation" of own waste drives margin |
| **Recycling** | Material recovery facilities processing recyclables | Margin geared to recycled-commodity prices; ESG-positioning and customer demand |
| **Environmental Solutions** | Hazardous-waste handling and field services | The fastest-growing, higher-margin expansion arm and a key M&A focus |
| **Renewable energy (RNG)** | Landfill-gas-to-energy projects | Sustainability optionality that monetises a byproduct of the landfill base |

The franchise's defining feature is **route density + landfill ownership**: owning the disposal endpoint and packing routes tightly together is what makes the network economics so hard for a new entrant to replicate.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Republic has one of the **widest, most durable moats in the [[industrials]] sector**, built on physical assets and local-network economics rather than technology:

- **Landfill scarcity / permitting** — building a new landfill is enormously difficult: permitting, zoning, environmental approvals and NIMBY opposition make new disposal capacity nearly impossible to add. Owning permitted landfills is a near-irreplaceable asset and the foundation of the moat.
- **Route density** — the more customers packed onto a given collection route, the lower the cost per stop. Incumbents with dense routes have a structural cost advantage a new entrant cannot match without first winning scale — a classic chicken-and-egg barrier.
- **Vertical integration / internalisation** — owning collection, transfer and disposal lets Republic "internalise" its own waste into its own landfills, capturing margin at each step and controlling the highest-cost input.
- **Pricing power** — long-term municipal and commercial contracts are frequently CPI-linked or repriced above inflation; the essential, non-discretionary nature of waste service gives durable [[switching-costs]]-like pricing leverage.
- **Scale & capital** — national scale spreads fixed costs (fleet, technology, landfill capex) and funds steady bolt-on M&A — a consolidation flywheel in a still-fragmented industry.

This is a textbook wide-moat, recurring-revenue compounder: the assets are scarce, the demand is non-discretionary, and pricing rises with or above inflation.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Pricing above inflation** — CPI-linked and repriced contracts let Republic push price ahead of cost inflation — the highest-quality earnings lever.
- **Bolt-on M&A** — ~$1 billion of acquisitions planned in 2026 across Recycling & Waste and Environmental Solutions; consolidating a fragmented industry is the durable growth engine.
- **Environmental Solutions expansion** — the higher-margin hazardous-waste and field-services arm is the fastest-growing segment and a strategic priority.
- **Renewable natural gas (RNG)** — landfill-gas-to-energy projects monetise a byproduct and add a green-energy revenue stream.
- **Fleet / route efficiency** — EVs, automation and route optimisation lower unit costs and lift margins.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly earnings and annual guidance updates (2026: revenue $17.05-$17.15B, adjusted EBITDA $5.475-$5.525B, adjusted EPS $7.20-$7.28), acquisition announcements, recycled-commodity and RNG economics, and fuel-cost trends.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Wide-moat, non-discretionary, recurring-revenue business with above-inflation pricing power — an "all-weather" defensive compounder.
- Scarce landfill assets and route density create near-insurmountable barriers to entry.
- Steady free-cash-flow growth funds a growing dividend plus ~$1B/year of accretive bolt-on M&A.
- Higher-margin Environmental Solutions and RNG add growth and ESG optionality on top of the stable core.

### Bear case
- A premium defensive multiple leaves limited upside and de-rates if rates rise (it trades partly as a bond proxy).
- Recycled-commodity price swings and fuel/labour-cost inflation pressure margins.
- Mid-single-digit EPS growth (~3% at the 2026 guidance midpoint) is steady but unexciting — a low-growth compounder, not a high-growth name.
- M&A integration risk and the eventual exhaustion of easy tuck-in targets.

## Key Risks

- **Commodity-price exposure** — recycled-material (paper, plastics, metals) and RNG prices swing recycling-segment margins.
- **Cost inflation** — fuel and labour are large variable costs; spikes squeeze margins until repriced.
- **Cyclicality in volumes** — commercial/industrial waste volumes soften in a downturn (muted by pricing power, but real).
- **Regulatory / environmental** — landfill permitting, emissions rules and environmental liability are ongoing exposures.
- **M&A integration** — a ~$1B/year acquisition program carries execution and overpayment risk.
- **Rate sensitivity** — as a low-volatility, dividend-growth, bond-proxy-adjacent name, the stock can de-rate when long-term yields rise.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Republic trades as a **low-beta, defensive industrial compounder** — valued on the durability and visibility of its free cash flow and adjusted EPS/EBITDA rather than on growth. Mechanically:

- **Quality / defensive premium** — the market pays up for recurring, non-discretionary, above-inflation-priced revenue; the multiple reflects earnings certainty, so the stock rarely looks "cheap."
- **Bond-proxy behaviour** — steady dividend growth and low volatility make it bond-proxy-adjacent; it tends to de-rate when long-term yields rise and outperform in risk-off/defensive rotations.
- **Low beta, low volatility** — a name traders use for defensive positioning; options are liquid but realised volatility is low.
- **Dividend-growth story** — a consistently rising dividend backed by robust free cash flow; total return is dividend + steady mid-single-digit EPS growth + buybacks/M&A accretion.
- **Sector-pair behaviour** — moves with [[waste-management]] and the waste-services complex ([[waste-connections]], [[gfl-environmental]]); the WM/RSG pair is the canonical relative-value trade in the sub-sector.

## Business Model & Segments

Resolute currently operates two producing gold mines in West Africa:

- Syama Gold Mine (80% owned) - located in southern Mali, Syama is a large-scale sulphide and oxide operation and has been Resolute's flagship asset for more than two decades.
- Mako Gold Mine (100% owned) - located in eastern Senegal, Mako is an open-pit operation that was acquired with the 2019 takeover of Toro Gold.

The company's principal growth asset is the Doropo Gold Project in Cote d'Ivoire, acquired from AngloGold Ashanti in May 2025 for approximately US$150 million together with the related ABC project tenements. Doropo has a definitive feasibility study (DFS) completed in June 2024 and has received approval of its Environmental and Social Impact Assessment, an environmental permit and a mining permit from the Council of Ministers in Cote d'Ivoire (valid for 14 years with potential extensions). Reserves at Doropo are approximately 2.5 million ounces at a US$2,000/oz gold price, with potential upside to 3.5-4 million ounces at US$3,000-4,000/oz price sensitivities.

The updated Mineral Resource estimate at 31 December 2025 was 347 million tonnes at 1.6 g/t gold for 17.63 million ounces of contained gold, reflecting the addition of Doropo and ABC.

## History

Resolute Mining Limited (formerly Resolute Limited, renamed in September 2001) traces its origins to gold mining activities in Australia, subsequently pivoting to become a leading African-focused gold producer. The company has operated ten gold mines across Africa and Australia over its history, producing more than 9 million ounces.

The Syama project in Mali has been a core asset since the 2000s, with Resolute expanding and upgrading processing facilities and transitioning the mine from oxide to sulphide ore. In 2019, Resolute acquired Toro Gold, adding the Mako Gold Mine in Senegal to the portfolio.

In November 2024, a major operating incident occurred in Mali: CEO Terence Holohan and two other employees were detained by Malian authorities in a tax dispute. Mali initially demanded approximately US$160 million (100 billion CFA francs), with the dispute largely centred on alleged unpaid taxes identified in a sector-wide audit. Resolute made an initial payment of US$80 million from existing cash, with further payments of approximately US$80 million to follow. The detained executives, including Holohan, were released on 21 November 2024. Syama operations continued throughout the dispute.

In May 2025, Resolute signed a binding agreement with AngloGold Ashanti to acquire the Doropo and ABC gold projects in Cote d'Ivoire for a total consideration of US$150 million. The transaction was positioned as the cornerstone of Resolute's transition into a multi-country West African producer.

## Recent Developments

For full year 2025, Resolute produced 277,000 ounces of gold with EBITDA of more than US$380 million and net operating cash flow of approximately US$140 million. Net cash at 31 December 2025 was approximately US$209 million.

For 2026, Resolute has guided group production of 250,000-275,000 ounces of gold at AISC of US$2,000-2,200/oz, with capital expenditure forecast at over US$300 million as construction of Doropo begins in the first half of the year. In the current gold price environment, the company anticipates more than US$500 million of EBITDA and free cash flow of more than US$200 million (excluding Doropo capital expenditure).

Management has indicated plans to expand to approximately 500,000 ounces per annum from 2028 once Doropo is in production, with a longer-term aspiration of approximately 750,000 ounces by the end of the decade.

CEO Chris Eger, who succeeded Terence Holohan following the 2024 Mali episode, has publicly stated his belief that Resolute has "all the makings" to extend the 2025 operational turnaround into 2026.

## Leadership

Resolute is led by Chief Executive Officer Chris Eger, with the Board and executive team comprising leaders with extensive African gold-sector experience across operations, project development, geology, community engagement and corporate finance.
