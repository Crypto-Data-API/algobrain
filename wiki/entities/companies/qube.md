**Qube Holdings Limited** (ASX: QUB) is an integrated freight & logistics company listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. Qube Holdings Limited (ASX: QUB) is Australia's largest provider of integrated import and export logistics services. The company is headquartered at 45 Clarence Street, Sydney, New South Wales, with operations across Australia, New Zealand and Southeast Asia.

## Business Model & Segments

Qube's business is organised into two core divisions: the Operating Division, and Qube's 50% interest in Patrick Terminals (Australia's leading container terminal operator).

The Operating Division comprises two business units:

- Qube Logistics and Infrastructure - road and rail transport of containers to and from ports, operation of container parks, customs and quarantine services, warehousing, international freight forwarding, bulk rail and containerised haulage storage and handling, operation of automotive, break-bulk, grain, intermodal and regional rail terminals, and the Moorebank Logistics Park (MLP) in Sydney.
- Qube Ports and Bulk - port logistics and stevedoring operations across more than 40 ports in Australia, New Zealand and Southeast Asia, including mine-to-ship bulk transport, stockpile management, storage, and specialised handling for energy, forestry, grain, automotive and project cargo customers.

Qube has freight handling and stevedoring facilities at 29 ports in Australia through subsidiaries and partnerships including P&O Automotive and General Stevedoring, Northern Stevedoring Services, Australian Amalgamated Terminals, PrixCar and the W Qube joint venture with Wilhelmsen Ships Service.

Patrick Terminals operates container terminals at the ports of Brisbane, Sydney, Melbourne and Fremantle, providing scale interconnection between Australia's international container shipping trade and the domestic supply chain.

Qube's strategy is to assemble an end-to-end, integrated logistics chain — from quay to warehouse to rail to regional terminal — so that throughput captured at one node feeds volume to the next. The Moorebank Logistics Park, Australia's largest intermodal precinct, is the physical embodiment of that integration thesis.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Qube's [[economic-moat|economic moat]] is **narrow but real**, rooted in hard-to-replicate physical assets and network position rather than brand or pricing power:

- **Infrastructure & land** — port leases, rail sidings, container parks and the Moorebank precinct are scarce, expensive and slow to permit. New entrants cannot easily duplicate the footprint (a barrier-to-entry / [[switching-costs|switching-cost]] effect for customers locked into integrated chains).
- **Patrick duopoly** — Australian container stevedoring is effectively a two-to-three player market (Patrick, [[dp-world]], Hutchison); the 50% Patrick stake gives Qube a share of that concentrated, capacity-constrained industry structure.
- **Integration / network effects** — the more nodes Qube controls (port → rail → warehouse), the stickier the end-to-end offer; Moorebank deepens this by co-locating warehousing with rail-linked container flow.
- **Scale in a fragmented market** — Qube is the largest pure-play listed integrated logistics operator in Australia, giving it bidding scale on national contracts that sub-scale truckers and 3PLs cannot match.

The moat's limits: stevedoring and haulage are **capital-intensive, low-margin** activities exposed to a powerful maritime-union workforce, regulated port access, and the commodity/trade cycle. The franchise compounds value through reinvestment and asset assembly, not through high returns on capital — reflected in single-digit ROE/ROA (see Fundamentals).

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Moorebank ramp** — the IMEX terminal and automated stacking cranes scaling toward design throughput is the single largest organic value driver; warehousing leasing on the precinct adds annuity-like income.
- **Bolt-on acquisitions** — Qube has a long record of acquiring sub-scale logistics businesses (e.g. Nexus Logistics, Dec 2025) and folding them into the network.
- **Trade & export volumes** — container imports/exports, agricultural (grain) and resource bulk exports drive Ports & Bulk; a proxy for Australian trade activity.
- **Patrick volumes & pricing** — container throughput growth and stevedoring rate increases flow through the 50% equity stake.
- **Infrastructure/decarbonisation freight** — project cargo for renewables, hydrogen and resources expansion.

## Bull vs Bear

**Bull case**
- Irreplaceable infrastructure footprint and Moorebank optionality not yet fully reflected in earnings.
- Duopoly-like Patrick position in container stevedoring with structural pricing support.
- Long runway of bolt-on consolidation in a fragmented Australian logistics market.
- The Feb 2026 Macquarie scheme validates strategic/infrastructure value at a ~28% premium.

**Bear case**
- Chronically low returns on capital (single-digit ROE/ROA); persistently negative free cash flow as capex funds the network.
- Earnings exposed to maritime-union industrial action, trade-cycle downturns, and large project impairments (e.g. the A$219m MITCo write-down).
- Heavy capital intensity means growth is bought, not earned.
- Absent the scheme, the trailing ~173x PE leaves little valuation cushion on depressed earnings.

## Key Risks

- **Deal risk (current)** — failure of the Macquarie scheme (FIRB/ACCC, shareholder vote) would re-expose the stock to fundamental valuation; a topping bid is the upside tail.
- **Industrial relations** — stevedoring/wharf labour disputes can halt operations and compress margins.
- **Cyclicality** — Ports & Bulk volumes track commodity exports and global trade; a downturn hits throughput.
- **Capital intensity / impairment** — large infrastructure projects (Moorebank, MITCo) carry execution and write-down risk.
- **Regulatory** — port access regimes, FIRB on foreign ownership, ACCC scrutiny of stevedoring concentration.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Since the February 2026 Macquarie scheme, QUB has traded primarily as a **merger-arbitrage / event-driven** name rather than on fundamentals: the share price is pinned near the A$5.20 cash terms, with residual spread reflecting deal-completion risk (FIRB/ACCC approval, the ~June 2026 shareholder vote) plus any permitted dividend. A failed or improved bid is the main two-sided catalyst. Mechanically:

- **Valuation lens** — the very high trailing PE (~173x) reflects the FY25 impairment-depressed earnings base, not normalised economics; on a normalised/EV-to-EBITDA basis the scheme price implies an infrastructure-style multiple. P/B ~2.9x.
- **Dividend** — modest yield (~1.5% historic) with partial franking; capital-light it is not, so the dividend is a secondary part of the story.
- **Beta / behaviour** — historically a moderate-beta industrial; under the scheme it now behaves like a low-volatility arbitrage instrument tethered to the cash terms.
- **Options/liquidity** — ASX single-name option liquidity is limited; positioning is almost entirely via the equity.
- **Catalysts** — scheme vote and regulatory approvals, half-year results (Feb/Aug), Patrick container-volume trends, Moorebank ramp.

## History

Qube's corporate lineage dates to July 2006, when former Patrick Corporation executives including Chris Corrigan departed following Toll Holdings' takeover of Patrick and teamed with Kaplan Funds Management to explore logistics opportunities. In January 2007, the KFM Diversified Infrastructure & Logistics Fund listed on the ASX.

In April 2007, the Fund acquired a 75% shareholding in P&O Automotive & General Stevedoring and a 50% shareholding in P&O Trans Australia from DP World. In June 2010, the Fund was renamed Qube Logistics. Qube Holdings Limited was listed on the ASX on 22 August 2011 following corporatisation of the logistics assets, and the name was refined to Qube Holdings Limited in November 2012 to distinguish the parent company from its containerised logistics division.

In August 2016, as part of the break-up of Asciano, Qube - together with consortium partners - purchased a 50% stake in Patrick Corporation, with the other 50% held by Brookfield. Qube has subsequently developed the Moorebank Logistics Park in Sydney, Australia's largest intermodal freight precinct, linking rail-based container movement with import-export supply chains and warehousing.

In December 2025, Qube completed a bolt-on acquisition of Nexus Logistics, further strengthening its Australian logistics footprint.

**Macquarie takeover (2026):** On 16 February 2026, a Macquarie Asset Management-led consortium (including UniSuper and Spain's Pontegadea) signed a binding Scheme Implementation Deed to acquire Qube at **A$5.20 cash per share** — an enterprise value of about **A$11.7 billion** and a ~27.8% premium. The Qube board unanimously recommended the scheme (absent a superior proposal and subject to an independent expert's report); UniSuper rolls its existing ~15.07% stake into the acquisition vehicle rather than taking cash. The deal is subject to FIRB and ACCC approval, with the shareholder scheme meeting expected around June 2026 and completion thereafter. (Source: Perplexity sonar / web search)

## Recent Developments

For the six months ended 31 December 2025, Qube reported revenue of A$2.36 billion (up 12.9%), underlying EBITA of A$196.3 million (up 9.8%) and underlying NPATA of A$157.5 million (up 10.1%). The Logistics & Infrastructure division delivered revenue of A$1.36 billion (up 24.4%) and EBITA of A$158.9 million (up 21.6%).

At Moorebank Logistics Park, the IMEX terminal is ramping up operations, with Qube testing four cantilever automated stacking cranes in the west yard; crane productivity and accuracy rates were reported largely in line with expectations. The MITCo joint venture (Moorebank interstate terminal asset) recorded a A$219 million impairment during the period.

For FY25 (year to 30 June 2025), Qube reported group underlying EBITDA growth of 18.5% and underlying NPAT of A$288 million (up 6.2% on FY24). FY26 guidance is for underlying NPATA and EPSA to be 6-10% above FY25.
