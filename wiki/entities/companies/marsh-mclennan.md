Marsh McLennan (NYSE: MRSH, formerly MMC) is the world's largest insurance broker and professional-services firm in risk, strategy and people, a [[s-and-p-500]] constituent in the [[financials|Financials]] sector. For traders it is a low-beta, fee-based compounder whose revenue follows commercial P&C insurance pricing cycles rather than underwriting risk — it carries no catastrophe exposure of its own. Founded 1905, headquartered in New York City, it is the bellwether of the insurance-broking cohort alongside [[aon]] and [[willis-towers-watson]].

## Business Overview & Segments

Marsh McLennan runs four operating companies organized under two reporting segments. The model is overwhelmingly fee- and commission-based — the firm places, advises on, and structures risk transfer for clients, but takes essentially no underwriting risk onto its own balance sheet. That makes it a capital-light, high-return-on-equity compounder rather than a balance-sheet insurer.

| Segment / business | What it is | Revenue mix (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **Risk & Insurance Services — Marsh** | World's largest commercial insurance broker; risk advisory and placement for corporates, plus Marsh McLennan Agency (US middle-market) | Largest single business; the franchise engine |
| **Risk & Insurance Services — Guy Carpenter** | Reinsurance broking and capital advisory for insurers and reinsurers | Smaller but high-value; geared to reinsurance pricing cycles |
| **Consulting — Mercer** | Health, wealth, retirement and career consulting; one of the largest institutional investment advisors/OCIO businesses | Large, recurring, advisory-fee heavy |
| **Consulting — Oliver Wyman** | High-end management/strategy consulting | Smallest; most cyclical (discretionary client spend) |

Risk & Insurance Services is roughly 60%+ of revenue, Consulting the remainder. Commission income is partly geared to the level of commercial insurance premium rates, so a softening commercial P&C rate environment is a structural top-line headwind even when client counts are stable. The November 2024 acquisition of **McGriff Insurance Services** (~US$7.75B) materially expanded the US middle-market agency business under Marsh McLennan Agency.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Marsh McLennan holds a wide [[economic-moat|economic moat]] built on scale, distribution, and entrenched client relationships rather than on any single product:

- **Scale and global distribution** — as the largest broker, Marsh has the deepest carrier relationships, the most placement leverage, and proprietary risk data that smaller brokers cannot replicate, creating an [[economic-moat|advantage]] that compounds with size.
- **High [[switching-costs]] and sticky relationships** — corporate risk programs, employee-benefits consulting (Mercer), and reinsurance structuring are advisory, multi-year, embedded relationships; retention rates are high and renewals are the default.
- **Capital-light, fee-based economics** — no underwriting/catastrophe risk on the balance sheet means stable margins and very high ROE; cash converts efficiently and funds both M&A and buybacks.
- **Roll-up flywheel** — Marsh McLennan Agency consolidates the fragmented US middle-market agency landscape (the McGriff deal is the latest leg), adding distribution at scale that independents cannot match.
- **Brand and talent** — Marsh, Mercer, Guy Carpenter and Oliver Wyman are category-leading brands that attract the senior advisory talent the business runs on.

The moat is wide but not frictionless: the broking oligopoly ([[aon]], [[willis-towers-watson]], [[arthur-j-gallagher]]) competes hard for the same large accounts, and a prolonged soft commercial-rate cycle compresses the commission tailwind for the whole group.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Organic (underlying) revenue growth** — the single most-watched print each quarter; mid-single-digit underlying growth is the base, with rate environment and new-business wins on top.
- **Commercial P&C and reinsurance pricing** — when rates harden, commission income rises mechanically; the current soft-rate phase is the key swing factor.
- **M&A roll-up** — continued tuck-ins under Marsh McLennan Agency (McGriff integration the near-term proof point); the consolidation runway in fragmented US agency markets is long.
- **Mercer health & wealth demand** — secular demand for benefits, retirement and OCIO/investment-advisory services as employers outsource complexity.
- **Margin expansion** — operating leverage and disciplined cost management have driven steady adjusted-margin gains.
- **Capital return** — consistent dividend growth plus buybacks; capital-light cash generation funds both.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Capital-light, fee-based compounder with no catastrophe/underwriting risk — one of the highest-quality business models in [[financials|Financials]].
- Long roll-up runway in fragmented US middle-market broking; McGriff and Marsh McLennan Agency extend it.
- Sticky, advisory, multi-year client relationships and high [[switching-costs]] underpin durable organic growth and pricing power.
- Steady margin expansion and reliable capital return (dividend growth + buybacks).
- Defensive, low-beta cash flows that hold up better than balance-sheet insurers in stress.

### Bear case
- Commission income is geared to commercial P&C rates; a sustained soft market caps organic growth for the whole broker cohort.
- Premium valuation leaves little room for an organic-growth disappointment.
- Integration and goodwill risk from a serial-acquisition strategy (McGriff and prior deals).
- Litigation/legacy tail risk — the US$425M Greensill charge is a reminder that advisory/consulting carries idiosyncratic legal exposure.
- Oliver Wyman (and to a degree Mercer) is exposed to discretionary corporate spend in a recession.

## Key Risks

- **Rate-cycle exposure** — softening commercial P&C and reinsurance pricing is the dominant top-line risk; revenue follows the pricing cycle even without losing clients.
- **Macro/discretionary-spend sensitivity** — consulting (Oliver Wyman especially) contracts when corporate budgets tighten.
- **Integration risk** — absorbing McGriff and ongoing tuck-ins; goodwill impairment if deals underperform.
- **Litigation/regulatory** — the Greensill charge and broker-compensation/contingent-commission scrutiny are recurring overhangs.
- **Valuation/positioning** — as a crowded quality-defensive name, any organic-growth miss can de-rate the multiple.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Marsh McLennan trades as a premium quality-compounder within [[financials]] — valued on the durability and growth of its fee stream rather than on book value or a credit cycle. Mechanically:

- **Insurance broking, not underwriting** — there is no NII or credit-cycle driver here as there is for a bank; the value driver is the *commission/fee tailwind from commercial insurance pricing* plus advisory fee growth. The stock re-rates on the organic-growth print and rate-cycle commentary, not on loss ratios.
- **Low beta, defensive** — capital-light cash flows and no catastrophe exposure make MRSH a lower-volatility alternative to balance-sheet insurers; it is often used as defensive [[financials]] exposure.
- **Dividend + buyback** — a steadily growing dividend and consistent repurchases; total return is fee-growth plus capital return, not a high starting yield.
- **Options** — liquidity is moderate and institutional rather than retail-driven; typical moves are earnings-centred around the quarterly organic-growth figure.
- **Trades as a cohort** — moves with [[aon]], [[willis-towers-watson]], [[arthur-j-gallagher]] and Brown & Brown on rate-cycle and reinsurance-pricing news.
