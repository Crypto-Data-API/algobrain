**Meridian Energy Limited** (ASX: MEZ) is a renewable utilities company listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. Meridian Energy Limited is New Zealand's largest electricity generator, producing approximately 30% of national electricity demand from a 100% renewable portfolio of hydro, wind, and emerging solar and battery storage assets. The company is majority-owned (51%) by the New Zealand Government, with minority interests held by public investors via listings on the NZX (MEL) and the ASX (MEZ).

## Business Model & Segments

Meridian operates across:

- **Generation:** Installed capacity of more than 2.9 GW across 7 hydro stations, 8 wind farms, a 100 MW battery energy storage system (BESS), and a grid-scale solar array. Hydro stations are located in the South Island - six on the Waitaki River and the Manapōuri power station (the largest hydro station in New Zealand by output).
- **Retail:** Electricity retailing to residential, business, and industrial customers in New Zealand under the Meridian and Powershop brands. Powershop additionally operates in certain overseas markets including Australia and the United Kingdom.
- **Services:** Solar installation, software development (including Powershop retail technology), professional and insurance-related services.

Meridian's largest single customer has historically been the New Zealand Aluminium Smelter (NZAS) at Tiwai Point (majority-owned by Rio Tinto), supplied from the Manapōuri scheme via a long-term contract.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Meridian is **not a commodity price-taker** in the miner sense — it is a regulated-market **gentailer** (generator + retailer) whose moat comes from irreplaceable physical assets and an integrated structure, not pricing power over a fungible commodity:

- **Irreplaceable low-cost hydro** — Meridian owns NZ's premier hydro schemes (Waitaki + Manapōuri), built decades ago, with very low marginal cost and effectively impossible to replicate (no new large hydro will be consented in NZ). This is a durable, asset-based [[economic-moat|moat]] — the lowest-cost slice of the NZ supply stack.
- **Gentailer integration** — owning both generation and a large retail book ([[utilities|utility]] retailing under Meridian/Powershop) is a structural hedge: retail margins cushion wholesale-price swings, smoothing earnings across the cycle. This vertical integration is the core defensive feature of the NZ gentailer model.
- **Scale & market position** — at ~30% of national generation, Meridian is NZ's largest generator; the NZ electricity market is a tight oligopoly of a handful of gentailers, a high-barrier structure.
- **100% renewable** — an ESG/cost advantage as carbon pricing rises and as data-centre/electrification demand seeks green power.
- **The honest caveat** — wholesale electricity *is* effectively a commodity, and Meridian's output is **hostage to hydrology**: a dry year (FY2025 drought) collapses cheap hydro volume and forces costly market purchases, while a wet/windy year (FY2026) drives a sharp rebound. So earnings, while moated on cost, carry large weather/volume beta.

Honest verdict: a genuine asset and integration moat (irreplaceable hydro, gentailer hedge, oligopoly), but with material hydrology-driven volume risk layered on top.

- **Renewable development pipeline** — FID decisions expected on Mt Munro (wind), Te Rere Hau (repowering) and Te Rahui Solar Stage Two; ~NZ$1.2bn of flagged investment.
- **Electrification & demand growth** — process-heat electrification, EV uptake and potential data-centre load lifting NZ electricity demand long-term.
- **Tiwai Point contract** — the multi-year smelter extension secured a large baseload buyer; further news is a recurring swing factor.
- **Hydrology normalisation** — recovery from the FY2025 drought (FY2026 saw second-best inflows on record) is the near-term earnings driver.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year/full-year results, monthly operating reports (lake storage, wind generation), wholesale-price volatility, and FIDs on new wind/solar.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Irreplaceable, low-cost, 100%-renewable hydro base in a high-barrier oligopoly.
- Gentailer integration smooths earnings; defensive [[utilities|utility]] cash flows with a solid dividend.
- Long runway of renewable development (wind/solar/BESS) into rising electrification demand.
- Strong FY2026 rebound shows the upside torque when hydrology cooperates.

### Bear case
- Earnings are hostage to South Island hydrology — a dry year (FY2025) can drive a statutory loss.
- Tiwai Point concentration: the smelter is the single largest customer; a future closure/renegotiation is a material demand risk.
- Capital-intensive development pipeline (~NZ$1.2bn) during a period of higher rates.
- 51% NZ Government ownership leaves a small free float and political/regulatory overhang on pricing.

## Key Risks

- **Hydrology / weather (volume)** — the dominant risk: drought cuts cheap hydro and forces costly purchases; wet/windy years swing the other way.
- **Wholesale price** — spot electricity volatility; while integration hedges it, extreme spikes during dry periods still hurt.
- **Customer concentration (demand)** — NZAS/Tiwai Point is the largest single load; closure or renegotiation would reshape the South Island market.
- **Capex / permitting** — consenting, grid connection and cost of new wind/solar/BESS; ~NZ$1.2bn pipeline execution.
- **Regulatory / political** — 51% government ownership; electricity-market reform and retail-pricing scrutiny.
- **Rates / FX** — utility valuations are rate-sensitive; AU/NZ listing and Powershop AU/UK add currency exposure.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Meridian's trailing P/E is **negative** in FY2025 owing to a drought-driven statutory loss, so it is not read on a single-year multiple; it trades as a **renewable-utility/gentailer yield-and-hydrology story**:

- **Lower beta, defensive** — as a [[utilities|utility]] with regulated-market cash flows, MEZ is less volatile than the commodity miners in this cluster; it screens as a defensive income holding.
- **Dividend-led total return** — a meaningful dividend yield (5y avg positive) is central to the thesis; capital return matters more than capital growth.
- **Hydrology is the swing factor** — the share price tracks lake-storage and wind-generation operating reports; the FY2025 drought loss and the FY2026 record-inflow rebound show how weather drives the earnings (and the stock).
- **Rate sensitivity** — like all utilities, valuation is sensitive to long-bond yields; the development pipeline adds capital intensity into that.
- **Liquidity quirk** — 51% NZ Government ownership means a relatively small free float, with liquidity split across the NZX (primary, MEL) and ASX (MEZ) lines.

## History

Meridian Energy was incorporated in 1998 as Hydro Energy Limited and rebranded to Meridian Energy Limited in March 1999. It was formed from the break-up of the Electricity Corporation of New Zealand (ECNZ) alongside Mighty River Power (now Mercury NZ) and Genesis Energy, following NZ electricity market reform.

Key milestones:

- **1999:** Formed from ECNZ split, taking over the Waitaki River and Manapōuri hydro schemes. The initial asset base had a book value of approximately NZ$2,073 million, with roughly three-quarters being generation plant.
- **2000s-2010s:** Expanded into wind generation with large wind farms such as West Wind, Te Uku, Te Apiti, and White Hill; expanded Powershop as a digital retail brand into Australia and the UK.
- **2013:** Partial privatisation via IPO and NZX/ASX dual listing; the NZ Government retained 51%.
- **2020-2023:** Prolonged negotiation with Rio Tinto over the Tiwai Point smelter, culminating in a multi-year extension of the smelter contract.
- **2020s:** Commissioned grid-scale solar, invested in battery energy storage (100 MW BESS), and expanded wind and solar development pipeline.

## Recent Developments

Underlying net profit fell from NZ$359 million to NZ$56 million, with a 23% decrease in energy margin (from NZ$1.276 billion to NZ$982 million). The reported net loss after tax was approximately NZ$452 million, reflecting drought-affected hydro inflows and a volatile wholesale market environment.

- EBITDAF of NZ$506 million.
- Operating cash flow of NZ$336 million.
- Net profit after tax of NZ$227 million (compared with a NZ$121 million loss in 1H FY25).
- Energy margin rose NZ$264 million (+59%) year-on-year, supported by record wind generation and the second-best lake inflow period on record.

Strategic priorities under new CEO Mike Roan (appointed July 2025) focus on sustained renewable development. Investment decisions over the next 12 months are expected for:

- **Mt Munro** (wind development).
- **Te Rere Hau** (repowering/ upgrade of existing wind assets).
- **Te Rahui Solar Farm Stage Two** (grid-scale solar expansion).

Meridian has flagged total upcoming investment in the order of NZ$1.2 billion.
