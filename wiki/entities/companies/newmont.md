Newmont (NYSE: NEM; ASX: NEM via CDIs) is the world's largest gold producer, headquartered in Denver, Colorado, in the [[materials|Materials]] sector. It is a constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]] index and, through its CHESS Depositary Interests, the [[asx-200]] — making it the highest-liquidity gold-miner exposure in both markets. For traders NEM is the default large-cap vehicle for the gold-miner trade: operationally leveraged to the gold price (roughly 2x spot beta historically), with record 2025–2026 margins as gold ran to all-time highs.

## Business Model & Segments

Newmont's primary product is gold, supplemented by material co-product and by-product copper, silver, lead, and zinc exposure. Following its recent portfolio optimisation, the group is focused on 11 managed Tier 1 assets - defined as mines with long lives, low operating costs, and favourable mining jurisdictions.

Key operations and joint ventures include:

- **Nevada Gold Mines (USA)** - joint venture operated by Barrick Gold with Newmont as 38.5% owner.
- **Boddington (Australia)** - large open-pit gold-copper operation in Western Australia.
- **Cadia (Australia)** and other ex-Newcrest Australian assets.
- **Lihir (Papua New Guinea)** - major gold operation acquired with Newcrest.
- **Ahafo (Ghana), Peñasquito (Mexico), Merian (Suriname), Yanacocha (Peru), Cerro Negro (Argentina), Éléonore and Red Chris (Canada),** and additional operations across the Americas.

Revenue is driven by gold ounces produced, average realised gold price, and by-product credits; earnings are sensitive to All-In Sustaining Costs (AISC) and gold price movements.

## History

The Newmont Company was founded in 1916 in New York by Colonel William Boyce Thompson as a holding company investing in worldwide mineral, oil, and related companies. Key milestones:

- **1917:** Made its first major gold investment, taking a founding 25% stake in Anglo American Corporation of South Africa.
- **1921:** Reincorporated as Newmont Corporation.
- **20th century:** Grew through exploration, discovery, and acquisition across Nevada, Peru, Ghana, and Australia, becoming a leading global gold miner.
- **2019:** Acquired Canadian miner Goldcorp for approximately US$10 billion; Newmont's ASX listing was subsequently established via CHESS Depositary Interest (CDI) structure linked to the Goldcorp combination.
- **May 2023:** Agreed to acquire Newcrest Mining Limited for approximately US$16.8 billion, adding Cadia, Lihir, Telfer, Red Chris, and other assets.
- **November 2023:** Newcrest acquisition completed, creating the world's largest gold producer and strengthening Newmont's ASX presence.
- **2024-2025:** Executed a major non-core divestiture program targeting approximately US$4.3 billion in gross proceeds, including sales of the Akyem mine in Ghana and the Telfer mine in Australia.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Newmont has **no [[economic-moat|moat]] over the price of its product**. Gold is a globally fungible commodity, and every miner — Newmont included — is a price-taker that sells into the same spot/futures market at the same realised price. There is no brand premium, no [[switching-costs|switching cost]], and no pricing power on the revenue line. This is the defining feature of the business and the reason Fred's framework classifies NEM as *cyclical / commodity* rather than a quality compounder: through-cycle [[economic-moat|moat]] in the conventional sense does not exist.

What Newmont *does* have is a **structural cost-and-asset advantage** — a durable edge on the *cost* side rather than the *price* side:

- **Tier-1 long-life asset portfolio.** Post-portfolio-optimisation, Newmont concentrates on ~11 managed Tier-1 assets — mines defined by long reserve lives, low operating costs, and favourable jurisdictions (e.g. Boddington, Cadia, Lihir, Tanami, Peñasquito, Ahafo, plus its 38.5% stake in Nevada Gold Mines). Long-life Tier-1 assets sit toward the **left of the industry AISC cost curve**, meaning Newmont stays cash-generative deep into a gold-price downturn that would push marginal producers into losses. That cost-curve position is the closest thing the company has to a moat.
- **Scale.** As the world's largest gold producer (~5–6 million oz/year), Newmont commands procurement leverage, technical depth, and access to capital that small-cap miners cannot match. Scale also lets it spread fixed exploration, technical-services, and ESG/permitting overhead across a far larger ounce base.
- **Jurisdiction diversification.** Operations span North America, South America, Australia, Africa, and PNG. No single mine or country dominates output, so an outage, strike, tax change, or expropriation at any one asset is survivable — a genuine advantage over single-asset or single-country juniors.
- **Reserve base.** Newmont holds one of the largest gold reserve and resource bases in the industry. Reserves are the raw material of future production; a deep, diversified reserve base reduces the urgency (and dilution risk) of acquiring growth at the top of the cycle.

The honest framing: Newmont's "moat" is **relative cost position and asset quality, not protection from the gold price**. When gold falls, Newmont's earnings fall with it — it simply falls later and less than higher-cost peers. See [[economic-moat]] for why commodity producers are structurally moat-light.

The Newcrest integration was, in part, an effort to lift portfolio quality toward that premium-peer standard.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

For a price-taker, "growth" is mostly **price × volume × cost discipline** rather than market-share gains:

- **Gold price** is the dominant driver. The spot price is itself a function of **real yields** (lower real rates raise gold's relative appeal), the **US dollar** (gold is inversely correlated to USD strength), and **central-bank buying**, which has been a structural demand pillar through 2024–2026. Because AISC is roughly fixed in the near term, every dollar of gold-price upside flows to margin with high operating leverage (~2x beta historically).
- **2026 production-trough → 2027 rebound narrative.** Management guides FY2026 to ~5.3Moz, down from ~5.9Moz in 2025, as it reinvests across key mines. 2026 is explicitly framed as a *trough year* — the catalyst is delivery of the **2027 production rebound**, which would lift volume just as (bulls hope) gold holds elevated.
- **Buyback execution.** Newmont funded a US$6B repurchase authorisation, with material buybacks already executed in 2026. Shrinking the share count amplifies per-share earnings and free cash flow; visible, consistent execution is a re-rating catalyst.
- **Divestiture proceeds.** The ~US$4.3B non-core divestiture program (e.g. Akyem, Telfer) deleveraged the balance sheet and funds capital return; remaining proceeds and further high-grading of the portfolio support the "quality up, debt down" story.
- **Reserve replacement / exploration.** Mines deplete; replacing produced ounces with new reserves (via brownfield exploration around Tier-1 assets and disciplined M&A) is what sustains the production base long-term. Reserve growth without dilution is a quiet but real driver of through-cycle value.
- **Recurring catalysts:** quarterly results and AISC trends, annual production/cost guidance, buyback pace, Fed/real-yield prints and USD moves, and central-bank gold-buying headlines.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- **Operational leverage to a strong gold tape.** With AISC near US$1,000–1,300/oz against realised prices that ran toward ~US$4,900/oz in early 2026, margin per ounce is historically extreme; if gold holds, free cash flow is enormous.
- **Self-help is working.** Record FY2025 free cash flow (~US$7.3B), debt paid down (~US$3.4B), a net-cash balance sheet, and a US$6B buyback — the divestiture-and-deleverage program delivered.
- **2027 production rebound** restores volume after the guided 2026 trough, layering volume growth on top of price.
- **Best-in-class scale and diversification** make NEM the most liquid, lowest-friction way to own the gold-miner trade, and the largest GDX holding benefits from passive miner-ETF inflows.
- **Central-bank buying + structurally lower real yields** provide a demand floor under gold that did not exist in prior cycles.

### Bear case
- **It is a leveraged bet on the gold price.** A reversal in gold — driven by rising real yields, a stronger USD, or central banks pausing purchases — would compress margins fast; ~2x downside beta cuts both ways.
- **AISC cost inflation.** Labour, energy, diesel, and reagent costs have structurally risen; if costs creep while gold softens, the wide margin narrows quickly.
- **Jurisdiction/sovereign risk** across PNG, Argentina, Ghana, Peru, Suriname, and Mexico (taxes, permitting, community disputes, currency, expropriation) can impair any given asset.
- **Integration and execution risk** on the Newcrest assets and the reinvestment program — capex overruns or production misses would dent the trough-to-rebound thesis.
- **No moat on price + reserve depletion** mean the business must continually spend to stand still; it is a cyclical, capital-intensive producer, not a compounder.

## Key Risks

- **Gold price reversal** — the single largest risk. Higher real yields, USD strength, or a pause in central-bank buying would pressure spot gold, and NEM amplifies the move (~2x beta) on the way down.
- **AISC cost inflation** — labour, energy, diesel, cyanide/reagents, and sustaining-capital creep erode margin; cost discipline at peak prices is the watch item flagged even in the Fred-framework view.
- **Jurisdiction / sovereign risk** — operations in **PNG (Lihir), Argentina (Cerro Negro), Ghana (Ahafo), Peru (Yanacocha), Suriname (Merian), and Mexico (Peñasquito)** carry exposure to royalty/tax changes, permitting delays, currency controls, community and labour disputes, and in the tail, expropriation.
- **Execution on Newcrest integration** — realising synergies and lifting ex-Newcrest assets (Cadia, Lihir, Red Chris) to Tier-1 standards; integration missteps or capex overruns would undercut the portfolio-quality thesis.
- **ESG / permitting / tailings** — tailings-dam stability, water use, and community/permitting risk are existential operational hazards in the sector; a tailings failure or lost social licence can halt a mine and inflict large remediation and reputational costs.
- **Reserve depletion** — mines deplete; failure to replace produced ounces forces growth-by-acquisition at cycle-top prices, risking dilution or overpayment.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Newmont trades fundamentally as a **leveraged proxy on spot gold**, not as a standalone earnings compounder. The mechanics that matter to traders:

- **~2x gold beta.** The stock has historically amplified gold's percentage moves in both directions, because AISC is near-fixed in the short run while the gold price (and thus margin per ounce) swings — operational leverage made visible. This makes NEM the high-octane way to express a gold view versus holding bullion or [[gold|gold]] ETFs directly.
- **GDX's largest holding.** As the biggest weight in the gold-miner ETF complex, NEM both moves with and partly drives gold-miner ETF flows; passive inflows/outflows into the gold-miner trade transmit to the stock.
- **Dual S&P 500 + ASX (CDI) listing.** NEM is the **largest gold weight in the [[s-and-p-500]]** and, via [[asx-200|CHESS Depositary Interests]], a constituent of the [[asx-200]] — giving it the highest-liquidity gold-miner exposure in both the US and Australian markets. (Note the AUD/USD reporting nuance flagged in the data block above: the CDI listing is not a separate AUD-reporting entity; Newmont reports in USD.)
- **Operational leverage to spot.** Because the cost base is comparatively sticky, the *change* in realised gold price drops almost entirely to gross margin — the source of the outsized earnings swings between the FY2023 loss-making period and the FY2025 record.
- **Modest dividend.** Newmont pays a dividend, but it is a secondary part of the return story; capital return now leans on buybacks (the US$6B authorisation) funded by record free cash flow rather than a high, fixed yield. Treat NEM as a price-leveraged total-return vehicle, not an income holding.
- **How it screens:** static valuation metrics (P/E, ROE) are flattered by peak-cycle margins and understate through-cycle volatility — see the *cyclical / commodity* framing in the Fundamentals block. Size the position around the gold cycle, not by static multiples.
