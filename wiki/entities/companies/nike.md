Nike, Inc. (NYSE: NKE) is the world's largest athletic footwear and apparel company, a constituent of both the [[s-and-p-500]] and the Dow Jones Industrial Average. For traders it is the bellwether for global sportswear demand, China consumer health, and tariff exposure — and since late 2024 a high-profile turnaround story under CEO Elliott Hill.

## Business Overview & Segments

Nike designs, markets, and sells footwear, apparel, and equipment under the Nike, Jordan, and Converse brands, sourcing nearly all production from contract factories in Vietnam, Indonesia, and China. Revenue splits across geographies (North America the largest, Greater China the swing factor) and channels — Nike Direct (DTC/digital) versus wholesale. The 2020-2023 DTC-first strategy under former CEO John Donahoe alienated wholesale partners and stalled product innovation; the company has been unwinding that since. As a [[consumer-discretionary|Consumer Discretionary]] bellwether, Nike's revenue is split by both brand/segment and the DTC-vs-wholesale channel mix that has been the central strategic debate.

| Segment / line | What it is | Revenue mix (qualitative) |

| **NIKE Brand — Equipment** | Bags, socks, accessories, balls | Small |

Geographically Nike reports North America (largest), EMEA, Greater China (the swing region for both growth and inventory risk), and Asia Pacific & Latin America.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Nike's [[economic-moat|economic moat]] is one of the strongest in [[consumer-discretionary|Consumer Discretionary]] and rests primarily on **intangible brand equity** rather than cost or network effects:

- **Brand & the Swoosh** — decades of marketing spend, athlete/team sponsorships (the Jordan and signature-athlete franchises), and cultural relevance create pricing power and demand that competitors cannot easily replicate.
- **Scale economics** — Nike's volume gives it leverage over contract manufacturers, logistics, and media buying that smaller brands lack.
- **Innovation pipeline & IP** — Air, React, Flyknit, ZoomX and the Jordan platform sustain a performance-credibility flywheel that justifies premium prices.
- **Distribution breadth** — presence across owned retail, digital, and wholesale gives reach few peers match.

The moat is **wide but cyclically tested**: the DTC overreach showed brand strength does not immunize the company from self-inflicted execution errors, and challengers like [[deckers-brands]] (HOKA), On Holding, and [[lululemon]] have taken share in performance running and premium athleisure. The turnaround thesis is fundamentally about re-widening a moat that narrowed through neglect of innovation and wholesale.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **"Win Now" turnaround execution** — rebuilding wholesale, reigniting product innovation, and clearing aged inventory under CEO Elliott Hill.
- **Performance-running comeback** — Pegasus/Vomero refresh and signature franchises to reclaim share lost to HOKA and On.
- **Wholesale re-acceleration** — restored relationships with [[foot-locker]], Dick's, and a return to [[amazon]] after a six-year absence.
- **Greater China recovery** — stabilization of the swing region after inventory and demand pressure.
- **Margin recovery** — supply-chain normalization and pricing offsetting tariff drag over time.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly earnings and futures-orders commentary, China retail-sales data, US tariff headlines, and major sponsorship/product launches.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Strongest brand in the category; turnaround momentum visible in North America wholesale-led growth.
- Innovation pipeline reviving; clearing inventory sets up cleaner future comparisons.
- Margin recovery as supply chain normalizes and pricing offsets tariffs over time.
- Iconic franchises (Jordan, Air) and a global distribution footprint no challenger fully matches.

### Bear case
- Tariff drag (guided ~$1.5B FY2026 cost) and Greater China weakness compress margins near-term.
- Credible challengers ([[deckers-brands]]/HOKA, On, [[lululemon]]) keep taking share in the highest-growth pockets.
- Turnaround is multi-year and non-linear — revenue inflection and margin recovery may not arrive together.
- Discretionary exposure: a weaker consumer hits footwear/apparel demand directly.

## Key Risks

- **Tariffs & sourcing** — concentrated production in Vietnam/Indonesia/China leaves Nike exposed to US tariff policy and freight shocks.
- **China demand** — Greater China is the most volatile region for both sell-through and inventory.
- **Competitive share loss** — HOKA, On, and others in performance running; [[lululemon]] in apparel.
- **Execution risk** — the turnaround depends on re-energizing innovation and wholesale without re-bloating inventory.
- **Cyclicality** — discretionary spending sensitivity means a consumer slowdown hits demand directly.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Nike trades as a brand-quality compounder whose multiple has compressed into a turnaround discount; the debate is whether revenue inflection and margin recovery can arrive together (see the Fundamentals block below for the figures as pulled). Mechanically:

- **Turnaround optionality** — the stock trades on evidence the "Win Now" plan is working; positive North America wholesale prints and futures-orders commentary move it more than the headline EPS.
- **Index/flow** — [[s-and-p-500]] and Dow component; among the most liquid single-name options in [[consumer-discretionary|Consumer Discretionary]], with elevated IV into quarterly earnings (off-calendar fiscal year ending May 31; reports typically late Sep / mid-Dec / late Mar / late Jun).
- **Margin sensitivity** — gross margin is the key line; tariffs and China inventory are the swing factors traders watch.
- **Capital return** — a dividend aristocrat-style track record of raises plus buybacks; the yield is moderate, not the primary draw — the equity story is the turnaround.
- **Beta** — moderate; less volatile than high-growth challengers but more cyclical than staples.

## 2025-2026 Developments

- **Turnaround under Elliott Hill** (CEO since October 2024): "Win Now" plan refocusing on sport-led product innovation, rebuilding wholesale relationships ([[foot-locker|Foot Locker]], Dick's Sporting Goods), and returning to Amazon as a sales channel after a six-year absence.
- **Tariff headwind:** Nike guided that US tariffs would cost approximately **$1.5 billion in fiscal 2026** and compress gross margin by ~1.2 percentage points; mitigations include shifting sourcing away from China toward Vietnam and selective price increases.
- **Q2 FY2026** (reported December 2025): revenue +1% y/y to **$12.4 billion**, with North America up 9% — the first meaningful wholesale-led growth after years of declines — but gross margin contracted ~300bp to 40.6% on tariffs and Greater China inventory pressure.
- **Product cycle:** renewed performance-running push (Pegasus/Vomero refresh), Nike Mind series, and Project Amplify announced in 2025 as innovation proof points.
