Duke Realty Corporation (former NYSE: DRE) was a US industrial REIT focused on logistics and warehouse properties. It ceased to exist as an independent company on **2022-10-03**, when [[prologis|Prologis]] (NYSE: PLD) completed an all-stock acquisition valued at roughly **$23 billion including assumed debt**. The name survives only as a case study in REIT consolidation and [[merger-arbitrage|merger arbitrage]] — no DRE security trades today. This page is a **completed-acquisition case study**, written in the past tense; DRE is delisted.

## What the Business Was

Duke Realty was a pure-play domestic industrial/logistics [[reit|REIT]] — it owned, developed, and leased warehouse and distribution real estate, having shed its legacy office and suburban portfolio to bet entirely on the e-commerce-driven logistics boom.

| Business segment / asset | What it was |
|---|---|
| Industrial/logistics property portfolio | ~548 logistics properties, ~167M sq ft, concentrated in Southern California, New Jersey, South Florida, Chicago, Dallas, Atlanta |
| Development platform | In-house land bank and build-to-suit/speculative development engine — a key reason Prologis valued the franchise |
| Tenant base | Bulk distribution and e-commerce fulfillment tenants riding the warehouse-scarcity cycle |
| REIT structure | Pass-through, dividend-paying [[reit]]; [[s-and-p-500]] constituent until delisting |

## Company History

- Founded in 1972 in Indianapolis, Indiana; IPO'd in 1986 and grew into one of the largest pure-play domestic industrial/logistics REITs.
- Over the 2010s Duke Realty divested office and suburban assets to become a 100% industrial REIT, riding the e-commerce warehouse boom.
- At acquisition it owned ~548 logistics properties totaling ~167 million sq ft, concentrated in Southern California, New Jersey, South Florida, Chicago, Dallas and Atlanta.
- It was an [[s-and-p-500]] constituent until its delisting.

## The Prologis Acquisition (2022) — The Deal

- **2022-05**: Prologis made public an unsolicited all-stock proposal after private overtures were rebuffed; Duke initially rejected it as insufficient.
- **2022-06-13**: boards unanimously agreed an all-stock deal valued at ~**$26 billion** including debt at announcement.
- **Terms**: 0.475 Prologis shares per Duke Realty share — a fixed exchange ratio, so the dollar value drifted with PLD's price; by closing the deal was worth ~$23 billion as REIT shares de-rated in the 2022 rate shock.
- **2022-09-30 / 2022-10-03**: shareholder approvals, then completion. DRE was delisted; holders became PLD shareholders.

**Strategic rationale:** Prologis acquired overlapping, high-quality logistics footprint in supply-constrained coastal and gateway markets, a development pipeline, and rent mark-to-market upside — consolidating its lead in global industrial real estate and capturing operating/leasing synergies.

## Merger-Arb / Trading Postmortem

DRE/PLD was a benchmark 2022 **stock-for-stock arb** (see [[merger-arbitrage]]):

- The classic position was **long DRE / short 0.475 PLD**, locking the ratio and hedging out PLD's price move.
- Because consideration was PLD stock with a **fixed exchange ratio**, an unhedged DRE holder was effectively long PLD beta — the headline value shrank ~12% into close as REIT shares sold off in the 2022 rate shock, even though the ratio never changed.
- The deal carried low antitrust/financing risk (no cash leg, friendly board), so the residual spread was tight; the dominant risk leg was the **acquirer's share price**, not deal break.
- Close mechanics were dateable: shareholder votes ~2022-09-30, completion 2022-10-03, DRE delisted at close with holders flipping to PLD.

## Trading Lessons

- **Fixed-ratio stock deals carry market risk**: the headline "$26B" shrank ~12% by close because consideration was PLD stock during a rising-rate REIT selloff. Arbs in stock-for-stock deals are short the acquirer's beta unless hedged via the ratio (long DRE / short 0.475 PLD).
- **Sector-consolidation signal**: the deal marked the top-tick era of industrial-logistics enthusiasm (2021–22 warehouse scarcity). Cap-rate compression peaked around the announcement; industrial REIT multiples compressed sharply within months — M&A at cycle peaks is a useful contrarian tell.
- **Bump pattern**: Prologis's path (rejected private bid, public bear-hug, ~10% sweetened final ratio) is a textbook example of how hostile-to-friendly REIT deals get done, relevant when handicapping current REIT M&A.

## Lessons / Why It Was Acquired

- **Scale and overlap economics**: Prologis could extract leasing, G&A, and capital-cost synergies by folding a high-quality national portfolio into its global platform — a strategic buyer paying for scale, not a financial buyer.
- **Cycle-peak consolidation**: the deal landed at the apex of warehouse-scarcity enthusiasm; large all-stock REIT mergers near cycle peaks are a recurring contrarian tell.
- **Pure-play attractiveness**: by exiting office/suburban and going 100% industrial, Duke made itself a clean, easily-integrated target precisely when industrial was the most-wanted property type.

## Risks That Applied (historical)

- **Rate sensitivity**: as a [[reit]], the equity (and the all-stock deal value) was exposed to the 2022 rate shock that de-rated the whole sector.
- **Cap-rate compression reversal**: industrial cap rates had compressed to cycle lows; a reversal hit NAVs and multiples shortly after the deal.
- **Acquirer-beta risk (during pendency)**: the fixed-ratio structure meant DRE holders bore PLD's price risk into close.
- **Geographic concentration**: heavy weighting to a handful of gateway markets concentrated demand/supply risk.
