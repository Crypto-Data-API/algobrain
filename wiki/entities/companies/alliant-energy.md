Alliant Energy (NASDAQ: LNT) is a regulated Midwest electric and gas utility holding company serving roughly 1 million electric and 425,000 natural gas customers in Wisconsin (Wisconsin Power & Light) and Iowa (Interstate Power & Light). It is an [[s-and-p-500]] constituent and, since 2025, one of the clearest "data-center load growth" plays among mid-cap regulated utilities.

## Business Overview & Segments

Alliant operates two regulated utility subsidiaries — Wisconsin Power & Light and Interstate Power & Light (Iowa) — plus a stake in American Transmission Co. Earnings come almost entirely from regulated rate-base growth: generation (rapidly shifting to solar, wind, gas peaking and battery storage), distribution, and transmission. Rate proceedings before the Public Service Commission of Wisconsin and the Iowa Utilities Commission set the recovery pace for its capital program. As a pure [[utilities|regulated utility]] holding company, Alliant has essentially no merchant/[[independent-power-producer|IPP]] exposure — its earnings are a function of allowed return on a growing rate base rather than wholesale power prices, which differentiates it sharply from [[vistra]] or [[constellation-energy]].

| Segment / subsidiary | What it is | Rate-base mix (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **Interstate Power & Light (IPL)** | Regulated electric + gas utility in Iowa; the larger of the two operating companies and the focal point of the data-center load surge (Cedar Rapids campuses) | Largest share of consolidated rate base; the growth engine for the 2026-2031 capex ramp |
| **Wisconsin Power & Light (WPL)** | Regulated electric + gas utility in Wisconsin; constructive PSC of Wisconsin jurisdiction | Substantial; steady distribution/transmission and renewables recovery |
| **American Transmission Co. (ATC) stake** | Equity interest in a multi-state FERC-regulated transmission company | Small but high-quality, FERC-regulated transmission earnings outside the retail rate base |
| **Renewables & generation transition** | Solar, wind, gas peaking and battery storage replacing legacy coal, recovered through rate base | Rising share as the coal-to-clean transition and data-center reliability needs drive new build |

Because nearly 100% of earnings are regulated, the investment case rests on capex execution, constructive rate-case outcomes (allowed ROE and equity-layer), and the durability of the data-center load pipeline — not on commodity price swings.

## 2025–2026 Developments

- **Data-center load boom:** Alliant has signed four major data-center customers, including a QTS campus in Cedar Rapids, Iowa (minimum $750M investment, announced Feb 2025) and a Google data center in Cedar Rapids. Q1 2026 materials cite ~60% demand growth in the pipeline, and management disclosed active negotiations for an additional 2–4 GW of load.
- **Capex super-cycle:** capital plan of ~$13.4B for 2026–2029, materially raised versus prior plans, driven by data-center load, renewables and grid investment.
- **Growth outlook:** retail electric sales expected to grow at ~11% CAGR from 2025 through 2031 — exceptional for a regulated utility — with the ramp concentrated 2027-2031.
- **2026 guidance:** EPS of $3.36–$3.46; dividend target $2.14/share (+5.4%), extending a 20+ year dividend-growth streak.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Alliant's [[economic-moat]] is the classic regulated-utility franchise: a legally protected service-territory monopoly over electric and gas distribution in defined parts of Wisconsin and Iowa, with returns set by regulators rather than competed away. The reinforcing layers:

- **Regulated monopoly franchise** — within IPL and WPL territories there is no competing distribution provider. Customers cannot switch wires, so revenue is a function of rate base × allowed return, not market share battles. This is a structural, regulation-granted moat rather than a brand or cost advantage.
- **Rate-base / allowed-ROE mechanics** — every dollar of prudently incurred capex (generation, grid, transmission) is added to rate base and earns an authorized return. Growth therefore comes from the capital program plus constructive rate treatment, giving unusually visible, low-variance earnings growth.
- **Constructive jurisdictions** — Wisconsin (PSC of Wisconsin) and Iowa (Iowa Utilities Commission) are generally viewed by investors as constructive regulatory environments, supporting forward-looking test years, rider recovery and reasonable allowed equity layers — a competitive advantage over utilities in adversarial states.
- **Transmission ownership** — the ATC equity stake adds FERC-regulated transmission earnings, a high-quality, formula-rate cash-flow stream that diversifies the regulatory mix.
- **Incumbency for data-center load** — large hyperscale loads must interconnect through the incumbent utility, so the data-center demand wave flows directly into Alliant's regulated rate base rather than to a third party.

The flip side is that a regulated moat caps upside as much as it protects downside: allowed ROEs are administratively set (high-single-digits to ~10%), so Alliant compounds steadily but cannot earn outsized merchant margins the way an [[independent-power-producer|IPP]] can in a tight power market.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Among the clearest data-center load-growth stories in regulated-utility form: ~11% sales CAGR and a 2-4 GW negotiation pipeline that converts AI demand directly into rate base.
- The ~$13.4B capex plan drives visible, low-variance rate-base and EPS growth with regulated returns — quality compounding without merchant risk.
- Constructive Wisconsin and Iowa regulation plus FERC transmission (ATC) support reliable recovery of the capital program.
- 20+ year dividend-growth streak and bond-proxy stability appeal to income and lower-volatility mandates.
- Less volatile than IPPs ([[vistra]], [[constellation-energy]]) while levered to the same secular electricity-demand wave.

### Bear case
- Classic bond-proxy: the multiple compresses when 10-year Treasury yields rise, independent of fundamentals.
- A large capex plan implies recurring equity issuance — dilution risk that can cap per-share growth.
- Data-center demand projections must convert into signed, energized load; delays, cancellations or cost-allocation fights between hyperscalers and ratepayers would deflate the growth premium.
- Regulated returns cap upside — no merchant torque if power prices spike.
- Renewables-transition execution and policy (tax-credit, interconnection, supply-chain) risk on a fast-growing build program.

## Key Risks

- **Interest-rate sensitivity** — as a bond-proxy, LNT de-rates when long rates rise; the regulated multiple is among the most rate-sensitive in the market.
- **Equity-issuance / dilution** — funding a materially larger capex plan typically requires new equity, diluting EPS growth if issued at weak prices.
- **Data-center demand conversion** — the growth premium assumes the 2-4 GW pipeline and ~11% sales CAGR materialize; non-conversion is the key idiosyncratic risk.
- **Regulatory outcomes** — adverse rate-case rulings (lower allowed ROE, thinner equity layer, disallowed costs) in Wisconsin or Iowa would slow earnings growth.
- **Renewables & policy** — execution risk on the coal-to-clean transition, plus exposure to federal clean-energy tax-credit and interconnection policy shifts.
- **Capital-market access** — a regulated utility's debt-heavy model depends on continuous low-cost access to debt and equity markets.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Alliant trades as a classic low-beta regulated-utility bond-proxy: the valuation is anchored to rate-base growth, allowed ROE and the spread between its dividend yield and Treasury yields rather than to commodity prices. Mechanically:

- **Bond-proxy behavior** — the stock tends to underperform when 10-year Treasury yields rise and outperform when they fall; the dividend yield is the primary valuation anchor.
- **Dividend & growth streak** — income investors buy LNT for a 20+ year dividend-growth streak and a mid-single-digit dividend-growth target, making total return (yield + steady EPS growth) the core thesis.
- **Premium for data-center growth** — since 2025 the market has awarded a premium multiple versus slower-growing utility peers, reflecting the ~11% sales-CAGR pipeline; that premium is the part most at risk if demand disappoints.
- **Thin options / low beta** — single-name options are thin; most exposure is taken via shares or utility ETFs (XLU/VPU). It is a lower-volatility name than the IPPs.
- **Pairs / relative value** — frequently set against [[wec-energy]], [[ameren]] and [[xcel-energy]] in regulated-utility relative-value trades.
