ACM Research Inc. (NASDAQ: ACMR) is a US-listed semiconductor equipment maker specialising in single-wafer wet cleaning, electroplating, furnace and advanced packaging tools, with the overwhelming majority of its operations, revenue and customers in mainland China through its majority-owned, STAR Market-listed subsidiary **ACM Shanghai**. For traders it is one of the purest listed plays on China's semiconductor self-sufficiency drive — and one of the most geopolitically exposed.

## Business Overview

ACM's flagship SAPS/TEBO megasonic cleaning technology competes against Lam Research, Tokyo Electron and Screen in wafer cleaning, and the company has expanded into ECD plating, vertical furnaces, track/coater-developer and panel-level packaging tools. Customer concentration is high: in 2024 the top four customers were 52.2% of revenue, with [[smic|SMIC]] around 14% (16.7% in 2023); other major customers include Hua Hong and YMTC. The US-listed parent holds ~82% of ACM Shanghai, creating a chronic holding-company discount debate (the Shanghai listing has at times traded at a multiple of the entire ACMR market cap). It is expanding US operations with a new Oregon facility slated for 2026.

## Business Segments / Products

ACM is a **broad-line semiconductor process-equipment** vendor that started in single-wafer wet cleaning and has fanned out into adjacent front-end and packaging steps — the deliberate playbook of a domestic Chinese champion building a full local toolset to reduce reliance on Western and Japanese suppliers.

| Product line | What it does | Competes with |
|---|---|---|
| **SAPS / TEBO wet cleaning** | Single-wafer megasonic cleaning (flagship) | [[lam-research]], Tokyo Electron, Screen |
| **ECP / ECD plating** | Electro-copper/electroplating for interconnect and packaging | [[applied-materials]], Lam |
| **Vertical / horizontal furnaces** | Thermal processing (oxidation, diffusion, anneal) | Tokyo Electron, Kokusai |
| **Track (coater/developer)** | Photoresist coat/develop, lithography-adjacent | Tokyo Electron, Screen |
| **Advanced / panel-level packaging** | Plating, cleaning and tools for advanced packaging | Lam, AMAT, ASMPT |

The strategic logic is **localization breadth**: by covering cleaning, plating, furnace, track and packaging, ACM can sell a wider basket into Chinese fabs that are explicitly trying to qualify domestic alternatives to imported tools. The risk mirror image is that this breadth concentrates the business in a single demand geography under active export-control scrutiny.

## Competitive Positioning / Peers — the China-localization angle

ACM's competitive story cannot be separated from geopolitics. Against the Western/Japanese semicap giants it is small; *within China* it is a leading domestic alternative, benefiting structurally as Chinese fabs are pushed (by policy and by export controls) to localize.

| Company | Overlap with ACMR | Scale vs ACMR | Strategic frame |
|---|---|---|---|
| [[applied-materials]] | Plating, packaging, broad front-end | Vastly larger | Global incumbent; China-restricted on leading-edge — the gap ACM fills domestically |
| [[lam-research]] | Wet clean (direct), plating, etch | Vastly larger | ACM's most direct cleaning competitor; export controls limit Lam's China leading-edge sales |
| Tokyo Electron | Cleaning, track, furnace | Vastly larger | Japanese incumbent; subject to allied export coordination |
| NAURA, AMEC (A-share) | Domestic Chinese semicap | Comparable / larger domestically | ACM's true peer set for "China self-sufficiency" exposure |
| [[smic]] | Customer, not competitor | — | Largest Chinese foundry; ACM's demand anchor (~14% of revenue) |

**Trading frame:** ACMR is the US-listed way to express *China semiconductor self-sufficiency* with a Western governance/disclosure wrapper. Easing of US-China chip rules and rising Chinese fab capex are tailwinds; tightening controls, an Entity-List action, or a domestic-peer share grab (NAURA/AMEC) are the headwinds.

## Bull vs Bear Case

**Bull case**
- Pure, liquid US-listed proxy for China semiconductor localization — a multi-year national-priority capex theme.
- Broadening product line (cleaning -> plating -> furnace -> track -> packaging) expands serviceable market per fab and deepens customer lock-in.
- ACM Shanghai (STAR Market) listing surfaces value; the parent's ~82% stake is a sum-of-the-parts argument when the Shanghai entity trades richly.
- Oregon facility diversifies operations and offers a partial hedge against the "all-China" perception.
- Growing into a ~$1B+ revenue scale with healthy gross margins for the equipment space.

**Bear case**
- **Existential export-control risk:** US rules already restrict supplying controlled hardware/software/technology to ACM Shanghai/ACM Korea; an Entity-List addition or tighter licensing could impair the supply chain.
- Extreme geographic concentration — essentially one demand geography, one policy regime.
- High customer concentration (top four ~52% of revenue; SMIC ~14%).
- VIE-style / parent-subsidiary structural discount and governance questions (the focus of the Kerrisdale short thesis).
- Domestic Chinese competitors (NAURA, AMEC) can take share with stronger local-champion positioning.
- China fab-capex cyclicality and potential overbuild.

## 2025-2026 Developments

- **FY2025**: revenue grew ~25%+ to roughly $1.0B despite a Q4 earnings miss reported in February 2026; shares remained volatile around the print.
- **2026 guidance**: ACMR guides revenue of **$1.08-1.175B** (midpoint ~25% growth); ACM Shanghai guides **RMB 8.2-8.8B** with 42-48% gross margin and R&D at 14-19% of revenue.
- **Export controls**: US rules prohibit supplying controlled hardware/software/technology to ACM Shanghai or ACM Korea without authorisation — a structural constraint on its supply chain, and the key headline risk. Tightening (or easing) of US-China chip rules moves the stock sharply in both directions.
- **Short-seller attention**: Kerrisdale Capital published an updated short thesis (November 2025) centred on governance and the parent/subsidiary structure — a known source of headline volatility.

## Valuation Framework (qualitative)

ACMR is unusual: a profitable, fast-growing equipment maker that trades at a persistent discount to its growth because of the **geopolitical and structural overhang**. Frame valuation around the specific risk wedges, not just growth:

- **China-export-control exposure** — the single biggest discount factor. The market caps the multiple because a single regulatory action (Entity List, licensing) could impair the supply chain. Easing of rules compresses this discount; tightening widens it.
- **Holding-company / sum-of-the-parts discount** — the parent owns ~82% of STAR Market-listed ACM Shanghai. When the Shanghai entity trades richly, the US-listed parent can imply a large conglomerate/structural discount; bulls treat this as latent value, bears as a governance trap.
- **Semi-cycle / China-capex beta** — revenue tracks Chinese fab capex (SMIC, Hua Hong), which has its own policy-driven cycle layered on top of the global [[semiconductors|semi]] cycle.
- **Customer concentration** — top-four ~52% of revenue means valuation must discount the risk of a single large customer's capex pause.

Net: ACMR's multiple is a *policy-risk-adjusted* growth multiple. The bull thesis is largely "the discount is too wide for the growth"; the bear thesis is "the discount exists for a reason and could become a permanent impairment."

## Capital Structure & Dilution

- **Dual-entity structure** is the defining capital-structure feature: US-listed ACMR (Fremont) is the ~82% parent of **STAR Market-listed ACM Shanghai**, with operations, revenue and customers overwhelmingly in mainland China. This creates the holding-company discount/SOTP debate and the governance scrutiny at the heart of the Kerrisdale short thesis.
- The Shanghai listing is itself a capital-raising vehicle (it has at times traded at a multiple of the entire ACMR market cap), so dilution/value can occur at the *subsidiary* level — a wrinkle most single-entity peers do not have.
- The business is **profitable and self-funding** at the operating level, so dilution risk is structural (parent/sub minority interest, SOTP optics) rather than a cash-runway concern.

## Risks

- **Export controls (primary):** US rules already restrict supplying controlled hardware/software/technology to ACM Shanghai / ACM Korea; further tightening or an Entity-List action is the key tail risk.
- **Geographic concentration:** essentially one demand geography and one policy regime.
- **Customer concentration:** top four ~52% of revenue; SMIC ~14%.
- **Structural / governance:** parent-subsidiary VIE-style discount and the questions raised in short reports.
- **Competitive:** domestic Chinese peers (NAURA, AMEC) competing for the same localization budget.
- **Cyclicality:** China fab-capex cycle and potential overbuild.
