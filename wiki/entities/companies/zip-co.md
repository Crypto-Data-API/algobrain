**Zip Co Limited** (ASX: ZIP) is an credit services company listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. Zip Co Limited (ASX: ZIP) is one of Australia's leading "buy-now-pay-later" (BNPL) and digital consumer finance platforms, with significant operations in the United States. Headquartered at 180 George Street in Sydney, Zip provides consumers with installment payment solutions and retail lines of credit, offered at the point of sale (online and in-store) through a large network of merchant partners.

## Business Overview & Segments

Zip is a balance-sheet [[buy-now-pay-later|BNPL]] / digital consumer-finance lender: it advances funds to consumers at the point of sale, pays merchants up-front (net of a merchant fee), and collects from the consumer over an installment schedule. It earns from three pools — merchant fees, consumer fees/interest, and (in Australia) account/late fees — while carrying the receivable on its own warehouse- and ABS-funded book. The business reports along geographic lines, with the US now the dominant growth engine and Australia/NZ the steady, cash-generating core.

| Segment / product | What it is | Role in the mix (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **US BNPL** (formerly Quadpay) | Pay-in-4 installments to US consumers, embedded via partners (Stripe, Google Chrome) and enterprise merchants | The growth engine — fastest-growing revenue and TTV; CEO relocating to the US to drive it |
| **Australia/NZ BNPL** (Zip Pay) | Interest-free everyday-spend wallet for smaller tickets | Mature, profitable, steady cash generation |
| **Zip Money / Zip Plus** | Reusable retail line of credit (up to ~6 months interest-free, larger limits) | Higher-yield AU consumer-credit product |
| **Personal loans** | Unsecured consumer lending in Australia | Smaller adjacency; higher-risk yield |
| **Merchant & payments services** | POS credit integrations, loyalty/rewards, in-app engagement | Distribution and stickiness rather than a standalone profit pool |

Zip is the highest-profile surviving pure-play [[buy-now-pay-later|BNPL]] on the [[asx-200]] after the sector's 2022-2023 shakeout (Afterpay was absorbed by Block). It sits in the [[financials]] sector under [[credit-services]] and is best understood as a leveraged consumer-credit [[financials|financial]] rather than a software company — see [[unit-economics]] below.

## History

- **2013**: Founded by Larry Diamond and Peter Gray as ZipMoney, offering digital consumer credit to Australian customers.
- **2015**: Listed on the ASX as ZipMoney Limited (ticker ZML).
- **2017**: Launched the Zip Pay product for everyday purchases.
- **December 2017**: Renamed from ZipMoney Limited to Zip Co Limited.
- **January 2018**: ASX ticker changed from ZML to ZIP.
- **August 2019**: Acquired PartPay Limited (New Zealand / UK BNPL) for international expansion.
- **June 2020**: Completed acquisition of US-based Quadpay Inc. for approximately US$296 million (with consideration around A$430 million at the time), rebranding to Zip in the US.
- **2020–2021**: Expanded into markets including the UK, Europe, South Africa, Mexico and the Middle East.
- **2022–2023**: Amid rising funding costs and a deteriorating BNPL sector, Zip undertook major restructuring, exited non-core international markets, cut costs and refocused on Australia/New Zealand and the US.
- **August 2023**: Cynthia Scott appointed Group CEO and Managing Director.
- **2024**: Returned to group cash profitability.
- **2026**: CEO Cynthia Scott announced plan to relocate to the US in H2 2026 to drive the now-dominant US growth engine.

## Recent Developments

Under Cynthia Scott, Zip has delivered strong earnings growth and US-led volume expansion:

- **FY25 (year to 30 June 2025)**: Group cash earnings grew 147% to A$170.3 million. Group operating margin approximately doubled to 15.8%.
- **US segment**: US transaction volume and revenue both grew more than 40% in FY25, with continued strong pipeline driven by partnerships including Stripe and Google Chrome, and new enterprise merchant signings.
- **FY25 detail**: Total income exceeded **A$1.08 billion**; group cash EBTDA was a record **A$170.3 million** at ~15.8% operating margin.
- **H1 FY26 (six months to 31 December 2025)**: Record group cash EBTDA of **A$124.3 million**; total income **A$664.0 million**; total transaction value (TTV) of **A$8.4 billion**; operating margin expanded to **18.7%**. Statutory NPAT of A$52.4 million. Net bad debts held at **1.7% of TTV**, within target.
- **April 2026 (post Q3 FY26)**: Zip **upgraded FY26 group cash EBTDA guidance to no less than A$260 million** after a strong third quarter, while reconfirming revenue-margin and TTV growth guidance.
- **Funding**: Completed an **A$300 million five-year public ABS term deal** in early February 2026 to extend portfolio duration and diversify funding — important for a balance-sheet BNPL lender.
- **Strategic focus**: Continued US growth over 40%, disciplined capital allocation and balance-sheet strengthening; Australia/NZ remains a profitable, steady cash-generating segment.
- **Product and partnerships**: Expansion of pay-in-4 availability through major payment networks and enterprise merchants; ongoing investment in credit risk analytics and in-app engagement.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Zip's [[economic-moat|economic moat]] is **narrow** — BNPL is a low-switching-cost, commoditising business where the durable edge is data and distribution rather than a structural lock-in:

- **Merchant network & distribution** — embedding in checkout flows (Stripe, Google Chrome, enterprise merchants) creates incumbency at the point of sale, the scarcest real estate in payments.
- **Credit-decisioning data** — a multi-year repayment dataset across cycles improves approval/loss trade-offs; this is the closest thing BNPL has to a compounding advantage.
- **Two-sided scale** — more merchants attract more consumers and vice-versa, a modest [[network-effects|network effect]], though far weaker than a card scheme's.
- **Funding access** — proven warehouse and public ABS programs (e.g. the Feb-2026 A$300m five-year term deal) lower the cost of the receivable book; smaller rivals struggle to fund through a downturn.

The moat is fragile: consumers multi-home across BNPL apps, card networks ([[visa]]/[[mastercard]]) and banks are pushing rival installment products, and regulation can compress economics across the board. The realistic edge is execution and scale, not a wide [[economic-moat|moat]].

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **US scale-up** — US transaction volume and revenue both grew >40% in FY25; the US is the swing factor on the whole equity story, reinforced by the CEO's planned 2026 relocation.
- **Operating leverage** — group operating margin expanding (≈15.8% FY25 → 18.7% H1 FY26) as revenue grows faster than the cost base; each guidance upgrade has re-rated the stock.
- **Enterprise & embedded distribution** — Stripe, Google Chrome and enterprise merchant signings widen the funnel without per-merchant sales cost.
- **Credit performance** — net bad debts held at ~1.7% of TTV (within target); stable losses are what let volume growth convert to profit.
- **Funding diversification** — public ABS issuance extends duration and lowers funding cost, key for a balance-sheet lender.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year (Feb) and full-year (Aug) results, quarterly TTV/credit updates, cash-EBTDA guidance upgrades, US merchant signings, ABS/funding-cost news.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- US engine compounding >40% with a long runway versus a far larger TAM than ANZ.
- Operating leverage is real — margins are widening and management keeps upgrading cash-EBTDA guidance.
- Credit losses are controlled (~1.7% of TTV), so growth is profitable growth, not subsidised volume.
- Now profitable and self-funding after the 2022-2023 restructuring; balance sheet strengthened via ABS.
- Re-rating optionality: a turnaround that delivers can compress its still-elevated multiple as earnings grow into it.

### Bear case
- **High-risk, high-volatility name** — a balance-sheet consumer lender geared to the US consumer; a downturn hits volume and bad debts simultaneously.
- Funding-market sensitivity: warehouse/ABS costs and availability can tighten sharply if rates or credit spreads back up.
- Regulation tightening in both Australia and the US (BNPL brought under credit rules) can structurally compress economics.
- Narrow moat: card networks, banks and PayPal can replicate pay-in-4 with more distribution and cheaper funding.
- Valuation prices in continued execution; any volume or credit disappointment de-rates a crowded, high-beta turnaround hard.

## Key Risks

- **Consumer-credit deterioration** — bad-debt creep above the ~1.7%-of-TTV target directly hits the P&L of a balance-sheet lender (see [[credit-risk]]).
- **Funding-market sensitivity** — reliance on warehouse facilities and ABS means cost-of-funds and rate-cycle exposure are first-order risks.
- **Regulatory** — BNPL being brought under consumer-credit regulation in AU and the US can cap fees, mandate affordability checks, and compress margins.
- **Competition / commoditisation** — card networks, banks, PayPal, Affirm and Klarna competing for the same merchant shelf.
- **Concentration & FX** — growing reliance on a single market (US) and AUD/USD translation as the US becomes dominant.
- **Volatility / positioning** — high-beta turnaround stock; sentiment swings on each guidance print magnify drawdowns.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Zip trades as a **high-beta consumer-credit turnaround**, valued on cash-EBTDA growth and the durability of US volume rather than on a stable earnings base. The right lens is BNPL [[unit-economics]]: revenue margin (income ÷ TTV), net bad debts (% of TTV), and the cost-of-funds spread — net transaction margin after losses and funding is what scales into group cash EBTDA. Mechanically:

- **Turnaround re-rating** — the stock has re-rated sharply on each cash-EBTDA upgrade (FY26 guidance lifted to ≥A$260m in April 2026); the multiple stays elevated because the market is paying for a growth/operating-leverage trajectory, not for current earnings.
- **High beta** — a leveraged play on US consumer spending and the rate cycle; it moves with US payments/fintech (Block/Afterpay, PayPal, Affirm) and risk appetite more than with the ASX.
- **No dividend** — capital is retained to fund the receivable book and growth; total return is entirely price-driven (a balance-sheet lender, not a yield stock).
- **Funding-spread sensitivity** — because it carries its own book, the equity is geared to credit spreads and ABS pricing as much as to volume.
- **Catalysts** — half-year (Feb) / full-year (Aug) results, quarterly TTV and credit updates, guidance upgrades, US merchant signings, ABS deals.
- **Options:** limited single-name options liquidity (ASX equity options are thin for mid-caps); most expression is via the underlying shares.
