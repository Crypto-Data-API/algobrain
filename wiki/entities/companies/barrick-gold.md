Barrick Mining Corporation (NYSE: B, TSX: ABX) — known as Barrick Gold Corporation until May 2025 — is the world's second-largest gold mining company by production, after [[newmont|Newmont Corporation]], with a growing copper business. Founded in 1983 by Peter Munk in Toronto, Barrick operates mines and projects across North America, South America, Africa, Papua New Guinea, and the Middle East, producing roughly 3.5-4 million ounces of [[gold]] annually. For traders, Barrick is the large-cap North American gold equity that functions as a leveraged proxy on the gold price, increasingly with copper optionality from the giant Reko Diq (Pakistan) and Lumwana (Zambia) projects.

## Name and Ticker Change (2025)

At its May 6, 2025 annual meeting, shareholders approved renaming the company **Barrick Mining Corporation**, reflecting the strategic pivot toward copper alongside gold. The NYSE ticker changed from **GOLD** to **B**, effective at the start of trading on May 9, 2025; the Toronto listing continues to trade as **ABX**. (Verified via WebSearch, 2026-06-10)

Fred McNaught discusses Barrick Gold as a peer comparison when analysing Australian gold miners such as [[northern-star|Northern Star Resources]] and [[evolution-mining|Evolution Mining]]. In the context of building a [[gold]] sector allocation, Barrick serves as the international large-cap reference point against which ASX-listed gold producers are measured on metrics like all-in sustaining cost (AISC), production volume, and sovereign risk profile.

## Key Assets and Operations

Barrick's most important asset is the Nevada Gold Mines joint venture (61.5% Barrick, 38.5% [[newmont|Newmont]]), formed in 2019 by combining the two companies' extensive Nevada operations into a single complex. Nevada Gold Mines is the single largest gold-producing operation in the world, encompassing the Carlin, Cortez, Turquoise Ridge, and other operations across Nevada's prolific gold trends. This JV was a landmark transaction in mining, driven by Bristow's thesis that consolidating overlapping Nevada assets would unlock billions in synergies through shared infrastructure, processing, and exploration.

Beyond Nevada, Barrick operates Tier One mines including Pueblo Viejo in the Dominican Republic (one of the world's largest gold mines by output), Loulo-Gounkoto in Mali, Kibali in the Democratic Republic of Congo (a joint venture with AngloGold Ashanti), and the Veladero mine in Argentina. The company also has significant copper production from its Lumwana mine in Zambia and the Reko Diq project in Pakistan, reflecting Bristow's strategic bet that copper exposure provides a valuable diversification within a precious metals portfolio, given copper's role in electrification and the energy transition.

## Business Segments

Barrick's pivot from "Barrick Gold" to "Barrick Mining" formalizes a two-metal business: a Tier-One gold portfolio plus a fast-growing copper franchise. The two metals carry different demand drivers (gold = monetary/safe-haven; copper = industrial/electrification), so the combined company offers diversified [[commodities|commodity]] exposure within a single equity.

| Segment | Key assets | Strategic role |
|---|---|---|
| **Gold (core)** | Nevada Gold Mines JV (61.5% Barrick / 38.5% [[newmont]]) — Carlin, Cortez, Turquoise Ridge; Pueblo Viejo (Dominican Republic); Loulo-Gounkoto (Mali); Kibali (DRC, JV with AngloGold); Veladero (Argentina) | The cash engine and the source of the "leveraged [[gold]] proxy" identity |
| **Copper (growth)** | Lumwana (Zambia) — expansion underway; Reko Diq (Pakistan) — one of the world's largest undeveloped copper-gold deposits | The diversification bet on electrification/energy-transition demand; the rationale for the company rename |

**The "Tier One" framework** is central to how Barrick defines quality. Bristow popularized the standard: a Tier-One asset is a mine with a long reserve life (typically 10+ years), large annual production (e.g., 500k+ oz gold-equivalent), and costs in the lower half of the industry cost curve. Barrick's strategy is to own only Tier-One and high-margin assets and to divest sub-scale operations.

**All-in sustaining cost (AISC)** is the single most important operating metric. AISC captures cash mining costs plus sustaining capital, royalties, and corporate overhead per ounce — the true "cost to keep producing." The spread between the [[gold]] price and AISC is the margin that drives Barrick's free cash flow, and it is why a relatively small move in the gold price produces an outsized move in earnings (operating leverage).

Barrick is one of two global gold "super-majors," and is increasingly compared to diversified base-metal miners as copper grows.

The competitive read: gold equities are largely **price-takers** on the commodity — they don't compete for customers, they compete for capital. Investors choose among them on AISC, reserve life, jurisdiction (sovereign risk), production growth, and capital discipline. Barrick's relative weakness versus [[agnico-eagle]] is its higher sovereign-risk footprint (Mali, DRC, Pakistan); its relative strength is the scale and quality of its Tier-One base plus copper optionality. The whole group trades as a basket via the GDX (senior miners) and GDXJ (juniors) ETFs, and ultimately as a high-beta expression of the physical [[gold]] price and [[gld|GLD]].

## Bull vs Bear Case

**Bull case**
- **Leveraged gold play.** With AISC well below the gold price, each ~US$100/oz move flows to the bottom line — operating leverage that beats holding physical [[gold]] or [[gld|GLD]] when gold rises.
- **Tier-One asset quality** and long reserve life provide durable, low-cost production.
- **Copper optionality** (Reko Diq, Lumwana expansion) diversifies revenue into the electrification theme and could re-rate the multiple toward diversified miners.
- **Capital discipline + fortress balance sheet** — low net debt and a shareholder-return focus, a deliberate contrast to the value-destroying M&A of the prior cycle.

**Bear case**
- **Sovereign risk** — the Mali / Loulo-Gounkoto standoff is the cautionary template; resource nationalism in Mali, DRC, Tanzania, and Pakistan can seize cash flow or assets.
- **Cost inflation** — rising labor, energy, and sustaining-capital costs lift AISC and compress margins even at high gold prices.
- **Gold-price dependence** — earnings live and die by a commodity Barrick cannot control; a gold downcycle hits hard given the operating leverage works both ways.
- **Management transition** — Bristow's abrupt 2025 departure removed a defining strategic voice amid the search for a permanent CEO.
- **Copper execution risk** — Reko Diq is a massive, multi-year, capital-intensive build in a high-risk jurisdiction.

## Valuation Framework

Gold miners are valued on a blend of cash-flow and asset-based metrics, all anchored to a [[gold]]-price assumption:

- **Gold-price beta / operating leverage** — the central driver. Sensitivity of free cash flow to the gold price (each ~US$100/oz move) is the headline figure; the lower the AISC, the higher the leverage.
- **AISC trajectory** — falling or stable AISC widens margins; rising AISC (cost inflation) erodes the gold-price tailwind.
- **Reserve life and reserve replacement** — production sustainability; mines deplete, so reserve life and the ability to replace ounces through exploration/M&A gate the long-run value.
- **NAV / P-NAV** — net asset value of the mine portfolio (discounted future cash flows of each asset) at a reference gold price; miners trade at a multiple of NAV that flexes with sentiment.
- **Sovereign-risk discount** — jurisdictions like Mali/DRC/Pakistan warrant a higher discount rate than Canada/Nevada, pressuring Barrick's multiple versus [[agnico-eagle]].
- **Copper re-rating optionality** — as copper grows, the market may apply a diversified-miner multiple to part of the business.

## Capital Return

Under the post-2019 (Randgold merger) philosophy, Barrick prioritized a fortress balance sheet (low net debt) alongside steady capital returns through a dividend (including performance-linked components tied to the net-cash position) and share buybacks. This disciplined approach is a deliberate repudiation of the prior era's top-of-cycle M&A, which produced the historical write-downs of over $15 billion (Pascua-Lama, African Barrick Gold) cited above. For income-oriented investors, the dividend is modest but the combination of buybacks and a strong balance sheet is the core capital-allocation pitch.

## Investment Profile

Barrick trades on the NYSE under the ticker GOLD — a symbol the company acquired in 2019, signaling its identity as the pre-eminent gold equity. For investors, Barrick functions as a leveraged play on the [[gold]] price: the company's earnings and free cash flow are highly sensitive to gold price movements, with each $100/oz increase in gold translating into hundreds of millions of dollars in incremental cash flow. Barrick's AISC is typically in the $1,000-$1,300/oz range, meaning the company generates substantial margins at gold prices above $2,000/oz.

Bristow's management philosophy emphasizes returning capital to shareholders through dividends and buybacks while maintaining a fortress balance sheet with low net debt. This approach contrasts with the previous era of gold mining M&A (2005-2013) when the industry — including Barrick under prior management — destroyed shareholder value through aggressive, top-of-cycle acquisitions at inflated prices. Barrick's historical write-downs of over $15 billion on assets like Pascua-Lama and the African Barrick Gold portfolio serve as a cautionary case study in [[risk-management]] and the dangers of empire-building in cyclical [[commodities]] industries.

## Sovereign and Operational Risks

Barrick's geographically dispersed portfolio exposes it to a range of sovereign risks. Operations in Mali, the DRC, Tanzania, and Pakistan involve navigating complex political environments, resource nationalism, and security challenges. The company has faced government disputes in multiple jurisdictions, including a high-profile tax dispute with the Tanzanian government in 2017 that was resolved through a framework agreement involving the government taking a 16% free-carried interest in Barrick's local operations. These episodes highlight a recurring theme in gold mining: the highest-grade, lowest-cost deposits are often located in jurisdictions with elevated political risk, creating a fundamental tension in portfolio construction.

## Mali / Loulo-Gounkoto Dispute (2023-2025)

The single most material recent event was Barrick's two-year standoff with Mali's military-led government over the Loulo-Gounkoto gold complex. Under a new mining code aimed at extracting more revenue at record gold prices, Mali in 2024-2025 appointed a provisional administrator over the complex, seized roughly three tonnes of gold (~US$400M), and detained Barrick employees. Barrick wrote off ~US$1B of revenue from the mine and pursued ICSID arbitration. The dispute was **resolved in November 2025**: Barrick agreed to pay ~US$430M to settle claims, regained operational control, secured the release of detained staff and seized gold, and withdrew the ICSID arbitration. (Verified via WebSearch, 2026-06-10) The episode is a live case study in resource nationalism and sovereign risk in gold mining.

## Leadership Change (2025)

On September 29, 2025, long-time CEO **Mark Bristow abruptly departed** after nearly seven years (he had led the company since the 2019 Randgold merger). The board appointed **Mark Hill** — a 30-year mining veteran with Barrick since 2006 — as Group COO and interim President and CEO, and launched a search (chaired by Brett Harvey) for a permanent successor. Bristow's exit, coming amid the Mali write-off, removed a defining strategic voice and introduced management-transition uncertainty.
