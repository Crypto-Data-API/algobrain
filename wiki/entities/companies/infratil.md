**Infratil Limited** (ASX: IFT) is an conglomerates company listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. Infratil Limited is a long-established infrastructure investment company dual-listed on the New Zealand Exchange (primary) and the ASX. Based in Wellington, Infratil holds a diversified portfolio of digital, renewable, transport and social infrastructure assets across New Zealand, Australia, the United States, Europe and Asia.

## Business Overview & Segments

Infratil is a **listed infrastructure investor** — effectively a publicly traded portfolio of long-duration infrastructure assets, externally managed by Morrison & Co. Though its ASX sector classification is Financials/Conglomerates, in substance it is an [[utilities|infrastructure and utility]] play: it owns stakes in data centres, renewable generation, telecoms, airports and healthcare imaging, earning returns through operating distributions and periodic asset recycling. As of recent disclosures roughly **66% of the portfolio is weighted to digital infrastructure** (data centres and telecoms) and **~21% to renewable energy**, with the remainder across healthcare and airports — making IFT the most direct large-cap Australasian vehicle for the **AI/hyperscale data-centre buildout** via its CDC holding.

Major investments in detail:

- **Digital infrastructure:**
  - CDC (Canberra Data Centres) - Infratil's largest single investment; a hyperscale data centre operator with facilities in Australia and New Zealand.
  - One NZ - New Zealand's second-largest mobile and broadband telecommunications network operator.
  - Gurin Energy, Galileo and Kao Data - data centre and digital infrastructure exposures in Asia and Europe.
- **Renewable energy:**
  - Manawa Energy (NZ) - renewable electricity generation.
  - Mint Renewables and related wind/solar platforms.
- **Social infrastructure / healthcare:**
  - Qscan Group (Australia) and RHCNZ Medical Imaging (New Zealand) - diagnostic imaging networks.
  - RetireAustralia - retirement village operator.
- **Transport:**
  - Wellington International Airport - 66% stake held via NZ Airports.

Infratil earns returns through operating distributions from portfolio companies and periodic asset recyclings. Because statutory NPAT is noisy (driven by fair-value movements on holdings), the market keys off **proportionate Operational EBITDAF** guidance instead.

## History

Infratil was founded in 1994 by the late Lloyd Morrison, a Wellington-based merchant banker. It was one of the world's first listed infrastructure funds, created in response to the significant privatisation of New Zealand infrastructure assets in the early 1990s. Its external manager, Morrison & Co, was founded by Morrison in 1988 and narrowed its focus to infrastructure investment and advisory in the early 1990s.

Key historical milestones include:

- **1998:** Acquired a 66% stake in Wellington International Airport (with Wellington City Council holding the remaining 34%).
- **2010s:** Built significant energy and renewable exposures through investments in Z Energy, Trustpower (now Manawa Energy for its generation business), and Tilt Renewables.
- **Ownership of CDC:** Progressively built its stake in CDC, positioning Infratil to benefit from the rapid growth in Australian and New Zealand data centre demand.
- **2022:** Acquired Vodafone New Zealand (alongside Brookfield), rebranded as One NZ.

Over 30+ years, Infratil has transitioned from a fund focused on New Zealand privatisations into a globally diversified infrastructure investor with heavy exposure to digital and renewable themes.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Infratil's [[economic-moat]] is the **moat of its underlying assets** plus a hard-to-replicate origination capability:

- **Irreplaceable infrastructure assets** — data centres (CDC), an airport, telecom networks and renewable generation are long-life, high-barrier assets with durable, often contracted cash flows; CDC in particular benefits from ~80% pre-contracted revenue and 30-year customer contracts.
- **Scarcity & scale in data centres** — CDC's hyperscale capacity, power access and government/sovereign customer relationships in Australia are extremely difficult to replicate, giving pricing power in a supply-constrained market.
- **Origination & management platform** — Morrison & Co's 30+-year track record sourcing, structuring and managing infrastructure gives Infratil access to assets and deals (e.g. the CDC pre-emptive rights) that passive investors cannot match.
- **Diversification** — spreading across digital, renewable, transport and healthcare smooths cash flows and recycles capital from mature assets into growth themes.

The offsetting weakness is the **external-management structure**: management and incentive fees paid to Morrison & Co are a recurring drag and a governance debate, and the value of unlisted holdings (especially CDC) is a matter of independent-valuation judgement rather than a market price.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **CDC / data-centre buildout** — the central growth driver; CDC has crossed 1 GW of contracted capacity and forecasts EBITDAF growth of more than 150% to over A$1 billion by FY28, backed by landmark contracts (the May 2026 555 MW, 30-year deal — the largest in Australian history).
- **AI / hyperscale demand** — Infratil is a leveraged Australasian expression of the AI-capex theme alongside [[nextdc]] and Goodman.
- **Renewables development** — Longroad Energy (US) and NZ wind/solar platforms scaling into the decarbonisation transition.
- **Asset recycling** — selling mature assets to fund the data-centre and renewable pipeline; periodic independent revaluations crystallise value.
- **One NZ** — telecom performance and monetisation of digital infrastructure.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year (November) and full-year (May) results, CDC contract announcements and revaluations, capital raisings, and EBITDAF guidance updates.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- CDC is a generational data-centre asset riding the AI/hyperscale wave; >150% EBITDAF growth to >A$1bn by FY28 with most revenue pre-contracted.
- Diversified, long-duration infrastructure cash flows with structural growth tilt (digital + renewable).
- Proven origination via Morrison & Co; private-market marks have repeatedly validated (or exceeded) carrying values (>30% uplift on the CDC pre-emptive deal).
- FY27 EBITDAF guidance of NZ$1,300-1,400m implies ~21% like-for-like growth.
- A liquid, listed way to own assets that are otherwise only accessible to private infrastructure funds.

### Bear case
- Heavy reliance on CDC valuation — carrying value rests on independent/private marks, not a market price; any AI-capex slowdown would hit the key asset and sentiment.
- External-management fee and incentive drag is a permanent cost and governance concern.
- Statutory NPAT is noisy (fair-value swings); the company can report large GAAP losses even as EBITDAF grows.
- Capital-intensive growth requires recurring equity raises (dilution) and carries balance-sheet leverage.
- Rate-cycle sensitivity: infrastructure valuations and the cost of the data-centre pipeline are exposed to interest rates.

## Key Risks

- **AI-capex / data-centre cyclicality** — the thesis is concentrated in CDC; a hyperscaler capex pause or oversupply would hit the central growth driver and valuation simultaneously.
- **Valuation opacity** — unlisted holdings are carried at independent valuations; a downward revaluation (or rate-driven re-rating) compresses NAV.
- **External-manager conflicts** — fees to Morrison & Co and alignment between manager and shareholders.
- **Leverage / funding** — debt at the holding and asset level, plus reliance on capital markets to fund the pipeline; covenant and dilution risk.
- **Interest rates** — higher rates raise discount rates on long-duration cash flows and the cost of capital for the buildout.
- **Concentration & FX** — single-asset concentration in CDC and currency exposure across NZ, AU, US, Europe and Asia.

## Recent Developments

For the full year ended 31 March 2025, Infratil reported proportionate operating earnings (EBITDAF) of NZ$986 million, up 8.6%. Growth was driven by CDC Data Centres, One NZ, Wellington Airport, and RetireAustralia.

- **CDC Data Centres:** EBITDAF of A$330 million (up A$59 million or 22% year on year), supported by commissioning of new capacity in Melbourne and New Zealand. CDC signed more than 230MW of new customer contracts during the year - its largest annual addition. CDC expects to roughly double EBITDAF over the following two years, with approximately 80% of that revenue already contracted.
- **CDC ownership:** Infratil and fellow shareholder the Future Fund exercised pre-emptive rights to acquire a 12.04% stake in CDC in a competitive sale process, with Infratil taking approximately 1.58%. The private market transaction implied a CDC valuation more than 30% above the prior independent valuation.
- **FY2026 guidance:** Proportionate Operational EBITDAF of NZ$1,000-1,050 million, representing approximately 9% like-for-like growth on FY2025.

Interim results for the half year ended 30 September 2025 reconfirmed momentum in the digital infrastructure portfolio and continued growth in renewables development.

### FY2026 results and 2026 updates (to June 2026)

Full-year results for the year ended 31 March 2026 were announced on **26 May 2026**:

- Proportionate Operational EBITDAF grew **11% to NZ$989 million**, driven primarily by CDC Data Centres and Longroad Energy.
- **FY27 guidance of NZ$1,300-1,400 million** — approximately a 21% like-for-like increase at the midpoint.
- Unimputed final dividend of 13.65 cps payable 29 June 2026, bringing total FY26 dividends to 20.9 cps.

**CDC's landmark contract (May 2026):** CDC signed the **largest data-centre contract in Australian history — a 555 MW, 30-year deal** with a US investment-grade customer, sending IFT shares up ~12% on the announcement. CDC has now crossed 1 GW of contracted capacity and forecasts EBITDAF growth of more than 150% to over A$1 billion by FY28. The deal added roughly 35% to total FY26 returns measured since 31 March alone.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Infratil trades as a **growth-infrastructure** name, not a yield utility — the market prices the CDC/AI-data-centre option, so it carries a higher multiple and lower yield than a traditional regulated [[utilities|utility]]. Because statutory earnings are distorted by fair-value movements, conventional P/E (which can swing negative) is unreliable; the stock is valued on a **sum-of-the-parts NAV** of its holdings and on proportionate EBITDAF growth. Mechanically:

- **Dividend / yield profile** — pays a modest, growing dividend (FY26 total 20.9cps; historical yield ~2%), generally **unimputed/partly imputed** — income is secondary to capital growth. This is a total-return, growth-tilted holding, not an income staple.
- **NAV vs. carrying value** — the key valuation debate is CDC's carrying value versus private-market transaction marks (the 2025 pre-emptive deal implied a >30% uplift); the stock re-rates on revaluations and landmark contracts (shares rose ~12% on the May 2026 555 MW CDC deal).
- **Beta** — moderate-to-high for an infrastructure name; it behaves as a sentiment proxy on AI-infrastructure capex alongside [[nextdc]] and Goodman, so it is more volatile than a regulated utility.
- **How it moves** — half-year (Nov) and full-year (May) results, CDC contract announcements and revaluations, asset recycling, capital raisings, and One NZ performance.
