Cazenove was the most prestigious of the traditional British stockbrokers, founded in London in 1823 by Philip Cazenove and long serving as stockbroker to the British royal family. For most of the twentieth century it was the dominant corporate broker in the City of London, acting for a large share of FTSE 100 boards. It was folded into a joint venture with JPMorgan in 2005 and fully acquired in 2010, surviving today as the **J.P. Morgan Cazenove** brand. Referenced in ITPM content as an example of the traditional City of London brokerage culture.

## History

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1823 | Philip Cazenove enters partnership in London; the firm that becomes Cazenove & Co is established |
| 1800s–1900s | Grows into the City's premier stockbroking partnership; becomes broker to the British royal family |
| 1986 | "Big Bang" deregulation — Cazenove is one of the very few major London brokers to remain an independent partnership while rivals sell to banks |
| 2001 | Incorporates (ending the partnership structure) and prepares for an IPO that is later shelved after the dot-com downturn |
| 2005 | Forms a 50/50 investment-banking joint venture with JPMorgan: **J.P. Morgan Cazenove** |
| 2010 | JPMorgan buys the remaining 50% (reported around £1 billion), making the brand a wholly owned part of [[jpmorgan-chase]] |
| 2013 | The separate wealth-management arm, Cazenove Capital, is acquired by Schroders |

## The Corporate Broking Model

Cazenove's franchise rested on **corporate broking** — a uniquely British arrangement in which a listed company retains a broker as its standing intermediary with the market: advising on shareholder sentiment, handling placings and rights issues, and quietly managing price-sensitive communication. At its peak Cazenove was corporate broker to more FTSE 100 companies than any rival. The model demonstrates two durable market lessons:

1. **Distribution and relationships are an edge** — Cazenove's placing power (the ability to get an equity issue away to loyal institutional clients in hours) was a structural [[edge-taxonomy|informational and structural edge]] that survived a century of competition.
2. **Culture as moat — and its limits** — the firm's discretion and blue-blood client network kept it independent through Big Bang, but the capital demands of modern underwriting eventually forced consolidation into a balance-sheet bank, a pattern repeated across City partnerships (Barings, Schroders, Warburg, Kleinwort).

## Trading Relevance

Cazenove is a historical entity — there is no listed security. Its relevance to traders is contextual: J.P. Morgan Cazenove remains a top UK equity research and corporate-broking franchise whose ratings changes still move London-listed stocks, and the firm's history is a case study in how brokerage economics, relationship capital, and regulation (Big Bang 1986) reshape market structure.
