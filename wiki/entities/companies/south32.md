**South32 Limited** (ASX: S32; LSE: S32; JSE: S32) is a diversified [[materials|base-metals and minerals]] miner listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. Spun out of [[bhp|BHP Billiton]] in 2015 as the home for its "non-core" assets, S32 produces bauxite, [[aluminium]], alumina, [[copper]], silver, lead, zinc and manganese across Australia, South Africa, Mozambique, Chile, Brazil and the United States. For traders it is a mid-cap, multi-commodity [[commodities|price-taker]] — a way to own a basket of energy-transition metals (especially manganese and aluminium) without single-mine concentration — whose share price tracks the through-cycle commodity complex rather than any company-specific moat. The company is headquartered at 100 St Georges Terrace, Perth, Western Australia, and employs approximately 8,892 people.

## Business Model & Segments

South32 reports across the following operating segments: Worsley Alumina, Brazil Alumina, Brazil Aluminium, Hillside Aluminium, Mozal Aluminium, Sierra Gorda, Cannington, Hermosa, Australia Manganese and South Africa Manganese.

Key operating assets include:

- Worsley Alumina - 86% interest in an integrated bauxite mining and alumina refining operation in Western Australia.
- Brazil Alumina and Aluminium - integrated alumina refining and aluminium smelting in Brazil.
- Hillside Aluminium - one of the world's largest aluminium smelters, in Richards Bay, South Africa.
- Mozal Aluminium - a major aluminium smelter in Mozambique (transitioning to care and maintenance in March 2026 due to power availability and pricing constraints).
- Sierra Gorda - 45% interest in the Sierra Gorda copper mine in Chile, acquired from Sumitomo Metal Mining and Sumitomo Corporation.
- Cannington - a major underground silver-lead-zinc mine in north-west Queensland.
- Hermosa - a zinc-lead-silver and manganese development project in southern Arizona, with the Taylor deposit (zinc-lead-silver) under construction and the Clark deposit (manganese-zinc) in study.
- Australia Manganese (GEMCO) and South Africa Manganese - major manganese ore producers.

The portfolio has been strategically skewed over recent years toward commodities aligned to the energy transition ([[copper]], zinc, manganese, [[aluminium]]) and away from steelmaking coal and nickel.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

As a diversified [[commodities|commodity price-taker]], South32 has **no pricing power** — it sells bauxite, alumina, aluminium, copper, manganese, silver, lead and zinc into global markets at prices set by exchanges and benchmarks ([[lme]], CRU). There is no [[economic-moat|economic moat]] in the franchise sense; the only durable advantage a miner can hold is **asset quality and cost-curve position**. On that honest framing:

- **Cost-curve position is mixed.** GEMCO (Australia Manganese) sits at the low end of the global manganese cost curve — a genuine first-quartile asset. Worsley alumina is competitive. But the aluminium smelters (Hillside, Mozal) are exposed to power prices and currency, and the Mozal care-and-maintenance decision is a direct admission that part of the smelting portfolio is uneconomic at prevailing input costs.
- **Diversification is the deliberate strategy.** Unlike a single-commodity miner ([[sandfire-resources]] in copper, [[west-african-resources]] in gold), S32 spreads risk across many metals and jurisdictions. This dampens single-commodity drawdowns but also dilutes upside in any one bull market and adds complexity — the portfolio has been actively *simplified* (coal, nickel exits) precisely because the market discounts diversified-miner complexity.
- **Through-cycle survival, not moat.** Low leverage (D/E ~0.08) and disciplined capital allocation are what let a price-taker survive troughs and reinvest — that is the realistic "edge," not any defensible margin.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Energy-transition metals** — copper (Sierra Gorda + study pipeline), zinc and manganese exposure aligned to electrification and steel/battery demand.
- **Hermosa Taylor** — the key organic growth project (zinc-lead-silver), targeting first production later this decade; the Clark manganese-zinc deposit is longer-dated optionality.
- **Portfolio simplification** — divestments of coal and nickel (Cerro Matoso) reduce complexity and may re-rate the multiple.
- **Manganese price cycle** — GEMCO's low cost position means manganese price swings flow heavily to earnings.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly production reports, half-year (Feb) and full-year (Aug) results, commodity-price prints ([[lme]] aluminium/copper/zinc, manganese ore indices), capex/dividend updates, and project FID/first-production milestones.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Diversified, energy-transition-weighted basket of base metals with a clean, low-leverage balance sheet (D/E ~0.08).
- Low-cost manganese (GEMCO) and competitive alumina provide through-cycle cash generation.
- Hermosa Taylor and the copper study pipeline add organic volume growth without bet-the-company risk.
- Active portfolio simplification (coal, nickel exits) could narrow the diversified-miner discount.

### Bear case
- Pure [[commodities|price-taker]] — earnings and the share price are hostage to commodity prices the company cannot control.
- Jurisdictional and power-cost risk: South Africa and Mozambique power constraints already forced Mozal into care and maintenance.
- Smelting assets sit higher on the cost curve and are squeezed when power and currency move against them.
- Diversified-miner complexity attracts a conglomerate discount; net income has swung from A$2.67B (FY22) to losses (FY23–FY24) on the commodity cycle.

## Key Risks

- **Commodity-price cyclicality** — multi-commodity exposure spreads but does not eliminate price risk; a synchronized base-metals downturn compresses every segment at once.
- **Power and input costs** — the aluminium smelters depend on affordable, reliable power; the Mozal decision shows how quickly an asset can turn uneconomic.
- **Jurisdiction / sovereign risk** — operations in South Africa, Mozambique, Brazil and Chile carry policy, currency and reliability risk beyond the Australian asset base.
- **Capital allocation at the cycle peak** — overpaying for growth or sustaining capex into a downturn (the FY23 US$1.3B Hermosa impairment is a cautionary example).
- **Reserve replacement & execution** — Hermosa Taylor delivery on time and budget is a meaningful swing factor.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

S32 trades as a **diversified-miner commodity beta play**, not a quality compounder. The headline trailing P/E (~96x) is meaningless through the trough of the cycle — net income collapsed to a small profit in FY25 after two loss years — and is exactly the kind of static-metric distortion the [[#Fred-framework view]] flags for commodity producers. Mechanically:

- **Commodity beta** — the equity is a leveraged read on a basket of base-metal prices; it moves with [[lme]] aluminium/copper/zinc, manganese ore and China demand sentiment, and broadly with [[materials]] and [[bhp]].
- **Dividend** — historically a yield name with a payout policy geared to earnings; through-cycle dividends are variable and partly [[franking-credits|franked]] (see Fundamentals), so the income stream is cyclical, not defensive.
- **How to size it** — value through the cycle on normalised earnings / EV-to-EBITDA, not on point-in-time P/E; counter-cyclical accumulation in commodity troughs is the typical playbook for price-takers.
- **Liquidity** — large, liquid ASX 200 constituent with secondary LSE/JSE listings; institutional ownership is high.

## History

South32 was demerged from BHP Billiton in 2015. Demerger was approved by shareholders on 6 May 2015 and shares commenced trading on the ASX, LSE and JSE on 2 June 2015. BHP Billiton shareholders received one South32 share for every BHP Billiton share held. Graham Kerr, a long-serving BHP executive, was appointed inaugural CEO.

South32 was established to separate BHP Billiton's then-considered non-core assets, encompassing a diverse range of commodities including alumina, aluminium, metallurgical coal, manganese, nickel, silver, lead and zinc. Over the following decade, South32 materially transformed the portfolio through a series of targeted acquisitions and divestments:

- 2018: Acquired Arizona Mining (owner of the Hermosa project) for approximately US$1.3 billion.
- 2022: Completed acquisition of a 45% stake in Sierra Gorda copper mine from Sumitomo Metal Mining and Sumitomo Corporation for US$1.55 billion.
- Subsequent years: divested metallurgical coal (South Africa), exited South African Manganese expansion, sold Illawarra Metallurgical Coal in Australia, and agreed to divest Cerro Matoso nickel in Colombia, significantly simplifying and focusing the portfolio.

In 2023, South32 took a US$1.3 billion impairment charge on the Hermosa project before subsequently approving a US$2.16 billion final investment decision for the Taylor deposit in 2024.

## Recent Developments

For H1 FY26 (six months to 31 December 2025), South32 delivered underlying EBITDA of US$1.1 billion with a group operating margin of 28.2%. Underlying earnings were US$45 million and group free cash flow was US$57 million, after US$338 million of growth capital investment. The company continued portfolio streamlining with the sale of Illawarra Metallurgical Coal and a binding agreement to divest Cerro Matoso nickel, positioning the portfolio toward critical minerals and reducing complexity.

Mozal Aluminium will transition to care and maintenance in March 2026 due to the inability to secure sufficient power supply at an economically viable price. Kerr cited Eskom's cross-border tariff structure implying a price of approximately US$100/MWh, while Mozal could afford around US$51/MWh. The company increased investment in base metals growth projects, including construction of Hermosa Taylor and an advancing pipeline of copper study/exploration opportunities.

On the leadership front, Matthew Daley will join South32 as Deputy Chief Executive Officer on 2 February 2026 and will assume the role of CEO when Graham Kerr steps down later in 2026, after more than a decade in the role.

## Leadership

South32 is led by outgoing Chief Executive Officer Graham Kerr, the inaugural CEO who has led the company since demerger. Matthew Daley has been appointed as Deputy CEO from 2 February 2026 and will succeed Kerr as CEO later in 2026. The Board and executive team comprise leaders with substantial experience across aluminium, manganese, zinc, copper, operations, sustainability and global commodities.
