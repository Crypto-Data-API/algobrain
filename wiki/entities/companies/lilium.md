> **Insolvency / delisting case study.** LILM no longer trades. Lilium's German operating subsidiaries filed for insolvency, Nasdaq suspended the shares on 6 November 2024, and the delisting became effective 31 March 2025. This page is written in past tense and retained as a historical case study in pre-revenue SPAC cash-burn and the eVTOL bubble. Nothing here implies the stock still trades.

Lilium N.V. was a German eVTOL (electric vertical take-off and landing) air-taxi developer that listed on Nasdaq via SPAC in 2021 (ticker LILM), became a heavily traded speculative retail/options name, and **collapsed into insolvency in 2024-2025**. It is now defunct as a listed company: Nasdaq suspended the shares on 6 November 2024 and the delisting became effective 31 March 2025. The stock is a case study in pre-revenue SPAC cash-burn risk.

## What the Business Was

Lilium was a **pre-revenue eVTOL aircraft developer**, not an operator. Its entire value rested on a single product program and the promise of future air-taxi services:

| Element | What it was | Status at the end |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| **The Lilium Jet** | A distinctive **ducted-fan** (electric jet) eVTOL targeting regional air mobility, pitched as longer-range than competing multirotor/tilt-rotor designs | Never certified; never flew commercially |
| **Certification effort** | EASA (Europe) and FAA (US) type-certification campaigns | Repeatedly delayed; the gating risk for the whole business |
| **Order book / LOIs** | Letters of intent and conditional orders from operators and fleet buyers | Non-binding; never converted to revenue |
| **Manufacturing** | Planned series production near Munich | Never reached commercial scale |

The architectural bet — a ducted-fan design for greater range — was also a heavier engineering and certification lift than simpler multirotor designs, deepening the capital and timeline risk that ultimately proved fatal.

## Company History

- **2015** — Founded near Munich by Daniel Wiegand and co-founders from TU Munich, developing the "Lilium Jet," a ducted-fan eVTOL targeting regional air mobility (longer range than competing multirotor designs).
- **September 2021** — Listed on Nasdaq via merger with Qell Acquisition Corp (SPAC of ex-GM executive Barry Engle), raising ~US$584M at an implied ~US$3.3B valuation. Never generated meaningful revenue; the jet never achieved certification or commercial flight.
- **2021-2024** — Burned through successive capital raises while pushing back certification timelines; sought (and failed to obtain) German and Bavarian state loan guarantees in late 2024.
- **October 2024** — Main German operating subsidiaries (Lilium GmbH and Lilium eAircraft GmbH) filed for insolvency/self-administration at the Weilheim court after the cash crisis became terminal. Nasdaq issued a delisting determination on 28 October 2024.
- **6 November 2024** — Nasdaq suspended trading in LILM shares.
- **December 2024 - February 2025** — A rescue by the "Mobile Uplift Corporation" investor consortium was announced but the funding failed to materialise; the subsidiaries filed for insolvency a second time in February 2025 and operations ceased, with most of the ~1,000 staff dismissed.
- **31 March 2025** — Nasdaq delisting became effective (Form 25-NSE; no appeal filed by the company). Equity holders were effectively wiped out.

## Competitive Positioning at the Time

Lilium competed in the small cohort of listed eVTOL "air-taxi" developers, almost all of which went public via SPAC in 2021. The category as a whole faced the same brutal arithmetic — years of certification with no revenue against finite cash — and survival came down to funding depth and balance-sheet backers, not aircraft design.

| Peer | Positioning vs. Lilium | Outcome |
|------|------------------------|---------|
| [[joby-aviation]] (JOBY) | Tilt-rotor design; deep-pocketed backers (Toyota, others), large cash buffer | Survived; the best-funded listed eVTOL vehicle for the theme |
| [[archer-aviation]] (ACHR) | Tilt-rotor; backed by Stellantis/United, strong capital raises | Survived; the other primary listed eVTOL vehicle |
| Vertical Aerospace (EVTL) | UK eVTOL peer | Also distressed; faced its own restructuring/dilution pressure |
| Lilium | Ducted-fan (jet) design; weaker funding, reliant on state-aid rumors | **Insolvent / delisted** |

The decisive variable was not technology but **who had the cash and backers to outlast the certification timeline.** [[joby-aviation]] and [[archer-aviation]] secured strategic OEM/airline capital; Lilium relied on successive raises and ultimately on German/Bavarian state-aid that never came. Within the eVTOL trade, capital flight from Lilium toward the funded survivors is the cleanest illustration of the sector's winner-take-funding dynamic.

## The Failure: Cash-Burn, Failed Rescues, and Insolvency

Lilium failed the classic pre-revenue SPAC way — it ran out of money before it could certify and sell a product:

1. **SPAC capital, no revenue.** The 2021 Qell SPAC merger raised ~US$584M at an implied ~US$3.3B valuation, but the jet never certified or flew commercially — so revenue stayed at essentially zero throughout.
2. **Burn vs. timeline.** Burn ran ~US$250M+/year against a repeatedly delayed EASA/FAA certification. Cash runway vs. certification date was the knowable, quantifiable kill metric — and the gap kept widening.
3. **Dilutive raises, then dependence on state aid.** Successive capital raises diluted holders while the company sought (and failed to obtain) German and Bavarian state loan guarantees in late 2024.
4. **First insolvency (Oct 2024).** The main German operating subsidiaries filed for insolvency/self-administration; Nasdaq issued a delisting determination (28 Oct 2024) and suspended trading in LILM (6 Nov 2024).
5. **Failed rescue (Dec 2024-Feb 2025).** A "Mobile Uplift Corporation" consortium rescue was announced but the funding never materialised; the subsidiaries filed insolvency a *second* time in February 2025, operations ceased, and ~1,000 staff were dismissed.
6. **Delisting effective (31 Mar 2025).** Form 25-NSE took effect with no appeal; equity holders were effectively wiped out. (See Sources for the exact filings/dates.)

## Merger-Arb / Trading Postmortem

There was no acquirer and no premium — Lilium is a collapse/dilution case, the opposite of the clean merger-arb names ([[splunk]], [[1life-healthcare|One Medical]]):

- **Binary state-aid headline trading.** The stock repeatedly spiked on German/Bavarian state-aid rumours in 2024; every rescue path failed. Trading binary government-rescue headlines in sub-US$1 stocks is negative-EV without strict position-sizing discipline — the [[short-squeeze]]-style pops were real but mean-reverting toward zero.
- **Delisting mechanics trap holders.** Nasdaq *suspension* froze listed liquidity well before the *formal* delisting; holders could not exit at meaningful prices, and post-suspension OTC trading was fractions of a cent. The lesson: suspension, not delisting, is the point of no return for liquidity.
- **Options carried issuer-survival risk.** [[implied-volatility]] stayed high, but long-dated calls on a pre-revenue issuer embed bankruptcy risk, not just volatility — options markets ceased with the delisting and long calls expired worthless.
- **Capital flight as the trade.** The cleaner expression of the eVTOL thesis was to rotate out of Lilium into the funded survivors [[joby-aviation]] / [[archer-aviation]] long before the terminal leg — the cash-runway gap was visible well in advance.

## Trading Lessons

- **SPAC pre-revenue risk:** Lilium joined Nikola, Virgin Orbit, and others in the 2021 SPAC cohort that destroyed >95% of equity value. Cash runway versus certification timeline was the knowable kill metric — burn was ~US$250M+/year against repeatedly delayed EASA/FAA certification.
- **Government-rescue speculation:** the stock repeatedly spiked on German/Bavarian state-aid rumours in 2024; all rescue paths failed. Trading binary state-aid headlines in sub-US$1 stocks is negative-EV without position sizing discipline.
- **Delisting mechanics:** Nasdaq suspension froze listed liquidity well before the formal delisting; holders could not exit at meaningful prices. OTC trading post-suspension was fractions of a cent.
- **Sector read-through:** capital flowed to better-funded eVTOL peers [[joby-aviation]] and [[archer-aviation]], which remain the listed vehicles for the theme as of June 2026.

## Risks That Materialized

- **Certification-timeline risk:** EASA/FAA type certification never arrived; repeated delays pushed revenue beyond the cash runway.
- **Cash-runway / burn risk:** ~US$250M+/year burn against zero revenue was the explicit kill metric.
- **Financing / rescue risk:** failed state-aid and a failed consortium rescue closed off every survival path.
- **Dilution risk:** serial raises diluted holders ahead of the wipeout.
- **Liquidity / delisting risk:** Nasdaq suspension froze exits before formal delisting; equity effectively went to zero.
