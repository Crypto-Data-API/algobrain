NEXTDC Ltd (ASX: NXT) is Australia's largest listed independent data centre operator and the ASX's primary pure-play on the [[artificial-intelligence|AI]]/cloud infrastructure build-out, operating 17 facilities as of March 2026 and expanding into Asia. A constituent of the [[asx-200]] in the [[technology|Information Technology]] sector, it is the listed Australian expression of the [[ai-data-center-power-demand|AI data-centre power-demand]] theme. For traders it is a pre-profit infrastructure growth stock priced on contracted megawatts, not earnings — the December 2025 OpenAI sovereign-AI agreement and a 296.8MW forward order book are the current core of the bull case.

> Historical note (June 2024 snapshot): NextDC then operated 13 data centres and was positioning for cloud/AI megatrends. (Source: [[alfred-report-nxt-asx]])

## Overview

NextDC is a growth-stage infrastructure company that prioritises revenue growth and capacity expansion over near-term profitability. With negative NPAT (capex and depreciation run ahead of billings), traditional valuation metrics (P/E, DCF) are not meaningful — investors price the contracted-but-not-yet-billing forward order book and the future cash flows from capacity build-out. The current core of the equity story (as of mid-2026) is the 296.8MW forward order book and the December 2025 OpenAI sovereign-AI agreement; see [[#Recent Developments]] and [[#Trading Relevance]] below for current figures.

## Business Model & Segments

NEXTDC designs, builds and operates a national fleet of data centres across Australia's major and strategic regional markets. Core services include rack, cage and private suite colocation, high-performance computing hosting, remote hands, mission-critical space, liquid cooling, build-to-suit and wholesale solutions, and edge data centre services. The company also provides IT asset disposal, e-waste recycling, and carbon-neutral infrastructure options.

Customers access a portfolio of self-service platforms, including ONEDC, a data centre infrastructure management solution; AXON, an interconnectivity platform enabling cross-connect and cloud fabric services; and NEXTGOV, a sustainable solution designed for government and defence workloads. NEXTDC operates direct cloud on-ramps to providers such as AWS, Microsoft Azure and Google Cloud, making its facilities a key point of interconnection within the Australian digital economy.

As of March 2026, NEXTDC operates 17 data centres across Melbourne, Sydney, Brisbane, Perth, Canberra, Adelaide, Darwin, the Sunshine Coast and the Pilbara region. The facilities are generally certified to Tier III or Tier IV standards and are recognised for NABERS 5-star energy efficiency ratings.

## History

NEXTDC was founded in May 2010 by Queensland-based technology entrepreneur Bevan Slattery, whose vision was to build a carrier- and vendor-neutral data centre platform for Australia and New Zealand. The company listed on the ASX under the ticker NXT in December 2010, raising approximately A$40 million to acquire properties and fund initial development.

The first facility, B1 Brisbane, went live in October 2011. In 2012 NEXTDC opened three Tier III facilities: M1 Melbourne, C1 Canberra and S1 Sydney. P1 Perth followed in 2013, extending the footprint nationally. In June 2012 Craig Scroggie was appointed Chief Executive Officer, with Slattery transitioning to Deputy Chairman before retiring from the executive in September 2013.

The company subsequently built a second generation of facilities, opening B2 Brisbane in 2017, M2 Melbourne (Tier IV) and S2 Sydney (Tier IV) in 2018, and commissioning P2 Perth (Tier III) in 2020 to bring the network to nine operational sites. In 2024, NEXTDC expanded further with the openings of D1 Darwin and A1 Adelaide, supporting regional connectivity and sovereign workloads. The company has consistently reinvested operating cash flows and raised equity and debt capital to fund a pipeline of new builds, scaling its contracted utilisation ahead of live-billing capacity.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

NEXTDC's [[economic-moat|moat]] is real but capital-intensive — it rests on assets and ecosystems that are slow and expensive for a rival to replicate:

- **Scarce powered land + grid connections** — the binding constraint on data-centre supply is no longer construction but access to large, firm electricity supply and approved grid connections in the right metros. NEXTDC's secured power pipeline is the single most valuable scarce input behind the [[ai-data-center-power-demand|AI data-centre power-demand]] wave.
- **Tier III/IV certified, NABERS-rated facilities** — uptime and efficiency certifications are prerequisites for hyperscaler and sovereign/government workloads; they take years and significant capital to earn.
- **Dense interconnection ecosystem** — direct cloud on-ramps (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) plus the AXON cross-connect fabric create network effects: the more carriers, clouds and enterprises present in a facility, the more attractive it is to the next tenant. Once racks, cross-connects and latency-sensitive workloads are installed, moving them imposes meaningful [[switching-costs]].
- **National fleet scale** — 17 sites across every major Australian metro give multi-region resilience and a one-stop national footprint that sub-scale operators cannot match.
- **Capital scale & access** — large committed debt facilities and repeated equity access let NEXTDC fund builds ahead of billing.

The honest counterpoint: this is a **capital-intensive, pre-profit** model. The moat is defensible once capacity is built and tenanted, but the company is structurally dependent on continued access to capital markets and on power/grid approvals it does not fully control.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **AI/cloud capex super-cycle** — demand for GPU-ready capacity is a derivative of hyperscaler and sovereign [[artificial-intelligence|AI]] spending; NEXTDC is the listed ASX proxy and trades with sentiment toward [[nvidia]] and the US hyperscalers.
- **Forward order book conversion** — a 296.8MW contracted-but-not-yet-billing book is expected to convert to billings across FY26–FY29; each tranche turning on is a revenue/EBITDA catalyst.
- **OpenAI sovereign-AI MOU** — the S7 Sydney campus (estimated ~A$7.6B build, up to 650MW) converting from MOU to binding contract is the single largest watch item.
- **Fishermans Bend AI Factory** — a ~A$2B AI Factory and Technology Campus in Melbourne extends the AI-capacity pipeline.
- **Asian expansion** — new-market entries (Kuala Lumpur, Auckland, Tokyo) widen the addressable footprint beyond Australia.
- **Funding capacity** — A$6.4B in debt facilities (Aug 2025) plus subordinated notes underwrite the build pipeline; demonstrated capital access is itself a catalyst that de-risks growth.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- The AI/cloud capex cycle is early and NEXTDC owns the scarcest inputs — powered land, grid connections and certified capacity — in Australia.
- A large contracted forward order book provides multi-year revenue visibility well ahead of recognition.
- The OpenAI MOU and Fishermans Bend point to a step-change in scale (sovereign AI, hundreds of MW) that is not yet in the billing base.
- Demonstrated access to multi-billion-dollar debt and equity funding lets the company build ahead of demand.
- Interconnection density and [[switching-costs]] make tenanted capacity sticky and high-return once live.

### Bear case
- Persistently **loss-making at NPAT** — capex and depreciation run ahead of billings, so P/E and DCF are not meaningful and the equity rests on faith in future cash flows.
- **Serial equity raiser** — repeated capital raises dilute holders; the model only works while capital markets stay open.
- **Long-duration, rate-sensitive** — the value is in cash flows years out, so rising bond yields compress the multiple hard.
- **AI-capex slowdown / cohort risk** — a digestion phase in AI infrastructure would de-rate the whole cohort; see [[ai-capex-vs-cash-flow-divergence]].
- **Execution & power risk** — multi-site, multi-gigawatt builds depend on power availability, grid approvals and on-time delivery; an MOU is not a binding contract.

## Key Risks

- **Funding / dilution dependence** — the build pipeline outruns operating cash flow; continued access to equity and debt is existential, and equity raises dilute.
- **Execution risk** — delivering multiple large builds (including hyperscale/sovereign campuses) on time and on budget is operationally demanding.
- **Power & grid constraints** — securing firm, large-scale electricity and grid connections is the industry's binding constraint; delays cap shippable capacity.
- **AI-capex de-rating** — any slowdown in hyperscaler/sovereign AI spending would compress contracted-MW-based valuations across the cohort.
- **Interest-rate sensitivity** — long-duration cash flows make the stock acutely sensitive to bond yields.
- **Customer concentration** — large wholesale/hyperscale tenants concentrate revenue; loss or delay of a marquee customer matters.
- **MOU-to-contract conversion** — headline agreements (e.g. the OpenAI MOU) must convert to binding, funded contracts to be worth their valuation premium.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

NEXTDC is **pre-profit**, so traditional earnings multiples are not meaningful — the trailing P/E is negative and DCF is heavily assumption-driven. Mechanically, the stock trades on:

- **Contracted megawatts and the forward order book** — the order-book-to-billings gap (296.8MW contracted vs 119.8MW billing at 1H FY2026) is the central valuation driver; the market pays for capacity years ahead of revenue recognition.
- **EBITDA trajectory and funding capacity** — underlying EBITDA growth and demonstrated access to capital (the A$6.4B facilities) underwrite the build-out narrative.
- **AI sentiment and bond yields** — as a long-duration, AI-levered growth name it is acutely sensitive to swings in AI enthusiasm and to interest rates; it carries sentiment beta to [[nvidia]], [[coreweave]] and US hyperscaler capex prints.
- **No dividend** — all cash is reinvested into capacity; there is no income return, so the entire thesis is capital-growth and contracted-pipeline-driven.

## Recent Developments

In FY2025 NEXTDC reported net revenue of approximately A$350.2 million, up 14% year-on-year, with EBITDA of A$216.8 million. The company reaffirmed FY2026 revenue guidance of A$390-400 million and disclosed a sharp increase in contracted utilisation driven by AI infrastructure deployments. The contracted forward order book approaches 300 megawatts of capacity not yet billing, with substantial additional capacity under construction.

In August 2025, NEXTDC secured A$6.4 billion in debt facilities the largest in its history while maintaining pro forma liquidity of A$5.5 billion. The company subsequently appointed Barrenjoey to lead a subordinated notes offering to help fund its Western Sydney and Asian expansion ambitions, and reporting has indicated a mandate for a A$1.8 billion loan tied to regional expansion.

In December 2025, NEXTDC signed a memorandum of understanding with OpenAI to develop sovereign AI infrastructure in Australia, including a hyperscale AI campus and large-scale GPU supercluster at its S7 site in Sydney, with estimated build costs of approximately A$7.6 billion and potential total power capacity of 650 megawatts. NEXTDC also announced a A$2 billion commitment to build an AI Factory and Technology Campus at Fishermans Bend in Melbourne.

**1H FY2026 (half year ended 31 December 2025, reported 25 February 2026)** — record result:

- Total revenue of A$231.8 million; net revenue up 13% to A$189.2 million; underlying EBITDA up 9% to A$115.3 million.
- Contracted utilisation up 137% year on year to 416.6MW against billing utilisation of 119.8MW — a forward order book of 296.8MW expected to convert to billings across FY26–FY29.
- The order-book-to-billings gap is the central valuation driver: the market is paying for contracted capacity years ahead of revenue recognition.

## Leadership

Craig Scroggie has served as Chief Executive Officer since June 2012, having joined from a leadership role in enterprise software and security. Founder Bevan Slattery retired from executive involvement in 2013 but remains an influential figure in the Australian digital infrastructure landscape. The Board oversees a management team with backgrounds in telecommunications, data centre engineering, capital markets and enterprise IT.
