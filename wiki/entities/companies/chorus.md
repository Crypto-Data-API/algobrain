**Chorus Limited** (ASX/NZX: CNU) is New Zealand's dominant wholesale fixed-line fibre network operator — a [[communication-services]]-classified company that functions, in practice, as a **regulated utility**. It owns and operates the backbone of New Zealand's broadband network — including the bulk of the national fibre footprint built under the government's Ultra-Fast Broadband (UFB) programme — and sells access to that network on a wholesale basis to retail telecommunications providers. Dual-listed on the NZX (primary) and the [[asx-200]], Chorus is best understood as a **regulated-asset-base (RAB), bond-like, dividend-focused infrastructure holding**, not a growth telco: its returns are set on a regulated asset base under a price-cap regime, so it trades on EBITDA, RAB, free cash flow and dividend yield rather than on accounting P/E.

## Business Overview & Segments

Chorus's core business is the provision of **wholesale** access to fixed-line broadband, data and voice services over its fibre and copper networks. It does not sell directly to consumers; instead, internet service providers (ISPs) such as Spark, One NZ and 2degrees purchase access products from Chorus and bundle them into retail plans. Chorus is a single-segment, infrastructure-led operator, but its revenue can be split by product line:

| Revenue line | What it is | Trajectory |
|---|---|---|
| **Regulated fibre access** | Wholesale UFB fibre connections sold to ISPs under a price-cap/RAB regime | The core and growth engine — fibre is ~92% of fixed lines, uptake ~72% of addresses passed and rising |
| **Copper-based services** | Legacy copper broadband/voice access | Structural decline — being progressively withdrawn as customers migrate to fibre |
| **Value-added network services** | Physical co-location, installation, wiring, consultancy | Smaller, complementary revenue |

The asset base comprises fibre cables, copper cables, ducts, poles, exchange buildings, cabinets and associated electronics — a hard-to-replicate national network. With the UFB capital build largely complete, the investment narrative is shifting from heavy capex toward **capex roll-off lifting free cash flow and dividends**, while copper-to-fibre migration removes a declining, lower-quality revenue stream.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Chorus has a **wide, regulation-anchored [[economic-moat|moat]]** — among the most durable on the ASX/NZX — because it owns a national fixed-line network that is uneconomic to duplicate:

- **Natural monopoly infrastructure** — building a second nationwide fibre/duct/pole network is prohibitively expensive, so Chorus faces no realistic facilities-based fixed-line competitor across most of its footprint. This is the textbook utility moat.
- **Regulated returns** — under New Zealand's price-cap fibre regime (in force since 2022), Chorus earns a regulated return on a defined regulated asset base (RAB), giving highly predictable, contracted-like cash flows with very high [[switching-costs]] for ISP customers who have no alternative network.
- **UFB concession scarcity** — Chorus won the bulk of the government's Ultra-Fast Broadband deployment contracts; that footprint and the associated regulatory framework are not contestable.
- **Falling marginal cost** — with the build done, incremental connections add high-margin revenue onto a fixed-cost network.

The moat's principal vulnerability is **regulatory rather than competitive**: the Commerce Commission sets the regulated WACC and revenue caps, so the value of the asset is ultimately a policy variable. The other long-term threat is **technological substitution** — fixed-wireless and 5G mobile broadband can erode the bottom end of fixed-line demand. But these are slow-moving risks; on a 5–10 year horizon Chorus's network position is exceptionally secure.

## History

Chorus was formed in 2011 when it was demerged from Telecom New Zealand (now Spark New Zealand) as a condition of Telecom's successful bid for UFB contracts. The demerger structurally separated New Zealand's dominant telecoms network from its retail operations. Chorus was subsequently awarded a large share of UFB deployment contracts and rolled out fibre-to-the-home across most of New Zealand's metropolitan and large regional areas through the 2010s. Since 2022, the company has operated under a price-cap regulatory regime for its fibre products.

## Recent Developments

For the financial year ended 30 June 2025, Chorus reported a resilient result, with revenue broadly stable at NZ$1,014 million (up from NZ$1,010 million), even as copper-related revenues declined by NZ$39 million. Fibre connections grew by 31,000 during the year, and fibre now represents 92% of Chorus's total fixed lines, with fibre uptake at 72.1% of addresses passed. Network data volumes grew 10% over the year to 8,741 petabytes. In June 2025 Chorus upgraded its most popular wholesale residential fibre plan from 300/100 Mbps to 500/100 Mbps at no additional cost for more than 700,000 households.

The most recent filing (annual) was lodged on 2023-06-30. See [[#Financial Reports]] below.

## Competitors / Peer Set

Chorus has no direct fixed-line network competitor across most of New Zealand — its relevant comparators are its wholesale ISP customers, technology substitutes, and other regulated infrastructure assets that trade on the same RAB/yield logic.

| Company | Relationship | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Spark New Zealand | Customer + mobile substitute | Chorus's former parent and largest wholesale customer; also competes via fixed-wireless/5G that can substitute fibre at the low end |
| One NZ / 2degrees | Customers + mobile substitute | Wholesale ISP customers whose mobile/fixed-wireless offerings are the partial substitution threat |
| Regional fibre networks (LFCs) | Footprint peers | Enable Networks, Northpower, Tuatahi — fibre in pockets outside Chorus's UFB area; not national competitors |
| Transurban, [[apa-group\|APA]] | Rate-sensitive infra peers | Australian regulated-infrastructure comparables for RAB/yield valuation, not operational rivals |
| Telstra / TPG (Australia) | Sector reference | Telco-infrastructure reference points for ASX investors |

The competitive frame is therefore unusual: Chorus's "competition" is substitution (mobile/fixed-wireless) and regulation, not a rival network.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Capex roll-off → free cash flow** — with the UFB build largely complete, falling capital expenditure lifts free cash flow and dividend capacity; this is the central re-rating driver.
- **Fibre uptake growth** — connections and uptake (~72% of addresses passed, growing ~31k connections/year) drive revenue onto a fixed-cost network.
- **Copper withdrawal** — retiring the declining copper network removes a low-quality revenue drag and reduces cost.
- **Regulatory resets** — Commerce Commission RAB and revenue-cap/WACC decisions; a favourable regulated WACC directly lifts allowed returns and valuation.
- **Data-demand growth** — network volumes grew 10% to 8,741 petabytes in FY2025; speed upgrades (e.g. 300/100 → 500/100 Mbps for 700k+ households) support value and uptake.
- **Dividend growth / capital management** — rising distributions or capital returns as cash flow improves.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Wide-moat natural-monopoly infrastructure with regulated, predictable, contracted-like cash flows.
- Capex roll-off as the UFB build completes lifts free cash flow and dividends — the structural FCF inflection.
- Fibre uptake still rising and copper drag falling, supporting stable-to-growing regulated revenue.
- Bond-like, defensive, high-yield holding — attractive in a falling-rate environment as a regulated-yield asset.
- A favourable regulated WACC reset would lift allowed returns and re-rate the stock.

### Bear case
- Highly leveraged (D/E 5.15x) — refinancing cost rises in a higher-rate world and squeezes thin reported net income.
- Regulatory risk is asymmetric: an adverse RAB/WACC reset directly cuts allowed returns and valuation.
- Mobile/fixed-wireless and 5G substitution can erode the low end of fixed-line demand over time.
- Bond-proxy sensitivity: rising long-bond yields de-rate the unit price hard.
- Copper-to-fibre transition can leave revenue gaps; NZD/AUD currency drag for ASX holders.

## Key Risks

- **Regulatory resets** — Commerce Commission decisions on the regulated asset base, revenue cap and WACC are the single biggest swing factor; an adverse reset cuts allowed returns.
- **Interest-rate / refinancing** — as a leveraged regulated-yield asset, the price is highly sensitive to long-bond yields, and high gearing raises refinancing cost in a higher-rate world.
- **Technological substitution** — 5G and fixed-wireless broadband eroding fixed-line demand at the margin.
- **Copper-to-fibre transition** — revenue gaps as copper is withdrawn faster than fibre uptake fills them.
- **Currency** — NZD/AUD translation risk for ASX-listed holders.
- **Thin accounting earnings** — heavy depreciation leaves net income (and headline P/E) almost meaningless; investors must value on EBITDA/RAB/FCF, which can mislead the unwary.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Chorus is a **regulated-utility / RAB infrastructure asset** and must be valued accordingly — on EBITDA, the regulated asset base, free cash flow and dividend yield, **not** on its accounting P/E. The headline ~1,000x+ P/E and very high D/E (5.15x) are normal artefacts of a capital-intensive regulated network where reported net income is thin after heavy depreciation. Mechanically:

- **EBITDA / RAB multiple** — the core valuation anchor; the stock is priced as a multiple of regulated cash earnings and the value of its regulated asset base, like other utilities (e.g. [[apa-group]], Transurban).
- **Dividend yield** — Chorus is a yield play; the distribution and its growth (funded by free cash flow as capex rolls off) are the primary return. Yield versus the [[new-zealand|NZ]]/AU long bond is what income investors watch.
- **Regulated WACC** — the allowed regulated return is effectively the discount rate on the asset; Commerce Commission resets move both earnings and the multiple.
- **Bond-proxy / rate sensitivity** — as a leveraged regulated-yield asset, the price is highly sensitive to long-bond yields and behaves like a low-beta bond proxy; it tends to rally on rate cuts and fall on yield spikes.
- **Free-cash-flow inflection** — the key forward driver is capex roll-off lifting FCF (FCF rose to NZ$162M-equivalent in FY2025), which underpins dividend growth.
- **Index / currency** — NZX-primary, [[asx-200]] secondary listing; ASX holders carry NZD/AUD currency exposure.
