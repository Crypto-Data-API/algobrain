**HomeCo Daily Needs REIT** (ASX: HDN) is an Australian retail [[real-estate|Real Estate]] Investment Trust ([[reits|REIT]]) listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index, focused on convenience-based retail property across Neighbourhood Retail, Large Format Retail, and Health & Services. It is externally managed by [[hmc-capital|HMC Capital]], an ASX-listed alternative asset manager, and invests in metro-weighted centres anchored by Woolworths, Coles, and Wesfarmers banners (Bunnings, Kmart, Target) that service essential, everyday consumer needs — making it one of the more defensive, distribution-led names in the [[australian-investing|A-REIT]] sector. See peers [[charter-hall-retail-reit]], [[bwp-trust]] and [[region-group]].

## Business Model & Segments

HDN owns and manages a portfolio of more than 50 convenience-based shopping centres across five Australian states. The geographic distribution includes centres in New South Wales, Queensland, South Australia, Victoria, and Western Australia, with a heavy skew toward metropolitan markets. Approximately 80% of the portfolio sits in metro catchments, and centres are typically anchored by major supermarket or retail brands.

The trust's three largest tenants are Woolworths, Wesfarmers (Bunnings, Kmart, Target), and Coles. The model relies on long-dated leases to national anchor tenants together with specialty tenants covering food, health services, pharmacy, and services retail. The portfolio's convenience orientation is designed to produce stable occupancy and recurring cash flow that supports regular unitholder distributions, generally paid in February, May, August, and November.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

HDN's [[economic-moat|moat]] is **moderate** — the durability comes from the *nature of the income*, not from irreplaceable assets. Convenience and daily-needs retail property is ultimately replicable, and a competitor with capital can build or buy comparable centres, so HDN does not enjoy the kind of structural lock-in that protects a tollroad or a CUDA-class software ecosystem. The advantages it does hold are reinforcing rather than singular:

- **Non-discretionary income base** — supermarket-anchored, daily-needs tenancies produce footfall and rent that are far less cyclical than discretionary mall income, which compresses the volatility of cash flow through the consumer cycle.
- **Metro land bank with development optionality** — ~80% metro weighting means many centres sit on valuable, well-located land with mixed-use, residential and higher-and-better-use redevelopment potential that can be unlocked over time.
- **Scale and platform via the manager** — external management by [[hmc-capital|HMC Capital]] provides deal flow, capital-markets access and asset-management capability that a standalone vehicle of this size would struggle to replicate.
- **Anchor-tenant quality** — leases to Woolworths, Wesfarmers (Bunnings, Kmart) and Coles are investment-grade-adjacent covenants with long weighted-average lease terms, underpinning occupancy above 99%.

The moat narrows on the large-format side, where Bunnings/big-box income is more discretionary and more contestable than pure supermarket convenience.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Development & mixed-use pipeline** — redevelopment, expansion and higher-and-better-use (residential/mixed-use) of metro land provides an organic, value-accretive growth lever beyond rent collection.
- **Rental growth** — CPI-linked and fixed escalations plus positive leasing reversion drove net rental growth of **+4.6%** across the portfolio in 2H 2025 (S&P commentary), supporting FFO.
- **Revaluation gains on a falling rate path** — cap-rate compression as rates ease lifts NTA; the December 2025 revaluation added **+A$219M gross / +A$143M net (+4.5%)**, taking NTA from A$1.47 to A$1.55 per unit.
- **Accretive acquisitions via the manager** — [[hmc-capital|HMC Capital]] can source and fund bolt-on convenience/large-format assets to grow scale and FFO per unit.
- **Refinancing & hedging** — the A$810M debt refinancing and ~70% of debt hedged to December 2026 lock in funding cost and protect distributable income against further rate moves.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Daily-needs, supermarket-anchored income is genuinely defensive — occupancy above 99% and net rental growth of +4.6% hold up through the consumer cycle.
- Falling rates re-rate the whole A-REIT complex: cap-rate compression lifts NTA (Dec-25 revaluation already added +A$219M gross) and supports the unit price.
- Metro land bank carries embedded development/mixed-use optionality not fully reflected in book value.
- A trading discount to NTA (P/B ~0.83) offers margin of safety for an income buyer collecting a reaffirmed 8.6c distribution.

### Bear case
- A REIT is a leveraged bond proxy — if long rates back up, cap rates re-expand, NTA falls and the rate-sensitive unit price de-rates.
- Large-format / big-box income is more discretionary and contestable than the pure supermarket core.
- External management by [[hmc-capital|HMC Capital]] introduces fee leakage and potential conflicts versus an internally managed REIT.
- Tenant concentration in Woolworths/Wesfarmers/Coles means anchor-renewal terms swing the portfolio.
- Limited franking on distributions reduces the after-tax appeal versus fully franked income alternatives.

## Key Risks

- **Rate / cap-rate sensitivity** — the dominant driver; rising long yields lift cap rates, cut NTA and de-rate the unit. ~70% of debt is hedged to Dec-2026, which caps but does not eliminate the exposure.
- **Large-format discretionary exposure** — Bunnings/big-box and bulky-goods income is more cyclical than the supermarket-anchored convenience core, softening the "pure non-discretionary" thesis.
- **External-management conflicts** — management fees paid to [[hmc-capital|HMC Capital]] and the usual alignment questions that attach to externally managed REITs.
- **Tenant concentration** — heavy reliance on Woolworths, Wesfarmers and Coles as anchors; adverse renewal or a strategic exit would dent income.
- **Gearing & refinancing** — leverage amplifies revaluation swings, and debt must be rolled at prevailing rates; the A$810M refinance addresses near-term maturities but funding cost remains a watch item.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

HDN is an **income-led A-REIT** — it is valued on distributable cash flow and asset backing rather than on growth multiples. The mechanics that matter:

- **FFO vs statutory profit** — the market watches **funds from operations** (FFO/unit 4.4c in 1H FY26; FY26 guidance 9.0c) and distribution (DPU 4.3c; FY26 guidance 8.6c), not statutory profit, which is dominated by non-cash revaluation movements (1H FY26 statutory profit jumped +108% to A$243.5M largely on revaluation gains).
- **NTA and discount-to-NTA** — net tangible assets sit at **A$1.55 per unit** (up from A$1.47), and the units typically trade at a discount to NTA (P/B ~0.83), the standard frame for whether the market is pricing the property book above or below carrying value.
- **Cap-rate / rate sensitivity** — as a leveraged property trust HDN behaves like a bond proxy: NTA and the unit price move inversely with AU long yields via the cap rate. ~70% of debt hedged to Dec-2026 dampens the income hit from rate moves.
- **Distribution yield + low franking** — the appeal is the recurring distribution; franking is low (≈0.03 per the fundamentals block), so the after-tax yield benefit for AU residents is smaller than for fully franked names.
- **How it trades** — moves with the A-REIT index (XPJ) and inversely with the AU 10-year yield; moderate liquidity and no meaningful single-stock options market, so exposure is taken as cash equity or via A-REIT sector ETFs.

## History

HomeCo Daily Needs REIT traces its origins to Home Consortium Limited (HomeCo), founded in 2017 by a group of Australian investors. A foundational event was the 2016 acquisition of Hydrox Holdings Pty Limited - the holding company for the Masters Home Improvement property portfolio - which provided more than 700,000 square metres of large-format retail space in a reported $725 million transaction. That real estate footprint underpinned the creation of HomeCo and its subsequent spin-off structures.

HDN was listed on the ASX in November 2020 via a $300 million initial public offering, entering the market with approximately $844 million of assets. It operates independently of Home Consortium Limited as of 31 December 2020, with [[hmc-capital|HMC Capital]] acting as manager.

In October 2021, HMC Capital announced a $2.2 billion merger deal with Aventus Group, a large-format retail landlord. The merger was completed in February 2022, with Aventus subsequently delisted from the ASX. The transaction materially expanded HDN's large-format retail exposure and scale.

## Recent Developments

For the half year ended 31 December 2025 (1H FY26), HDN reported revenue from ordinary activities of $190.4 million, up 6% year on year. Statutory profit after tax increased 108% to $243.5 million, driven by revaluation gains and improved contributions from equity-accounted investments. Funds from operations (FFO) per unit was 4.4 cents (up from 4.3 cents), and distribution per unit (DPU) was unchanged at 4.3 cents.

The December 2025 asset revaluations delivered a $219 million gross uplift ($143 million net) on the June 2025 portfolio value, representing a 4.5% increase. Net tangible assets per unit rose from $1.47 at 30 June 2025 to $1.55. The trust also completed an $810 million debt refinancing and reported that approximately 70% of debt is hedged until December 2026.

Management reaffirmed full-year FY26 guidance of 9.0 cents FFO per unit and 8.6 cents DPU. The trust declared two interim distributions of 2.15 cents per unit each for the 2026 financial year.
