The Cooper Companies (NASDAQ: COO) is a global medical-device maker built on two franchises: CooperVision, the world's #2 soft contact lens manufacturer, and CooperSurgical, a leader in fertility and women's health. A [[s-and-p-500]] constituent in the [[healthcare|Health Care]] sector, it matters to traders as a formerly premium-multiple quality compounder whose stock de-rated sharply through 2025 on slowing lens-fitting growth, prompting a formal strategic review announced in December 2025.

## Business Overview & Segments

The Cooper Companies is a [[healthcare|Health Care]] device maker that, despite its single corporate roof, is really two distinct franchises with little operational overlap: **CooperVision** in soft contact lenses and **CooperSurgical** in fertility and women's health. Roughly two-thirds of revenue comes from CooperVision and one-third from CooperSurgical. The split-business structure is precisely why the December 2025 strategic review matters — the two units could plausibly be valued, financed, and owned separately.

| Segment | What it is | Revenue / role |
|---|---|---|
| **CooperVision** | Soft contact lenses — daily silicone hydrogels (**MyDay**, **clariti**), monthly toric/multifocal (**Biofinity**), and **MiSight**, the first FDA-approved myopia-control lens for children | ~2/3 of revenue; the core franchise and the long-duration growth story |
| **CooperSurgical** | Fertility (IVF media, genomics, donor services), labor & delivery devices, and contraception (**Paragard** IUD) plus broader women's-health products | ~1/3 of revenue; structural-growth category, but lumpy quarter to quarter |

CooperVision competes in a rational four-player contact-lens oligopoly (see [[#Economic Moat & Competitive Position|moat section]]) with strong recurring-revenue characteristics — lenses are a consumable patients re-order on a fixed cadence. MiSight is the strategic prize: pediatric myopia control is an under-penetrated, clinically validated category with a multi-decade demand tail as global myopia prevalence rises. CooperSurgical rides the secular growth of assisted reproduction but is exposed to clinic-cycle volumes and reimbursement.

Two data caveats on the [[#Fundamentals|fundamentals]] below: the 42.4% ROE in FY2021 and the ~28% 5-year-average net margin are inflated by a large one-time non-cash tax benefit booked that year — underlying net margins run high-single to low-double digits. Separately, a **4-for-1 stock split (February 2024)** rebased all per-share figures, so pre- and post-split EPS and price levels are not directly comparable.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Cooper's [[economic-moat|economic moat]] is narrow-to-wide depending on the segment, and rests on several reinforcing layers:

- **Oligopoly structure** — the global soft-contact-lens market is a rational four-player oligopoly (CooperVision, [[johnson-and-johnson]] Vision, [[alcon]], [[bausch-lomb]]). Capital intensity, manufacturing know-how, and regulatory approval requirements keep new entrants out; pricing discipline is generally constructive.
- **Recurring revenue & fitting stickiness** — lenses are a consumable with high reorder rates, and patients face real [[switching-costs]]: once an eye-care professional fits a patient to a specific lens geometry and material, both patient and practitioner are reluctant to re-fit. The prescription anchors demand to the incumbent brand.
- **Regulatory / clinical barriers** — MiSight is FDA-approved for myopia control in children, a status earned through multi-year clinical trials. That approval is a durable barrier rivals must replicate trial-by-trial.
- **Fertility scale** — CooperSurgical has assembled scale in IVF consumables and genomics through acquisition, giving it breadth across the fertility workflow that smaller players cannot match.

The moat is real but not impregnable: contact lenses are mature in developed markets, growth depends on mix (toric/multifocal/myopia) and new fittings, and the fertility business is more fragmented and cyclical than the lens franchise. The bear case is that slowing US lens-fitting activity exposes how cyclical the "compounder" really is.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Myopia control (MiSight)** — the single most important long-duration driver; rising global myopia prevalence plus an FDA-approved, clinically backed product creates a large, under-penetrated TAM.
- **MyDay capacity expansion** — daily silicone-hydrogel lenses are the fastest-growing lens segment; adding manufacturing capacity directly unlocks revenue.
- **Mix shift** — toric and multifocal lenses carry higher value per wearer than spherical dailies; continued premiumization lifts revenue per fitting.
- **Fertility structural growth** — assisted reproduction is a secular-growth category; CooperSurgical's IVF and genomics franchise rides that tailwind when clinic volumes cooperate.
- **Strategic-review outcome** — a potential CooperSurgical separation or other structural action is the dominant near-term catalyst and the source of break-up/value-unlock interest.
- **FX** — a large ex-US revenue share makes the dollar a swing factor on reported growth.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly earnings (October fiscal year), strategic-review updates, myopia-category data, and fertility-cycle volume reads.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Myopia control (MiSight) is an early-innings, FDA-validated category with a multi-decade demand tail as global myopia rises.
- Recurring-revenue lens economics plus fitting stickiness make the core franchise resilient and cash-generative.
- The strategic review can unlock value — separating CooperSurgical could let the market value two cleaner stories than one conglomerate discount.
- The GAAP–adjusted EPS gap (FY2025 GAAP $1.87 vs non-GAAP $4.13) means underlying earnings power is materially understated by the headline figure.
- A de-rated multiple on a historically premium compounder offers re-rating upside if growth reaccelerates.

### Bear case
- US lens-fitting activity slowed through 2025 — the "compounder" looks more cyclical than the premium multiple assumed.
- Channel-inventory caution and fertility softness can compound a top-line slowdown.
- The fertility business is lumpy and lower-quality than the lens franchise; scale has not translated cleanly into margin.
- The large GAAP-to-adjusted EPS gap cuts both ways — it can flag aggressive adjustments rather than understated earnings.
- A strategic review is uncertain: a separation may not materialize, or may not unlock the value bulls expect.

## Key Risks

- **Soft US lens-fitting** — the primary 2025 fundamental drag; new fittings drive future recurring revenue, so a slowdown compounds.
- **Channel inventory** — distributor/retail destocking can mask or amplify end-demand trends quarter to quarter.
- **Fertility softness** — CooperSurgical volumes are cyclical and reimbursement-sensitive; the category's lumpiness adds earnings volatility.
- **FX** — large ex-US revenue exposure means a stronger dollar pressures reported growth and margins.
- **Strategic-review execution** — the value-unlock thesis depends on the board actually delivering a separation or other action on acceptable terms.
- **Competition** — share pressure from [[johnson-and-johnson]] Vision, [[alcon]], and [[bausch-lomb]] in dailies and specialty lenses, including in the emerging myopia-control category.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Cooper trades as a formerly premium-multiple quality compounder that **de-rated sharply through 2025** as lens-fitting growth slowed — moving from a defensive-growth medtech to an event-driven situation. Mechanically:

- **GAAP vs adjusted EPS gap** — the central valuation debate. FY2025 GAAP diluted EPS was **$1.87** while non-GAAP EPS was **$4.13** (per the company's FY2025 results); which figure you anchor to drives wildly different multiples, so the stock is unusually sensitive to how the market treats the adjustments.
- **No dividend** — Cooper pays no meaningful dividend; capital return runs through buybacks, so total return is earnings-growth-and-multiple driven, not yield-driven.
- **Event-driven on the strategic review** — the December 2025 review makes COO a candidate for break-up/value-unlock trades; headlines on a potential CooperSurgical separation move the stock more than routine prints.
- **Medtech beta** — COO trades with broader specialty-medtech risk appetite alongside names like [[align-technology]], [[stryker]], and [[abbott-laboratories]]; it is held across healthcare (XLV) and medical-device (IHI) ETFs as an [[s-and-p-500]] constituent.
- **Liquidity** — moderate options liquidity; earnings (October fiscal year) and review updates are the recurring implied-move catalysts.
