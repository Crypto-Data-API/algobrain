Copart (NASDAQ: CPRT) is the world's largest online salvage-vehicle auction marketplace, selling totaled and damaged cars on behalf of insurance companies to dismantlers, rebuilders and exporters through its VB3 virtual-bidding platform. A [[s-and-p-500]] and Nasdaq-100 constituent in the [[industrials|Industrials]] sector (with end-market exposure adjacent to [[consumer-discretionary]] auto), it is a textbook quality compounder: debt-free, ~34% net margins, and protected by one of the cleaner wide moats in the market — two-sided [[network-effects]] over an irreplaceable land bank inside an effective duopoly. Its stock nonetheless nearly halved over the year to mid-2026 on insurance-volume softness and a de-rating of a previously premium multiple, turning a serial outperformer into a closely watched quality-at-a-discount debate. See [[economic-moat]] for the moat framework and [[network-effects]] for the marketplace dynamics that anchor the bull case.

## Business Overview & Segments

Copart operates 250+ storage/auction facilities across 11 countries, with the US dominant. **Sellers** are mostly auto insurers consigning total-loss vehicles (plus banks, fleets, dealers, rental/fleet operators and charities); **buyers** are dismantlers, rebuilders, used-car dealers and a large international export base bidding online. Revenue is overwhelmingly **service fees from both sides of each auction** rather than the value of the cars themselves, which is what gives the model its asset-light, high-margin, capital-light economics. The long-term tailwind is **rising total-loss frequency**: [[adas]] sensors, cameras and EV battery/repair complexity raise the cost to repair, so insurers write off damaged vehicles at lower and lower damage thresholds — structurally expanding the salvage pool that flows through Copart's lanes. Fiscal year ends 31 July.

Copart does not report rich product-line segmentation — it operates as essentially one global salvage-auction marketplace — but the economics break down along the following lines:

| Line / driver | What it is | Role in the model |
|---|---|---|
| **Service / agency fees (sellers)** | Consignment, listing, storage, towing, title and processing fees charged to insurers and other sellers per vehicle | Core, recurring, high-margin; scales with assignment volume |
| **Service / buyer fees** | Bidding, gate, transaction and payment fees charged to the global buyer base | Second leg of the two-sided take; rises with auction participation and ASPs |
| **Purchased-vehicle / principal** | Vehicles Copart buys outright (e.g. via "Copart Direct" / non-insurance channels) and resells | Smaller, lower-margin, more capital-intensive; adds reach but dilutes the pure-fee margin |
| **VB3 platform / technology** | The proprietary virtual-bidding auction engine and member tools | The distribution rail; lets a US salvage car attract a buyer in West Africa, Eastern Europe or the Middle East |
| **International** | UK, Germany, Brazil, Spain, UAE/Middle East and other markets | The growth frontier — replicating the US land-and-network playbook abroad |

The economic engine is the **service-fee model layered over a two-sided marketplace**: Copart rarely takes vehicle inventory risk, instead clipping fees on volume that flows through its yards, which is what produces ~40%+ EBITDA margins and ROIC well above its cost of capital.

## Economic Moat

Copart sits behind one of the cleaner **wide moats** in the [[industrials|Industrials]] complex (see [[economic-moat]]). Unlike a commodity producer that is a price-taker, the salvage-auction franchise compounds several reinforcing, hard-to-replicate advantages:

- **Two-sided [[network-effects]]** — the defining moat. More salvage supply (insurer consignment) attracts more global buyers; a larger, more competitive buyer pool produces higher sale prices and faster liquidation, which makes Copart the most attractive place for insurers to send the next total-loss vehicle. The flywheel feeds itself and gets harder to dislodge with scale.
- **Irreplaceable land bank** — 250+ permitted storage/auction yards, many on large parcels near major metropolitan areas. This real estate is nearly impossible to recreate: NIMBY zoning, environmental permitting and the sheer acreage required mean a new entrant cannot simply buy comparable land near demand centres. The yards also provide **catastrophe surge capacity** — when a hurricane or hailstorm produces tens of thousands of total losses at once, only an operator with idle land can absorb the spike, which is precisely when insurers most need the service.
- **Effective duopoly** — Copart and IAA (owned by [[rb-global]]) together dominate US salvage auctions. A consolidated two-player structure supports pricing discipline and makes share gains a grind rather than a price war.
- **Switching costs / insurer integration** — Copart's systems are wired into insurers' total-loss workflows (claims integration, title processing, settlement). Re-plumbing that integration and retraining adjusters is friction that protects the installed base.
- **Asset-light, fee-based economics** — because revenue is mostly service fees rather than vehicle cost-of-goods, incremental volume drops to the bottom line at very high margins, funding both the land bank and buybacks.

The honest caveat: the moat protects *share and margin*, not *volume*. Assignment volume is set by accident frequency, miles driven, insurer behaviour and total-loss thresholds — exogenous variables Copart does not control. The 2025–2026 episode is a live demonstration that a wide moat does not immunise the business against a cyclical and behavioural softening in the *number* of cars flowing through the lanes.

## 2025–2026 Developments

- **Q2 FY2026 (quarter ended 31 Jan 2026):** revenue ~$1.1B, **down 3.6%** YoY; net income $350.7M (-9.5%); diluted EPS $0.36 vs $0.40 — a rare contraction, driven by softer insurance-unit volumes and lower purchased-vehicle revenue.
- **Q3 FY2026 (quarter ended 30 Apr 2026):** stabilisation — revenue ~$1.2B (+2.1%), gross profit +3.7%, diluted EPS $0.43 (+2.4%).
- **Stock drawdown:** shares fell roughly **47% over the 52 weeks to April 2026** (trading near $33 split-adjusted), on consecutive earnings misses, declining insurance assignment volumes, fee-increase pushback from insurers, and de-rating of the previously premium multiple (~35x+ earnings into the drawdown).
- Counterpoints in the bull case: fortress balance sheet (effectively zero debt, current ratio >8x per the [[#Fundamentals|fundamentals]]), continued international growth, and the structural total-loss-frequency tailwind.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Rising total-loss frequency** — the structural engine. As [[adas]] systems, cameras and EV battery packs raise repair costs, more accidents end in a total loss, expanding the salvage pool Copart monetises regardless of overall accident counts.
- **International expansion** — replicating the US land-and-network model in the UK, Germany, Brazil, Spain and the Middle East is the longest-dated growth lever and the main offset to mature-US-volume sensitivity.
- **Catastrophe (CAT) events** — hurricanes, floods and hailstorms produce surges of total-loss vehicles; CAT volumes both lift near-term assignments and *showcase* the land-bank moat that competitors cannot match.
- **Used-vehicle prices / ASPs** — the Manheim used-vehicle value index drives auction sale prices and therefore the fee base; firmer used-car prices lift revenue per unit.
- **Buyer-base globalisation** — deeper international demand (export buyers) raises realised prices on US salvage, improving the value proposition to insurer sellers and reinforcing the network flywheel.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly earnings (Copart's July fiscal year-end means prints land roughly Sep/Nov/Feb/May), CAT-season headlines, Manheim index releases, insurer total-loss-frequency data, and any fee-structure changes.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Wide, durable moat — two-sided [[network-effects]], irreplaceable land, an effective duopoly with IAA, and deep insurer integration that compounds over time.
- Structural total-loss-frequency tailwind expands the addressable salvage pool for years.
- Asset-light, fee-based model throws off ~40%+ EBITDA margins, high ROIC and abundant free cash flow on a debt-free balance sheet.
- International expansion is a long runway that is far from mature.
- The 2025–2026 de-rating offers a rare chance to own a serial compounder at a much lower multiple than its history.

### Bear case
- Assignment **volume** — not share — is softening; the moat protects margin, not the number of cars flowing through the lanes, and volume is set by exogenous insurer/driving factors.
- Insurers are pushing back on fee increases, capping the per-unit pricing lever just as volumes wobble.
- The stock was priced for perpetual compounding (~35x+ earnings into the drawdown); even after de-rating it is not statistically cheap, so further volume disappointment can keep compressing the multiple.
- Long-term EV/repair-technology trends are double-edged: if repairability improves or accident frequency falls (better ADAS *preventing* crashes), the total-loss pool could grow more slowly than bulls assume.
- Crowding in quality/compounder factor portfolios amplified the drawdown and can do so again.

## Key Risks

- **Insurance assignment-volume softness** — the dominant near-term risk; fewer total-loss consignments hit revenue directly and de-rate the multiple simultaneously.
- **Fee-increase pushback** — insurers are large, concentrated counterparties; resistance to fee hikes caps the pricing lever.
- **Used-vehicle price cyclicality** — falling Manheim values pull down auction ASPs and the fee base.
- **Premium-multiple de-rating** — a quality stock priced for compounding is vulnerable to sharp re-rating on any growth wobble, as 2025–2026 showed.
- **EV / repair-trend uncertainty** — the total-loss-frequency thesis depends on repair costs staying high; shifts in vehicle repairability, ADAS-driven accident *prevention*, or insurer behaviour could slow the structural tailwind.
- **Concentration / regulatory** — heavy reliance on a handful of large insurer sellers; any change in their total-loss policy, plus zoning/environmental scrutiny of yards, are tail risks.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Copart has historically traded as a **premium, low-volatility quality compounder** — a debt-free, high-ROIC marketplace that the market was happy to pay 30–40x earnings for on the strength of its moat and steady growth. The 2025–2026 episode broke that pattern: shares fell roughly 47% over the 52 weeks to April 2026 on consecutive volume-driven earnings disappointments, compressing the multiple toward the high-teens/low-20s and turning the stock into a **quality-at-a-discount** debate rather than a buy-at-any-price compounder. Mechanically:

- **No dividend** — capital return is via buybacks and reinvestment; the thesis is total-return compounding, not yield.
- **Fortress balance sheet** — effectively zero debt, current ratio >8x (see the [[#Fundamentals|fundamentals]] block) — so solvency is a non-issue and the business can invest and repurchase through any downcycle.
- **Lower beta than the megacaps, but now carries event risk** — once a sleepy low-vol name, CPRT now gaps on quarterly prints because the volume question is unresolved; earnings have become the key dating mechanism.
- **Options** — adequate but modest liquidity versus a megacap; the de-rating has widened the realised-vol regime relative to its calm history.
- **Valuation framing** — best judged on quality-adjusted earnings/FCF and the trajectory of assignment volumes and international growth, not a single trailing multiple; the bull case is that the moat justifies re-rating once volumes stabilise (as the Q3 FY2026 print tentatively suggested).
