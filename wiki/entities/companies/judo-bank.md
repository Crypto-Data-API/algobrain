Judo Bank (ASX: JDO) is Australia's first purpose-built SME (small and medium enterprise) bank, designed to challenge the Big Four's dominance in business lending. Founded in 2016 by Joseph Healy and David Hornery — both former senior executives at [[nab|NAB]] — Judo was built around the thesis that Big Four banks had abandoned relationship-based business lending in favour of centralised, algorithm-driven credit models that poorly served SMEs.

## Overview

Judo Bank does not offer retail banking, share trading, or investment services. It is purely a business bank — accepting business deposits and making business loans. Its differentiation is the old-school relationship banking model: dedicated business bankers who visit clients, understand their businesses, and make lending decisions based on business fundamentals rather than purely automated credit scoring.

Judo listed on the ASX in November 2021 at $2.36 per share in one of the most high-profile fintech IPOs in Australian history. The share price subsequently declined significantly as rising interest rates, tightening credit conditions, and the broader fintech de-rating weighed on the stock.

## History

- **2016**: Founded by Joseph Healy (ex-NAB Head of Business Banking) and David Hornery (ex-NAB)
- **2018**: Received full banking licence from [[apra|APRA]] — one of the first new bank licences granted under APRA's new restricted licensing framework
- **2019**: Rapid loan book growth — filled a genuine gap in SME lending that Big Four banks had neglected
- **2020**: COVID-19 stress tested the SME lending book — government guarantee schemes (SME Guarantee Scheme) supported lending volumes
- **2021**: IPO on the ASX at $2.36 per share. Raised ~$560 million. Significant institutional interest including Myer Family Investments, OPTrust (Canadian pension fund), and Abu Dhabi sovereign wealth fund
- **2022-2023**: Share price declined significantly from IPO level — affected by rising interest rates, tightening credit conditions, and general market de-rating of fintech/growth stocks
- **2024**: Continued building loan book, path to sustainable profitability remains a key investor focus. Market cap ~AUD $1-2B

## Business Model

### What Judo Does
- **Business lending**: Secured and unsecured business loans to Australian SMEs (typically $250K-$25M)
- **Business deposits**: Term deposits and at-call deposits from business customers
- **Relationship banking**: Each business client has a dedicated banker who understands their industry and makes lending decisions with local market knowledge
- **Property-secured and cashflow lending**: Lends against commercial property, residential property (investment), business assets, and cashflow

### What Judo Does NOT Do
- No retail banking (no personal accounts, home loans, credit cards)
- No share trading or brokerage
- No [[superannuation]] products
- No investment platform
- No consumer products of any kind

### The SME Lending Gap

Judo was founded on the observation that Big Four banks had systematically under-served SME borrowers:

- Big Four banks centralised business lending decisions — moving away from relationship managers who understood local businesses
- Automated credit models favoured large corporates and asset-backed lending over cashflow-based SME lending
- SME customers faced long approval times, impersonal service, and frequent banker turnover
- Post-Royal Commission regulatory burden made Big Four banks even more risk-averse in business lending

Judo aimed to fill this gap with experienced bankers (many poached from Big Four banks) who could assess SME credit risk based on business fundamentals rather than just property collateral.

## Business Segments

Judo is a deliberately **monoline** bank — there is no retail, wealth, markets or international division. Its profit is a single spread business with two complementary funding pillars:

| Segment | What it is | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| **SME lending (the loan book)** | Secured and unsecured business loans to Australian SMEs, typically A$250K–A$25M | The growth engine; relationship-banker originated, priced for the cashflow/relationship niche |
| **Business deposits** | Term deposits and at-call deposits from business and retail savers | Funding pillar; competitive rates needed to attract deposits without a Big-Four branch network |
| **Wholesale / warehouse funding** | Securitisation warehouses and wholesale facilities | Supplements deposits; more rate-sensitive and a key structural dependency |

Because the model is a single business line, Judo's earnings are an unusually pure read on **SME loan-book growth × net interest margin − funding cost − credit losses − operating cost**. There are no other divisions to smooth the cycle.

## Competitive Positioning & Peers

Judo competes head-on with the [[australian-banks-overview|Big Four]]'s business-banking arms — most directly with [[nab|NAB]], the traditional SME-banking leader and the alma mater of Judo's founders. Unlike a regional such as [[bendigo-adelaide-bank|Bendigo]], Judo is a **focused, pure-play challenger**, not a scaled-down universal bank.

| Bank | Ticker | Tier | Relationship to Judo |
|------|--------|------|----------------------|
| **Judo Bank** | JDO | Challenger | Pure-play SME relationship lender; the disruptor |
| [[nab\|NAB]] | NAB | Big Four | The incumbent SME-banking leader; Judo's founders are ex-NAB |
| [[commonwealth-bank\|CommBank]] | CBA | Big Four | Largest bank; scale business-banking arm |
| [[westpac\|Westpac]] | WBC | Big Four | Big business book |
| [[anz\|ANZ]] | ANZ | Big Four | Business + institutional |
| [[bendigo-adelaide-bank\|Bendigo & Adelaide]] | BEN | Regional | Other listed ASX non-major; diversified, dividend-paying |

**Structural position:** Judo's edge is **relationship-based credit underwriting** — experienced bankers assessing business fundamentals rather than purely automated, property-collateral-driven models. That lets it win SME customers underserved by the majors and charge a relationship premium. The cost of that model is a **deposit-funding disadvantage** (no cheap, sticky transaction-account base like the Big Four), heavier reliance on term and wholesale funding, and the execution risk of scaling a young bank to profitability. Versus [[bendigo-adelaide-bank|Bendigo]], Judo is smaller, higher-growth, higher-risk, and (unlike BEN) pays no dividend.

## Valuation Framework

As a young, growing, monoline bank, Judo is valued on a **different basis from a mature payer like [[bendigo-adelaide-bank|Bendigo]]** — it is part growth story, part bank, so the market weighs scaling potential against capital adequacy and credit quality:

- **Loan-book growth** — the headline driver. Judo's equity story is built on compounding the SME loan book toward scale; the rate and *quality* of growth is the primary valuation lever.
- **Net interest margin (NIM)** — Judo runs a relatively **high NIM (~2.5–3%)** versus the majors, reflecting its niche pricing — but that margin must be defended against funding costs and competition.
- **Cost of funding** — the structural weak point. Without a cheap deposit franchise, Judo leans on term deposits and **wholesale/warehouse funding**, which are more expensive and rate-sensitive; the deposit-to-loan mix is a key metric.
- **Cost-to-income ratio** — as a scaling bank, operating leverage is central: getting cost-to-income down as the book grows is the path to the target ROE.
- **Return on equity (ROE)** — the destination metric. Judo's investment case is that ROE rises toward a sustainable double-digit level as it scales; failure to get there is the core bear argument.
- **Credit quality / impairments** — SME lending is higher-risk and more cyclical than mortgages; the level and trend of arrears and bad-debt charges is critical, especially in a high-rate environment.
- **Capital (CET1) & price-to-book** — like all banks it operates under [[apra|APRA]] capital rules; with no dividend, value accrues through book-value growth, so P/B vs prospective ROE is the relevant frame rather than dividend yield.

In short: Judo is valued on **book-value growth, target ROE and execution-to-scale**, discounted for SME credit risk and funding-cost disadvantage — not on yield.

## For Investors and Traders

Judo is a high-risk, high-conviction bet on disrupting Big Four business banking in Australia.

**Bull case**: Addressing a genuine gap in Australian business banking — SMEs are genuinely underserved by Big Four banks. Experienced founders and management team (ex-NAB senior executives). Growing loan book with strong net interest margins. If Judo reaches sustainable profitability and scale, it could re-rate significantly. The SME banking market is large (~$500B+ in business lending in Australia).

**Bear case**: Share price has declined significantly from IPO — investors who bought at IPO are underwater. Path to sustained profitability has been slower than expected. Rising interest rates increase the risk of SME loan defaults. Judo lacks the deposit base of Big Four banks — more reliant on wholesale and term deposit funding, which is more expensive and rate-sensitive. Small market cap and low liquidity — share price can be volatile. No dividends — purely a capital growth play.

**What Judo tells us about Australian banking**: Even if Judo does not succeed as a listed investment, its existence demonstrates a real structural gap in Australian business banking. The Big Four's centralised credit models and post-Royal Commission risk aversion have created genuine underservice of SME borrowers — a theme that any investor analysing Australian bank stocks should understand.

**Practical trading note**: JDO is a low-liquidity stock. Daily trading volumes are thin compared to Big Four bank stocks. Wide bid-ask spreads are common. This is not a stock for short-term trading — it is a long-term, conviction-driven position. Use limit orders and be prepared for significant price swings on low volume.

## Trading & Options Playbook

- **Index membership:** Judo is a smaller ASX name; depending on its market cap it sits in or around the [[asx-200]] / All Ordinaries — well outside the index's banking heavyweights, so passive sponsorship is modest.
- **Liquidity:** Low liquidity and wide spreads versus the Big Four. Use **limit orders**, expect slippage on size, and treat it as a position (not trading) holding.
- **Options:** Single-stock options are thin to non-existent compared with the deep CBA/NAB/WBC/ANZ option markets; the practical expression is the underlying.
- **No franking benefit:** With **no dividend**, there are no [[franking-credits|franking credits]] — so it offers none of the grossed-up-yield appeal that draws [[smsf|SMSF]] and income investors to [[bendigo-adelaide-bank|Bendigo]] or the majors. This is a pure capital-growth bet.
- **ADR / offshore:** No major US listing/ADR; offshore holders access it directly on the ASX. The register includes notable institutions (e.g. OPTrust, Abu Dhabi sovereign wealth) from the IPO.
- **Catalyst watch:** half-year/full-year results (loan-book growth, NIM, cost-to-income, ROE trajectory, arrears), RBA rate decisions (funding cost and SME-default sensitivity), and Australian SME/business-credit conditions.
- **Sector pairing:** Often considered alongside [[bendigo-adelaide-bank|BEN]] as the two listed ASX non-major banks, or traded as a relative-value/disruption bet against the Big Four business-banking incumbents.

## Capital Return

Judo currently returns **no capital to shareholders** — it pays **no dividend** (0% yield) and is reinvesting all earnings into growing the loan book and reaching scale. Implications:

- **Pure growth/capital-appreciation play** — total return depends entirely on share-price re-rating as profitability improves, not on income.
- **No franking appeal** — unlike the dividend-paying majors and [[bendigo-adelaide-bank|Bendigo]], it offers no [[franking-credits|franked]] income, so it is unattractive to yield-focused and [[smsf|SMSF]] income investors.
- **Future optionality** — should Judo reach sustainable target-ROE profitability and build surplus CET1 capital, the initiation of a dividend would itself be a re-rating catalyst; until then capital is retained.

## Risks

- **Path-to-profitability / execution risk** — the central risk; reaching sustainable target ROE has been slower than the IPO thesis implied, and the model must scale to work.
- **Funding-cost / liquidity risk** — heavy reliance on term and wholesale/warehouse funding (no cheap Big-Four deposit base) makes margins rate-sensitive and funding access a structural vulnerability.
- **Credit risk** — SME lending is higher-risk and more cyclical than mortgages; a young, fast-grown book is unseasoned, and rising rates raise default risk.
- **Competition** — the Big Four (especially [[nab|NAB]]) can defend or reclaim SME share with superior funding and scale.
- **Liquidity / volatility** — small market cap, thin trading, wide spreads and large price swings on low volume.
- **No dividend** — no income cushion; total return is entirely capital-appreciation-dependent and IPO buyers are underwater.
- **Regulatory / capital risk** — subject to [[apra|APRA]] capital and prudential requirements that constrain growth and gearing.
