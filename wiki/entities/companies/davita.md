DaVita (NYSE: DVA) is one of the two companies (with Fresenius Medical Care) that dominate US kidney dialysis, treating ~200,000 patients across roughly 2,600 US outpatient centers plus international operations. An [[s-and-p-500]] constituent and long-time [[berkshire-hathaway]] holding, it trades as a leveraged cash-flow/buyback story: negative book equity from relentless repurchases makes the headline D/E (75x) meaningless — watch interest coverage and free cash flow instead.

## Business Overview & Segments

DaVita is, in effect, **half of a regulated kidney-dialysis duopoly** — with Fresenius Medical Care, it dominates U.S. outpatient dialysis. Revenue is overwhelmingly outpatient hemodialysis delivered across roughly 2,600 U.S. centers (plus international and adjacent services) in the [[healthcare|Health Care]] services space.

| Business line | What it is | Role (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **U.S. dialysis** | Outpatient hemodialysis across ~2,600 centers treating ~200,000 patients | The overwhelming majority of revenue and profit |
| **Integrated Kidney Care (IKC)** | Value-based / capitated contracts managing total cost of kidney care | Strategic growth bet; shifts from fee-for-service toward outcomes |
| **Hospital / acute dialysis** | Inpatient dialysis services contracted to hospitals | Smaller, complementary volume |
| **International** | Dialysis clinics outside the U.S. | Diversification away from U.S. reimbursement |

The economics hinge on **payer mix**: roughly 90% of patients are government-reimbursed (Medicare/Medicaid) at near-breakeven rates, while the ~10% with **commercial insurance** generate effectively all the profit. The duopoly with Fresenius creates stable pricing but deep regulatory entanglement — CMS sets the Medicare ESRD **bundled rate** annually, the single most important external variable. DaVita returns nearly all free cash flow via **buybacks**; share count has roughly halved over a decade, driving the headline equity below zero — which is why the reported ROE and 75x D/E in the fundamentals are meaningless. Read interest coverage (~5x), free cash flow, and the ~$8B+ absolute debt load instead.

> **Clinical/healthcare caveat:** DaVita treats medically fragile end-stage-renal-disease patients; treatment volumes are sensitive to patient mortality (flu seasons, the pandemic), and reimbursement is set by government policy. The long-run GLP-1 thesis — that anti-obesity/diabetes drugs slow chronic-kidney-disease progression and shrink the dialysis population — is genuinely unsettled and long-dated. Nothing here is clinical or investment advice.

## 2025-2026 Developments

- **Ransomware attack (2025-04-12):** Interlock ransomware disrupted operations; protected health information of ~2.69M individuals compromised; ~$13.5M of direct costs booked in Q2 2025. Treatment volumes were already soft in 2025 (elevated patient mortality from flu season, storm disruptions), making 2025 a below-trend year.
- **Berkshire Hathaway trimming:** Berkshire (holder since 2011, historically ~40%+ of the float) continued scaling back its DVA stake through 2025 under a share-repurchase-linked agreement — a persistent technical overhang disclosed via 13F/13D filings.
- **Q1 2026 (reported 2026-05-05):** revenue $3.416B (vs ~$3.36B est), operating income $482M, diluted EPS from continuing ops $2.87 (consensus $2.33 — a 23% beat); OCF $321M, FCF $140M; repurchased 3.0M shares at average $133.70.
- **Guidance raised:** FY2026 adjusted EPS lifted to **$14.10–$15.20** on higher volume forecasts and lower patient-care costs.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

DaVita holds a **narrow, duopoly-and-scale** [[economic-moat|moat]] rather than a brand or pricing-power moat:

- **Duopoly structure** — DaVita and Fresenius control the large majority of U.S. outpatient dialysis, limiting price competition and deterring new entrants in a capital- and regulation-heavy business.
- **Scale & density** — a national footprint of ~2,600 centers spreads fixed costs and creates local clinic density that is hard to replicate; patients and nephrologists value proximity ([[switching-costs|switching costs]]).
- **Regulatory entanglement as a barrier** — ESRD reimbursement complexity, certificate-of-need rules, and clinical-quality requirements raise the bar for new competition.
- **Commercial-payer mix economics** — the ~10% commercially insured patients fund profitability; managing payer mix and contracts is a core competitive skill.
- **Capital-allocation engine** — disciplined, relentless buybacks have compounded per-share value (the model that attracted [[berkshire-hathaway|Berkshire]]).

The moat is **narrow and policy-fragile**: the same government that anchors volume sets near-breakeven reimbursement on ~90% of patients, commercial-payer mix is under structural and legal pressure, and the GLP-1/CKD thesis is a long-dated demand overhang. This is a "value with concerns" profile, not a clean compounder.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Entrenched dialysis duopoly with stable pricing, recurring treatment volume, and high barriers to entry.
- Powerful free-cash-flow and buyback engine has roughly halved the share count over a decade, compounding per-share value (the [[berkshire-hathaway|Berkshire]] thesis).
- Integrated Kidney Care adds a value-based growth vector beyond fee-for-service.
- Treatment volumes should normalize as the elevated-mortality drag (pandemic, flu seasons) fades.
- Low correlation to the market makes it a useful idiosyncratic, defensive-ish holding.

### Bear case
- ~90% of patients are near-breakeven government-reimbursed; CMS rate cuts hit profit directly.
- Commercial-payer-mix erosion — legal/regulatory pressure on the ~10% that funds nearly all the profit — is the core structural risk.
- Heavy leverage (~$8B+ debt) against negative book equity leaves little cushion if cash flows wobble.
- GLP-1 anti-obesity/diabetes drugs are a long-dated overhang on the dialysis population.
- Berkshire's continued trimming is a persistent technical overhang on the stock.

## Key Risks

- **CMS reimbursement cuts** — the annual ESRD bundled rate governs ~90% of patients; any cut flows straight to profit.
- **Commercial-payer-mix erosion** — the ~10% commercially insured patients generate effectively all the profit; legal, regulatory, or contractual pressure here is the single largest structural risk.
- **Leverage** — ~$8B+ debt against negative book equity; read interest coverage and FCF, but the absolute load is a real constraint if volumes deteriorate.
- **GLP-1 / CKD demand** — anti-obesity and diabetes drugs slowing chronic-kidney-disease progression could shrink the long-run dialysis population.
- **Volume sensitivity** — elevated patient mortality (flu seasons, pandemics) and storm disruptions can make any given year below-trend.
- **Cybersecurity & litigation** — the 2025 Interlock ransomware breach (2.69M individuals) plus ballot initiatives/labor actions in California and antitrust scrutiny are recurring liabilities.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

DaVita trades as a **leveraged, buyback-driven cash-flow story**, not a dividend or growth name — the low-double-digit trailing P/E in the data below reflects policy risk and the absence of a dividend, while per-share value is compounded through aggressive repurchase. Mechanically:

- **No dividend** — DaVita pays none; the entire capital-return story is buybacks, which is why share count has roughly halved over a decade and ROE/D-E look distorted.
- **Negative book equity** — the headline 75x D/E and triple-digit ROE in the fundamentals are artifacts of buyback-driven negative equity; underwrite interest coverage (~5x), free cash flow, and absolute debt instead.
- **Idiosyncratic / low beta to the market** — the stock is driven by ESRD reimbursement policy and company-specific catalysts more than the macro tape.
- **Berkshire overhang** — [[berkshire-hathaway|Berkshire]]'s long-running stake (and its gradual, repurchase-linked trimming) is a persistent technical factor in the share price.
- **Earnings behavior** — the stock trades on treatment volume, payer mix, and FY adjusted-EPS guidance versus expectations, and reacts sharply to CMS rate notices and GLP-1 headlines.
