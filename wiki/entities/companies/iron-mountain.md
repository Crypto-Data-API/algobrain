Iron Mountain (NYSE: IRM) is a specialty REIT that dominates physical records storage and is rapidly scaling a hyperscale data-center business. For traders it is a hybrid story: a sticky, cash-generative legacy storage franchise funding one of the fastest-growing [[data-centers|data-center]] platforms in the REIT space, making the stock sensitive to both rates and AI-infrastructure sentiment. It is a constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]] index.

## Business Overview & Segments

Iron Mountain serves about 240,000 customers (including most of the Fortune 1000) across three pillars — a sticky, cash-generative legacy storage core funding a fast-growing data-center and services platform. The structural story is a **secular pivot**: the recurring cash flow thrown off by the physical-records franchise is being redeployed into one of the fastest-growing [[data-centers|data-center]] businesses in the [[reits|REIT]] universe, riding [[ai-data-center-power-demand|AI-driven data-center power demand]].

| Segment / pillar | What it is | Profile (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **Records & Information Management (RIM)** | The legacy core: physical document and media storage (boxes, tapes), with very high retention and contractual annual price escalation | Mature cash cow — slow-growing volumes but durable, high-margin recurring revenue that funds growth capex |
| **Data Centers** | Hyperscale and colocation campuses (Northern Virginia, Phoenix, London, Frankfurt, Mumbai, etc.), leasing tens of MW per quarter to cloud and AI tenants | The primary growth engine — capital-intensive but high-return development, levered to the AI build-out |
| **Asset Lifecycle Management (ALM) & Digital Solutions** | IT asset decommissioning/recycling/resale and document digitization (scanning, workflow software) | Faster-growing services adjacencies; double-digit growth, capital-light versus the data-center build |

As a [[reit|REIT]], the market values IRM primarily on **AFFO per share** and data-center leasing momentum rather than GAAP EPS — which is why the headline P/E in the fundamentals below looks extreme while AFFO multiples are conventional.

## 2025-2026 Developments

- **FY2025 results (reported Feb 2026):** record revenue of $6.9B, up 12.2% y/y (organic +10%); adjusted EBITDA $2.6B; AFFO $1.5B ($5.17/share).
- **Data center acceleration:** Q4 2025 data-center organic revenue grew ~37% y/y, with 43 MW of new leasing in the quarter and renewal pricing up 9% (cash) / 12% (GAAP). Management guides to at least 25% data-center revenue growth in 2026.
- **Dividend:** 10% increase effective January 2026 ($0.864/quarter) — the fourth consecutive annual raise.
- **2026 guidance:** revenue $7.625–7.775B, adjusted EBITDA $2.875–2.925B, AFFO $1.705–1.735B — continued double-digit growth.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Iron Mountain's [[economic-moat|moat]] is one of the most durable in real estate, rooted in **switching-cost inertia** on the legacy storage side and **scale** across the platform:

- **Storage switching costs** — once a customer's records, tapes, and archives are deposited, the cost and effort of indexing, retrieving, and physically relocating them is high, while the marginal cost of leaving boxes in place is low. The result is exceptionally high retention (boxes that come in rarely leave) and contractual price escalators that compound revenue per box over decades. This is a textbook [[switching-costs|switching-cost]] moat.
- **Scale and density** — the largest global storage footprint (a vast network of facilities serving ~240,000 customers) yields cost and route-density advantages a sub-scale competitor cannot replicate.
- **Trust / compliance** — chain-of-custody, regulatory retention, and data-destruction credibility make Iron Mountain the default for risk-averse enterprises and regulated industries.
- **Capital and land for the pivot** — the cash flow and balance-sheet access from RIM, plus owned land and power relationships, underwrite the [[data-centers|data-center]] build-out far faster than a standing start.

The competitive question is **two-sided**: on storage the moat is wide but the underlying volumes face slow secular decline (paper digitization); on data centers IRM is a credible but not dominant developer competing against scaled specialists. The secular pivot toward [[ai-data-center-power-demand|AI data-center power demand]] is the lever that re-rates the moat from "declining cash cow" to "growth compounder."

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Data-center leasing (MW signed)** — the headline growth metric; quarterly megawatts leased and the development pipeline drive the AFFO trajectory and the multiple.
- **[[ai-data-center-power-demand|AI data-center power demand]]** — the secular tailwind; AI training/inference capacity needs make power-and-land-backed data-center capacity scarce and valuable.
- **ALM & digitization** — IT asset decommissioning/resale and document digitization, both compounding at double digits and diversifying away from paper.
- **RIM price escalation** — contractual annual storage-rental increases lift revenue per box even as physical volumes flatten or slowly decline.
- **AFFO growth & dividend raises** — a multi-year record of double-digit AFFO growth funding consecutive annual dividend increases (the 2026 raise was the fourth straight).
- **Hyperscaler capex** — read-throughs from cloud/AI hyperscaler capital-spending commentary set the demand backdrop for the data-center segment.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly earnings (Feb/May/Aug/Nov), leasing/MW disclosures, AFFO guidance revisions, dividend actions, and Treasury-yield moves.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- A sticky, high-retention RIM cash cow funds one of the fastest-growing [[data-centers|data-center]] platforms in the REIT space — a self-funding "old business pays for the new business" engine.
- Direct exposure to the [[ai-data-center-power-demand|AI data-center power-demand]] super-cycle, with strong data-center organic growth and firm renewal pricing.
- A reliable dividend grower with consecutive annual raises, backed by double-digit AFFO growth.
- Contractual storage price escalators compound revenue even as paper volumes plateau, and ALM/digitization diversify the mix.
- Scale and switching-cost inertia make the legacy base unusually defensible versus most cyclical real estate.

### Bear case
- The legacy paper-records business faces slow secular decline as enterprises digitize.
- High leverage (characteristic of REITs) makes the equity rate-sensitive; a higher-for-longer rate regime compresses the multiple and raises financing costs.
- The data-center pivot is capital-intensive — execution slips, cost inflation, or development funding needs could force dilutive equity or strain the balance sheet.
- Data-center growth depends on a concentrated set of hyperscale tenants and on hyperscaler capex staying elevated.
- AFFO payout is high; a growth or rate shock that pinches AFFO would pressure both the dividend-growth story and the valuation.

## Key Risks

- **Leverage & rate cycle** — like most REITs, IRM runs meaningful debt (the negative book equity below distorts the headline D/E); equity value is sensitive to Treasury yields and refinancing costs.
- **Data-center execution & capex intensity** — the growth engine requires sustained, large-scale development; cost overruns, power/permitting constraints, or leasing slowdowns would hit returns and may require external capital (dilution risk).
- **Secular decline in paper records** — long-run digitization erodes physical-storage volumes; pricing escalators offset but do not reverse the trend.
- **Dividend coverage vs AFFO payout** — the dividend-growth thesis rests on continued AFFO growth; a shortfall pressures coverage.
- **Hyperscaler / tenant concentration** — data-center revenue leans on a small number of large cloud/AI tenants, so a capex pause among them transmits directly to leasing.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Iron Mountain is a [[reits|REIT]] and **must be valued on REIT metrics, not GAAP earnings**. The headline P/E shown in the fundamentals block (~200x) is an artifact of REIT accounting — heavy depreciation on a large real-asset base suppresses GAAP net income — and is not a meaningful gauge of value. Mechanically:

- **AFFO is the anchor** — the market prices IRM on **AFFO (adjusted funds from operations) per share** and the AFFO multiple, with FFO as the cruder cousin. On AFFO the multiple is conventional even though the P/E looks extreme; AFFO-per-share growth (double-digit in recent years) is the central driver of total return.
- **NAV / NTA** — analysts also cross-check against net asset value / net tangible assets, valuing the storage and data-center real estate (and development pipeline) and netting debt; the data-center build adds embedded NAV as capacity is leased up.
- **Dividend** — IRM trades partly as an income vehicle. The market dividend yield runs in the low-single-digit-percent range (the ~2% area on the 5-year-average yield context in the data block; treat the API "dividend yield" field as a non-literal definition, not the market yield), and the high GAAP dividend payout ratio is normal for a REIT that pays out of AFFO rather than net income.
- **Dual identity** — the stock trades simultaneously as a **rate-sensitive REIT** (moving with Treasury yields and the broader REIT complex) and as an **AI-infrastructure proxy** (re-rating on data-center leasing and hyperscaler-capex sentiment, correlating with [[digital-realty]] and [[equinix]]). Which factor dominates shifts with the macro regime.
- **Beta** — a moderate-beta name relative to the high-growth tech complex, but with above-typical-REIT sensitivity given the data-center growth optionality embedded in the multiple.
