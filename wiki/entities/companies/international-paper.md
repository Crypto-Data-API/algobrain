International Paper (NYSE: IP) is one of the world's largest producers of corrugated/containerboard packaging, now reshaped by the 2025 acquisition of UK-based DS Smith into a transatlantic packaging giant. For traders it is a cyclical [[s-and-p-500]] [[materials|Materials]] name in the middle of an aggressive 80/20 restructuring and an announced split into two public companies — a special-situations setup more than a steady compounder. Founded 1898; headquartered in Memphis, Tennessee.

## Business Overview & Segments

IP's core business is industrial packaging — containerboard and corrugated boxes used to ship goods — which makes demand a direct read on goods consumption, e-commerce, and the industrial cycle. Pricing follows containerboard benchmarks; costs are dominated by fiber (recycled and virgin), energy, and freight.

| Business / line | What it is | Status (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **North American Packaging** | Containerboard mills + corrugated box plants in the US | The core franchise; to become one of the two split companies |
| **EMEA Packaging** | European corrugated/containerboard, greatly expanded by **DS Smith** (2025) | To be spun off as an independent EMEA packaging company (~12-15 months) |
| **Global Cellulose Fibers** | Fluff/specialty pulp (diapers, hygiene) | Agreed to be **divested** (Jan 2026) — exiting to become pure-play packaging |

After acquiring DS Smith in 2025 the company gained a large European footprint, and in January 2026 it agreed to sell its Global Cellulose Fibers business, sharpening the focus on packaging. CEO **Andy Silvernail** has driven a turnaround built on the **"80/20" performance system** (concentrating resources on the most valuable customers/products), aggressive footprint rationalization, and cost cuts. The reported numbers in the Fundamentals section reflect a low-margin, capital-intensive cyclical business mid-transformation — the equity story is the **restructuring and the split**, not current earnings.

## 2025-2026 Developments

- **DS Smith acquisition (2025):** IP completed its acquisition of DS Smith, creating a leading sustainable-packaging player across North America and EMEA and significantly expanding scale.
- **Two-company split (announced Jan 2026):** Alongside FY2025 results (reported 29 January 2026), IP announced plans to separate into two independent public companies — a North American packaging business and an EMEA packaging business — with the EMEA spin-off expected within ~12-15 months.
- **Global Cellulose Fibers sale (Jan 2026):** IP agreed to divest its Global Cellulose Fibers segment, completing the pivot to a pure-play packaging company.
- **Footprint rationalization:** In 2025 IP closed ~20 EMEA facilities and cut ~1,400 roles (targeting >$160M run-rate savings), and guided to at least seven more closures and 700+ further job cuts in EMEA during 2026.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

IP's [[economic-moat|moat]] is the weakest of the three large containerboard names covered here — **scale without best-in-class cost or integration**. Where it exists:

- **Scale and footprint** — the largest US containerboard producer and, post-DS Smith, a transatlantic packaging leader; scale gives purchasing power and customer reach.
- **Oligopoly structure** — like the rest of the group, IP benefits from a consolidated US containerboard market where pricing behavior is rational.
- **Integration (improving)** — mill-to-box integration exists but historically lagged the discipline of [[packaging-corporation-of-america]]; the 80/20 program is partly an effort to close that gap.

The honest framing is harsher than for PCA or Vulcan: IP is a **price-taker commodity producer** in a capital-intensive, cyclical industry, and its through-cycle returns and margins have lagged peers (see the Fundamentals "Speculative / unprofitable" Fred profile — low ROE/ROA, thin EBITDA margin). It has **no local-pricing-power moat** (unlike aggregates) and **less integration advantage** than PCA. The investment case is therefore explicitly a **turnaround + breakup value-unlock** (Silvernail's 80/20 reset and the EMEA spin), not a quality-compounder moat story. Success means restructuring IP *into* a wider-moat operator; failure means it stays a low-return cyclical.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **80/20 restructuring** — Silvernail's performance system, footprint rationalization, and cost cuts (>$160M run-rate EMEA savings targeted) are the primary margin lever.
- **EMEA spin-off** — the planned separation into two public companies (North America + EMEA) within ~12-15 months is the central value-unlock catalyst.
- **DS Smith integration** — synergies and scale from the 2025 acquisition across North America and EMEA.
- **Cellulose Fibers divestiture** — completing the pivot to pure-play packaging sharpens the story and raises cash.
- **Containerboard pricing & box volumes** — industry price increases and e-commerce/industrial demand provide cyclical upside.
- **Margin convergence** — closing the gap to higher-margin peers (PCA) is the through-cycle prize if 80/20 works.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Special-situation value unlock: the EMEA spin plus Cellulose Fibers sale could surface value trapped in a conglomerate-discounted cyclical.
- Silvernail's 80/20 reset and aggressive cost-out can lift IP's chronically low margins toward peer levels.
- DS Smith adds scale and a leading European footprint; synergies and a focused pure-play packaging story.
- Cyclical leverage: in a containerboard up-cycle, a restructured, higher-margin IP has significant earnings torque.

### Bear case
- Lowest-quality of the box majors today — low ROE/ROA and thin margins (Fred "Speculative / unprofitable" profile); the moat is weak.
- Execution risk on a complex, multi-front transformation (acquisition integration + footprint cuts + a corporate split) all at once.
- Commodity price-taker with no pricing-power moat; a containerboard downturn hits a still-low-margin business hard.
- Capital-intensive with restructuring charges and elevated interest costs (low interest coverage in Fundamentals); the split itself carries dis-synergy and cost risk.

## Key Risks

- **Execution / restructuring** — the 80/20 reset, DS Smith integration, EMEA closures, and the two-company split are happening simultaneously; mis-steps are costly.
- **Containerboard-cycle pricing** — a price-taker; a demand or pricing downturn compresses already-thin margins.
- **Volume cyclicality** — box demand tracks goods consumption, e-commerce, and the industrial cycle.
- **Input costs** — fiber (recycled and virgin), energy, and freight inflation.
- **Spin-off risk** — separation costs, dis-synergies, and execution risk on creating two viable standalone companies; transatlantic/FX and European-demand exposure.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

IP trades as a **cyclical special-situation / turnaround** — the market values the restructuring trajectory and breakup optionality more than current (depressed) earnings. The headline P/E in Fundamentals sits on low GAAP EPS, and the Fred-view flags the low-return, capital-intensive profile honestly. Mechanically:

- **Beta** — roughly market-level to modestly above; a cyclical box stock that moves with the industrial cycle and containerboard sentiment.
- **Dividend** — a meaningful yield historically, but the payout sits above depressed earnings and could be reshaped by the split — dividend sustainability/allocation across the two future companies is a live question (the API "dividend yield" field in Fundamentals is a non-market definition).
- **What moves it** — containerboard price-increase announcements, quarterly volume/margin trends, **restructuring-savings delivery**, the **EMEA spin-off timeline**, DS Smith integration progress, and packaging-demand / industrial-production data.
- **Expression** — liquid listed options; a common cyclical/box-stock proxy and a special-situations/event-driven setup. Trades with [[smurfit-westrock]], [[packaging-corporation-of-america]], and [[amcor]]; broadly tracks the industrial cycle.
