Tether Limited is the company behind [[usdt|USDT (Tether)]], the world's largest [[stablecoins|stablecoin]] with a market capitalisation exceeding $110 billion. A subsidiary of iFinex Inc (which also owns the Bitfinex cryptocurrency exchange), Tether is simultaneously one of the most profitable and most controversial companies in finance -- generating ~$6.2 billion in net profit in 2023 with approximately 100 employees, while facing persistent questions about reserve transparency and regulatory compliance.

## Overview

Tether was originally founded in 2014 as "Realcoin" by Brock Pierce, Reeve Collins, and Craig Sellars, before rebranding to Tether. The company issues [[usdt|USDT]], a stablecoin pegged 1:1 to the US dollar, across multiple blockchains.

As of 2025, Tether is led by **CEO Paolo Ardoino**, who previously served as CTO of Bitfinex. The shared leadership between Tether and Bitfinex has been a persistent source of criticism, as it blurs the line between a stablecoin issuer (which should operate independently) and a trading platform (which is a user of that stablecoin).

## Corporate Structure

Tether's corporate structure is opaque compared to competitors like [[circle|Circle]]:

- **Parent company**: iFinex Inc (registered in the British Virgin Islands)
- **Subsidiaries**: Tether Limited (stablecoin issuer), Bitfinex (cryptocurrency exchange)
- **Shared ownership**: iFinex owns both Tether and Bitfinex. Key personnel overlap
- **Domicile history**: Originally Hong Kong-based, moved to BVI
- **El Salvador licence**: Tether obtained a digital asset service provider licence in El Salvador in 2023, signalling a shift toward jurisdictions friendly to crypto
- **No public financial statements**: Tether is privately held and does not publish audited financial statements

## Revenue and Profitability

Tether is one of the most profitable financial companies in the world:

| Metric | 2023 |
|--------|------|
| Net profit | ~$6.2 billion |
| Revenue source | Yield on ~$110B+ in reserve assets (primarily US Treasuries at 4-5%) |
| Employees | ~100 |
| Profit per employee | ~$62 million |

For context, JPMorgan Chase -- the world's largest bank -- earned $49.6B in net income in 2023 with ~300,000 employees ($165K per employee). Tether's profit-per-employee ratio is among the highest of any company in history.

Like [[circle|Circle]], Tether retains all yield on reserves. [[usdt|USDT]] holders receive none of this yield. See [[stablecoin-yields]].

## Reserve Composition

As of Q4 2024, Tether reports the following reserve breakdown:

| Asset Category | Approximate Share |
|---------------|------------------|
| US Treasury securities | ~85% |
| Bitcoin | ~5% |
| Cash and bank deposits | ~5% |
| Gold and precious metals | ~3% |
| Secured loans | ~1% |
| Other investments | ~1% |

The reserve composition has **improved significantly** since 2021, when Tether held substantial commercial paper (since eliminated), crypto assets, and secured loans to affiliated entities. The shift toward US Treasuries reflects both regulatory pressure and the profitability of higher interest rates.

### Attestation vs Audit

Tether publishes **quarterly attestation reports** by BDO Italia. Critical distinction:

- An **attestation** confirms that reserves exist at a specific point in time (a "snapshot")
- An **audit** examines financial controls, processes, internal systems, and verifies statements over a period -- much more comprehensive

No **Big Four accounting firm** (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG) has ever conducted a full audit of Tether. For a company managing over $110 billion in liabilities, this level of opacity is extraordinary. By comparison, [[circle|Circle]] receives monthly attestations from Deloitte and is pursuing a public IPO (which requires SEC-audited financial statements).

Tether has repeatedly stated a full audit is forthcoming but has not delivered one.

## Regulatory and Legal History

### NYAG Settlement (2021)

The New York Attorney General investigated Tether and Bitfinex from 2019:

- Found that Tether's reserves were **not always 100% backed by cash**, as Tether had publicly claimed
- Reserves at various times included commercial paper, loans to affiliated entities, and other non-cash assets
- Tether and Bitfinex **commingled funds** and misrepresented reserves to the public
- **Settlement**: $18.5 million fine, quarterly transparency reports, no admission of fraud
- Tether was banned from operating in New York State

### CFTC Fine (2021)

The Commodity Futures Trading Commission fined Tether **$41 million**:

- Found that USDT was fully backed by reserves for only **27.6% of the days** in a 26-month sample period (2016-2018)
- Tether had made "untrue or misleading statements and omissions of material fact" about its reserves

### Bitfinex Loan (2018-2019)

In late 2018, Tether lent **$850 million** to Bitfinex to cover funds seized by various governments from payment processor Crypto Capital:

- This loan came from USDT reserves -- meaning Tether was using customer-backing funds to bail out its sister exchange
- The NYAG investigation revealed this arrangement, which Tether had not publicly disclosed
- The loan was subsequently repaid, but it demonstrated the governance risk of shared ownership between a stablecoin issuer and an exchange

### US DOJ Investigation

Bloomberg reported in 2021 that the US Department of Justice was investigating Tether for potential bank fraud and anti-money laundering violations:

- The investigation reportedly examined whether Tether concealed the crypto-related nature of transactions from banking partners
- As of 2025, no charges have been filed
- Tether has stated it cooperates with law enforcement

### MiCA Non-Compliance

Under the EU's [[stablecoin-regulation|MiCA regulation]] (effective June 2024), stablecoin issuers must obtain an EMI licence or bank charter to operate in the European Economic Area. Tether has **not** obtained MiCA compliance, leading several European exchanges (Bitstamp, Kraken Europe, others) to delist or restrict USDT trading in the EEA. This represents a significant structural shift in the European stablecoin market toward [[usdc|USDC]].

## Systemic Importance

Despite -- or perhaps because of -- its controversies, Tether is arguably the most systemically important entity in crypto:

- **USDT is the backbone of crypto liquidity**: More trading volume flows through USDT than any other asset. BTC/USDT is the most traded pair globally
- **Emerging market dependency**: Estimated ~50% of USDT usage is in emerging markets for dollarisation and remittances, not trading. Millions of people in Argentina, Turkey, Nigeria, and Southeast Asia hold USDT as a stable store of value. See [[stablecoin-use-cases]]
- **Exchange dependency**: [[binance|Binance]], OKX, Bybit, and most major non-US exchanges are built around USDT pairs
- **If Tether failed**: A loss of confidence in USDT would trigger the most severe crash in crypto history. USDT is the primary source of liquidity for most trading pairs -- its collapse would cause cascading liquidations, exchange insolvencies, and potentially the destruction of hundreds of billions in market value

The market has effectively concluded that USDT's **redeemability track record** matters more than its **governance transparency**. Tether has processed billions in redemptions through every major crypto crash (2018, 2020, 2022, FTX collapse) without issue.

## Tether's Investments

Using its substantial profits, Tether has diversified beyond stablecoins:

- **Bitcoin**: ~$5B+ in BTC holdings (as of late 2024)
- **Sustainable energy**: Investments in Bitcoin mining and renewable energy projects
- **Artificial intelligence**: Venture investments in AI companies
- **Neurotechnology**: Brain-computer interface research
- **Education**: Various educational initiatives

These investments are somewhat unusual for a stablecoin issuer -- they are made from Tether's equity/profits, not from USDT reserves (according to Tether). However, the lack of audited financials makes it difficult to verify the separation between reserve assets and investment assets.
