Evergy (NASDAQ: EVRG) is the regulated electric [[utilities|utility]] serving ~1.7M customers in Kansas and western Missouri, formed by the 2018 merger of Westar Energy and Great Plains Energy, and a constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]]. For traders it has transformed from a sleepy bond proxy into one of the cleanest regulated-utility plays on [[ai-data-center-power-demand|data-center load growth]], after landing gigawatt-scale contracts with Google, Meta, and Panasonic in its service territory. Founded 1909 (legacy roots), headquartered in Kansas City, Missouri.

## Business Overview & Segments

Evergy is **almost entirely regulated** (Kansas Corporation Commission and Missouri PSC jurisdictions), earning a regulated return on a growing **rate base** of generation, transmission, and distribution assets. The historic knock on the company was anemic load growth; that reversed in 2024-2025 as the Kansas City region attracted hyperscaler and advanced-manufacturing projects — Panasonic's EV-battery plant (described as the world's largest) and Meta's Missouri data center both reached commercial operation, and Google broke ground on a second KC-area data-center campus. The regulatory bargain: large new load spreads fixed costs over more megawatt-hours, supporting rate-base growth with less customer-bill pressure.

| Segment / jurisdiction | What it is | Role (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **Evergy Kansas** (KCC-regulated) | Vertically integrated electric generation, transmission, distribution | Core rate-base earnings; Kansas regulatory construct |
| **Evergy Missouri** (Missouri PSC) | Vertically integrated electric utility (West / Metro) | Core rate-base earnings; home of much of the new large-load growth |
| **Transmission** | FERC-regulated transmission investment | Steady rate-base growth leg |
| **Generation fleet** | Coal, gas, nuclear (Wolf Creek), wind/solar | Being reshaped toward gas + renewables to serve contracted data-center load |

The structural point: Evergy earns a regulator-approved return *on* invested capital (rate base), so the growth algorithm is **capex → rate base → EPS** — not unit pricing. New [[ai-data-center-power-demand|data-center load]] is powerful because it both justifies large new generation capex (rate-base growth) and spreads fixed costs over more sales.

## Economic Moat

A regulated utility's moat is a **legal monopoly**, the widest and most durable kind in structure but capped in upside by regulation:

- **Regulated monopoly franchise** — Evergy is the sole provider of electric service in its territory; customers cannot switch suppliers, giving near-zero demand competition.
- **Rate-base economics** — the regulator allows Evergy to earn a set return (allowed ROE) on prudent capital investment, converting capex into a predictable earnings stream — a [[economic-moat|moat]] underwritten by law rather than by product differentiation.
- **Irreplaceable network** — the transmission and distribution grid is a natural monopoly; duplicating it is uneconomic and politically impossible.
- **Demand anchor** — the new gigawatt-scale data-center contracts lock in load growth that backstops the rate-base expansion.

The flip side: the moat caps upside. Returns are set by regulators, so the variables that matter are the **allowed ROE**, rate-case timing (regulatory lag), and the cost of the debt funding the capex (interest coverage of ~2.6x is a live issue during the build — see Fundamentals).

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Data-center load** — gigawatt-scale electric-service agreements (Google, Meta, Beale Infrastructure) anchor the load-growth thesis; more contracts are the key catalyst.
- **Capex super-cycle** — the five-year capital plan jumped ~24% to ~$21.6B, mostly new generation (gas + renewables) to serve contracted load — among the largest plan increases in the regulated group.
- **Rate cases** — Kansas and Missouri orders determine how promptly the capex earns a return (regulatory lag risk).
- **Generation build** — new gas and renewable capacity additions feed rate base.
- **Recurring catalysts** — new large-load contract announcements, rate-case orders, integrated-resource-plan updates, quarterly load-growth disclosures, and Treasury yields (the sector's perennial driver).

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Best-in-class clean exposure to [[ai-data-center-power-demand|data-center load growth]] in a constructive two-state regulatory footprint.
- Large capex plan converts directly into rate-base and EPS growth at the top of the long-term target range.
- Regulated monopoly = highly predictable, defensive cash flows and a growing dividend.
- Re-rating optionality as more large-load contracts are signed.

### Bear case
- Heavy capex must be financed; interest coverage ~2.6x means rising rates and debt costs squeeze the build economics.
- Regulatory lag — capex earns nothing until rate cases conclude; an adverse order caps returns.
- Rate-sensitive valuation: long-Treasury-yield spikes de-rate the whole sector regardless of fundamentals.
- Data-center contracts could slip, downsize, or shift the load-growth timeline.

## Key Risks

- **Interest-rate sensitivity** — like all regulated [[utilities|utilities]], EVRG trades inversely to long-term Treasury yields; rising rates compress the yield-proxy valuation *and* the financing cost of the capex plan.
- **Regulatory** — unfavourable Kansas/Missouri rate-case outcomes or allowed-ROE cuts; regulatory lag on capex recovery.
- **Execution** — delivering ~$21.6B of capex on time and on budget; generation-build delays.
- **Demand concentration** — heavy reliance on a few hyperscaler contracts to deliver the load-growth thesis.
- **Balance sheet** — financing a large capex program at ~2.6x interest coverage.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Evergy trades as a **regulated-utility yield + rate-base-growth** name, with an added AI/data-center growth premium that has re-rated it against slower-growth peers. The classic utility valuation lens is dividend yield and rate-base/EPS growth, not earnings multiples on cyclical profit. Mechanically:

- **Low beta, rate proxy** — moves inversely to long-term Treasury yields; a defensive, income-oriented holding (XLU factor) plus a data-center growth kicker.
- **Dividend** — a core, growing dividend is central to total return; the payout is funded from regulated cash flows.
- **Relative value** — the premium vs no-growth utility peers expands and contracts with AI-capex sentiment; EVRG is the cleaner data-center-utility expression next to [[ameren]] and [[american-electric-power]].
- **Options** — moderate liquidity; mostly trades via the utilities factor (XLU) rather than active single-name gamma.
- **Catalysts** — contract announcements and rate-case orders can re-rate the multiple; Fed/rate moves drive the sector.
