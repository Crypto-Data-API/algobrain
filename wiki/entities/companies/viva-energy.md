**Viva Energy Group Limited** (ASX: VEA) is an oil & gas refining & marketing company listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. Viva Energy Group Limited (ASX: VEA) is one of Australia's leading integrated energy companies, supplying approximately a quarter of Australia's liquid fuel needs. Headquartered at 720 Bourke Street in Melbourne's Docklands, Viva operates Australia's largest single-operator fuel and convenience retail network, the Geelong Refinery in Victoria, a nationwide fuel import terminal and pipeline network, and commercial/industrial fuel, lubricant, chemicals, polymers and aviation businesses across Australia, Singapore and Papua New Guinea.

## Business Overview & Segments/Assets

Viva Energy is an integrated [[energy|energy]] downstream business: it imports, refines, distributes and retails liquid fuels across Australia, and increasingly monetises the foot traffic at its forecourts through convenience retail. The economics are a barbell — a **volatile, spread-driven refining and supply business** on one side and a **steadier, volume-and-margin convenience/retail business** on the other. Crucially, Viva is largely a **price/spread taker**: it does not set the crude price, the wholesale fuel price, or the global refining crack spread that drives the Geelong Refinery's margin.

The franchise is best understood as **infrastructure + retail distribution**, not a branded pricing-power business: the durable assets are the import terminals, pipelines and refinery, and the breadth/density of the retail network — not a premium consumer brand.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Viva's [[economic-moat|economic moat]] is **moderate and asset/scale-based rather than brand-based** — honest framing matters here, because fuel is a commodity and retail-fuel switching costs are essentially zero (drivers buy on price and location, not loyalty):

- **Import, terminal & pipeline infrastructure** — Australia's largest fuel import terminal and pipeline network (24 terminals). This logistics backbone is expensive, slow and permit-heavy to replicate, and underpins both the commercial and retail supply chains. This is the most durable advantage.
- **Retail network density & site quality** — more than ~1,330 sites give national coverage and scale in fuel purchasing and convenience supply. Location quality (high-traffic forecourts, OTR's food-led format) is the real retail edge, not the brand on the canopy.
- **Shell brand licence** — Viva operates the Shell brand in Australia under licence, lending recognition at the forecourt, but this is a licensed badge, not owned pricing power.
- **Geelong Refinery + Fuel Security Services Payment floor** — the only major refinery exposure on the east coast outside [[ampol]]; the Australian Government's Fuel Security Services Payment provides a margin support floor (commitment to operate through 2030+), partially insulating downside in weak refining cycles. But the upside is still a **spread taker** — Viva captures the refining crack, it does not set it.
- **Where the moat is thin** — refining is a global-commodity spread business with no pricing power; retail fuel is price-transparent and competitive; the convenience shop margin is the genuine value-add but faces supermarkets, c-store chains and QSR for the same wallet.

Net: a scale/infrastructure moat with a convenience-retail value-add layered on top — defensible distribution, but earnings quality is hostage to the refining cycle until the convenience mix grows large enough to dominate.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **OTR / Reddy Express convenience rollout** — the central growth thesis: converting Coles Express sites to the OTR-led, food-and-convenience format and rolling out new OTR stores (35 opened in 2025), lifting shop margin per site and same-store sales.
- **Geelong refining margin recovery** — the single largest earnings swing factor; a stronger crack spread (FY25 lifted to ~US$9.60/bbl after a strong Q4) flows almost directly to profit, with the Fuel Security Services Payment cushioning the downside.
- **ULSG project completed** — the multi-year ultra-low-sulphur gasoline upgrade at Geelong (on schedule, largely on budget) keeps the refinery compliant with 2024 fuel-quality standards and protects its operating licence.
- **Commercial volumes (aviation & broad demand)** — record FY25 commercial sales (~11.8 billion litres); aviation recovery and resources/transport demand drive the high-volume Commercial & Industrial segment.
- **OTR integration synergies** — extracting back-office, supply-chain and loyalty synergies from the ~A$1.15bn OTR acquisition; successful integration is what justifies the post-acquisition leverage and amortisation drag.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- **Convenience earnings stability** — the OTR/Reddy Express convenience network builds a recurring, higher-margin, less-cyclical base that de-risks the group away from pure refining beta over time.
- **Refining upside optionality** — when crack spreads recover, Geelong's earnings leverage is large and largely uncapped; the Fuel Security Services Payment floor limits how bad the downside gets.
- **Hard-to-replicate infrastructure** — the import terminal, pipeline and refinery network is a genuine barrier to entry that underpins both commercial and retail supply.
- **Scale leader** — supplying ~a quarter of Australia's liquid fuel and running the largest single-operator retail network gives purchasing scale and national reach.

### Bear case
- **Leverage post-OTR** — elevated balance-sheet leverage (D/E ~1.6x, liabilities/equity ~7.7x) after the Coles Express and OTR acquisitions is a real watch item, especially in a risk-off regime or weak refining cycle.
- **Refining volatility** — Geelong's margin is a global-spread taker; a sustained weak-crack environment compresses the most volatile earnings line.
- **GAAP losses** — the company is currently GAAP loss-making (negative headline P/E), reflecting refining-cycle softness, OTR acquisition amortisation and impairments — the equity story depends on through-cycle normalised earnings, not the headline number.
- **Integration & execution risk** — the convenience transformation must deliver synergies and same-store growth to justify the capital deployed.

## Key Risks

- **Refining-cycle volatility** — the Geelong refining margin is the dominant earnings swing factor and is set by global crack spreads outside Viva's control; weak cracks can swing the group to losses.
- **Leverage** — post-acquisition D/E (~1.6x) and liabilities/equity (~7.7x) raise financial risk; refinancing and rate sensitivity matter, particularly against cyclical refining cash flows.
- **ACCC / regulatory** — competition-authority scrutiny (the OTR deal required divestiture of 25 Coles Express sites), fuel-quality mandates, and policy on refinery support all bear on the model.
- **EV transition / structural fuel demand** — long-run electrification erodes liquid-fuel volumes; the convenience pivot is partly a hedge against declining forecourt fuel demand.
- **Integration risk** — failure to realise OTR/Reddy Express synergies and same-store growth would leave the leverage without the offsetting earnings uplift.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Viva trades as a **through-cycle refining-plus-convenience play**, and the headline multiple is misleading: the GAAP net loss produces a negative P/E that should not be read as a valuation signal. Mechanically:

- **Earnings dominated by the Geelong refining margin** — the single most volatile swing factor (full-year FY25 ~US$9.60/bbl after a strong Q4 rebound); the stock often reacts more to crack-spread moves and refining-margin commentary than to the headline result.
- **Convenience as the stabiliser** — the OTR/Reddy Express convenience network is intended to grow a steadier, higher-margin earnings base that the market can capitalise more reliably than refining profits.
- **Through-cycle read** — because of refining-cycle volatility, the franchise is better valued on normalised through-cycle refining margins, retail volumes and free cash flow than on a single year's GAAP earnings (hence the negative trailing P/E).
- **Leverage overhang** — elevated post-OTR leverage (D/E ~1.6x) means the equity is geared to both refining margins and balance-sheet risk; de-leveraging and integration progress are key re-rating levers.
- **Dividend** — Viva pays a dividend (a final dividend of 0.94 cents per share was declared as part of FY25 capital returns), but capital-return capacity is tied to the refining cycle and the convenience build-out.

## Business Model & Segments

Viva Energy reports through three segments:

- **Convenience & Mobility** — Australia's largest fuel and convenience retail network, with more than 1,330 service stations. Operates under the Shell, Coles Express (progressively rebranding as Reddy Express), Liberty, Westside, OTR, S24 and Smokemart & Gift Box (SMGB) brands. The acquisitions of Coles Express (May 2023) and the South Australian OTR Group (2024) transformed this segment into an integrated convenience-led retailer.

- **Commercial & Industrial** — supplies fuels, lubricants, polypropylene and specialty hydrocarbon products to aviation, marine, transport, resources, construction, agriculture, manufacturing and wholesale customers. Includes wholesale brands such as Reliable Petroleum, Mogas Regional and Ausfuel.

- **Energy & Infrastructure** — the Geelong Refinery, which refines crude oil into petroleum products, plus manufacture of bitumen, aviation gasoline (AvGas), aromatic and aliphatic solvents, and polypropylene. Includes Australia's largest fuel import terminal and pipeline network (24 terminals nationwide) and related storage infrastructure.

## History

- **Early 20th century**: Shell Australia established as the local downstream business of Royal Dutch Shell, operating refining and retail across Australia (approximately 120 years of heritage).
- **February 2014**: Vitol signed a binding agreement with Shell to acquire its Australian downstream business (excluding aviation) for approximately A$2.9 billion.
- **August 2014**: Vitol completed the acquisition and launched Viva Energy Australia to own the Geelong Refinery, nationwide import terminals and pipelines, and more than 1,100 Shell-branded retail sites.
- **17 July 2018**: Viva Energy listed on the ASX in a A$2.65 billion IPO — the largest Australian IPO in four years at the time.
- **2020–2021**: Geelong Refinery secured Australian Government Fuel Security Services Payment support, committing to operate the refinery through 2030 and beyond.
- **May 2023**: Completed the acquisition of the Coles Express retail business, adding more than 700 stores and creating Australia's largest single-operator fuel and convenience retail network.
- **2024**: Completed the acquisition of South Australia-based OTR Group for approximately A$1.15 billion, subject to divestiture of 25 Coles Express sites under ACCC undertakings.
- **2024–2025**: Launched the Reddy Express brand, progressively replacing Coles Express, and announced the broader OTR-led convenience repositioning.

- **Reddy Express rollout**: Continued progressive conversion of Coles Express sites to the Reddy Express brand.
- **Strategic review**: Ongoing integration of OTR back-office systems, supply chain and loyalty programmes into the Viva Energy platform.
