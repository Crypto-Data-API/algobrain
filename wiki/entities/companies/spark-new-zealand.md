**Spark New Zealand Limited** (NZX/ASX: SPK) is New Zealand's largest integrated telecommunications and digital-services company, dual-listed on the [[asx-limited|ASX]] and NZX and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. Headquartered at "Spark City" in Auckland, it delivers mobile, broadband, fixed voice, IT and cloud services across consumer and enterprise markets. For traders it is a defensive, high-yield [[communication-services|Communication Services]] name currently mid-way through a deep cost-out and divestment programme to defend cash flow and the dividend — the New Zealand structural analogue to Australia's [[telstra]].

## Business Overview & Segments

Spark is the incumbent New Zealand telecom — the #1 mobile operator and a top-two fixed-broadband retailer — that has spent a decade pivoting from a legacy copper-and-voice utility into a connectivity-plus-digital-services group. Revenue is dominated by recurring mobile and broadband subscriptions; the strategic theme since 2023 has been a "back to connectivity" simplification: aggressive cost-out, headcount reduction, and divestment of non-core assets (most notably the partial sale of its data-centre arm) to protect free cash flow and a generous, partly-imputed dividend.

| Segment / line | What it is | Revenue mix (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **Mobile** | ~2.7M mobile connections, 5G network, postpaid/prepaid, handsets | Largest single business and the growth/defence engine — >NZ$1B annual revenue |
| **Broadband** | ~687k fixed connections across fibre, HFC and fixed-wireless access (FWA) | Large and stable; FWA is the strategic lever to bypass Chorus wholesale economics |
| **Voice** | Legacy fixed-line voice | Structurally declining; managed for cash, not growth |
| **IT Products & IT Services** | Enterprise/government hardware, managed services, cloud, cybersecurity | Lumpy, lower-margin; periodically rationalised |
| **High-tech & Data Centres** | Spark's data-centre platform (75% sold to Pacific Equity Partners, closed Jan 2026), IoT | Now an associate/infrastructure interest rather than a consolidated growth engine |
| **Procurement & Partners** | Wholesale and partner channels | Smaller, supporting line |

The durable franchise is the **mobile network plus spectrum and brand**; the bear-case overhang is that the most attractive infrastructure asset (data centres) has been partly sold and that broadband economics are squeezed between the regulated [[chorus]] fibre wholesale layer and price-led competition.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Spark's [[economic-moat|moat]] is **narrow but real**, resting on incumbency rather than technology leadership:

- **Spectrum and network scale** — owning the largest mobile network and spectrum holdings in a three-player market (Spark, One NZ/Vodafone, 2degrees) creates high fixed-cost barriers and pricing discipline that a structurally consolidated market rewards.
- **Brand and distribution** — the "Spark" brand (ex-Telecom) carries the broadest retail footprint and the deepest enterprise/government relationships in New Zealand.
- **Switching friction** — bundled mobile + broadband households and enterprise contracts create modest [[switching-costs]], though consumer churn is real in a price-competitive market.
- **Capital intensity as a barrier** — building competing 5G and fixed-wireless infrastructure is prohibitively expensive, protecting the incumbent's installed base.

The limits of the moat: New Zealand telecom is a **mature, regulated, price-competitive oligopoly** with little organic volume growth. Fibre access is wholesaled through [[chorus]] (the demerged network sibling), so Spark does not own the last-mile economics on most broadband — fixed-wireless is its lever to recapture margin. The moat defends cash flow and the dividend; it does not deliver compounding growth, which is why the equity story is "cost-out + capital return," not expansion.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Cost-out delivery** — the central value lever; headcount cut from ~5,291 (end-2024) to ~4,043, with EBITDAI margin recovery the proof point.
- **Fixed-wireless access (FWA)** — substituting Spark-owned 5G FWA for Chorus-wholesaled fibre to recapture broadband margin.
- **Mobile ARPU and 5G monetisation** — pricing discipline and data growth in a consolidated three-player market.
- **Capital recycling** — proceeds from the data-centre sale applied to debt reduction; further non-core divestments possible.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year (Feb) and full-year (Aug) results, EBITDAI and free-cash-flow guidance, dividend declarations and imputation level, mobile/broadband churn and ARPU prints, AUD/NZD moves (affecting the ASX line).

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Dominant incumbent in a rational, consolidated three-player mobile market with pricing discipline.
- Deep cost-out and asset-light pivot defend free cash flow; the high, partly-imputed dividend is the core attraction.
- Data-centre sale proceeds de-lever the balance sheet; FWA recaptures broadband margin from the Chorus wholesale layer.
- Defensive, low-beta Communication Services exposure with NZ$-linked cash flows — a yield/ballast holding.

### Bear case
- Mature market with negligible organic growth; the story is cost-out and capital return, not expansion.
- Dividend-sustainability is the central debate after recent cuts — if free cash flow underperforms guidance, the payout is at risk.
- Selling the data-centre stake removed the most attractive structural-growth asset for near-term cash.
- Price competition from 2degrees/One NZ pressures ARPU; legacy voice keeps shrinking; execution risk in the restructure.

## Key Risks

- **Dividend cut risk** — the dominant trading debate; a generous payout funded by recovering, not abundant, free cash flow.
- **Pricing/churn pressure** — NZ telecom is price-competitive; ARPU and churn trends drive the top line.
- **Legacy decline** — fixed voice and copper-era revenue continue to erode.
- **Execution risk** — delivering the cost-out and restructure without damaging service or share.
- **Regulatory** — NZ Commerce Commission oversight of telecom pricing and the Chorus fibre wholesale framework.
- **FX** — NZD-functional cash flows translated to an AUD-reported, AUD-listed line; AUD/NZD moves affect the ASX quote.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Spark trades as a **defensive, high-yield Communication Services utility-analogue**, not a growth stock. The investment case is the dividend and its sustainability, underwritten by the cost-out programme and balance-sheet de-leveraging.

- **Dividend-led total return** — the headline attraction is the yield (partly imputed for NZ/eligible holders); capital appreciation depends on cost-out delivery and multiple stability, not revenue growth.
- **Low beta / defensive** — moves less than the broad index; a portfolio-ballast and income holding rather than a high-octane name.
- **Liquidity** — moderate on the ASX line; primarily an equity/income vehicle, not an options market.
- **Catalysts move the multiple** — EBITDAI/free-cash-flow guidance and dividend decisions, not topline surprises, are what re-rate the stock.
- **FX overlay** — AUD/NZD shifts the ASX-quoted price for the same NZD fundamentals.

## Recent Developments

Spark is in the midst of a significant transformation programme focused on core connectivity:

- **1H FY26 results (period to 31 December 2025)**: Reported net profit up nearly 83% to approximately NZ$64 million; reported EBITDAI up 10.3% to approximately NZ$448 million; revenue down approximately 1% to NZ$1.9 billion.
- **Data centre divestment**: 75% interest in Spark's data centre (DC Co) — 23 MW of built capacity across 11 operating data centres plus development pipeline on Auckland's North Shore and expansions at Takanini — sold to Pacific Equity Partners. Transaction closed 30 January 2026. Proceeds applied to debt reduction.
- **Workforce restructure**: Headcount reduced from approximately 5,291 at the end of 2024 to approximately 4,043, a reduction of more than 1,200 roles.
- **Strategic partnerships**: Reinforced with Nokia, Infosys, Hewlett Packard Enterprise and Microsoft across network, IT and cloud delivery.
- **Refocus on connectivity**: Narrower operating footprint emphasising mobile and broadband while rationalising broader digital services portfolio.
- **FY26 guidance (reaffirmed)**: the board reaffirmed FY26 EBITDAI guidance of NZ$1,010m–NZ$1,070m and free-cash-flow guidance of NZ$290m–NZ$330m, and declared an H1 FY26 dividend of 8 cents per share (50% imputed) — the dividend-sustainability question being the central trading debate after recent cuts (Verified via Perplexity, 2026-06-10).
