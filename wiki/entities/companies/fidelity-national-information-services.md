Fidelity National Information Services (NYSE: FIS) is a financial-technology infrastructure provider whose software runs core banking, capital-markets and payment systems for thousands of banks worldwide. A constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]], it matters to traders as a serial corporate-restructuring story: the disastrous $43B Worldpay acquisition (2019), its write-down and partial divestiture (2023-2024), and the 2025 asset swap with [[global-payments|Global Payments]] have made it a recurring event-driven and value-trap debate. (Note: distinct from Fidelity Investments and from Fidelity National Financial, its former parent.)

## Business Overview & Segments

FIS sits in the [[financials|Financials]] sector (Transaction & Payment Processing) with a strong [[technology|technology]] character — its software runs core banking, capital-markets and payment systems for thousands of banks worldwide. It is a [[s-and-p-500]] constituent. Post-Worldpay, the business is refocusing on two core segments.

| Segment | What it is | Role / characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| **Banking Solutions** | Core processing platforms, digital banking, card issuing and fraud tools for US/international banks | Sticky, recurring-revenue, high-switching-cost contracts — the franchise core |
| **Capital Market Solutions** | Trading, treasury, risk and post-trade software for buy-side, sell-side and corporates | Faster-growing, higher-margin segment |
| **(Exited) Merchant / Worldpay** | Merchant acquiring via Worldpay (~$43B, 2019) | Written down and divested 2023-26; FIS is exiting acquiring entirely |

- **Worldpay history**: FIS bought merchant acquirer Worldpay for ~$43B in 2019, took a ~$17.6B write-down in 2023, sold a 55% majority stake to GTCR (deal closed Jan 2024, valuing Worldpay at ~$18.5B), and retained 45%.
- The accounting scars of the Worldpay saga explain the strange 5y-average figures in the fundamentals below (5y avg net margin of -132% reflects the giant 2022 impairment; latest-year figures are the representative ones).

## 2025-2026 Developments

- **April 2025 — three-way asset swap**: FIS agreed to sell its remaining 45% Worldpay stake (to [[global-payments|Global Payments]], which acquired all of Worldpay) and simultaneously acquire Global Payments' Issuer Solutions business for ~$13.5B enterprise value. The swap refocuses FIS on bank/issuer processing and exits merchant acquiring entirely. Deal progression through 2025-2026 has been the dominant stock narrative (as of June 2026). (Verified via Perplexity, June 2026.)
- **Capital returns**: ongoing buybacks plus a dividend (see payout figures below) — management has pitched the post-swap FIS as a steadier, higher-recurring-revenue compounder.
- **Trader watch items**: integration/execution noise around the Issuer Solutions closing, client attrition risk during migration, and whether organic growth (historically low-single-digit) can re-rate the stock from its ~15x trailing PE.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

FIS's [[economic-moat|moat]] is anchored in bank-software switching costs but has been **repeatedly damaged by capital-allocation missteps**:

- **Switching costs (Banking & Capital Markets)** — core-banking and capital-markets platforms are mission-critical, deeply integrated and extremely costly to migrate; contracts are multi-year and recurring, the durable heart of the moat. See [[switching-costs]].
- **Oligopoly** — US core banking is a three-player market ([[fiserv|Fiserv]], FIS, Jack Henry); incumbency and regulatory/compliance entanglement raise barriers.
- **Breadth in capital markets** — a broad post-trade/treasury/risk software suite serving buy- and sell-side is hard to replicate.

The recurring problem is **strategy and capital allocation, not the underlying moat**: the ~$43B Worldpay acquisition and ~$17.6B write-down destroyed value and distracted management, and serial restructuring has made FIS a chronic event-driven/value-trap debate. The post-Worldpay, issuer-focused FIS is a cleaner business — but execution credibility is the swing factor.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Worldpay/Issuer Solutions asset swap** — exiting acquiring and buying Global Payments' Issuer Solutions (~$13.5B EV) refocuses FIS on bank/issuer processing; integration progress is the dominant narrative.
- **Capital Markets growth** — the higher-margin, faster-growing segment is the cleanest organic driver.
- **Recurring banking revenue** — sticky core-processing contracts provide a steady floor.
- **Capital returns** — ongoing buybacks plus a dividend; management pitches a steadier, higher-recurring-revenue compounder post-swap.
- **Re-rating optionality** — any organic-growth re-acceleration off a ~15x trailing PE would re-rate the stock.
- **Recurring catalysts** — M&A closings/integration milestones, quarterly earnings (organic growth, margins), and any activist involvement.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- The post-swap FIS is a cleaner, higher-recurring-revenue bank/issuer-processing business at a modest ~15x trailing PE.
- Sticky core-banking and capital-markets contracts provide a durable, recurring floor with high switching costs.
- Buybacks + dividend return capital; a successful Issuer Solutions integration adds scale and growth.
- Exiting merchant acquiring removes the Worldpay overhang and simplifies the story.

### Bear case
- Chronic value trap: serial restructuring, low single-digit organic growth and a history of value-destructive M&A.
- Integration/migration risk on the Issuer Solutions deal; client attrition during platform migrations.
- The capital-allocation track record (Worldpay) undermines trust in management's next big move.
- A low multiple can stay low if organic growth never re-accelerates.

## Key Risks

- **Capital-allocation history** — the Worldpay saga (~$43B in, ~$17.6B write-down) is the defining risk; the market discounts another misstep.
- **Integration/execution** — Issuer Solutions closing, integration noise and client-attrition risk during migrations.
- **Low organic growth** — historically low-single-digit; a stalled re-rating keeps the stock a value trap.
- **Leverage** — D/E ~0.7x with a weak current ratio (0.85x); refinancing/covenant sensitivity.
- **Event-driven volatility** — deal headlines and restructuring milestones drive discrete repricings more than fundamentals.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

FIS trades as a **serial-restructuring, event-driven value name** rather than a steady compounder, and its history makes the market skeptical of management:

- **Low multiple, value-trap debate** — a ~15x trailing PE reflects low organic growth and the Worldpay scars; the bull case is a re-rating on a cleaner post-swap business, the bear case is a permanent value trap.
- **Event-driven** — M&A closings, activist involvement (historically) and restructuring milestones drive discrete repricings more than macro beta.
- **Dividend + buyback** — pays a dividend (the ~4.2% 5y-average API yield in the data block reflects the API definition, not the market yield) plus buybacks; capital return supports the floor while growth is debated.
- **Optics caveat** — the 5y-average figures (e.g. -132% net margin) are distorted by the 2022 Worldpay impairment; the latest-year figures and forward (post-swap) economics are the representative anchor, not trailing averages.
- **Liquidity/correlated names** — liquid large-cap chain; IV spikes around deal headlines and earnings; moves with the payments cohort [[global-payments]], [[fiserv]], [[mastercard]], [[visa]].
