GE Asset Management (GEAM) was the investment management arm of [[general-electric|General Electric]], managing GE's pension fund and institutional assets. It was sold to [[state-street|State Street]] in 2016 and no longer exists as an independent firm — it survives in the wiki primarily as a case study in corporate pension management and as a reference point in ITPM content on professional trading careers.

## History

- GEAM's roots trace to GE's in-house pension investment operation (GE Investment Corporation); it was organized as a registered investment adviser headquartered in Stamford, Connecticut.
- At its peak it was one of the largest corporate-affiliated asset managers in the US, running GE's defined-benefit pension plan plus third-party institutional mandates (insurance, endowments, other pensions) across equities, fixed income and alternatives — roughly **$100-110B in AUM** in its final years.
- **March 2016** — GE announced the sale of GEAM to **State Street** (State Street Global Advisors) for up to **~$485M**, as part of CEO Jeff Immelt's broad divestiture program (alongside the GE Capital wind-down) refocusing GE on industrial businesses. The deal closed **July 1, 2016**; most GEAM staff transferred to SSGA. GE retained pension *obligations* — only the management business was sold.

## Trading and Career Lessons

- **Pension underfunding as an equity overhang**: even after selling the manager, GE's pension deficit (peaking around $28-31B GAAP underfunding in 2016-2017, among the largest in corporate America) became a major bear thesis on GE stock during its 2017-2018 collapse. Asset-management fees were a sideshow next to the liability. Lesson: when analyzing industrials, read the pension footnote.
- **Corporate asset managers are strategic orphans**: GEAM's sale illustrates that captive asset managers get divested when the parent needs cash or focus — a recurring M&A pattern (cf. other corporate pension managers absorbed by scale players).
- **Career context (ITPM)**: referenced in ITPM material as an example of the institutional buy-side career path — large corporate pension managers like GEAM historically trained portfolio managers who later moved to mutual funds and hedge funds.
