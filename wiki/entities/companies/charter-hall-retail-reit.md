**Charter Hall Retail REIT** (ASX: CQR) is a retail [[reits|real estate investment trust]] listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. It owns a national portfolio of convenience-oriented [[real-estate|retail property]], is externally managed by [[charter-hall|Charter Hall Group]], and is positioned as Australia's leading owner of property leased to convenience retailers: a mix of neighbourhood and sub-regional shopping centres anchored by major supermarkets (Woolworths, Coles, Aldi), plus a substantial convenience net-lease retail portfolio (service stations, fuel, large-format retail) leased on long, CPI-linked terms to investment-grade tenants such as bp, Ampol, Bunnings and Endeavour Group. The non-discretionary, supermarket/CPI-linked income base makes CQR one of the more defensive names in the A-REIT sector — held mainly for a [[franking-credits|fully/partly franked]] distribution yield that, like the [[net-tangible-assets|NTA]], is sensitive to the [[interest-rates|rate]] cycle.

## Business overview & segments

CQR's portfolio is structured into two principal pools:

- **Convenience Shopping Centres:** Predominantly supermarket-anchored centres with Woolworths, Coles or Aldi majors, supported by specialty retail, services and food. Non-discretionary spend characterises the tenant mix.
- **Convenience Net Lease Retail:** Service station, fuel and large-format retail assets leased on long, typically CPI-linked or fixed-escalation leases to tenants including bp, Ampol, Bunnings, Kmart and Endeavour Group.

| Segment | What it is | Income character |
|---|---|---|
| **Convenience Shopping Centres** | Neighbourhood and sub-regional centres anchored by Woolworths, Coles or Aldi supermarkets, with specialty retail, services and food | Non-discretionary, supermarket-anchored foot traffic; specialty rents with turnover overage; defensive but cyclically exposed to consumer spending |
| **Convenience Net Lease Retail** | Service stations, fuel and large-format retail (incl. the bp Australia portfolio) leased to investment-grade tenants — bp, Ampol, Bunnings, Kmart, Endeavour Group | Long-WALE, single-tenant, CPI-linked or fixed-escalation rent; bond-like and the lower-volatility growth engine — now ~49% of portfolio value |

The trust's strategy emphasises resilient rental cash flows, disciplined capital recycling out of lower-growth assets into higher-growth convenience-net-lease property, and partnering with [[charter-hall|Charter Hall Group]]'s active asset-management and development capability. Revenue is generated almost entirely from contracted rental income, which dampens earnings volatility but caps organic upside to lease escalators (largely CPI-linked) plus leasing spreads and development.

## Economic moat & competitive position

CQR's [[economic-moat|moat]] is **defensive rather than wide**. The durable advantages are real but bounded:

- **Non-discretionary, supermarket-anchored income** — neighbourhood and sub-regional centres anchored by Woolworths, Coles and Aldi capture essential, repeat foot traffic that holds up through the consumer cycle and is structurally less exposed to e-commerce than discretionary fashion/department-store malls. This is the source of the income resilience the market pays a premium for.
- **Long-WALE convenience net lease** — the fuel/service-station and large-format pool (including the bp Australia portfolio) is let on long leases to investment-grade tenants with CPI-linked or fixed escalators, giving bond-like, inflation-protected cash flows and built-in organic growth.
- **External management by [[charter-hall|Charter Hall Group]]** — access to one of Australia's largest property platforms confers scale, deal origination, development capability and financing relationships a sub-scale REIT could not match alone.
- **Scale within its niche** — positioned as Australia's leading owner of convenience-retail property, with the density of relationships (supermarket majors, fuel retailers) that comes with it.

The limits matter: retail property is a **replaceable, commodity asset** with no pricing power beyond the contracted lease, returns are highly sensitive to the [[interest-rates|rate]] and [[capitalization-rate|cap-rate]] cycle, and external management introduces a fee drag and potential conflicts (see Key risks). CQR competes for both tenants and acquisition assets, and its cost of capital — not a proprietary edge — is the swing factor on whether it can grow accretively.

## Growth drivers & catalysts

- **Capital recycling into convenience net lease** — selling lower-growth, more discretionary assets and redeploying into long-WALE net-lease retail (now ~49% of portfolio value, up from 39% at 30 June 2025) lifts WALE, defensiveness and embedded escalation.
- **CPI-linked rent escalation** — a large share of leases escalate with inflation or at fixed rates, giving contracted organic income growth without re-leasing risk.
- **Development, remixing and leasing spreads** — centre redevelopment, specialty remix and positive re-leasing on supermarket and specialty space.
- **Cap-rate compression on rate cuts** — as the [[interest-rates|rate]] cycle eases, [[capitalization-rate|cap rates]] can tighten and lift property values and [[net-tangible-assets|NTA]]; H1 FY26 already showed like-for-like cap rates tightening 11bps to 5.55%.
- **Accretive acquisitions** — bolt-on convenience-retail and net-lease deals where Charter Hall's platform sources off-market opportunities and CQR's cost of capital allows accretion.
- **Lower cost of debt** — the new ~A$1.6 billion facility (eight lenders) extended weighted-average debt maturity to ~4.0 years and cut the weighted-average margin ~40bps, reducing interest expense and supporting distributions.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year (February) and full-year (August) results with operating EPS, [[net-tangible-assets|NTA]], occupancy and cap-rate marks; distribution guidance; RBA rate decisions; asset sales versus book value (the proof-point for any NTA discount).

## Bull case vs Bear case

### Bull case
- Defensive, non-discretionary, supermarket-anchored income holds up through the consumer cycle and is relatively insulated from e-commerce.
- Growing long-WALE net-lease pool (~49% of portfolio) adds bond-like, CPI-linked cash flows and lifts portfolio quality.
- Record ~99.1% occupancy and tightening cap rates (H1 FY26) signal a healthy portfolio and a possible valuation inflection as rates ease.
- Conservative ~29% gearing and a refinanced, longer-dated, cheaper debt book give balance-sheet headroom and protect distributions.
- Trades below NTA (P/B ~0.82) on a high distribution yield — mean-reversion upside if the market re-rates defensive A-REITs as rates fall.

### Bear case
- Returns are rate-driven: higher-for-longer rates pressure cap rates, [[net-tangible-assets|NTA]] and the relative appeal of the yield.
- Supermarket-tenant and major-anchor concentration — a few majors (Woolworths, Coles, Aldi, bp, Ampol) drive a large share of income.
- Consumer-spending softness hits specialty rents, turnover rent and re-leasing spreads even if anchors are stable.
- External management ([[charter-hall|Charter Hall]]) imposes fees and potential conflicts versus an internally managed REIT.
- Limited organic upside beyond CPI escalators and development; growth depends on accretive acquisitions, which depend on cost of capital.

## Key risks

- **Interest-rate / cap-rate sensitivity** — the dominant risk; higher rates lift cap rates, cut property values and [[net-tangible-assets|NTA]], and make the distribution yield less competitive versus cash and bonds.
- **Tenant / anchor concentration** — heavy reliance on a small set of supermarket majors and net-lease counterparties (Woolworths, Coles, Aldi, bp, Ampol, Bunnings, Endeavour Group); a major tenant's credit or strategy shift would matter.
- **Consumer-spending softness** — weak retail conditions pressure specialty income, occupancy and re-leasing spreads.
- **External-management conflicts and fees** — base and performance fees paid to [[charter-hall|Charter Hall]] reduce returns, and the manager's incentives may not always align with unitholders.
- **Gearing in a downturn** — although ~29% gearing is conservative, falling asset values raise look-through gearing and can pressure covenants and distributions in a severe property downturn.

## Valuation & how the stock trades

CQR is a yield-and-NTA instrument, valued on REIT-specific metrics rather than an equity earnings multiple. The framework (qualitative; figures below are those already reported in this page, not forecasts):

- **FFO / operating earnings vs statutory profit** — the market values CQR on recurring **operating earnings / funds from operations** (H1 FY26 operating earnings A$75.6m, ~13.0 cents per unit; FY26 guidance ≥26.4cps operating EPS), not statutory net profit, which swings with non-cash property revaluations.
- **NTA and discount to NTA** — units trade around or below net tangible assets (P/B ~0.82 per the data block), so the discount-to-NTA is a core valuation lens; asset sales at or above book are the proof-point that the discount is too wide.
- **Cap-rate and rate sensitivity** — property values and NTA move inversely with [[capitalization-rate|capitalisation rates]], which track the [[interest-rates|rate]] cycle; the like-for-like cap rate was ~5.55% at H1 FY26 after tightening 11bps. Falling rates are the bull catalyst, rising rates the bear.
- **Distribution yield and franking** — the investment case is income-led: a high distribution yield (FY26 distribution guidance 25.5cps) with partial [[franking-credits|franking]] that lifts the after-tax yield for Australian-resident and SMSF investors.
- **Defensiveness / low beta** — non-discretionary, supermarket-anchored income gives CQR a lower-beta, more defensive profile than discretionary mall REITs ([[scentre-group]], [[vicinity-centres]]); it tends to outperform on a defensive rotation and lag in a risk-on, discretionary-retail recovery.
- **How it trades** — moves with the A-REIT index and inversely with AU bond yields; the key prints are half-year/full-year results (operating EPS, NTA, occupancy, cap rates), distribution guidance, refinancing terms, and capital-recycling/M&A news. Liquidity is mid-cap with no meaningful single-stock options market — exposure is typically cash equity or via A-REIT sector ETFs.

## History

Charter Hall Retail REIT was established on 21 July 1995, making it one of the longer-established retail REITs in Australia. It became part of the Charter Hall platform through Charter Hall Group's reorganisation of its listed retail fund following the acquisition of management rights from Macquarie in 2010. Over the subsequent decade CQR progressively repositioned the portfolio away from larger discretionary-exposed sub-regional centres toward convenience-based assets, and in recent years built out a substantial convenience-net-lease retail platform via the bp Australia service station portfolio (co-owned with Charter Hall Long WALE REIT) and other long-leased acquisitions.

## Recent Developments

For the half year ended 31 December 2025 (H1 FY26), CQR reported operating earnings of A$75.6 million, equivalent to 13.0 cents per unit, up 3.4% on H1 FY25. Portfolio occupancy reached a record 99.1% and weighted average capitalisation rates tightened by 11 basis points on a like-for-like basis to 5.55%. The convenience shopping centre portfolio recorded net valuation growth of A$87 million (+3.8%) and the convenience net lease retail portfolio saw growth of A$66 million (+3.0%). The net lease retail portfolio now represents 49% of total portfolio value, up from 39% at 30 June 2025. Balance sheet gearing was 29.2%, and CQR agreed terms to refinance into a new A$1.6 billion facility with eight lenders, extending weighted average debt maturity to 4.0 years and reducing the weighted average margin by 40 basis points. FY26 operating earnings guidance is no less than 26.4 cents per unit (up 4%) with distribution guidance of 25.5 cents per unit (up 3.3%).
