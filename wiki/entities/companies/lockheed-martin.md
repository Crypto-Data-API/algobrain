Lockheed Martin (NYSE: LMT) is the world's largest [[defense]] contractor by revenue and prime contractor of the F-35 fighter program, a core [[industrials|Industrials]] constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]]. For traders it is the benchmark defense-budget and geopolitical-risk proxy: a high-dividend, government-revenue compounder whose stock moves on Pentagon budgets, conflict headlines, and program-execution charges. Its [[economic-moat|economic moat]] rests on sole-source prime-contractor positions and multi-decade platform programs — above all the F-35, a multi-nation installed base that throws off a long-tail sustainment annuity.

## Business Overview & Segments

Lockheed reports four segments. **Aeronautics** — the largest — is built on combat aircraft (the F-35 Lightning II, F-16 Fighting Falcon, F-22 Raptor sustainment) and the C-130 airlifter. **Missiles and Fire Control (MFC)** spans air-and-missile defense (PAC-3 interceptors), precision strike (HIMARS, GMLRS, JASSM/LRASM, Javelin) and hypersonics. **Rotary and Mission Systems (RMS)** houses Sikorsky helicopters (Black Hawk, CH-53K) and the [[radar]]/combat-system franchise (Aegis, sensors). **Space** delivers satellites, missile-warning constellations, strategic missiles (Trident II/Fleet Ballistic Missile), and the Orion crew vehicle. Around three-quarters of revenue comes from the US government, with foreign military sales (FMS) the chief growth lever beyond it.

The **F-35 is the single most important driver: 27% of total consolidated sales in 2025 and 67% of Aeronautics sales** — making F-35 production lots, sustainment contracts, and foreign sales the core variables in any LMT model. The high ROE (~75%) in the fundamentals below reflects a buyback-shrunk equity base, not operational leverage risk; coverage and cash generation remain strong.

## 2025-2026 Developments

- **FY2025 (reported 29 Jan 2026)** — Sales up US$1.6B (+6%) YoY, driven by US$1.9B of higher F-35 production/sustainment volume, partly offset by lower classified-program volume. **Operating profit fell 17% (-US$437M)**, weighed down by program charges booked during 2025 (Q2 2025 charges on a classified Aeronautics program and helicopter programs).
- **Missile demand supercycle** — Continued elevated orders for munitions (PAC-3, HIMARS, JASSM) on US/European rearmament and Middle East conflict replenishment; new F-35 contract awards announced through late 2025.
- **Q1 2026 (quarter ended 29 Mar 2026)** — Sales -1% YoY (lower classified volume -US$325M and F-16 -US$145M, offset by F-35 sustainment +US$325M); operating profit -14% on US$125M of unfavorable F-16 and US$55M C-130 profit adjustments, partially offset by US$130M favorable F-35 adjustments.
- **Dividend** — US$13.67/share paid over the latest fiscal year (yield ~2.5-3%); 20+ year streak of annual increases continues.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Lockheed's [[economic-moat|moat]] is structural and regulatory rather than commercial, and it is among the most durable in the [[industrials|Industrials]] sector:

- **Prime-contractor / sole-source program lock-in** — Major platforms are awarded to a single prime for the program's life. Once Lockheed wins (e.g. the F-35), the customer is committed for decades; there is no competitive re-bid for the airframe.
- **F-35 installed base + sustainment annuity** — The F-35 is a multi-decade, multi-nation fleet expected to run into the 2070s. Production is only half the story: each aircraft generates a long-tail, high-visibility stream of sustainment, upgrade, and spares revenue — an annuity that competitors cannot contest.
- **High [[switching-costs|switching costs]]** — Re-competing a fielded weapons platform means re-certifying airworthiness, retraining crews, rebuilding a global supply/depot chain, and re-integrating allied logistics. The cost and risk of switching primes is prohibitive, which is the source of the moat's durability.
- **Classified-program barriers** — A large slice of Aeronautics (Skunk Works) and Space revenue is classified. Security clearances, accredited facilities, and decades of program relationships are barriers no new entrant can clear quickly.
- **Government-revenue concentration as a moat** — ~75% of revenue is US-government; the Pentagon's preference for a small number of qualified, cleared, scale primes entrenches the incumbent oligopoly ([[defense]] consolidation since the 1990s left only a handful of primes).
- **Scale and barriers to entry** — Capital intensity, regulatory (ITAR/security) overhead, and the multi-decade program cycle make greenfield entry effectively impossible; the competitive set is fixed.

The flip side: the same government dependence caps pricing power (cost-plus and negotiated margins), exposes the firm to budget and continuing-resolution risk, and concentrates demand in one buyer.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Missile/munitions supercycle** — US and allied stockpile replenishment (PAC-3, HIMARS/GMLRS, JASSM/LRASM, Javelin) after Ukraine and Middle East conflicts; MFC is the fastest-growing segment with elevated book-to-bill.
- **European / NATO rearmament** — Sustained budget growth across NATO members lifts FMS demand for F-35, missiles, and air-defense systems.
- **F-35 production + sustainment** — New production lots, the global sustainment ramp as fleets mature, and Block 4/TR-3 upgrade content.
- **Hypersonics** — Conventional Prompt Strike, ARRW/glide bodies, and long-range fires as a new program category.
- **Golden Dome / homeland missile defense** — US layered missile-defense architecture as a multi-year demand pool for interceptors, sensors, and space-based tracking.
- **Space** — Nuclear modernization (Trident II/FBM), Next-Gen missile-warning constellations, and human spaceflight (Orion).
- **International FMS sales** — Foreign military sales are the primary lever to grow beyond the ~75% US-government base.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly results (Jan/Apr/Jul/Oct), the US defense budget / NDAA / continuing-resolution cycle, F-35 lot negotiations and delivery pauses, and geopolitical escalations (the stock typically gaps up on conflict onset).

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Multi-decade, sole-source program backlog (F-35, missiles, space) gives unusually high revenue visibility for a cyclical-looking name.
- Missile/munitions supercycle and NATO rearmament drive a structural, not cyclical, demand step-up in MFC.
- Defensive, low-beta cash compounder: 20+ year dividend-growth streak plus aggressive buybacks return most free cash flow.
- Geopolitical optionality — the stock is a near-pure hedge that gaps up on conflict onset and rising defense budgets.
- Sustainment annuity on a growing global F-35 fleet underpins decades of high-visibility, high-margin service revenue.

### Bear case
- Government concentration caps pricing power and exposes the P&L to budget caps, continuing resolutions, and shutdown risk.
- Fixed-price and classified programs carry recurring charge risk — 2025's Aeronautics and helicopter charges cut operating profit 17%.
- F-35 cost, schedule, and TR-3/Block 4 execution risk on the company's single most important program.
- Thin margins (single-digit net) and a buyback-shrunk, highly-levered equity base (D/E >3x) leave little cushion.
- Risk-on tech rallies leave defensive defense names behind; valuation re-rates only on conflict/budget narratives.

## Key Risks

- **Program charges** — Fixed-price and classified-program cost overruns are the recurring idiosyncratic risk; 2025 saw charges on a classified Aeronautics program and helicopter programs that cut operating profit 17%.
- **Budget / continuing-resolution risk** — Revenue rides the US defense budget; CRs, debt-ceiling fights, and shutdowns delay awards and obligations.
- **F-35 cost and schedule** — The company's largest program faces upgrade (TR-3/Block 4) execution risk and periodic delivery pauses; any sustained problem hits the core of the model.
- **Customer concentration** — ~75% US-government revenue means one buyer with monopsony pricing power.
- **Fixed-price contract risk** — Inflation and supply-chain stress on firm-fixed-price work compress margins with limited recourse.
- **Balance sheet / leverage** — High D/E (3.23x) and a buyback-shrunk equity base amplify the impact of any earnings shortfall; rising rates raise refinancing and discount-rate pressure on a yield-led name.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

LMT trades as a defensive, income-and-backlog compounder rather than a growth multiple. The key valuation anchor is the **defense backlog** and **book-to-bill**: a backlog worth more than a year of sales and consistent ≥1.0x book-to-bill underwrite forward revenue visibility, so investors pay for durability of the order book, not near-term growth. Mechanically:

- **Defensive, low-beta character** — underperforms in risk-on tech rallies, outperforms on conflict and budget-growth narratives; it is the benchmark expression of the defense-budget and geopolitical-risk theme.
- **Geopolitical proxy** — the stock typically gaps up on conflict onset and rising defense budgets, and de-rates on peace dividends or budget-cap fears.
- **Dividend + buybacks** — ~2.5-3% yield with a 20+ year streak of annual increases, plus large buybacks; capital return (not growth) is the total-return story, which makes the name modestly rate-sensitive.
- **Program-charge idiosyncratic risk** — the principal source of single-name downside surprises (vs peers) is fixed-price/classified charges, as in 2025.
- **ETF membership** — a core holding in defense/aerospace ETFs (ITA, XAR); flows in those complexes move LMT alongside [[rtx]], [[northrop-grumman]], and [[general-dynamics]].
- **Options** — liquid monthly chains; defense names see IV spikes around geopolitical events more than around earnings.
