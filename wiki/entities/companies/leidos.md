Leidos (NYSE: LDOS) is the largest U.S. government IT and [[defense]] services contractor, spun out of SAIC's heritage business in 2013, providing digital modernization, cyber, intelligence/ISR processing, hypersonics support, and managed health services (notably VA disability exams and the DHMSM military health record) to defense, intelligence, and civil agencies. Classified within the [[industrials|Industrials]] sector and a constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]], it is a lower-beta way to trade U.S. government spending and [[defense]]-tech/AI adoption — a "picks-and-shovels" services name whose revenue tracks federal budgets, recompete cycles, and book-to-bill rather than big-ticket platform procurement. The investment case rests on a deep, sticky backlog, an entrenched cleared workforce, and a valuation that trades at a discount to the hardware-heavy [[defense]] primes.

## Business Overview & Segments

Leidos's revenue is overwhelmingly U.S. government **services** — a mix of cost-plus, time-and-materials, and fixed-price contracts — rather than product sales, which gives it lower margins but far steadier, contract-backed cash flows than a platform manufacturer. The Health & Civil segment (anchored by VA medical disability examinations) has been the standout margin driver in recent years. The **NorthStar 2030** strategy, unveiled in 2025, reorients investment around five growth pillars: space and maritime, energy infrastructure, digital modernization and cyber, mission software, and managed health services.

| Segment | What it is | Revenue mix (qualitative) |
|---|---|---|
| **National Security & Digital** | Digital modernization, cyber, intelligence/[[defense]] mission IT, ISR data processing, software and systems integration for defense and intelligence agencies | The largest segment — the core government-IT franchise |
| **Health & Civil** | VA medical disability examinations, federal health IT, life-sciences and civil-agency programs | Smaller by revenue but the **standout margin driver**; VA exam volumes are the key swing factor |
| **Commercial & International** | Security-detection products (airport/border scanning), international defense and civil work outside the core U.S. franchise | Smaller, more product- and globally-exposed leg |
| **Defense Systems** | Maritime/undersea systems, hypersonics support, autonomy, sensors and mission hardware — the more product-like, engineering-intensive work | Growth pillar under NorthStar 2030 (space/maritime, mission software) |

The business is best understood as an **outsourced engineering and IT workforce** for the federal government: success is measured by backlog, book-to-bill, and the ability to win and re-win long-duration contracts, not by unit shipments.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Leidos's [[economic-moat|economic moat]] is narrow-to-moderate and built on **incumbency and qualification barriers** rather than proprietary technology:

- **Backlog visibility & embedded incumbency** — long-duration government contracts (often 5–10 years with option periods) create a large, sticky backlog. Incumbents enjoy a structural advantage at recompete because they already run the program, hold the relationships, and carry the lowest transition risk.
- **Cleared workforce & security clearances** — a major share of the workforce holds active security clearances (Secret/Top Secret/SCI). Clearances take months-to-years and significant cost to obtain, so a cleared, program-experienced staff is a scarce asset that imposes real [[switching-costs]] on the customer and a hiring barrier on would-be competitors.
- **Past-performance qualification** — federal contracting weights demonstrated past performance heavily; only firms with a track record on similar programs can credibly bid the largest awards, which entrenches the handful of scaled primes.
- **Scale** — as the largest pure government-IT services contractor, Leidos can staff and bid mega-awards (multi-billion-dollar IDIQ/task-order vehicles) that subscale competitors cannot, and amortize bid-and-proposal and overhead costs across a wider base.

The moat's limit: services contracts are competitively rebid, pricing is scrutinized, and the work is labor- rather than IP-driven — so the edge is durability and qualification, not pricing power.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **NorthStar 2030 pillars** — concentrated investment in space and maritime, energy infrastructure, digital modernization and cyber, mission software, and managed health services; the strategy aims to shift mix toward higher-growth, higher-margin work.
- **Defense-tech & [[artificial-intelligence|AI]] adoption** — embedding AI/ML into ISR processing, cyber defense, and the Trusted Mission AI portfolio as agencies modernize.
- **Missile-defense C2 / sensing** — positioning in Golden Dome-adjacent missile-defense command-and-control and sensing work as a new demand pool.
- **Backlog & book-to-bill** — record backlog conversion and funded-backlog growth are the primary forward indicators; large recompetes and new awards (e.g., the MACRO II task order) feed the pipeline.
- **Federal IT budgets & modernization mandates** — secular push to modernize legacy government systems, plus the recurring Health & Civil annuity (VA exam volumes).
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly book-to-bill and backlog prints, VA exam volume trends, protest outcomes on mega-awards, federal budget-resolution and continuing-resolution timing.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- **Cheap relative to [[defense]] primes** — trades around a low-teens P/E (~13x noted in Fundamentals), a clear discount to platform primes and to higher-margin peer Booz Allen.
- **Record, sticky backlog** — multi-year funded and total backlog provides strong revenue visibility and recompete momentum.
- **Health & Civil margins** — the VA disability-exam franchise has been a high-margin, growing annuity lifting blended profitability.
- **Capital returns** — strong free cash flow funds ongoing buybacks and a growing dividend.
- **Secular [[defense]]/AI tailwind** — modernization, cyber, and AI adoption across agencies expand the addressable services market.

### Bear case
- **Federal-spending dependence** — government shutdowns, continuing resolutions, and DOGE-style efficiency drives can delay awards, defer billings, and pressure the federal IT budget.
- **Recompete risk** — losing a large incumbent contract at rebid is the single biggest idiosyncratic threat to revenue and backlog.
- **Leverage** — debt-to-equity around 1.06x (see Fundamentals) is elevated versus lower-levered industrials, raising rate sensitivity.
- **Health & Civil normalization** — VA exam volumes and margins could normalize off recent highs, removing a key profit tailwind.
- **Lower-growth services** — a labor-driven services model has structurally lower margins and slower growth than product/IP-led defense or software names.

## Key Risks

- **Federal budget / shutdown / continuing-resolution risk** — and DOGE-style federal efficiency cuts that could shrink or delay civilian IT and services spending.
- **Contract recompete losses & protests** — incumbency helps but is not guaranteed; mega-award protests can stall revenue recognition for quarters.
- **Health & Civil margin normalization** — dependence on VA disability-exam volumes makes that segment's margin a concentrated swing factor.
- **Leverage** — balance-sheet debt (D/E ~1.06x) limits flexibility relative to cash-rich peers if rates rise or cash flow softens.
- **Customer concentration** — revenue is overwhelmingly U.S. government, so policy, appropriations, and administration priorities dominate the outlook.

## Trading Relevance

- **Gov-services basket:** trades with Booz Allen ([[booz-allen-hamilton]], BAH), CACI, SAIC, and [[general-dynamics]] GDIT exposure; sensitive to government shutdown/continuing-resolution headlines, DOGE-style efficiency initiatives and federal IT budget cycles rather than platform procurement.
- **Catalysts:** quarterly book-to-bill, VA exam volumes, protest outcomes on mega-awards, federal budget resolution timing.
- **Options:** moderately liquid; modest IV.
- **Risk:** contract recompete losses, federal civilian spending cuts, margin normalization in Health & Civil.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Leidos trades as a **defensive, cheap-versus-primes** way to own U.S. government spending. Mechanically:

- **Valuation discount** — at roughly a low-teens P/E (~13x noted in the Fundamentals section), the stock is inexpensive relative to the hardware-heavy [[defense]] primes and to higher-margin services peer Booz Allen ([[booz-allen-hamilton]]); the discount reflects lower margins and recompete risk rather than distress.
- **Lower beta** — services revenue and contract backlog dampen cyclicality, so the stock is a lower-beta expression of the [[defense]]/government-spending theme than the primes or hardware names.
- **Basket behavior** — moves with the gov-services complex (Booz Allen, CACI, SAIC, GDIT) and reacts to shutdown/continuing-resolution and federal-budget headlines more than to platform-procurement news.
- **[[options|Options]]** — moderately liquid with modest implied volatility; earnings (book-to-bill, backlog, guidance) are the main scheduled catalyst.
- **Capital return** — strong free cash flow funds ongoing buybacks and a growing dividend; the income plus buyback yield is part of the total-return case (see Fundamentals for the dividend figures as pulled).
