Spire Global, Inc. (NYSE: SPIR) is a space-based data and analytics company that operates a constellation of small "LEMUR" CubeSats collecting radio-frequency (RF), GNSS radio-occultation (weather), and signal-tracking data, which it sells as subscription data feeds and analytics. It is a small-cap, capital-intensive "space data" name that went public via SPAC in 2021 and has since restructured around a narrower, higher-margin data business.

## Business Overview

Spire's "data-as-a-subscription" model monetises a deployed satellite constellation across three core areas: **weather** (radio-occultation data sold to forecasters and governments), **aviation** (ADS-B aircraft tracking), and **maritime / space services** (selling satellite capacity and "Space Services" — building/operating payloads for third parties). The strategic story of 2024-2026 is a pivot away from lower-margin or non-core lines toward focused data and Space Services, alongside cost cuts to chase profitability. A defining event: Spire sold its **maritime data business to Kpler** in 2024, reshaping the revenue base and providing balance-sheet relief.

## 2025-2026 Developments

- **Maritime divestiture (2024)**: completed the sale of its maritime data business to Kpler, exiting a large product line to concentrate on weather, aviation, and Space Services (Verified via Perplexity, 2026-06-10).
- **FY2025 financials**: revenue of roughly $80M, reflecting the post-divestiture, slimmed-down business; the company continued to operate at a loss and prioritised cost reduction and a path to cash-flow sustainability.
- **Focus**: emphasis on weather/data analytics and Space Services contracts (government and commercial), where AI/ML models turn raw satellite signals into forecasting and tracking products.

## Business Segments / Products

Spire's core asset is its **LEMUR (Low Earth Multi-Use Receiver) CubeSat constellation** — a fleet of small, low-Earth-orbit (LEO) nanosatellites carrying software-defined radio-frequency (RF) sensing payloads. Rather than imaging the Earth optically (the [[planet-labs]] approach), Spire *listens*: each satellite captures RF signals — GNSS occultation, ADS-B, AIS, and other emissions — that are downlinked, processed, and sold as recurring **data subscriptions**. The economic model is "data-as-a-subscription": once the constellation and ground network are built, incremental data sales carry high gross margins, and [[artificial-intelligence|AI]]/ML pipelines convert raw RF signals into finished forecasting and tracking products. Spire also rents its space infrastructure to others via Space Services, converting capex into a managed-service revenue stream.

| Product line | What it sells | Buyers | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Weather** | GNSS radio-occultation (RO) profiles — temperature, pressure, humidity vs. altitude | National met agencies, NOAA-style forecasters, defense weather, commercial forecasters | Flagship recurring-data line; RO data feeds numerical weather-prediction models |
| **Aviation** | ADS-B aircraft position/track data from space | Air-navigation service providers, airlines, analytics firms | Space-based ADS-B covers oceanic/remote airspace ground receivers cannot |
| **Maritime** | AIS ship-tracking data | Trade/commodity analytics, insurers, governments | **Divested to Kpler in 2024** — no longer a Spire product line |
| **Space Services** | Space-as-a-Service: build, launch & operate hosted payloads / dedicated satellites for third parties | Governments, commercial constellation operators | Turns Spire's manufacturing + ops stack into managed-service revenue; backlog-driven |

Post-divestiture, the business is concentrated on **Weather + Aviation data subscriptions** and **Space Services**, the lines management views as higher-margin and more strategic.

## Competitive Positioning / Peers

Spire occupies a niche within the broader space-data and geospatial-intelligence complex. Its differentiation is **RF/signals data** (weather RO, ADS-B, AIS) rather than optical imagery, and a **subscription** model rather than per-image sales. Its weakness is scale: it is a small/micro-cap competing against far larger imagery players, government primes, and entrenched weather-data incumbents (national met services and large meteorological-data vendors).

## Bull vs Bear Case

**Bull case** — recurring-revenue data-subscription model with high incremental margins; a slimmed-down, focused business post-Kpler with a clearer path toward cash-flow sustainability; differentiated RF/weather niche with defensible RO and space-based ADS-B coverage; Space Services converts constellation capex into managed-service backlog; leverage to government/defense and climate-data spending and the broader space-economy theme.

**Bear case** — chronically unprofitable small/micro-cap with thin liquidity; capital-intensive constellation that must be continually refreshed; customer concentration and dependence on government budget cycles; dilution/financing overhang and going-concern sensitivity; competition from far larger geospatial, imagery, and weather-data providers that can outspend it; revenue base shrank with the maritime divestiture.

## Capital Structure & Dilution Risk

Spire's business is **capital-intensive**: maintaining and refreshing a LEO CubeSat constellation requires continual capex (satellite builds, launches, and ground-network operations), which has historically outrun operating cash flow and necessitated external financing. That history of **equity dilution and financing dependence** is a recurring overhang for shareholders, and **going-concern** considerations have been part of the investment debate. The **2024 divestiture of the maritime data business to Kpler** provided qualitative balance-sheet relief and helped fund the pivot toward the focused weather/aviation/Space-Services model (no proceeds figure is asserted here). The combination of ongoing losses, capex needs, and financing reliance keeps capital structure and dilution at the center of the bear case.

## Risks

- **Capital intensity / constellation refresh** — continual capex to replenish aging LEMUR satellites pressures free cash flow.
- **Customer concentration** — dependence on a limited set of government and large commercial buyers, and on government budget cycles.
- **Dilution / financing risk** — history of equity raises; going-concern sensitivity if losses persist.
- **Ongoing losses** — not consistently profitable; path to cash-flow breakeven unproven.
- **Competition** — larger geospatial, imagery, and weather-data providers can outspend and out-scale Spire.
- **Constellation obsolescence / technical risk** — satellite failures, launch delays, or RF-sensing tech becoming dated.
