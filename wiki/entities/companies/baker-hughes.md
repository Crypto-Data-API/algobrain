Baker Hughes (BKR) is one of the world's three largest oilfield-services-and-equipment companies, alongside [[schlumberger|SLB]] and [[halliburton]], but it has increasingly repositioned itself as a broader [[energy]]-technology firm with major LNG and industrial exposure. For traders it is both an oil-services cyclical and a growing play on the AI-driven power buildout ([[ai-data-center-power-demand]]), making it less of a pure rig-count proxy than its peers. It is a constituent of the [[s-and-p-500]] index in the [[energy]] sector.

## Business Overview & Segments

Baker Hughes runs two reporting segments. This dual structure differentiates BKR from peers: IET's equipment-and-services backlog gives it a longer-cycle, more visible revenue stream than the spot-cyclical services book.

| Segment | What it is | Cycle character | Demand driver |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Oilfield Services & Equipment (OFSE)** | Drilling, completions, production and subsea equipment for upstream oil & gas | Spot-cyclical — levered to E&P capex and rig activity | Global oil & gas spending, rig count, subsea project FIDs |
| **Industrial & Energy Technology (IET)** | Gas turbines, compressors, LNG liquefaction equipment, industrial process & condition-monitoring tech | Longer-cycle, backlog-driven (the structural growth engine) | LNG export buildout, [[ai-data-center-power-demand|data-center power generation]], industrial energy transition |

IET has become the structural growth engine, capturing demand from LNG export buildout and, newly, data-center power generation — a profile closer to an industrial-equipment OEM than a pure oil-services cyclical.

## 2025-2026 Developments

- **Record IET backlog:** IET closed 2025 with an order backlog exceeding US$32.4B and US$14.9B of full-year orders, driven by gas turbines, compression, and LNG technology. (Verified via Perplexity (sonar) / WebSearch, 2026-06-10)
- **Data-center pivot:** Baker Hughes went from zero data-center orders in 2024 to roughly US$1B in 2025, including 16 NovaLT gas turbines for Frontier Infrastructure's Wyoming/Texas data centers (~270 MW behind-the-meter). CEO Lorenzo Simonelli has framed data-center and LNG power demand as a durable growth vector beyond oilfield services.
- **LNG wins:** orders tied to NextDecade's Rio Grande LNG Train 5 and six LM9000 turbines for Commonwealth LNG.
- **Portfolio reshaping:** continued streamlining (e.g., divestiture of the Waygate inspection-technology business) to concentrate on rotating equipment and LNG.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Baker Hughes holds a moderate [[economic-moat|economic moat]] that is unusually concentrated in its IET franchise rather than the more commoditized services book:

- **Installed-base aftermarket (IET)** — turbines, compressors and LNG equipment generate decades of high-margin service, parts and upgrade revenue on an installed base; this is the most durable, lowest-cyclicality part of the company and the core moat element.
- **Technology & qualification barriers** — large-frame gas turbines and LNG liquefaction trains are engineered, certified, long-lead-time products supplied by a handful of credible OEMs; [[switching-costs]] and reference-project credentials are high.
- **Three-firm oligopoly (OFSE)** — alongside [[schlumberger]] and [[halliburton]], Baker Hughes is one of only three full-line global oilfield-services providers, giving scale, breadth and global logistics that smaller players cannot match.
- **Backlog visibility** — a >US$32.4B IET order backlog provides multi-year revenue visibility rare in [[energy]] services.
- **LNG & power positioning** — established relationships with LNG developers and, newly, data-center power buyers position BKR for the gas-fired power buildout.

The moat's limit: OFSE is competitively bid and cyclical with oil prices, so static valuation metrics mislead mid-cycle; the IET backlog is the better read on forward earnings quality.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **LNG export buildout** — orders tied to projects like NextDecade's Rio Grande LNG Train 5 and Commonwealth LNG drive multi-year IET equipment-and-service revenue.
- **Data-center power generation** — a new vector: BKR went from zero data-center orders in 2024 to ~US$1B in 2025 (incl. 16 NovaLT turbines for Frontier Infrastructure); [[ai-data-center-power-demand|AI-driven power demand]] is framed as a durable growth driver.
- **IET backlog conversion** — a >US$32.4B backlog converting into revenue is the central re-rating story; book-to-bill is the key metric.
- **Oil & gas cycle** — OFSE remains levered to global E&P capex, rig activity and subsea FIDs; an upcycle adds operating leverage.
- **Portfolio focus** — divestitures (e.g., Waygate) concentrate the company on rotating equipment and LNG, improving mix.
- **Recurring catalysts** — quarterly orders/backlog (especially IET book-to-bill), LNG FID announcements, data-center power awards, oil-price and global E&P capex guidance, dividend/buyback updates.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- IET has transformed BKR from a pure oil-services cyclical into a longer-cycle energy-equipment compounder with a >US$32.4B backlog.
- Twin secular tailwinds — LNG export buildout and AI/data-center gas-fired power — give multi-year, visible IET demand.
- High-margin installed-base aftermarket on turbines/compressors is durable, recurring revenue.
- One of only three full-line global oilfield-services providers; scale and breadth in OFSE.
- Improving mix and portfolio focus (divestitures) plus dividend and buyback capital return.

### Bear case
- OFSE remains levered to the oil & gas cycle; a capex downturn or oil-price slump hits the services book and sentiment.
- Through-cycle returns have been poor (5y ROE/ROA negative per the data block) — the 2020 collapse shows the downside.
- Gas-turbine and LNG markets are competitive ([[ge-vernova]], Siemens Energy, MHI) and order timing is lumpy.
- The data-center power theme is early and could prove smaller or slower than the bull case implies.
- Static valuation metrics flatter mid-cycle; commodity-cycle exposure makes the multiple unstable.

## Key Risks

- **Oil & gas cyclicality** — OFSE revenue tracks E&P capex and rig activity; the 2020 downcycle produced deeply negative margins and returns (see 5-period trend below).
- **Through-cycle profitability** — the negative 5y average EBITDA margin and ROE reflect prior troughs; static metrics can flatter or mislead mid-cycle.
- **Order lumpiness** — large IET/LNG awards are episodic; book-to-bill can swing sharply quarter to quarter.
- **Competition** — [[ge-vernova]], Siemens Energy and MHI in turbines/LNG; [[schlumberger]] and [[halliburton]] in services.
- **Data-center theme execution** — the power-generation pivot is new (~US$1B in 2025 from zero); durability is unproven.
- **Commodity & macro** — oil-price weakness, global E&P capex discipline, and FX exposure across a global footprint.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Baker Hughes' static valuation metrics (trailing PE ~21x per the data block) are misleading mid-cycle because OFSE's commodity-cycle exposure swings reported margins and returns — the 5-year averages are even negative, reflecting the 2020 collapse; the IET equipment-and-service backlog is the better read on forward earnings quality (see the Fundamentals block below for the figures as pulled). Mechanically:

- **Cyclical beta** — BKR trades with the oil & gas cycle and oil prices, but the IET backlog has made it less of a pure rig-count proxy than [[schlumberger]] or [[halliburton]].
- **Dividend** — a real, growing dividend plus buybacks; capital return is part of the post-2020 discipline story (the data block's dividend fields are sparse this pull).
- **Earnings behavior** — quarterly orders and IET book-to-bill move the stock as much as the headline EPS; LNG FIDs and data-center power awards are episodic catalysts.
- **Theme torque** — increasingly correlated with the AI-power-infrastructure basket (gas-turbine OEMs, [[ge-vernova]]) on top of the traditional [[energy]]-cycle correlation.
