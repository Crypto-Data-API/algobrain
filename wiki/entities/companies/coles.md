Coles Group Limited (ASX: COL) is one of the two dominant Australian grocery retailers — alongside [[woolworths|Woolworths]], the pair form an effective national grocery duopoly with a combined ~65% market share. Coles operates approximately 850 supermarkets alongside Coles Liquor stores, the flybuys loyalty program and Coles Financial Services. As a [[consumer-staples]] business with non-discretionary demand, fully-franked dividends and low earnings volatility, COL is a core defensive, low-beta income holding in the [[asx-200]]. It was demerged from [[wesfarmers|Wesfarmers Ltd]] in November 2018, becoming a separately listed ASX company.

Fred McNaught uses the Coles [[demergers|demerger]] as a key case study. He notes that Coles performed "much better than anticipated" as an independent company after separating from Wesfarmers — illustrating how demergers can unlock value when a subsidiary was previously underappreciated within a conglomerate structure. Fred also analyses Coles through [[gmroi|GMROI (Gross Margin Return on Inventory)]] metrics, comparing its inventory efficiency against competitor [[woolworths|Woolworths]] as a practical application of retail fundamental analysis.

## Business Model & Segments

Coles operates through two principal reporting segments:

- **Supermarkets:** The core business, comprising more than 800 Coles-branded supermarkets nationally, offering fresh food, groceries, general merchandise and private-label products, supported by the coles.com.au e-commerce platform and home delivery / click-and-collect services.
- **Liquor:** Approximately 900 stores trading under the Liquorland (mainstream), First Choice Liquor Market (big-box) and Vintage Cellars (premium) brands, together with online fulfilment.

Coles Financial Services distributes credit cards, insurance and personal loans under the Coles brand, while flybuys is one of Australia's largest loyalty programs. The company is a major retail media business, monetising its store network and digital platforms for supplier-funded advertising. Key strategic programs include "Simplify and Save to Invest," aimed at structural cost reduction, and ongoing investment in automated customer fulfilment centres (CFCs) for online grocery.

## History

The Coles name dates back to 1914, when G.J. Coles opened the first "Coles Variety Store" in Collingwood, Melbourne. The business expanded through the 20th century into supermarkets and department stores, eventually merging with the Myer chain to become Coles Myer Ltd. In 2007, Coles Group was acquired by Wesfarmers for approximately A$22 billion – one of the largest takeovers in Australian history. Following a major turnaround under Wesfarmers, Coles was demerged and relisted on the ASX in November 2018 as a standalone pure-play food, liquor and convenience retailer.

## Recent Developments

For the full year ended 30 June 2025 (FY25), Coles reported group sales revenue of A$44.3 billion (up 3.6%), led by a 4.3% increase in supermarkets sales revenue and a 1.1% rise in liquor sales revenue on a normalised basis. Group EBITDA from continuing operations (excluding significant items) was A$3.94 billion, up 11% on a normalised basis. The "Simplify and Save to Invest" program delivered A$327 million of benefits, and e-commerce sales grew strongly (supermarkets +24.4%, liquor +7.2%). In the first eight weeks of FY26, supermarkets sales revenue was up 4.9% (7% excluding tobacco), underpinned by volume growth and value investment. The CEO has publicly flagged escalating retail crime, particularly in Victoria, as an operational headwind.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Coles holds a wide [[economic-moat|economic moat]] built less on a single advantage than on the reinforcing economics of a national-scale grocery duopoly:

- **Scale & duopoly economics** — together with [[woolworths|Woolworths]], Coles controls roughly two-thirds of Australian grocery spend. That scale confers buying power over suppliers, fixed-cost leverage across distribution, and the ability to fund cost-out and automation that sub-scale rivals cannot match.
- **Store-network density** — ~800+ supermarkets occupying prime catchment real estate (often on long leases / land-banked sites) make Coles physically convenient to the bulk of the population; replicating the footprint is prohibitively expensive, a durable barrier to entry.
- **Supply chain & private label** — owned distribution centres, automated CFCs and a deep private-label range (Coles Brand) lift margins and create a cost advantage that compounds with scale.
- **flybuys loyalty data moat** — one of Australia's largest loyalty programs gives Coles a proprietary first-party data asset on household shopping behaviour, which feeds personalised pricing/promotion and underpins the retail-media business.
- **Retail media** — monetising store and digital traffic for supplier-funded advertising is a high-margin flywheel that grows with scale and data, widening the gap versus smaller grocers.

The moat is **wide but capped**: pricing power is politically and regulatorily constrained (recurring ACCC scrutiny of supermarket pricing, promotions and land-banking since the 2024 inquiries), and the duopoly's profitability invites disruption from hard-discounter [[aldi|Aldi]] and the scale/online threat of [[amazon|Amazon]]. ## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **"Simplify and Save to Invest" cost-out** — the structural efficiency program (A$327M of benefits in FY25) funds price investment and protects margin.
- **CFC automation** — Ocado-style automated customer fulfilment centres lower the cost-to-serve online grocery and lift e-commerce profitability as they ramp.
- **Retail-media monetisation** — supplier-funded advertising on flybuys data is a high-margin growth lever with structural runway.
- **E-commerce growth** — online supermarket sales have compounded at double digits; penetration still has headroom versus global peers.
- **Population growth** — Australian population growth (immigration-led) is a steady tailwind to grocery volumes for a non-discretionary staple.
- **Private-label penetration** — deepening Coles Brand share lifts blended margin and customer stickiness.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year results (February) and full-year results (August), quarterly sales updates, ACCC/regulatory developments on supermarket pricing and land-banking, and CFC automation milestones.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- Defensive, non-discretionary demand and a duopoly structure deliver low-volatility, all-weather earnings — a classic [[consumer-staples]] compounder.
- Scale, store density, private label and flybuys data form a wide, durable moat that sub-scale rivals cannot replicate.
- Self-help levers (Simplify and Save cost-out, CFC automation, retail media) support margin and earnings even in a low-growth top line.
- Fully-franked dividend plus reliable cash generation make COL a core income holding; franking credits enhance the after-tax yield for Australian residents.
- The 2018 [[demergers|demerger]] from [[wesfarmers]] unlocked value as a focused pure-play — Coles has performed "much better than anticipated" as an independent company.

### Bear case
- Low net margin (~2-3%) means small cost shocks (wages, energy, shrinkage) hit earnings hard — operating leverage cuts both ways.
- Regulatory/political risk: ACCC scrutiny of pricing, promotions and land-banking can cap pricing power and impose remedies.
- Structural margin pressure from [[aldi|Aldi]] discounting and the long-term [[amazon|Amazon]] online threat.
- Retail crime (management-flagged, especially in Victoria) is a measurable and rising cost headwind.
- Tobacco-category decline is a structural drag on supermarket sales growth; CFC build-out is capital-intensive with execution risk.

## Key Risks

- **Regulatory / ACCC** — intervention on pricing, promotions, supplier terms and land-banking; supermarket pricing has been a recurring political theme since the 2024 inquiries.
- **Competitive margin pressure** — [[aldi|Aldi]] hard-discounting and [[amazon|Amazon]]'s online/scale expansion eroding price perception and share.
- **Retail crime** — escalating theft/shrinkage (management-flagged, particularly Victoria) directly hits the thin net margin.
- **CFC execution** — automated fulfilment build-out is capital-intensive; delays or under-utilisation would weigh on returns.
- **Tobacco decline** — structural category shrinkage is a persistent drag on supermarket sales growth.
- **Cost inflation** — wage, energy and supply-chain cost inflation against a ~2-3% net margin leaves limited absorption room without price increases that invite regulatory attention.
