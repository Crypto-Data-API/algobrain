**Qantas Airways Limited** (ASX: QAN) is Australia's flag carrier and one of the most recognisable airline brands globally, listed on the [[asx-limited|Australian Securities Exchange]] and a constituent of the [[asx-200]] index. Headquartered in Mascot, New South Wales, it operates full-service and low-cost (Jetstar) flying plus a highly profitable Qantas Loyalty business. For traders it is the dominant ASX airline exposure — a cyclical industrial whose earnings swing with travel demand, fuel and capacity, but which is underpinned by a near-duopoly domestic market and a loyalty franchise that lends earnings resilience.

## Business Overview & Segments

Qantas is a diversified aviation and loyalty group rather than a single airline. It runs a two-brand flying strategy — the premium, full-service Qantas brand and the low-cost [[jetstar|Jetstar]] brand — that lets the group address both ends of the market, plus a [[customer-loyalty-program|loyalty]] business whose economics look more like a payments/data franchise than an airline. Revenue in FY25 was approximately **A$23.8 billion**, supported by high load factors, fleet renewal and continued growth in loyalty membership and earnings. The group reports across four segments:

| Segment | What it is | Character / economics |
|---|---|---|
| **Qantas Domestic** | Full-service domestic passenger operation serving all Australian capital cities, large metropolitan centres and many regional hubs under the Qantas brand | The profit core: a near-[[duopoly]] domestic market (Qantas + Jetstar vs [[virgin-australia]]) with strong corporate and government share and dominant slots at constrained airports — structural pricing power |
| **Qantas International** | Long-haul and regional international operations across Asia, the Pacific, North America, Europe and Africa; Qantas is one of the few carriers to fly to all seven continents; home of the **Project Sunrise** ultra-long-haul ambition | The most competitive, capital-intensive and cyclical segment — exposed to global capacity, fuel and FX; the A350-1000ULR fleet targets premium non-stop east-coast-to-London/New-York flying |
| **Jetstar Group** | Low-cost carrier group flying domestic, trans-Tasman and short-to-medium-haul international, including Jetstar Asia and affiliated operations | Defends the budget end against rivals such as [[rex|Rex]]/Bonza, lowers group unit cost, and stimulates leisure demand the full-service brand cannot economically serve |
| **Qantas Loyalty** | Qantas Frequent Flyer programme, credit-card and retail partnerships, insurance distribution, health-and-wellness offerings and Qantas Shopping | A high-margin, capital-light annuity — earnings are far more stable than the flying businesses and underpin group resilience through the cycle (see [[economic-moat]]) |

The strategic point for investors is that the **flying businesses are cyclical and capital-hungry, while Loyalty is a resilient, recurring earnings engine** — Loyalty's stability is what lets the group return capital and invest through downturns when the airline P&L is volatile.

## Economic Moat & Competitive Position

Airlines are, as a class, a textbook *bad* business — structurally capital-intensive, cyclical, fuel- and labour-exposed, and prone to thin or negative returns through the cycle. Qantas's [[economic-moat|economic moat]] is therefore best understood as **narrow on the flying businesses but materially wider once Loyalty is included**:

- **Domestic near-[[duopoly]]** — together Qantas and Jetstar hold the dominant share of the Australian domestic market (the chief rival being [[virgin-australia]]). Control of slots and gates at constrained airports, a deep corporate/government contract base, and the two-brand structure (premium + budget) give the group genuine domestic pricing power — the most defensible part of the airline franchise.
- **Qantas Loyalty / Frequent Flyer franchise** — the closest thing the group has to a *wide* moat. The programme is a capital-light, high-margin annuity with strong network effects (members, airline and credit-card partners, and retail merchants reinforce each other), high switching friction, and earnings far steadier than flying. It is the structural reason the equity story is more than a pure cyclical.
- **Brand, network and scale** — "the Flying Kangaroo" is one of the most recognised brands in Australia, with a safety reputation, a comprehensive domestic/international network, and lounges and frequent-flyer benefits that anchor high-value corporate and premium-leisure flyers.

The key caveat: **strip out Loyalty and the moat narrows sharply.** International long-haul is fiercely competitive and offers little durable advantage; group returns on capital are thin and volatile through the cycle, and the balance sheet carries heavy lease and debt obligations against a small (and accounting-distorted) equity base. The investable edge is the *combination* of a protected domestic duopoly plus a resilient loyalty annuity, not airline operations in isolation.

## Growth Drivers & Catalysts

- **Fleet renewal & unit-cost reduction** — the multi-year Airbus order book (48 **A321XLRs**, 29 **A220-300s**, and 12 **A350-1000ULRs**) replaces older, less fuel-efficient aircraft, lowering unit costs and improving the customer offer. Delivery cadence is itself a recurring catalyst.
- **Project Sunrise** — the A350-1000ULR enables non-stop ultra-long-haul flying from Australia's east coast to **London and New York**, expected to begin in **H1 CY2027**. It is high-margin premium optionality not yet reflected in earnings, with first delivery scheduled for October 2026 (final assembly began in Toulouse, October 2025).
- **Qantas Loyalty growth** — continued expansion of Frequent Flyer membership, credit-card and retail partnerships, and ancillary revenue compounds the group's most stable, capital-light earnings stream.
- **Capacity & yield discipline** — disciplined domestic capacity (especially relative to [[virgin-australia]]) sustains load factors and yields; the duopoly structure rewards restraint over share-grab.
- **Capital returns** — buybacks and a (resumed) **fully franked dividend** — H1 FY26 included a A$300m interim base dividend (19.8 cents/share, +20%) — are a direct catalyst and a signal of balance-sheet repair.
- **Recurring catalysts** — half-year and full-year results, jet-fuel (kerosene) price trends, capacity/load-factor and yield commentary, fleet-delivery and Project Sunrise milestones, and competitive moves by Virgin.

## Bull Case vs Bear Case

### Bull case
- **Domestic pricing power** — the Qantas + Jetstar near-[[duopoly]] over [[virgin-australia]] supports durable domestic yields and margins.
- **Loyalty annuity** — a high-margin, capital-light, resilient earnings engine that smooths the airline cycle and underwrites capital returns.
- **Fleet renewal** — A321XLR/A220/A350-1000ULR deliveries lower unit fuel and operating costs and refresh the customer product.
- **Project Sunrise optionality** — premium, high-margin ultra-long-haul (east coast to London/NY) from H1 CY2027 is upside not yet in the numbers.
- **Capital returns** — resumed fully franked dividends and buybacks reward shareholders and signal balance-sheet confidence; franking is especially valuable to AU-resident holders.

### Bear case
- **Cyclical demand** — a consumer or macro downturn hits discretionary travel hard; airline earnings are highly operationally geared.
- **Jet-fuel volatility** — kerosene is a swing cost line; hedging only partially insulates margins.
- **Capital intensity & execution** — large fleet capex and the risk of delivery delays or cost overruns (including on the Project Sunrise A350-1000ULRs).
- **Labour / industrial-relations pressure** — wage and IR disputes are a recurring cost and operational-disruption risk.
- **Reputational / regulatory overhang** — the 2023-24 "ghost flights" consumer-law episode left lingering brand and regulatory scrutiny.
- **Thin equity base / high financial leverage** — heavy debt and lease obligations against a small, accounting-distorted equity base amplify downside and make book-based ratios unreliable.

## Key Risks

- **Jet-fuel volatility** — kerosene prices (and the AUD/USD rate they are paid in) are the single largest cost swing factor.
- **Demand softness** — discretionary travel is cyclical; a consumer downturn compresses both volumes and yields.
- **Labour-cost & industrial relations** — wage pressure, enterprise-bargaining disputes and strike risk raise costs and threaten operational continuity.
- **Operational disruption** — weather, IT outages, safety/maintenance events and supply-chain (aircraft/engine) constraints can hit reliability and reputation.
- **AUD weakness** — a softer Australian dollar raises USD-denominated costs (fuel, aircraft, leases, maintenance).
- **Brand / regulatory scrutiny** — ongoing reputational and regulatory overhang following the 2023-24 consumer-law and "ghost flights" issues.
- **Balance-sheet leverage** — high debt and lease load against a thin equity base limits the cushion in a severe downturn.

## Valuation & How the Stock Trades

Qantas trades the way cyclical airlines typically do, and its valuation should be read qualitatively rather than off headline book-based ratios:

- **Low single-digit-to-high-single-digit P/E** — like most airlines, the market assigns a modest earnings multiple that compresses near cyclical peaks (when earnings are high but expected to fall) and expands at troughs. The figures pulled in the Fundamentals block below show a P/E in the high single digits.
- **High-beta cyclical** — the stock tends to **lead in travel-demand recoveries and lag in downturns**; realised volatility is high around results, fuel-price moves and capacity/guidance updates.
- **Distorted book equity → unreliable P/B & ROE** — Qantas's small and at times negative book equity (from years of losses, buybacks and lease accounting) makes price-to-book and ROE statistically meaningless; the eye-watering ROE and D/E figures in the Fundamentals block are an artefact of a thin/negative equity denominator, **not** evidence of quality. Lean on EV-, cash-flow- and earnings-power-based framing instead.
- **Franking matters** — dividends are **fully franked**, which materially lifts the after-tax yield for Australian-resident investors versus the headline cash yield (see [[australian-investing]]).
- **Traded mainly via ordinary shares** — single-stock options liquidity is modest by global standards, so most exposure and hedging is expressed through the shares themselves.

## Recent Developments

For FY25 (year ended 30 June 2025), Qantas reported revenue up 8.6% to approximately A$23.8 billion, underlying profit before tax of A$2.39 billion (up 15%) and statutory profit after tax up 28% to A$1.61 billion.

For H1 FY26, underlying profit before tax reached A$1.46 billion (up 5%), statutory profit after tax was A$925 million and underlying earnings per share rose 7% to 68 cents. The Board approved a fully franked interim base dividend of A$300 million (19.8 cents per share), a 20% increase, to be paid on 15 April 2026.

Qantas continues to progress a major fleet renewal program. It has 48 A321XLRs on order (including 20 additional units announced in August 2025), 29 A220-300s on order, and 12 Airbus A350-1000ULRs on order for Project Sunrise. The first A350-1000ULR entered final assembly in Toulouse in October 2025, with delivery scheduled for October 2026 and the first non-stop ultra-long-haul flights from Australia's east coast to London and New York expected in the first half of calendar 2027.

In the past 12 months, Qantas has taken delivery of 17 new aircraft, including the Asia-Pacific's first A321XLRs, supporting domestic and short-haul international renewal.

The most recent filing (annual) was lodged on 2023-06-30. See [[#Financial Reports]] below.

## Leadership

Qantas is led by Group Chief Executive Officer and Managing Director Vanessa Hudson, who emphasised at the 2025 AGM that strong financial performance is enabling significant investment in new aircraft and customer initiatives. The Board and executive team comprise leaders with backgrounds in aviation, finance, operations and consumer marketing.
