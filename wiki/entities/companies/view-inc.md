View, Inc. was a smart-glass (electrochromic dynamic glass) company that went public via [[spac|SPAC]] in 2021. **It is no longer publicly tradable**: View filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy on April 2, 2024, went private in a restructuring backed by Cantor Fitzgerald and RXR Realty, and emerged from bankruptcy on May 22, 2024. The former Nasdaq-listed equity (ticker VIEW) was effectively wiped out and delisted. No current trading relevance. The page is retained as a clean **[[de-spac|de-SPAC]] failure case study**.

> **Defunct as a public company.** Retained for historical reference only. At filing, View had ~$65.3M cash against ~$359M debt and had lost ~$426M over the prior 12 months on ~$129M revenue.

## What the Business Was

View manufactured **electrochromic dynamic glass** — architectural window glass that tints electronically in response to sunlight, occupancy, and a building-management system, marketed as reducing HVAC load, improving occupant comfort, and lowering a building's energy footprint. The "AI" framing came from View's cloud platform, which used sensor telemetry and predictive control to optimise tint states across a building automatically.

The product was technically credible but **capital-intensive**: each installation required custom glass manufacturing, integration with building electrical/IT systems, and a long commercial-construction sales cycle. Revenue scaled far more slowly than the cash burn required to run a dedicated glass fab, which is the structural reason the business could not reach self-funding before exhausting its [[de-spac|de-SPAC]] cash.

### Business segments / products

| Segment | What it was | Structural problem |
|---------|-------------|--------------------|
| **View Smart Glass** | Core electrochromic dynamic glass for commercial buildings | Custom-manufactured, capex-heavy, slow construction sales cycle |
| **View Smart Building Platform** | Cloud software for automated tint control, sensors, telemetry (the "AI" layer) | Thin software margin atop a hardware/fab cost base |
| **Adjacent building products** | Network/IoT building hardware extensions | Did not change the unit economics |

The defining feature was that View was, at its core, a **glass-manufacturing company** carrying fab-scale fixed costs while selling into the long, lumpy commercial-construction cycle — a profile that needs sustained capex long before it can self-fund.

## Competitive Positioning at the Time

View was the highest-profile pure-play in dynamic glass, but it competed against both specialist electrochromic rivals and, more importantly, the cheap, "good-enough" incumbent building-envelope technologies (conventional low-E glass, blinds, automated shades). Its differentiation was real; its problem was that the value proposition did not justify the cost premium fast enough to outrun the cash burn.

| Peer / alternative | Positioning vs. View | Contrast |
|--------------------|----------------------|----------|
| SageGlass (Saint-Gobain) | Electrochromic glass inside a large, profitable building-materials parent | Survived because it was a product line of a diversified incumbent, not a standalone burning de-SPAC cash |
| Conventional low-E glass + automated shading | Cheap, proven, lower-capex building-envelope solutions | The real competitive ceiling: "good enough" alternatives capped View's pricing power |
| Smart-building / building-automation platforms (Honeywell, Siemens, Johnson Controls) | Deep-pocketed incumbents in building controls | Could bundle controls without owning a glass fab |

The takeaway mirrors the other failures in this cohort: a credible technology owned by a sub-scale, cash-hungry standalone loses to incumbents who can absorb the same capability inside a profitable franchise. View had no [[merger-arbitrage]]-style premium exit — there was no strategic buyer for the equity, only a restructuring that wiped it out.

## AI Involvement (historical)

[[artificial-intelligence|AI]]-powered smart building glass for energy optimization: cloud-based predictive tint control driven by sensor data and weather inputs. As with many 2021-era [[spac|SPAC]] deals, the "AI" label was a thin software layer over a fundamentally hardware/manufacturing business — a recurring tell in the [[de-spac]] cohort.

## Why this is a SPAC-failure case study

View is a textbook example of the 2020-2021 [[spac|SPAC]] / [[de-spac|de-SPAC]] boom-and-bust:

- **Pre-revenue-scale company taken public on projections.** SPAC mergers (unlike traditional IPOs) allowed sponsors to market forward-looking revenue forecasts; View's pitch leaned on a large projected addressable market for dynamic glass.
- **High cash burn, capital-intensive hardware.** A glass-manufacturing business needs sustained capex; the [[de-spac]] cash raised was insufficient to bridge to profitability.
- **Redemptions and a sinking trust.** Like most 2021 de-SPACs, heavy shareholder redemptions reduced the cash actually delivered to the company versus the headline trust size.
- **Going-concern spiral.** Mounting losses (~$426M over the trailing 12 months on ~$129M revenue at filing), a debt load (~$359M) dwarfing cash (~$65.3M), and an inability to raise affordable new capital ended in Chapter 11.
- **Equity wipeout.** Common shareholders (the post-SPAC public float) were effectively zeroed in the restructuring, with the recapitalised entity going private under Cantor Fitzgerald and RXR Realty.

This arc — credible technology, uneconomic unit/capital structure, projection-driven [[spac|SPAC]] listing, redemptions, dilution, and a near-total equity loss — is the canonical [[de-spac]] failure pattern that played out across dozens of 2021-vintage names.

## The Failure: Going-Concern Spiral to Chapter 11

The terminal sequence, in order:

1. **De-SPAC cash, capex business.** View went public via [[spac|SPAC]] in 2021 on projections, but ran a capex-heavy glass-fab business whose burn dwarfed revenue scaling.
2. **Redemptions shrank the trust.** Like most 2021 de-SPACs, heavy shareholder redemptions cut the cash actually delivered to the company versus the headline trust size — starving the business of the capital its projections assumed.
3. **Mounting losses, debt > cash.** At filing, View had **~$65.3M cash against ~$359M debt** and had lost **~$426M over the prior 12 months on ~$129M revenue** (figures carry the original data disclaimer — see Sources). Debt dwarfed cash; losses dwarfed revenue.
4. **Chapter 11 (April 2, 2024).** Unable to raise affordable new capital, View filed Chapter 11.
5. **Private restructuring, equity wipeout.** Backed by Cantor Fitzgerald and RXR Realty, View went private and emerged from bankruptcy on May 22, 2024. The former Nasdaq-listed equity (VIEW) was **effectively wiped out and delisted** — common holders (the post-SPAC public float) were zeroed.

## Merger-Arb / Trading Postmortem

There was **no acquirer bidding for the equity at a premium** — this is a dilution/wipeout case, not a merger-arb case (contrast the clean cash deals [[splunk]] and [[1life-healthcare|One Medical]]):

- **No premium exit.** Unlike an acquired company, View's restructuring transferred ownership to creditors/sponsors (Cantor Fitzgerald, RXR Realty); public common stock recovered ~nothing. There was no spread to capture — only downside to avoid.
- **Dilution and reverse-split dynamics.** Like the other [[de-spac]] failures ([[lilium]], [[nikola]]), View's path featured the distress toolkit — sinking cash, rising dilution risk, and the going-concern overhang — that telegraphs the wipeout to anyone watching the balance sheet.
- **Screen-survivorship trap.** View still appears in some thematic "AI smart glass" screens despite being defunct; trading a delisted/zeroed ticker off a stale screen is a real operational risk (see Trading Relevance).
- **No tradeable squeeze edge.** Whereas [[nikola]] produced violent [[short-squeeze|short squeezes]] for years, View's collapse was a slower going-concern grind into Chapter 11, with the public equity simply extinguished in restructuring.

## Lessons / What Killed It

- **Hardware economics beat software narrative.** A glass fab is capex-heavy and scales slowly; the "AI" cloud layer was a thin margin atop a fundamentally manufacturing cost base.
- **De-SPAC redemptions starve the business.** The headline trust never arrives in full; View raised on projections but received far less usable cash.
- **"Good enough" incumbents cap pricing power.** Conventional low-E glass, shading, and incumbent building-controls vendors limited how fast View could justify its premium.
- **No strategic buyer for sub-scale capex tech.** Unlike acquired peers, there was no premium takeout — the equity was extinguished in restructuring.

## Risks That Materialized

- **Cash-burn / going-concern risk:** ~$426M trailing-12-month loss on ~$129M revenue; debt (~$359M) dwarfing cash (~$65.3M) at filing (original disclaimer applies — see Sources).
- **Financing risk:** inability to raise affordable new capital forced Chapter 11.
- **Dilution / redemption risk:** de-SPAC redemptions and subsequent dilution undercut the capital base.
- **Equity-wipeout risk:** common holders zeroed in the Cantor Fitzgerald / RXR Realty restructuring; VIEW delisted.
