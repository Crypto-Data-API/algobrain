MakersPlace was a curated [[nft|NFT]] marketplace focused on digital art, launched in 2019 and backed by Uncork Capital. It operated an invitation and application-based model for artists, positioning itself as a premium destination for digital creators and collectors, and gained significant attention for hosting early NFT drops by Beeple (Mike Winkelmann), whose work later sold for $69.3 million at [[christies|Christie's]] — the most expensive NFT sale in history. **The platform announced its shutdown on 2025-01-16 and is now defunct**, one of the most prominent casualties of the post-2022 NFT market collapse.

## History

MakersPlace was founded in 2019 by Ryoma Ito, Yash Nelapati, and Dannie Chu, all former engineers at Pinterest and other Silicon Valley companies. The platform launched with backing from Uncork Capital (formerly SoftTech VC) and positioned itself as a curated marketplace where digital artists could create, sell, and collect authenticated digital creations.

The platform distinguished itself early on by focusing on the artist experience and providing tools to help creators enter the NFT space. MakersPlace offered features like digital signatures embedded in the artwork's metadata and credit card payments — lowering the barrier to entry for collectors unfamiliar with cryptocurrency.

MakersPlace's most significant early moment came through its relationship with Beeple (Mike Winkelmann), one of the most prolific digital artists in the world. Beeple used MakersPlace for his early NFT drops in 2020, including a series of works that sold for hundreds of thousands of dollars. This relationship helped establish MakersPlace's credibility and prestige within the emerging NFT art market.

Beeple's trajectory from MakersPlace drops to the historic $69.3 million sale of "Everydays: The First 5000 Days" at [[christies|Christie's]] in March 2021 brought unprecedented mainstream attention to NFT art — and by extension, to the curated marketplace ecosystem that MakersPlace was part of.

## Shutdown (January 2025)

MakersPlace announced late on **2025-01-16** that it was ceasing operations immediately, citing "ongoing market challenges and funding difficulties." Management said that despite remaining cash, the prolonged NFT market downturn made securing further investment unrealistic. Key wind-down facts:

- NFT minting, token imports and new account creation were disabled immediately; NFT transfers were scheduled to cease by **June 2025**.
- Unused funds were returned to investors; employees received severance. The company had raised **$30M in August 2021** from investors including Eminem, Sony Music, and Coinbase Ventures.
- Because all MakersPlace NFTs are minted on [[ethereum|Ethereum]], the tokens themselves survive the platform and remain tradable on secondary marketplaces such as [[opensea|OpenSea]].

**Trading lesson:** platform risk is distinct from asset risk in crypto — a venue can die while on-chain assets persist, but curation-premium and discovery value (a large part of what collectors paid for) dies with the venue. NFT trading volumes fell >95% from the 2021-22 peak, and venture-funded marketplaces without protocol revenue could not outlast the drawdown.

## Platform Characteristics

- **Curation model**: Application and invitation-based artist approval
- **Blockchain**: [[ethereum|Ethereum]] (ERC-721 standard)
- **Payment options**: Both cryptocurrency (ETH) and credit card payments
- **Digital signatures**: Artwork metadata includes creator's digital signature for authentication
- **Edition types**: Supports both 1/1 unique works and limited editions
- **Collector tools**: Portfolio management, bidding system, and activity tracking

## Artist and Collector Experience

MakersPlace invested heavily in making the NFT art experience accessible to mainstream audiences:

- **Fiat on-ramp**: Credit card purchases allowed collectors to buy NFTs without owning cryptocurrency
- **Custodial wallets**: The platform offered managed wallets for users not comfortable with self-custody
- **Discovery features**: Curated collections, featured artist spotlights, and editorial content
- **Collaboration tools**: Artists could create collaborative works with shared royalties

These features positioned MakersPlace as a bridge between the traditional art world and the crypto-native NFT ecosystem.

## Competitive Position

MakersPlace occupied the curated tier alongside [[superrare|SuperRare]] and [[knownorigin|KnownOrigin]]:

- **vs. [[superrare|SuperRare]]**: Both curated, but MakersPlace offered more mainstream-friendly features (credit card payments, custodial wallets) and had early ties to Beeple
- **vs. [[knownorigin|KnownOrigin]]**: Similar curated approach; MakersPlace had a stronger Silicon Valley foundation and US-centric artist base
- **vs. [[opensea|OpenSea]]** / **[[rarible|Rarible]]**: MakersPlace's curation stood in contrast to open, permissionless marketplaces
- **vs. [[christies|Christie's]]** / **[[sothebys|Sotheby's]]**: Traditional auction houses entered NFTs in 2021; MakersPlace served as the crypto-native equivalent for high-end digital art

## Significance

MakersPlace's most enduring contribution to the NFT space may be its role in Beeple's trajectory. By providing an early, credible, and accessible platform for Beeple's initial NFT experiments, MakersPlace helped incubate the relationship between one of the world's most prolific digital artists and the blockchain art market. The path from MakersPlace drops (hundreds of thousands of dollars) to the [[christies|Christie's]] sale ($69.3 million) demonstrated how crypto-native platforms could serve as launchpads for artists who would go on to achieve recognition in the traditional art world.

The platform's emphasis on fiat payment options and custodial wallets also represented an early recognition that mainstream NFT adoption required reducing friction for non-crypto-native users — a lesson that the broader Web3 ecosystem has continued to learn.

MakersPlace's Silicon Valley DNA (founders from Pinterest engineering) brought product design sensibilities that contrasted with the more crypto-native approaches of competitors, resulting in a polished user experience aimed at bridging the gap between traditional digital art communities and the emerging NFT collector base.
