# Nuclear Renaissance → Small-Cap Reactor & Fuel Long

## Headline

Per [[ai-data-center-power-demand]], hyperscaler AI capex requires gigawatt-scale 24/7 baseload power that **renewables alone cannot deliver** — wind/solar are intermittent, gas peakers face emissions / NIMBY pushback, and existing nuclear capacity is oversubscribed. Three public small-caps are pure-plays on the next wave: **OKLO** (advanced fast reactor), **SMR** (NuScale modular reactor), **BWXT** (DOE-contracted fuel + reactor manufacturing). Distinct from [[ai-layoff-trap-capex-beneficiaries|the capex-beneficiaries narrative]] (which holds CEG / NRG large-cap utilities); this is the **small-cap pure-play layer** for asymmetric R/R via options. URA / CCJ / URNM secondary basket captures the fuel-cycle leg.

## Thesis

Five reinforcing inputs support the basket:

1. **AI baseload power demand** — hyperscaler PPA appetite is structurally exceeding gas + renewables capacity. Microsoft signed Three Mile Island restart (2024); Amazon / Google have signed multi-gigawatt nuclear PPAs. Pipeline of similar announcements is dense through 2026.
2. **Regulatory tailwind** — NRC has accelerated license modernization; DOE Section 50001 loan guarantees + IRA Section 45U production tax credits provide explicit financing for new builds. Bipartisan support unusual in the current political environment.
3. **Public market scarcity** — only ~5–10 truly investable public nuclear pure-plays. The hyperscaler appetite far exceeds available equity; supply/demand for the names themselves is structurally tight.
4. **Fuel-cycle squeeze** — uranium spot prices are ~$80–95/lb; Russia is locked out of US conversion/enrichment via 2024 ban; new Western capacity at LEU (Centrus) and CCJ (Cameco) is years from full ramp. Fuel scarcity supports long-cycle pricing power.
5. **Stagflation defensive** — utility-adjacent + commodity exposure provides partial inflation hedge while avoiding the [[ai-cogs-creep|AI COGS creep]] hitting SaaS.

The small-cap basket carries higher idiosyncratic risk than utilities, but **option vol is rich enough that defined-risk structures (call verticals) capture the convex upside without unbounded bleed**. R:R 4:1 reflects this — accept higher vol cost for convex payoff.
