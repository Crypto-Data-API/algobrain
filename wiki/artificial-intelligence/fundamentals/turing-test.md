# Turing Test

The **Turing Test**, proposed by Alan Turing in his 1950 paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," asks whether a machine can exhibit intelligent behavior indistinguishable from a human. A human evaluator converses with both a machine and a human (without knowing which is which) — if the evaluator cannot reliably tell them apart, the machine passes the test.

## Has AI Passed the Turing Test?

Modern [[foundation-models|LLMs]] like [[anthropic|Claude]] and [[openai|GPT-4o]] can sustain conversations that are difficult to distinguish from human responses in many contexts. Controlled three-party imitation-game studies in 2025 reported that leading models were judged to be the human more than half the time under specific personas — a result widely described as the test being "passed" in its classic form. Most researchers now treat the Turing Test as effectively surpassed and a poor measure of intelligence: it rewards convincing imitation rather than reasoning, factual accuracy, or understanding. Benchmarks for reasoning, math, coding, and tool use have replaced it as the practical yardsticks.

For trading, the relevant question is not "Can AI imitate a human analyst?" but "Can AI produce better analysis than a human analyst?" These are very different questions, and the answer to the second remains "sometimes, for specific tasks, but not consistently across all domains."

## Trading Relevance

The Turing Test matters for trading in one specific context: **AI-generated content in markets**. If AI can produce research notes, social media posts, and earnings analysis indistinguishable from human-written content, then:

- Social media [[nlp-sentiment-analysis|sentiment signals]] become less reliable (bots generating fake sentiment)
- AI-generated analyst reports may flood information channels
- The premium on verified, authoritative sources increases
