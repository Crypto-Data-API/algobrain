# Robotics & Embodied AI

**Robotics and embodied AI** deal with intelligent systems that interact with the physical world — autonomous vehicles, warehouse robots, surgical systems, humanoid robots, and drones. While not directly a trading *tool*, robotics is one of the most investable AI sectors. Understanding the technology helps evaluate companies building or deploying robotic systems: Tesla (Optimus, FSD), NVIDIA (Isaac, DRIVE), Boston Dynamics, Amazon (warehouse automation), and dozens of public and private companies.

## The Robotics Investment Thesis

### Bull Case
- Physical labour is ~50% of global GDP — automation is the largest addressable market in history
- AI capabilities ([[foundation-models]], [[reinforcement-learning|RL]], [[computer-vision-overview|CV]]) are finally sufficient for real-world deployment
- Ageing demographics in developed nations create labour shortages that robots fill
- Self-driving alone is a multi-trillion-dollar opportunity (transportation, logistics, ride-hailing)

### Bear Case
- The "last 10%" of reliability is the hardest — self-driving has been "2 years away" for a decade, and GM wrote off ~$10B on Cruise before shutting its robotaxi program in 2024
- Physical robots are expensive, fragile, and slow to deploy compared to software
- Regulatory barriers (liability for autonomous vehicle accidents, medical robot approval)
- Human dexterity and adaptability remain far ahead of robotic capability for general tasks
- The timeline for humanoid robot ROI is highly uncertain

### The Picks-and-Shovels Play
Regardless of which robotics companies win, the infrastructure providers benefit:
- [[nvidia-ai|NVIDIA]]: GPU compute for training + simulation (Isaac, Omniverse, DRIVE)
- Sensor manufacturers: lidar (Luminar, Velodyne), cameras, radar
- Cloud compute: simulation workloads for [[sim-to-real|sim-to-real]] training
- AI chip companies: edge inference chips for onboard robot computation
