# Philosophy of Mind & AI

**Philosophy of mind** asks how mental states (beliefs, desires, experiences) relate to physical processes. For AI, the central question is: can computation give rise to genuine mental states, or is it fundamentally different from biological cognition? The answer determines whether AI can ever truly "understand" markets or will always be a sophisticated pattern matcher.

## Key Positions

### Functionalism
**Mental states are defined by their functional role** — what they *do*, not what they're made of. If a silicon system has the same input-output relationships as a brain, it has the same mental states.

- **Implication for AI**: [[foundation-models|LLMs]] could genuinely "understand" if they achieve the right functional organisation
- **AI companies' implicit assumption**: Most AI companies operate under soft functionalism — capability = intelligence

### Biological Naturalism (Searle)
**Mental states are biological phenomena** — consciousness is produced by specific biological processes that silicon cannot replicate, regardless of computational equivalence.

- **Implication for AI**: AI can simulate but never replicate understanding (see [[chinese-room-argument]])
- **Trading implication**: Hard ceiling on what AI can achieve — limits long-term AI company upside

### Computationalism (Strong AI Thesis)
**The mind is literally a computer** — thinking is computation, and any system performing the same computation has the same mental states.

- **Implication for AI**: Digital computers can, in principle, be conscious and intelligent
- **This is the maximalist AI thesis**: If true, [[artificial-general-intelligence|AGI]] and beyond are achievable

### Embodied Cognition
**Intelligence requires a body** — cognition is grounded in physical interaction with the world, not abstract symbol manipulation.

- **Implication for AI**: Disembodied LLMs can never truly understand; [[robotics-overview|robotic]] AI might
- **Trading implication**: Favours robotics companies over pure software AI

### Predictive Processing
**The brain is a prediction machine** — it constantly generates predictions about sensory input and updates based on prediction errors. Intelligence is hierarchical prediction.

- **Implication for AI**: [[foundation-models|LLMs]] (which are literally next-token prediction machines) may be closer to biological cognition than critics admit
- **A bridge theory**: Connects modern AI architecture to neuroscience

## The Practical Synthesis

For trading, the philosophical position matters less than the observable capabilities:

| What AI Can Do (Regardless of Understanding) | What AI Cannot Do (Yet) |
|----------------------------------------------|------------------------|
| Process information faster than humans | Understand *why* markets move (causation) |
| Identify patterns in vast datasets | Exercise genuine judgment under novel uncertainty |
| Generate plausible analysis and hypotheses | Have conviction, intuition, or "feel" for markets |
| Execute consistent, disciplined processes | Adapt to truly unprecedented situations |
| Scale analysis across thousands of securities | Understand the human psychology driving markets |

The most successful trading approach treats AI as a **powerful tool operated by human judgment** — regardless of whether the tool "understands" anything.
