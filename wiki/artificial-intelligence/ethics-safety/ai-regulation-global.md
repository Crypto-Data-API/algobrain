# Global AI Regulation

The global regulatory landscape for AI is evolving rapidly, with the EU leading on comprehensive legislation, the US taking a sector-specific approach, and China implementing national AI standards. Regulatory developments are **market-moving events** for AI companies — creating compliance costs, defining competitive advantages, and triggering sector re-ratings.

## Major Regulatory Frameworks

### European Union: AI Act (2024)

The world's first comprehensive AI law.

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Enacted** | Entered into force Aug 2024, with obligations phased in over 2025-2027 |
| **Approach** | Risk-based classification (unacceptable → high → limited → minimal) |
| **High-risk AI** | Credit scoring, hiring, medical devices, critical infrastructure, **trading algorithms** |
| **Requirements** | Conformity assessment, [[explainability-interpretability|explainability]], [[algorithmic-bias|bias testing]], human oversight, data governance |
| **Penalties** | Up to €35M or 7% of global revenue |
| **Foundation models** | Additional transparency requirements (training data disclosure, compute reporting) |

**Phasing (as of mid-2026)**: The Act phases in over time. The bans on **prohibited practices** (e.g. social scoring, untargeted facial scraping) and **AI-literacy** obligations applied first (Feb 2025). Rules for **general-purpose / foundation models** applied next (Aug 2025). The bulk of the stringent **high-risk system** obligations — the ones that catch most trading and credit AI — phase in later (2026-2027). Exact provision-by-provision timing should be checked against the official EU timetable, as implementing acts and the GPAI Code of Practice continue to firm up the detail.

**Trading impact**: European operations of all major AI companies must comply. Compliance cost is non-trivial but creates a moat for compliant companies.

### United States: Sector-Specific Approach

| Action | Year | Scope |
|--------|------|-------|
| **Executive Order on AI** (Biden) | Oct 2023 | Safety testing, reporting for large models, federal AI use — substantially rescinded/replaced under the Trump administration in Jan 2025, shifting toward a lighter, pro-innovation federal stance |
| **Trump AI policy** | 2025 | Deregulatory pivot; emphasis on US AI dominance, infrastructure (data centres/power), reduced federal "burden" — reduces near-term US compliance risk relative to EU |
| **SEC predictive analytics** | Proposed 2023 | Disclosure when AI optimises investor interactions |
| **OCC SR 11-7** | Existing | Model risk management for banks (applies to AI) |
| **CFPB** | Ongoing | AI in lending, credit scoring fairness |
| **FTC** | Ongoing | Deceptive AI practices, consumer protection |
| **State laws** (CO, IL, etc.) | 2024-2025 | AI in hiring, insurance, consumer protection |

The US has no single comprehensive AI law equivalent to the EU AI Act. Regulation is fragmented across agencies and states.

### China

| Framework | Focus |
|-----------|-------|
| **Interim Measures for Generative AI** (2023) | Content generated must be "true and accurate," no undermining state power |
| **Algorithm Recommendation** (2022) | Transparency in recommendation algorithms |
| **Deep Synthesis** (2023) | Deepfake labelling requirements |
| **AI safety standards** (ongoing) | National standards for AI testing and evaluation |

### United Kingdom

| Framework | Focus |
|-----------|-------|
| **Pro-innovation approach** (2023) | Principles-based, sector regulators interpret for their domain |
| **AI Safety Institute** (2023) | Frontier model evaluation and safety testing |
| No comprehensive AI law | Intentionally lighter than EU |

## Regulatory Impact on AI Stocks

| Regulatory Event | Market Impact |
|-----------------|--------------|
| **EU AI Act passage** | Short-term: compliance cost concern (AI stocks dipped). Long-term: advantages compliant leaders |
| **US AI Executive Order** | Minimal immediate impact (voluntary aspects); reporting requirements for large models |
| **SEC AI proposals** | Negative for AI-driven advisory/fintech; positive for compliance tech companies |
| **China AI restrictions** | Limits Chinese AI companies' capabilities; benefits US/EU competitors |
| **Copyright rulings** (NYT v OpenAI) | Existential risk for training data dependent models; watch for settlement/ruling |

## The Regulatory Arbitrage

Different jurisdictions' approaches create strategic optionality:
- **EU**: Strictest → highest compliance cost → strongest consumer trust
- **US**: Moderate → lower cost → faster innovation
- **UK**: Lightest → attract AI companies → risk of under-protection
- **China**: Government-aligned → different competitive dynamics

Companies operating globally must comply with the strictest applicable regime (usually EU), which effectively sets the global standard.
