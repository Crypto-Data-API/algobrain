# AI Governance Attacks

**AI governance attacks** are a category of attacks on DAO governance that are newly economically viable because large language models have collapsed the cost of producing convincing proposals, coordinating sockpuppet voters, and running persuasive campaigns across social channels. The attack surface is not new — Sybil attacks on DAOs have always been possible — but the *cost structure* has changed in a way that moves several previously-theoretical attacks into the realm of routine threats that protocols must actively defend against.

This is the governance-side counterpart to [[proof-of-humanity]] (which solves the identity layer) and sits alongside [[ai-agent-daos]] (which addresses the governance-agent interaction problem). None of the three fully solves the problem alone.

## Why AI Changes the Attack Cost Structure

Three specific capabilities that LLMs have made cheap are the core of the cost change:

1. **Convincing natural-language output at scale** — writing 100 plausible governance proposals, each tailored to a specific DAO's tone and history, used to require hundreds of hours of human labor. It now costs dollars.
2. **Coordinated persona operation** — running hundreds of social-media accounts that engage with each other in realistic ways (debating, agreeing, endorsing) used to require a team of operators. A single LLM director can now orchestrate the same effect.
3. **Real-time reactive discourse** — responding to counterarguments in forums, adjusting tactics based on how votes are trending, and keeping personas internally consistent over weeks of engagement — previously the bottleneck that limited the scale of social manipulation. LLMs dissolve this bottleneck.

None of these individually would break governance. The combination moves attacks from "theoretically possible but expensive" to "routine operational capability."

## The Main Attack Classes

### 1. Proposal Spam

An attacker generates a high volume of plausible-looking governance proposals, burying legitimate proposals in noise and exhausting the attention budget of voters. This is the simplest attack and the one most DAOs already experience in mild forms. LLMs make proposal spam qualitatively worse because the generated proposals are individually convincing enough that dismissing them requires actual reading, not pattern-matching.

Defenses: proposal bonds (require capital at stake), reputation-weighted filtering, structured proposal templates that resist LLM pattern-matching.

### 2. Sockpuppet Voter Coordination

An attacker controls many addresses, each with enough tokens to vote, and coordinates their votes through an off-chain LLM director that adjusts strategy based on how the vote is trending. The addresses individually look like normal small-token holders; the aggregate behavior is coordinated.

Defenses: quadratic voting (reduces the advantage of many small holders coordinating), conviction voting (rewards long-term commitment), on-chain reputation systems, [[proof-of-humanity|proof-of-humanity]] layers for one-person-one-vote systems.

### 3. Persuasive Proposal Laundering

An attacker submits a genuinely harmful proposal — one that drains treasury, changes key parameters, or disables safety mechanisms — but wraps it in language that has been LLM-optimized to sound like a routine protocol upgrade. Unlike spam, this attack relies on quality over quantity: one proposal, carefully crafted, that slips through careful review because it has been tuned against the specific DAO's voter base and discourse norms.

Defenses: mandatory human review of all treasury-affecting proposals, time locks on execution, formal verification for proposals that change protocol parameters.

### 4. Social-Layer Attacks on Delegates

Many large DAOs use delegated voting, where a small number of delegates vote on behalf of many token holders. LLMs make it cheap to run large-scale social engineering against delegates — crafting targeted DMs, creating false reputational signals around competing delegates, or inundating delegates with apparently diverse constituent feedback that is actually generated by one attacker. The delegates become the choke point; compromise them and you compromise the whole vote.

Defenses: diverse delegate populations, authenticated constituent feedback channels, delegate rotation policies.

### 5. Meta-Governance Attacks

For DAOs that depend on external governance tokens (veCRV holders voting on Curve gauge weights, for example), the attacker doesn't need to attack the target DAO directly — they attack the upstream governance that the target DAO trusts. LLMs don't change the structure of this attack but they make it cheaper to run the social-layer component that complements the tokenomic component.

## The Prompt-Injection Cross-Over

When a DAO uses an AI agent as part of its governance process (summarizing proposals, ranking priorities, flagging risks), the agent itself becomes an attack target via [[prompt-injection]]. A proposal crafted with a hidden prompt-injection payload can cause the summarizer agent to misrepresent its content to voters, or cause the risk-flagger to approve a harmful proposal. This is a specific version of the general AI-agent-DAO problem covered in [[ai-agent-daos]] but worth naming separately because it's a low-cost attack with high leverage.

## Historical Incidents

As of early 2026, no single high-profile AI-governance attack has publicly destroyed a major DAO — which is itself interesting because the underlying capabilities have clearly been available for more than a year. The most plausible explanations:

- **Attacks that succeed are often not disclosed**. A treasury drain framed as a "governance mistake" is structurally hard to distinguish from an external attack.
- **Simpler attacks are still preferred**. Direct key theft, smart contract exploits, and social engineering of human team members remain cheaper and more reliable than sophisticated LLM-driven governance attacks.
- **DAO treasuries are smaller than people think**. The EV of attacking a mid-size DAO is often below the cost of a serious coordinated campaign.

The "why haven't we seen this yet" question is one of the genuinely open empirical questions in the AI×crypto space, and the honest answer is that it may be happening at low intensity without public attribution, or may be waiting for DAO treasuries to grow large enough to justify the operational cost.

## Defenses: A Layered Approach

No single defense is sufficient. A layered approach typically includes:

1. **Identity layer** — [[proof-of-humanity]] for one-person-one-vote components; stake-based for token-weighted components
2. **Economic layer** — proposal bonds, slashing, time locks, quadratic voting
3. **Human-review layer** — mandatory human review of treasury-affecting proposals above threshold
4. **AI-assisted defense** — using ML to flag suspicious proposal patterns, anomalous voter coordination, and linguistic signatures of LLM-generated content (an AI arms race where both sides use ML)
5. **Process layer** — delegate rotation, time-locked upgrades, emergency multisig overrides

The irony is that defending against AI governance attacks almost certainly requires using AI defensively — which introduces all the problems of [[ai-oracles|AI oracles]] and [[prompt-injection]] into the defense layer itself.
