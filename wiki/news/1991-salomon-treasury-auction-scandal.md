# Salomon Brothers Treasury Auction Scandal (1991)

In **August 1991**, **Salomon Brothers** — then the dominant US Treasury bond dealer with a reputation for aggressive trading — admitted that its Treasury desk head **Paul Mozer** had submitted **fraudulent customer-name bids in multiple Treasury auctions** (1990-1991), violating the Treasury's 35% bidder-share limit. The scandal nearly destroyed Salomon Brothers; **Warren Buffett** (Salomon's largest shareholder via Berkshire Hathaway) personally intervened as interim chairman to save the firm.

The scandal eventually:
- Forced Salomon to pay a $290M settlement (largest at the time) — $190M in fines and forfeitures plus a $100M restitution fund.
- Resulted in **John Meriwether's** resignation as Salomon vice chairman (Meriwether later founded [[ltcm-collapse-1998|LTCM]] in 1994).
- Precipitated Salomon's eventual sale to Travelers Group (1997), then merger into Citigroup.
- Re-shaped Treasury auction rules, including reformed bidder identification.

## Background

**Salomon Brothers** in 1990 was the most powerful US bond dealer:
- Largest Treasury Primary Dealer.
- Dominant in Government Bond and Mortgage trading.
- Headed by John Gutfreund (CEO since 1981); rose to fame via Michael Lewis's *Liar's Poker* (1989).

**Paul Mozer** (head of US Government Securities trading from 1988) ran the Treasury auction desk. The Treasury imposed a **35% bidder-share limit** to prevent any single firm from cornering an auction.

## The Mechanism

Mozer's scheme operated across **multiple Treasury auctions in 1990-1991**:

1. **Submit Salomon's own 35% bid** at the auction.
2. **Submit additional bids in customer names** (Mercury Asset Management, Tudor Investment, Quantum) — sometimes **without authorization** from those customers.
3. **Allocate the additional acquired Treasuries** to Salomon's own book at favorable prices.
4. **Effectively control >35%** of the issue, particularly the on-the-run 2-year notes.

Why on-the-run 2-years specifically:
- Most-traded Treasury maturity.
- Used as benchmark for repo financing.
- Cornering the on-the-run created a backwardation in repo (Salomon could lend bonds at premium since alternative was "fail").
- Created profitable [[on-off-the-run-treasury-arbitrage|on-off-the-run]] basis trade.

## The Discovery

**February 21, 1991:** One of the auctions later identified as involving unauthorized customer-nominee bids submitted by Mozer (part of the conduct covered by the eventual settlement).

**May 22, 1991:** The notorious corner — Salomon, through its own bid plus bids placed in customer names, controlled roughly **94% of the $12.26B 2-year note auction**, far above the Treasury's 35% bidder-share limit. This created an acute squeeze in the on-the-run 2-year note in the repo market.
- Salomon submitted bids for itself plus bids in the names of customers including Mercury Asset Management and Tudor Investment.
- Tudor and Mercury later disclaimed authorization.

**April-August 1991:** Treasury, SEC, and the Department of Justice investigated.

**August 9, 1991:** Salomon publicly disclosed the scandal:
- Admitted Mozer had submitted unauthorized customer bids.
- Estimated 4 separate auctions affected (Dec 1990 - Feb 1991).
- Mozer fired; CEO John Gutfreund and President Tom Strauss informed but didn't disclose.

**August 17, 1991:** Salomon CEO Gutfreund, President Strauss, and Vice Chairman John Meriwether all forced to resign.

**August 18, 1991:** Warren Buffett (Berkshire owned $700M Salomon convertible preferred) personally took over as interim chairman.

## Buffett's Rescue

Buffett's role over August 1991 - May 1992:
- **Repaired regulatory relationships** — met personally with SEC, Federal Reserve, Treasury.
- **Implemented "shrink-or-die" strategy** — cut bonus pool, fired culture-of-aggression traders.
- **Famously stated:** *"Lose money for the firm and I will be understanding. Lose a shred of reputation for the firm and I will be ruthless."*
- **Avoided criminal indictment of the firm** — Salomon settled for $290M total ($190M fines/forfeitures + $100M restitution fund).
- **Returned firm to profitability** by 1993.

Buffett's intervention is widely credited with saving Salomon from total collapse and avoiding broader bond-market disruption. Buffett later said it was "one of the most important things I've done in my career — and one of the most stressful."

## Triangular Arb Aspects

The scandal created multiple arbitrage opportunities:

**During the corner (Dec 1990 - Aug 1991):** Specialist arbs detected the abnormal repo backwardation in on-the-run 2-years. Trade: short on-the-run 2-years (borrowed at premium), long off-the-run 2-years. Captured the artificial backwardation; ~10-50 bp per month for 8 months.

**During the disclosure (Aug 9-18, 1991):** Salomon's stock fell 30%+; bond market briefly dislocated. Specialist arbs short Salomon equity + long bond market captured both legs.

**Post-Buffett rescue:** Specialist arbs long Salomon stock + long Salomon convertibles captured the recovery as Buffett restored credibility.

## The Mozer Connection

Paul Mozer's loss to the firm was relatively modest in immediate dollar terms — but the reputational and regulatory damage was catastrophic.

**Personal outcomes:**
- Mozer convicted of submitting false records.
- Sentenced to 4 months prison + 4 months house arrest + $1.1M fine.
- Permanently barred from securities industry.

**Institutional fallout:**
- John Meriwether (forced out of Salomon) founded **LTCM in 1994** — bringing the same arbitrage culture to a hedge fund. LTCM blew up 1998 (see [[ltcm-collapse-1998]]).
- The "swap-spread arbitrage" that LTCM relied on was conceptualized at Salomon during the 1980s under Meriwether and his "Arbitrage Group."
- Many ex-Salomon arbitrage-group members ended up at LTCM, then dispersed to JWM Associates (Meriwether's post-LTCM fund), Convergence Capital, etc.

## Lessons for Traders

1. **Auction integrity matters.** The Treasury's 35% rule was specifically to prevent the corner Mozer attempted. Cornering creates backwardation but reputational risk dwarfs the profit.
2. **Cultural enabling.** Salomon's 1980s aggressive trading culture (per *Liar's Poker*) made Mozer's fraud detectable but un-investigated for ~9 months.
3. **Buffett-style intervention works.** Buffett's reputation + personal commitment + regulatory diplomacy saved Salomon. Generalizable: when a firm faces existential reputational risk, a senior outside party with credibility can be worth $1B+.
4. **The Salomon → LTCM → 2008 chain is real.** Same trading philosophy (relative-value bond arbitrage) propagated through multiple firms and crises. Pattern recognition: **swap-spread arb + leverage + concentration** has caused multiple blow-ups.
5. **Pre-disclosure short-the-issuer is lucrative.** Salomon's 30% drop on August 9, 1991 was partially anticipated by traders observing the Treasury investigation; pre-positioning shorts captured.

## Aftermath

- 1991-1996: Salomon recovered under Buffett's chairmanship; gradually returned to profitability.
- 1997: Salomon acquired by Travelers Group ($9B).
- 1998: Travelers-Salomon merged with Citicorp to form Citigroup.
- 2003: Salomon brand name retired.
- Treasury auction rules tightened: bidder identification requirements; expanded TRACE-style monitoring.
