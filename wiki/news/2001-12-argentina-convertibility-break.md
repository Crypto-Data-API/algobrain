# Argentina Convertibility Break (December 2001)

In **December 2001**, Argentina simultaneously abandoned its 10-year-old **Convertibility Plan** (1 peso = 1 USD currency board), defaulted on **$93B in sovereign debt** (largest sovereign default in history at that point), and froze bank deposits ("**el Corralito**"). The peso eventually fell from 1.00 to 3.90 per USD over 18 months. Triggered three presidential resignations in two weeks, riots that killed 39 people, and locked an estimated $50B+ in dollar-denominated savings. Macro funds with anti-Argentina positioning made meaningful profits; long-only EM bond funds were devastated.

## Background

The **Convertibility Plan** was Domingo Cavallo's 1991 anti-hyperinflation reform: peso pegged 1:1 to US dollar, currency-board structure required dollar reserves backing 100% of monetary base. Successful 1991-1998:
- Inflation collapsed from 5000% (1989) to <5% by mid-1990s.
- GDP growth 5-8% annually 1991-1997.
- Foreign investment poured in.

**Strain emerged 1998-2001:**
- USD strength globally (1995-2001) made peso uncompetitive.
- Brazilian real devaluation (January 1999) — Brazil is Argentina's largest trade partner.
- Fiscal deficit rising as growth slowed.
- IMF programs in 2000, 2001 (totaling $40B+) failed to stabilize.

## The Cascade

**November 30, 2001:** Bank run accelerates; Argentine deposits down $4.6B in a week.

**December 1, 2001:** Cavallo announces "el Corralito" — withdrawal limits of $250/week. Frozen ~$50B in dollar-denominated savings. Riots erupt nationwide.

**December 19-20, 2001:**
- President de la Rúa declares state of emergency.
- 39 protesters killed in riots.
- December 20: De la Rúa resigns; flees Casa Rosada by helicopter.

**December 23, 2001:** Interim President Adolfo Rodríguez Saá announces $93B default — largest sovereign default ever at that point. Held office 7 days before resigning December 30.

**January 6, 2002:** Eduardo Duhalde (third interim president in 2 weeks) abolishes Convertibility. Peso officially "asymmetric pesification" — most contracts converted to peso at 1:1 (debtors win, creditors lose), some at 1.40, dollar deposits frozen further.

**Float path:**
| Date | ARS/USD | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Dec 1, 2001 | 1.00 | Last day of Convertibility |

## The Restructuring

Argentina's $93B default took **18 years to fully resolve**:

- **2005:** Initial restructuring offered ~33¢ on the dollar; ~76% of bondholders accepted.
- **2010:** Second offer; ~92% acceptance (combined).
- **2014:** Hold-out litigation (NML Capital / Paul Singer / Elliott Management) forces Argentina into another technical default after Judge Griesa ruling.
- **2016:** Macri government settles with hold-outs at ~75¢ on the dollar — Elliott Management profits estimated at **$2.4B** on $617M cost basis (4x return over 15+ years).
- **2018-2020:** New IMF $57B program; Argentina defaults again in 2020 ("ninth default").

## Triangular Aspects

The Argentina crisis offered dramatic triangular dislocations because the peso was fragmented across multiple official rates plus parallel "blue dollar":

**Triangle 1: ARS-USD-BRL.** Argentine peso vs Brazilian real (Argentina's largest trade partner) reflected fundamentals; cross-rate dislocations against direct USD pricing offered arb windows.

**Triangle 2: ARS official vs ARS NDF (offshore non-deliverable forward) vs ARS blue.** Multiple peso prices coexisted. Offshore NDF traded at predictive rates; blue (parallel) market diverged. Cross-rate spread = 50-200% during chaos.

**Triangle 3: Argentine sovereign Eurobonds vs CDS vs domestic peso bonds.** Triangle of: hard-currency sovereign debt at 20¢, dollar-denominated domestic debt forcibly converted to pesos (post-pesification), CDS spreads pricing recovery. Specialist arbs traded across the three.

**Triangle 4: ARS-USD-USDC stablecoin (post-2018 emergence).** Modern echo: Argentine retail used USDT/USDC as dollar substitute when official ARS was inaccessible. Triangle BLU-MEP-USDT-USD opens recurring arbs.

## Winners

**NML Capital / Elliott Management (Paul Singer)** — Bought hold-out Argentine bonds at 20-30¢ for 15 years; settled at 75¢+ in 2016. **$2.4B+ profit** over 15-year hold. The textbook distressed-sovereign trade.

**Aurelius Capital, Bracebridge** — Smaller hold-out funds; similar multiples on smaller bases.

**Macro funds short Argentine debt 2001** — Tudor, Soros (recovered Russian losses), Caxton; estimated $100-300M each.

**Citadel and Renaissance** — Mean-reversion strategies caught 2002 recovery rally.

**Argentine bond restructuring specialists** — Boutique sovereign-debt funds (Greylock, Gramercy) profited on multiple Argentine restructurings 2005-2020.

## Losers

**Argentine retail savers** — $50B+ frozen in Corralito; many destroyed.

**Long-only EM bond funds** — PIMCO, Templeton EM debt funds took 30-50% drawdowns.

**Spanish banks** — Significant Argentine exposure; BBVA and Santander took multi-billion losses.

**Italian retail bondholders** — Tens of thousands held Argentine bonds via local advisors; lost 70%+ over years.

**IMF** — Reputational damage for failed 2000-2001 programs.

## Aftermath

- 2002-2007: Argentina recovery on commodity boom; GDP growth 8%/year average.
- 2007-2015: Christina Fernández de Kirchner regime, repeated FX controls and parallel-market spread (cepo).
- 2014: Technical default again over hold-out litigation.
- 2016-2018: Macri liberalization; followed by 2018 currency crisis.
- 2020: 9th sovereign default.
- 2023: Milei election triggered new economic restructuring; peso devalued ~50% on day of inauguration.

## Lessons for Traders

1. **Currency boards eventually break** unless backed by serious fiscal discipline. Argentina (1991-2001), Hong Kong (1983-present), Bulgaria (1997-present) — only the latter two survived; both maintain extreme fiscal conservatism.
2. **Sequential EM crises pattern:** Asia '97 → Russia '98 → Brazil '99 → Argentina '01 → Turkey '01. Macro funds rotate through.
3. **Hold-out distressed sovereign trades require 10-20 year horizons.** Singer/Elliott's Argentine trade was 15+ years; the patience moat is real.
4. **Triangulation across official + NDF + parallel markets** is a recurring EM-arb structure (Argentina, Venezuela, Egypt, Nigeria all exhibit this today).
5. **Asymmetric pesification = legal contract repudiation.** The 2002 Argentine policy explicitly violated dollar contracts; lawyers became the bottleneck for any recovery.
