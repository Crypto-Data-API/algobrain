# Russia Ruble Default (August 1998)

On **August 17, 1998**, Russia simultaneously **devalued the ruble** (band widened from 5.27-7.13 to 6.0-9.5 per USD), **defaulted on $40B of GKO domestic ruble debt**, and imposed a **90-day moratorium on commercial debt payments** to foreign creditors. The ruble subsequently collapsed from 6.3 to ~21 per USD over four months. The shock triggered a global cascade that broke [[ltcm-collapse-1998|Long-Term Capital Management]], wiped out emerging-markets dedicated funds, and forced a Fed-coordinated bank rescue. A handful of funds with anti-EM positioning made enormous profits.

## Background

Russia transitioned from Soviet planned economy to capitalism through the 1990s with mixed success: hyperinflation, asset stripping, oligarch concentration, weak tax collection. By 1997 the ruble was managed within a corridor (5-6 per USD) supported by **GKO bonds** (short-term ruble Treasuries paying 30-100%+ yields denominated in rubles).

**The "ruble carry trade":**
- Foreign investors bought GKOs at face value in rubles.
- Hedged FX risk via forward sale of rubles.
- Earned the GKO yield minus forward premium — typically 10-30% USD-denominated returns.
- Estimated $20B+ in foreign GKO holdings by mid-1998.

**Crisis triggers (1997-1998):**
- Asian Financial Crisis (July 1997-1998) reduced commodity demand → oil price fell from $25 to $11.
- Russian fiscal: oil/gas revenue collapsed; tax collection fragile; deficit ballooning.
- IMF emergency package $22.6B (July 1998) bought 30 days; ran out by mid-August.
- Central Bank reserves fell from $20B to $11B by August 1998.

## The Default

**August 17, 1998 announcement:**

1. **Devalue ruble** band from 5.27-7.13 to 6.0-9.5 per USD.
2. **Default on GKO and OFZ debt** (~$40B in rouble-denominated obligations).
3. **90-day moratorium** on all commercial debt to foreign creditors.

**Within hours:**
- Ruble traded at 7.0 (top of new band) and accelerated.
- Russian banks lost interbank access globally.
- Russian sovereign bonds (USD-denominated Eurobonds) crashed from 90 to 20-40 cents on the dollar.

**Path:**
| Date | RUB/USD | Comment |
|------|---------|---------|
| Aug 14, 1998 | 6.30 | Pre-default |

Total ruble loss vs USD: 70-75% in 4 months.

## Triangular Aspects

The ruble crisis created intense cross-rate dislocations across August-October 1998:

**Triangle 1: USD-RUB-DEM.** EUR didn't exist yet; DEM was the dominant European reference. RUB/DEM cross-rate broke from forward-implied parity by 200-500 bp during the most volatile sessions.

**Triangle 2: RUB-USD-emerging Asian currencies.** The ruble crisis triggered a second wave of Asian-EM weakness; Indonesian rupiah, Korean won, Thai baht all repriced. Cross-rates fragmented.

**Triangle 3: GKO yield vs FX-forward vs Russian Eurobonds.** The classical "synthetic dollar yield via ruble + forward" trade exploded. Pre-crisis it was 10-30% APR; during/after crisis it was -100% (default).

**Triangle 4: USD-RUB-Brent crude.** Oil and ruble are mechanically linked (oil exports). Cross-rate dislocations between the three confirmed the structural fundamental.

## Winners

**Soros / Quantum Fund** — Per Soros's own account in *The Crisis of Global Capitalism* (1998), Quantum had been **long Russian assets going into the crisis** and lost ~$2B. *Not* a winner here.

**Tudor Investment / Paul Tudor Jones** — Reportedly short ruble and short Russian Eurobonds; estimated $200-500M profits.

**Caxton / Bruce Kovner** — Multi-leg short-EM positioning generated mid-9-figure profits.

**Brevan Howard precursor (Jacob Goldfield at Goldman)** — Goldman macro desk had short EM exposure; profits sized for prop book.

**Renaissance Medallion** — Statistical strategies caught the early move and rode it.

**Anti-LTCM specialists (Citadel, etc.)** — Funds running short-correlation trades benefited from LTCM's forced unwind across all positions.

## Losers

**Long-Term Capital Management** — Catastrophic. LTCM had massive Russian arbitrage positions (long GKOs hedged by FX forwards). When Russia defaulted, the forwards were unenforceable; LTCM's hedge evaporated. The Russian losses combined with simultaneous swap-spread blowouts (correlation = 1) destroyed the fund. Federal Reserve coordinated bank rescue on September 23, 1998. See [[ltcm-collapse-1998]].

**Long Term Credit Bank of Japan (LTCB)** — Failed October 1998; partly due to Russian exposure.

**Bankers Trust** — Russia exposure plus other losses; acquired by Deutsche Bank June 1999.

**Soros** — $2B loss as noted above.

**Russian banks** — Most major commercial banks insolvent; SBS-Agro, Inkombank, Menatep failed.

**EM-dedicated funds** — Many wound up in 1998-1999.

## Aftermath

- **September 23, 1998:** Fed-coordinated $3.6B private-sector LTCM rescue (14 banks).
- **September 29, 1998:** Fed cuts rates 25 bp (first of 3 emergency cuts).
- **October 1998:** Yen carry-trade unwound violently as risk-off intensified; USD/JPY -15% in 3 days.
- **1999-2000:** Russia eventually negotiated GKO restructuring; ~50% recovery for foreign holders over years.
- **1999:** EM crisis spread to Brazil (January 1999 real devaluation — see [[1999-01-brazil-real-devaluation]]).

## Lessons for Traders

1. **Sovereign default + currency moratorium + forward-contract repudiation is the unhedgeable scenario.** LTCM thought their FX forwards hedged ruble risk; Russia simply refused to honor them.
2. **Carry trades concentrate in countries where you cannot enforce contracts.** GKO carry was 30%+ APR for years — should have flagged the obvious.
3. **Cascading correlations break risk-arb books.** LTCM's various uncorrelated positions all moved together when liquidity vanished.
4. **Triangular cross-rates confirm regime shifts.** RUB-DEM, RUB-Asian, RUB-oil all dislocated together.
5. **Counter-positioning is asymmetric.** Tudor and Caxton's short-EM stance was the right side of the trade with multi-x payoff vs limited carry-cost downside.
