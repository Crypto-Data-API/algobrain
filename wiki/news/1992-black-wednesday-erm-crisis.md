# Black Wednesday — UK ERM Crisis 1992

On **September 16, 1992**, the United Kingdom was forced to withdraw the pound sterling from the European Exchange Rate Mechanism (ERM) after failing to defend the £/DEM peg above its required floor of 2.7780. The Quantum Fund (George Soros, with Stanley Druckenmiller as portfolio manager) had built a $10 billion+ short position in sterling and is widely credited with profits of approximately $1 billion+ — the trade that earned Soros the moniker *"the man who broke the Bank of England."*

## Background

The UK joined the ERM on October 5, 1990 at a central rate of GBP/DEM 2.95, with a ±6% band (floor 2.7780, ceiling 3.1320). The peg required UK interest rates to track Bundesbank rates — but the UK economy was in recession while German rates rose sharply post-reunification (1990-1992).

By summer 1992 the contradiction was acute:
- UK unemployment 10%, recession.
- Bundesbank discount rate 8.75% (raised July 16, 1992).
- Speculators saw an unsustainable peg.

The trigger: **Helmut Schlesinger** (Bundesbank president) gave a Wall Street Journal / Handelsblatt interview published the morning of Sep 15 saying that even after Italy's devaluation (the previous weekend), broader ERM realignment might be needed. Sterling shorts piled in.

## Mechanics of the Trade

Druckenmiller, as Quantum's portfolio manager, identified the trade in early summer. Soros pushed for larger sizing ("go for the jugular"). The fund:

1. Borrowed sterling — short positions via FX forwards and options across multiple prime brokers.
2. Converted to DEM and other ERM currencies.
3. Held until the peg broke.
4. Reversed at GBP/DEM 2.50-2.70 — a 10-15% move in days.

Estimated peak notional ~$10B. Reported P&L: $1B-$2B (net of complementary losses on Italian, Spanish, Swedish positions).

## Triangular Aspects

The crisis created multiple [[crisis-currency-triangular-arbitrage|triangular dislocations]]:

- **GBP-DEM-ITL:** Italian lira had been forced out Sep 13. Cross-rates between GBP, DEM, and ITL became inconsistent for hours during Sep 16.
- **GBP-DEM-FRF:** France defended the FRF peg successfully (with Bundesbank cooperation), creating a GBP-DEM-FRF triangle that re-established by Sep 17.
- **GBP-DEM-SEK:** Sweden raised marginal lending rate to 500% (briefly) defending the krona; ultimately failed Nov 19, 1992. Quantum had Swedish positions too.

## Other Winners

Druckenmiller (later credited the trade as primarily his idea), Quantum: $1B+. Bruce Kovner (Caxton Associates), Paul Tudor Jones, and Louis Bacon (Moore Capital) reportedly added $300-500M each on parallel ERM short positions. Smaller hedge funds and some prop desks at Goldman, Salomon, and Bankers Trust also participated.

## Consequences

- UK Treasury lost £3.3B intervening (£27B gross spend, recovered partially).
- Norman Lamont's career ended (resigned May 1993).
- UK abandoned the path to euro adoption.
- Conservative Party trailed Labour in polls for 18 years.
- Bundesbank credibility cemented as anchor for European monetary policy.
- ERM-II rules redesigned post-1996; bands widened to ±15%.

## Lessons for Arb Traders

1. **Pegs are policy, not price.** When the policy commitment cracks, the price is irrelevant.
2. **Asymmetric payoff.** Defended peg → small downside (carry cost of short). Broken peg → multi-percent move in days.
3. **Triangulation closes last.** Cross-rates between non-attacked pairs reprice slowly enough to permit a second-order triangle trade.
4. **Cooperation matters.** France/Germany defended FRF/DEM jointly — that pair held. UK was alone.
5. **Insider information from central bankers can leak through interviews.** Schlesinger's WSJ comments were the proximate trigger.
