# Brazil Real Devaluation (January 1999)

On **January 13, 1999**, Brazil abandoned its 4.5-year-old Plano Real currency anchor, allowing the real to float. The exchange rate moved from **R$1.21 per USD to ~R$2.16 in three weeks** — the real lost roughly **44% of its value** (equivalently, USD/BRL rose ~78%). Triggered by Russian-default contagion and unsustainable fiscal-monetary tension between the federal government and state governors (Itamar Franco's Minas Gerais 90-day moratorium being the proximate flash). Macro hedge funds that had short-real positioning made $200-500M each; long-only EM funds were wiped out.

## Background

The **Plano Real** (1994) ended Brazil's hyperinflation by:
- Anchoring the new real currency to the US dollar (initially 1:1, gradually devalued to ~1.21 by 1998).
- Tight monetary policy (interest rates 30-50%).
- Privatization and fiscal reform.

By mid-1998 the structure was strained:
- Federal fiscal deficit ~7% of GDP.
- Asian crisis (1997) and Russian crisis (August 1998) had drained foreign reserves from $75B to $35B.
- Interest rates at 50% (real terms ~30%) — strangling growth.
- IMF/World Bank package $41.5B announced November 13, 1998 to defend the peg.

The IMF package bought ~60 days. Markets remained skeptical.

## Trigger: Itamar Franco's Moratorium

On **January 6, 1999**, **Itamar Franco** — former Brazilian president (1992-1994) and now governor of Minas Gerais state — announced a **90-day moratorium on Minas Gerais' federal debt obligations**. This was a political shot: Franco was signaling that state-federal fiscal relations had broken down.

Markets interpreted: if states default to federal government, federal balance sheet collapses; peg untenable.

**Reserve drain:**
- January 6: $36B reserves.
- January 12: $30B (lost $6B in 5 sessions defending peg).
- January 13: Central Bank governor Gustavo Franco (no relation to Itamar) resigned.
- January 13 PM: Brazil widened the band; effectively floated.
- January 15: Full free float adopted.

## The Move

| Date | BRL/USD | Notes |
|------|---------|-------|
| Jan 5, 1999 | 1.21 | Stable peg |
| Jan 13, 1999 | 1.32 | Band widened |
| Jan 15, 1999 | 1.46 | Free float |
| Jan 28, 1999 | 1.85 | Free fall |
| Feb 2, 1999 | 2.16 (peak intraday) | Bottom |
| Mar 31, 1999 | 1.72 | Stabilization |

Total: the real lost ~44% of its value vs USD over 3 weeks at the R$2.16 intraday peak (USD/BRL +78%); it then partially recovered to ~R$1.72 by end-March 1999.

Brazilian Bovespa stock index fell 10% on January 13 alone, but recovered fully within 6 months as exporters benefited from the weaker currency.

## Triangular Aspects

The Brazilian crisis offered cleaner triangular trades than the prior Asian/Russian cascades because Brazilian markets remained more open and liquid:

**Triangle 1: BRL-USD-DEM.** Cross-rates between BRL/USD and BRL/DEM diverged 100-300 bp during the chaos.

**Triangle 2: BRL-USD-Mexican peso.** Mexican peso (post-Tequila Crisis stabilization) held; cross-rate between BRL/MXN broke from forward-implied by hundreds of bp.

**Triangle 3: BRL-Bovespa-Brady bond.** Brazilian dollar-denominated Brady bonds vs local-currency stocks vs FX provided a three-leg liquidity-stress trade.

**Triangle 4: NDF (non-deliverable forward) vs spot vs interest rate parity.** Pre-floats, BRL NDFs traded offshore. After float, the basis between NDF and on-shore spot collapsed, creating pure arbitrage for those with cross-jurisdictional access.

## Winners

**Tudor Investment Corp / Paul Tudor Jones** — Short-real positioning since fall 1998; estimated **$300-500M profit**. Tudor was already in defensive EM mode post-Russia.

**Soros / Quantum Fund** — *Recovered from Russia losses* by short-real positioning; reportedly **$200-400M profit**. Stanley Druckenmiller-led.

**Caxton / Bruce Kovner** — Short-real and short-Russian-bond positioning; estimated $200M+.

**Citadel** — Multi-strategy gains.

**Macro funds with short-EM curve** — Brevan Howard precursor groups, Hayman precursor.

## Losers

**Long-only Brazil-dedicated funds** — Most wound up.

**Brazilian retail dollar-denominated debtors** — Mortgage payments doubled overnight.

**Brazilian banks** — Significant insolvencies; Banco do Brasil and others required recapitalization.

**Some hedge funds with long-Brazil bond carry trades** — Got the timing wrong on the IMF package.

## Aftermath

- **March 2, 1999:** New Central Bank Governor Arminio Fraga (formerly of Soros Fund Management) appointed.
- **March 1999:** Brazil adopted **inflation-targeting** regime (replacing exchange-rate anchor).
- **June 2000:** Real had stabilized around 1.80-1.85 vs USD.
- **2002-2003:** Lula election triggered second BRL crisis (real briefly hit 4.00 in October 2002 before recovering).
- **2007-2014:** Brazil boomed on commodity supercycle; real rallied to 1.55 by mid-2011.

## Lessons for Traders

1. **Sequential EM crises are the rule.** Asia 1997 → Russia 1998 → Brazil 1999 → Argentina 2001 → Turkey 2001 — pattern of contagion is highly predictable.
2. **Sub-sovereign defaults presage sovereign defaults.** Itamar Franco's Minas Gerais moratorium was the canary; markets correctly read it as a federal-fiscal signal.
3. **Macro funds rotate through EM crises.** Tudor, Soros, Caxton recovered Russian losses on Brazil within 6 months.
4. **Inflation-targeting beats exchange-rate anchors** for emerging markets — Brazil's post-1999 framework has been largely successful.
5. **Triangle dislocations during EM crises last days, not minutes** — unlike G10 crises where they last seconds. EM gives time for sophisticated multi-venue arb.
