# 2025 Tariff-Driven Market Volatility

## What Happened

Throughout 2025, tariff uncertainty became the dominant driver of global market volatility. Average U.S. tariff duties rose from 2.4% at the start of the year to between 9.6% and 13% by year-end, depending on the measurement period. Tariff revenue surged to an estimated $214-264 billion — roughly triple the 2024 level. Studies found that nearly 90% of tariff costs were passed directly to U.S. consumers rather than absorbed by foreign exporters. Food prices climbed 2.8% above baseline as agricultural imports were hit. China's share of U.S. imports fell below 10% as supply chains decoupled. Despite promises that tariffs would revitalize American manufacturing, factory jobs actually declined amid input cost increases and retaliatory measures.

## Market Impact

- **S&P 500**: Experienced repeated 3-5% swings tied to tariff headlines rather than fundamentals
- **VIX**: Averaged well above historical norms for the year, with frequent spikes above 30
- **Consumer staples**: Outperformed as defensive positioning dominated risk-off periods
- **USD**: Strengthened during escalation phases, weakened during pause announcements
- **[[bitcoin]]**: Oscillated between behaving as a risk asset (selling off with stocks) and a hedge (rallying on dollar weakness)
- **Commodities**: Soybeans, copper, and industrial metals were direct tariff proxies
- **Emerging market equities**: Underperformed as global trade contraction hit export-dependent economies

## Why It Matters

The 2025 tariff environment created a market regime where traditional fundamental analysis took a backseat to policy headline tracking. Earnings estimates became unreliable because input costs changed with each tariff announcement. The 90% consumer pass-through rate meant that tariffs functioned as a regressive tax, weighing on consumer spending and GDP growth. For crypto markets specifically, the tariff volatility tested whether digital assets could serve as a genuine macro hedge or remained correlated risk assets. The answer was "both" — depending on the week — which complicated any simple [[risk-on-risk-off-framework]] application.

## Aftermath

The persistent volatility exhausted many market participants. Hedge fund performance diverged sharply between those with tariff-aware models and those relying on pre-2025 playbooks. The legal and constitutional challenges to tariff authority culminated in the [[2026-02-20-supreme-court-tariff-ruling]], which invalidated a significant portion of the tariffs. However, the administration's response to that ruling showed that tariff-driven volatility would persist into 2026. Supply chain reshoring, once theoretical, became an active corporate priority, permanently altering global trade flows.

## Trading Lessons

- **Regime awareness beats conviction**: Traders who adapted to the tariff-driven regime outperformed those clinging to pre-2025 models
- **Headline velocity matters**: The speed of tariff announcements and reversals meant that daily monitoring was essential
- **Consumer pass-through data leads equity moves**: Tracking import price indices gave early warning of margin compression
- **Crypto is not a clean hedge**: BTC behaved as both risk-on and risk-off during 2025, requiring dynamic positioning
- **Volatility itself is tradeable**: Elevated VIX created consistent premium-selling opportunities for disciplined options traders
- **Supply chain data reveals winners and losers**: Vietnam, Mexico, and India equity markets benefited from China substitution
