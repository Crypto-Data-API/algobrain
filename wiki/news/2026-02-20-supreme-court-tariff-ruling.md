# Supreme Court Strikes Down Liberation Day Tariffs

## What Happened

On February 20, 2026, the U.S. Supreme Court ruled that President Trump had exceeded his authority under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) for approximately 70% of the tariffs imposed since April 2025. The ruling specifically invalidated the "Liberation Day" tariffs and the fentanyl-related tariffs on China, Mexico, and Canada. Markets initially rallied sharply on expectations that the tariff burden would be lifted and economic uncertainty reduced. However, the relief was short-lived. On February 21, just one day later, the Trump administration announced a 15% global tariff under new legislation and invoked Section 122 of the Trade Act for an additional 10% surcharge on all imports. The rally reversed violently, creating yet another tariff-driven whipsaw.

## Market Impact

- **S&P 500**: Rallied ~3% on the ruling, then gave back gains and turned negative the next day
- **VIX**: Dropped on Feb 20, spiked back above 25 on Feb 21
- **USD**: Weakened on the ruling (tariff removal bullish for trade), then strengthened on new tariff announcement
- **Agricultural commodities**: Rallied briefly on hopes of China trade normalization, reversed on new tariffs
- **Import-dependent retailers**: Surged then sold off within 24 hours
- **Industrials**: Whipsawed as the manufacturing cost outlook changed twice in two days
- **Bond market**: Yields initially dropped (risk-on), then reversed as inflation expectations reset

## Why It Matters

The Supreme Court ruling tested a critical question: can legal checks on executive power reduce market uncertainty? The answer, in this case, was no. While the Court invalidated the original legal basis for the tariffs, the executive branch simply found alternative statutory authority to reimpose them. This demonstrated that tariff risk was not a legal problem but a political one — as long as the administration prioritized tariffs as policy, the specific legal mechanism was interchangeable. For traders, the lesson was that betting on legal resolution of tariff uncertainty was a trap. The [[risk-on-risk-off-framework]] had to account for political will, not just legal constraints.

## Aftermath

The whipsaw destroyed traders who had positioned for a clean resolution of tariff uncertainty. Many who bought the rally on Feb 20 were stopped out on Feb 21. The new 15% global tariff plus 10% surcharge effectively replaced the invalidated structure with a slightly lower but still historically elevated tariff regime. The ruling's practical impact was minimal — effective tariff rates remained well above pre-2025 levels. The event contributed to the broader [[2026-market-regime-overview]] of persistent policy uncertainty. International trading partners expressed frustration that court rulings did not produce lasting trade normalization.

## Trading Lessons

- **Legal victories do not guarantee policy change**: The administration's rapid workaround demonstrated that tariff risk transcends any single legal authority
- **Never chase the first move on a legal ruling**: The Feb 20 rally was a trap; waiting 24-48 hours for the full response was essential
- **Whipsaws punish overleveraged positions**: Two sharp reversals in two days liquidated both bulls and bears
- **Political will is the fundamental variable**: Legal constraints are temporary if the executive branch is determined to maintain a policy
- **Hedging tariff risk requires ongoing cost, not one-time bets**: Protective options and diversification were more effective than directional conviction
- **Watch for alternative authorities**: When one legal basis is removed, track what replacement mechanisms are available
- **International reactions compound domestic moves**: Trading partners' responses added a second wave of uncertainty after the initial whipsaw
