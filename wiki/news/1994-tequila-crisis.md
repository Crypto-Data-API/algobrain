# Tequila Crisis — Mexican Peso 1994

The **Tequila Crisis** was the December 1994 collapse of the Mexican peso peg to the US dollar, devaluing the currency from MXN 3.4/USD to MXN 7.2/USD over six weeks. It triggered the first major emerging-markets contagion of the post-Bretton-Woods era ("Tequila Effect" hitting Argentina, Brazil, Chile), and required a $50 billion US-led bailout orchestrated by Treasury Secretary **Robert Rubin** and IMF Managing Director **Michel Camdessus**.

## Background

Mexico maintained a crawling peg under President Carlos Salinas (1988-1994). NAFTA took effect January 1, 1994, drawing record foreign portfolio inflows. The peg was supported by:

- **Tesobonos:** dollar-indexed peso-denominated short-term debt — converted peso devaluation risk into US-dollar liability for the Mexican government.
- Foreign reserves $25B+ at start of 1994.
- US-anchored credibility via NAFTA.

But fundamentals deteriorated through 1994:

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| Jan 1, 1994 | NAFTA in force; Zapatista uprising (EZLN) in Chiapas |
| Mar 23, 1994 | PRI presidential candidate Luis Donaldo Colosio assassinated in Tijuana |
| Sep 28, 1994 | PRI Secretary General José Francisco Ruiz Massieu assassinated |
| Oct-Nov 1994 | Reserves fall from $25B to $12B; capital flight accelerates |
| Dec 1, 1994 | Ernesto Zedillo inaugurated; Salinas leaves office |
| Dec 19, 1994 | Government announces 15% peso devaluation (band widening) |
| Dec 22, 1994 | Floating peso; immediate 50% drop |

## Mechanics of the Crisis

Tesobonos were the time bomb. By December 1994, ~$30B Tesobonos outstanding, mostly held by foreign investors. As reserves fell, redemption demands accelerated. The Banco de México could not meet Tesobono maturities in dollars without a bailout.

The peg-defense math:

- Reserves $12B (Dec 1994).
- Tesobono maturities Q1 1995: $28B.
- Result: insolvency within 90 days unless restructured or bailed out.

## The Bailout

Robert Rubin, newly confirmed Treasury Secretary, used the **Exchange Stabilization Fund** ($20B) without Congressional approval — controversial but legally sanctioned. Combined with $17.8B IMF, $10B BIS, $1B Canada, $3B commercial banks. Total $51.8B announced January 31, 1995.

Conditions:
- Mexico pledged oil revenues as collateral via NY Fed account.
- Macroeconomic adjustment program.
- Bank-rescue program (FOBAPROA — eventually cost ~15% of Mexican GDP).

Repaid in full by January 1997, three years ahead of schedule.

## Trader P&L

**Winners:**
- FX shorts on the peso (Tudor, Caxton, Soros, Robertson's Tiger) — estimated $200-500M each.
- Tesobono short positions hard to find counterparty for, but those who structured them via OTC swaps with US banks profited.
- Triangular peso arbs across MXN-USD-DEM, MXN-USD-CAD as cross-rates fragmented hour-to-hour during Dec 19-22.

**Losers:**
- Mexican banking system insolvent.
- US foreign-portfolio investors locked into Tesobonos redeemed at devalued forward rates.
- Argentina (Convertibility Plan tested), Brazil, Chile — capital flight contagion.
- Long-Term Capital Management (founded 1994) had small Mexico exposure but learned wrong lessons about EM convergence trades.

## Triangular Aspects

During Dec 19-22, 1994:
- **MXN-USD-CAD:** USDCAD traded normally; USDMXN gapped; MXNCAD (rarely directly quoted) opened wide for hours.
- **MXN-USD-DEM:** European banks reluctant to quote MXN; cross-rate via USD became the only path; banks charged 100-200 bp spread.
- **Argentine peso (then 1:1 convertibility):** Tequila contagion forced Argentina to defend its own peg (held until 2001), opening short-window MXN-USD-ARS triangulation for those bold enough.

## Lessons

1. **Currency mismatches are time bombs.** Tesobonos dollarized the liability without dollarizing the asset.
2. **Political shocks accelerate but don't cause crises.** Colosio assassination revealed an already-fragile peg.
3. **External bailout sets a precedent.** Mexico repaid; LTCM was bailed out 1998; sovereign-rescue norms shifted.
4. **Crisis windows are 48-96 hours.** Triangle dislocations close fast.
