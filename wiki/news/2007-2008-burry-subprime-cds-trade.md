Between 2005 and 2008, [[michael-burry|Michael Burry]] — running Scion Capital with approximately **$600M-$1B in AUM** at peak — bought credit-default-swap protection on specific tranches of [[subprime-mortgage|subprime-mortgage]]-backed securities. After **two years of paying premium and being mocked**, the trade exploded as subprime defaults cascaded in 2007-2008. Scion's fund earned approximately **$700M for investors**; Burry personally made approximately **$100M**. Beyond the headline P&L, the trade is the canonical case study for **information arbitrage at glacial speed**: an edge generated by reading documents nobody else would read, structured into instruments that paid only after years of patience. See [[fastest-profitable-trades]] for the broader pattern, [[convex-tail-hedge-arbitrage]] for the structural framework, and [[michael-burry]] for the trader's biography.

## What was the trade

### The instrument: tranched MBS CDS

Subprime mortgage-backed securities (MBS) pool thousands of individual mortgages and slice the cash flows into **tranches** rated from AAA (most senior, last to take losses) down through BBB and unrated equity (first-loss).

Burry's recognition: ratings agencies were rating BBB tranches of subprime MBS as if they had a 1-3% probability of default — based on historical models that assumed continued rising home prices and historical default rates. Burry's primary-document analysis showed:

- The underlying mortgages had **fraudulent income verification**, **adjustable "teaser" rates**, and **97-100% loan-to-value ratios**.
- When teaser rates reset (mostly scheduled for 2007), monthly payments would jump 30-100%.
- Borrowers couldn't afford the reset payments.
- Refinancing required rising home prices, which were structurally about to reverse.
- The BBB tranche of these securities — which absorbed losses after only ~5% of the underlying pool defaulted — was almost certain to be wiped out.

The instrument that paid off if BBB tranches defaulted was a **credit-default swap** on those specific tranches: pay a periodic premium (~2% per year of notional) to receive the par value if the tranche defaulted. Banks were happy to sell this protection because (a) they thought subprime was safe, (b) the premium was easy revenue, and (c) the structures were illiquid enough that banks didn't have to mark them to market accurately.

### Why this was uniquely structured

Three structural features made the trade work — and made it nearly impossible for anyone else to put on:

1. **Buy CDS on specific tranches, not general MBS exposure.** Most short-the-housing thesis attempts (e.g., shorting HBC stocks, shorting REITs, shorting the broad MBS market) were either inefficient (high carry, ambiguous tracking) or inaccessible (banks wouldn't sell broad MBS protection at the same scale). Tranche-specific CDS was the most direct, most levered, most asymmetric expression.
2. **Banks had to design the contracts.** In 2005, no standard CDS contract existed for individual MBS tranches. Burry had to convince Goldman Sachs (and later Deutsche Bank, Morgan Stanley) to design and sell him the protection. The deals took weeks to structure, hundreds of pages of documentation per trade, and required Burry to provide detailed thesis presentations.
3. **The trade required sustained capital availability over multiple years.** From 2005 entry to mid-2007 catalyst, Burry paid annual premiums totaling ~$5-15M per year against a fund that was simultaneously suffering LP redemption pressure. He invoked a contractual lockup provision in 2006 to prevent forced liquidation.

## The carry-cost agony (2005-2007)

Burry's positions bled premium for two years before the catalyst arrived. The fund's mark-to-market reflected:

- **2005**: -1% to -3% per quarter as premiums accrued; LPs uncomfortable but tolerable.
- **2006**: -8% to -15% drawdown as housing market stayed strong; CDS marks deteriorated despite Burry's thesis being correct in the underlying loans. LP redemption requests mounted; Scion's investor base collapsed from ~$1B to ~$600M (some investors withdrew before the lockup, others were prevented).
- **Q1 2007**: LP communications increasingly hostile; Burry refused to liquidate. LPs sued in some cases.
- **Mid-2007**: First subprime defaults cascading; CDS marks began moving; positions transitioned from -10% mark to +100%+ mark within months.
- **Late 2007 - 2008**: Positions paid out at par as the underlying tranches were written down to zero.

This carry-cost agony is not unique to Burry. It is a **structural feature of all far-out tail trades**: the trade looks wrong for years before it looks right, and most investors lack either the structural ability (lockups, dedicated capital) or psychological ability (riding through 15-20% drawdowns on a thesis bet) to hold.

## Why 2007 was the catalyst, not earlier

Burry's thesis was correct on the underlying loans from 2005 forward — but the catalyst that converted thesis-correctness into mark-to-market profit was specific:

- **Teaser-rate reset waterfall**: 2006-vintage subprime mortgages had 2-year teaser periods, resetting in 2007-2008.
- **Home-price plateau**: National home prices peaked in late 2006 and began declining in early 2007.
- **First subprime ABS index drops**: ABX.HE 06-2 BBB- index fell from 100 to 70 in February 2007, then 70 to 30 by July 2007.
- **First major lender failures**: New Century Financial (Apr 2007), American Home Mortgage (Aug 2007), Countrywide (writedowns Sept 2007).
- **Bear Stearns hedge funds collapse**: July-August 2007. Two Bear Stearns subprime funds wiped out, signaling the broader market that AAA-rated MBS could lose money.

By August 2007, Burry's positions had moved from far-underwater to massively profitable. He began closing positions in tranches over 2007-2008, realizing the bulk of the ~$700M fund profit by mid-2008.

## Why the trade is repeatable in principle

The Burry framework — see [[convex-tail-hedge-arbitrage]] and [[fastest-profitable-trades]] — is replicable but not easy. Three repeatable principles:

### 1. Read the primary documents

Burry's edge was reading individual MBS prospectuses. In 2005 this was tedious and unrewarding; the documents were hundreds of pages each, and one needed to read dozens to confirm a pattern. Almost no one did the work.

The modern analog: read the primary documents in any market segment that's grown faster than human attention can track. Examples:

- **DeFi protocol whitepapers and audit reports** before mass adoption (per [[2026-exploit-target-watchlist]]).
- **CLO/CDO documentation** (the 2010s analog of subprime MBS).
- **SPAC merger prospectuses** during 2020-2021.
- **Crypto token vesting schedules and unlock terms** (per [[token-unlock-arbitrage]]).
- **Stablecoin reserve attestations and mechanisms** (per [[synthetic-stablecoin-depeg-arbitrage]]).

The opportunity is in the gap between "available data nobody reads" and "narrative-driven pricing." Reading wins.

### 2. Structure for asymmetry

Burry didn't short housing in obvious ways. He found the most-asymmetric instrument: tranche-specific CDS where premium was small (~2% / year) but payoff was par-recovery in a default scenario. The premium-to-payoff ratio was on the order of 50:1.

Modern analogs:

- **Crypto put options on stablecoin issuers' tokens** (cheap when implied vol is low; large payoff in depeg scenarios).
- **CDS on emerging-market sovereigns** when 5-year spreads are tight and political stress is rising.
- **Long-dated VIX call options** during periods of vol-suppression.
- **Tranche-specific bond shorts** in 2008-2009 mortgage analogs (modern subprime-auto loans, leveraged loans, BBB-rated CLOs).
- **Synthetic positions on locked / illiquid assets** that give convex exposure to liquidity events.

### 3. Lock up the capital

Burry's contractual lockup was the unsung hero of the trade. Without it, his LPs would have liquidated at the bottom and crystallized losses in 2006. Most multi-year tail trades fail not because the thesis is wrong but because the capital doesn't survive the carry phase.

Modern analogs:

- **Closed-end vehicle structures** for tail-hedge strategies (lock up capital for 5-7 years).
- **Personal-account trading** where the trader controls the capital (no LP pressure).
- **Reserved hedge fund tranches** for tail strategies, with longer lockups than the main fund.
- **Structured products** sized for survivability over the full carry period.

For retail, this means: only put capital you can afford to leave underwater for 2-3 years on this kind of trade. Sizing as 5-10% of total capital, not 50%.

## Why most "Big Short replays" fail

After *The Big Short* book and film popularized the trade, many traders attempted to replicate the framework — short housing, short corporate credit, short crypto, short EM bonds. Most failed because:

1. **They identified macro themes, not specific instruments.** Burry didn't short "housing" — he shorted specific BBB tranches of specific MBS. Macro themes don't have asymmetric instruments; specific securities sometimes do.
2. **They couldn't structure the trade.** The CDS contracts Burry got from Goldman in 2005 don't exist in standardized form for most modern themes. Replicating requires either bespoke bank-counterparty work (high barriers) or accepting less-asymmetric instruments (equity puts, ETF shorts).
3. **They didn't survive the carry.** Most retail and hedge-fund attempts at long-dated tail trades fail because the carry cost > the patience of the capital. 6-month attempts at decade-long structural trades produce only carry losses.
4. **They were wrong on the catalyst.** Burry was right both on the loans AND on the timing of teaser-rate resets. Many post-Burry attempts have been right on the structural thesis but wrong on the catalyst window.

## Lessons for repeatability

1. **Edge-by-attention beats edge-by-IQ.** Most asymmetric trades are unlocked by reading documents others won't bother with. The work is tedious; the rewards are large but only realized after years.
2. **Asymmetric instruments are rare and worth searching for.** Most "shorts" in financial markets have ~1:1 risk-reward at best. Trades with 50:1 or 100:1 asymmetry — like Burry's CDS or Ackman's 2020 hedge — exist only in specific instruments at specific times.
3. **The carry phase tests psychology more than the catalyst phase.** Anyone can hold a position when it's making money. Holding a position when LPs are suing you and the thesis looks broken is the actual difficulty.
4. **Lockups are a feature, not a bug.** Capital that can be redeemed at the worst time will be. Pre-commit your structure to survive the trade.
5. **The catalyst is rarely the news event everyone cites.** People cite "Lehman bankruptcy" as the GFC catalyst; for subprime CDS, the actual catalyst was teaser-rate resets in 2007 — months before Lehman. Catalyst identification is its own discipline.
6. **You will be wrong about timing even if right about thesis.** Burry's thesis was correct from 2003-04. He didn't make money until mid-2007. Position sizing and capital structure must accommodate this gap.

## Modern analogs

Per [[ai-amplified-exploit-arbitrage]], [[2026-exploit-target-watchlist]], and [[convex-tail-hedge-arbitrage]]:

- **DeFi protocol-specific shorts** based on reading whitepapers and audit reports — analogous to Burry reading MBS prospectuses. See [[compound-fork-donation-short]], [[multi-dvn-bridge-config-arbitrage]].
- **Stablecoin mechanism shorts** based on understanding the actual collateral structure (Ethena's perp-funding dependence, USDM's reserve composition).
- **Token-unlock shorts** based on reading vesting schedules.
- **CLO BBB-tranche shorts** as the 2020s analog of subprime BBB.
- **EM sovereign CDS** during quiet periods preceding political crises.

The Burry framework is more accessible to current traders than the original (better data infrastructure, more standardized contracts, more transparent on-chain data) — but the patience requirement is identical.
