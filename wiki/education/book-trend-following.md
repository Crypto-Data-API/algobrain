## Overview

**Trend Following: How to Make a Fortune in Bull, Bear, and Black Swan Markets** by Michael Covel, first published in 2004 and substantially updated in 2017, is the popular history and philosophical case for systematic [[trend-following]]. Covel is not a quant — he's a journalist and documentarian — and the book reads as part case-study collection, part biography of legendary trend traders ([[bill-dunn]], [[ed-seykota]], John W. Henry, [[richard-dennis]]), and part argument that trend-following is the most reliable long-term trading approach available.

Where [[following-the-trend|Clenow's *Following the Trend*]] gives you the actual rules and code, Covel gives you the *case*: 30+ years of audited returns from trend-following CTAs, the rationale for why trend works, the psychology required to stick with it, and the catalog of crisis years (1987, 1998, 2000-02, 2008, 2020) where trend strategies thrived while equity portfolios crashed.

## Key Themes

### The Trend-Following Hall of Fame
Covel profiles, with detailed track records:
- **[[bill-dunn]]** (Dunn Capital) — 25%+ annualized for decades, multiple 50%+ drawdowns survived
- **John W. Henry** — Built JWM Associates from a $40K trading account to billions; later bought the Boston Red Sox
- **[[ed-seykota]]** — Pioneer of computer-driven trend-following; reportedly compounded a $5K account to $15M+
- **[[richard-dennis]] and the [[turtle-traders|Turtles]]** — The famous experiment in teachable systematic trading
- **Larry Hite** (Mint Investment Management) — 30%+ returns with single-digit drawdowns through the 1980s
- **David Harding** (Winton Capital) — Modern flagship trend CTA; later expanded into multi-strategy

### Why Trend Works
- **Markets trend more than random walk theory predicts.** The accumulation of slow-moving institutional flow, central bank policy, and global macro themes creates persistent drift that fast traders are too small to dissipate.
- **Crisis alpha.** Trend systems thrive in periods of sustained directional moves, which often coincide with equity drawdowns. This is structural, not accidental.
- **Discipline > prediction.** Trend traders explicitly do not predict — they react to price. The edge is in the consistency of the reaction, not in the accuracy of any forecast.

### Why Most Investors Can't Do It
- Long flat periods (years of low or negative returns) drive investors away
- Major drawdowns (-25% to -40%) terrify allocators
- Performance comes in lumps (a few big years interspersed with many flat years)
- The average trend system has a win rate around 35-45% — most trades lose
- Cutting losers fast and letting winners run is psychologically hard

## Key Takeaways

- **Trend-following has been profitable across asset classes and decades.** Audited track records from CTAs spanning 1970s onward show consistent (if lumpy) positive returns.
- **The strategy is not market-timing — it's reaction to price.** No forecasting, no fundamentals, no opinion. Buy when price breaks higher; sell when price breaks lower.
- **Diversification across many uncorrelated markets is the engine.** A trend system on a single market is fragile; on 50+ markets, the system is robust.
- **Risk management is the first principle.** Position sizing, stop-losses, and portfolio-level volatility targeting matter more than entry signals.
- **Drawdowns are features, not bugs.** Investors who flee during the drawdowns harvest none of the long-run premium. Sticking through the drawdowns is the cost of admission.
- **Crisis years are when trend earns its keep.** 2008: most trend CTAs +20-40% while equities -38%. 2022: trend up 20%+ while bonds and stocks both negative. This is the diversification benefit allocators are paying for.
- **Trend-following is one of the most-studied yet least-followed strategies.** Despite extensive academic literature confirming its long-run profitability, only a tiny fraction of institutional money is allocated to systematic trend.
- **Mechanical execution beats discretionary judgment over time.** Multiple systematic trend traders have outperformed discretionary peers; the consistent, rules-based execution is itself an edge.

## Who Should Read This

Anyone considering allocating to managed futures or building a systematic strategy. Discretionary traders who want to understand why systematic trend-following works (and why it produces better risk-adjusted returns over time than most discretionary approaches). Allocators evaluating CTAs need both Covel (for the case) and [[following-the-trend|Clenow]] (for the mechanics).

The book is light on technical detail — Covel doesn't give you rules to trade. For implementation, read Clenow's *Following the Trend* or *Trading Evolved* alongside.

## How It Applies to AI Trading

Trend-following is the simplest profitable systematic strategy and a benchmark every ML researcher should compare against. The book's value for systematic traders is in the *psychology and persistence* required to deploy a system live — Covel's case studies of traders who stuck with their systems through brutal drawdowns are the operational counterpart to the math of [[fortune-formula|Fortune's Formula]].

For strategy validation: if your fancy ML model can't outperform a simple breakout-and-trail system on the same data, it's not adding alpha. The trend-following baseline is the floor.
