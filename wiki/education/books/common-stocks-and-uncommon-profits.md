## Overview

**Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits** by Philip Fisher, published in 1958, is the pioneering work on qualitative growth-investing. While Benjamin Graham focused on quantitative metrics and margin-of-safety, Fisher argued that the most important determinants of a stock's long-term performance are qualitative factors that financial statements cannot capture: management quality, competitive advantage, innovation capability, and corporate culture. Fisher's approach shifted the question from "Is this stock cheap?" to "Is this company exceptional?"

Fisher introduced the [[scuttlebutt|"scuttlebutt"]] method — a systematic process of gathering intelligence about a company from customers, suppliers, competitors, former employees, and industry experts before investing. This investigative approach, more akin to journalism than accounting, was revolutionary in an era when most investors relied solely on published financial statements. Fisher's "15 points to look for in a common stock" remain one of the most comprehensive qualitative checklists in investment literature.

Warren Buffett has famously described his investment philosophy as "85% Graham and 15% Fisher," crediting Fisher with teaching him that it is better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price. This synthesis of Graham's quantitative discipline (see [[security-analysis]]) and Fisher's qualitative growth focus produced the most successful investment career in history, demonstrating that both approaches are complementary rather than contradictory.

## Core Thesis

The overwhelming majority of investment profit comes from a small number of genuinely outstanding companies held for very long periods, *not* from buying statistically cheap mediocrities. The job of the investor is therefore to identify rare, well-managed firms with durable competitive advantages and long growth runways — and then do almost nothing. Because quality is qualitative, the financial statements are a starting point, not the answer; the decisive information must be gathered through investigation ([[scuttlebutt]]).

## The 15 Points (Section Themes)

Fisher's central chapter, "What to Buy: The 15 Points to Look For in a Common Stock," is the book's backbone. Paraphrased, they cover:

1. Products/services with enough market runway for years of sales growth.
2. Management's determination to keep developing new products as current lines mature.
3. R&D effectiveness relative to the company's size.
4. An above-average sales/marketing organization.
5. A worthwhile profit margin.
6. Action being taken to maintain or improve margins.
7. Outstanding labor and personnel relations.
8. Outstanding executive relations.
9. Depth of management (not dependence on one person).
10. Good cost analysis and accounting controls.
11. Industry-specific clues (e.g., real estate, patents, insurance reserves) that reveal competitive position.
12. A long-range rather than short-range profit outlook.
13. Whether future growth will require dilutive equity financing.
14. Management that is candid with investors in good times *and* bad.
15. Management of unquestionable integrity.

Other major sections cover: **when to buy** (timing around fundamentals, not the market cycle), **when to sell** ("three reasons, and only three"), the **"don'ts" for investors** (e.g., don't overstress diversification, don't quibble over eighths, don't follow the crowd), and **how to develop an investment philosophy**.

## Key Concepts and Takeaways

| Concept | What it means |
|---------|---------------|
| **[[scuttlebutt]]** | Gather intelligence from the business grapevine — customers, suppliers, rivals, ex-employees — before buying. |
| **The 15 points** | A qualitative checklist for identifying genuinely exceptional companies. |
| **Quality over cheapness** | Management, moat, innovation, and culture drive long-run value more than current P/E or book value. |
| **Long holding periods** | Outstanding companies, once found, should be held for decades through volatility. |
| **The "three reasons to sell"** | Sell only if you made a mistake, the company no longer passes the 15 points, or you find a far superior opportunity — never just because price rose. |
| **Don't overstress diversification** | A few deeply understood, high-conviction holdings beat many superficially known ones. |
| **Management integrity is non-negotiable** | Candor about bad news is a hallmark of trustworthy management; avoid self-dealing or spin. |
| **Reinvestment compounds** | Profits plowed into R&D and growth typically compound faster than dividends paid out. |
| **GARP precursor** | Pay a fair (not bargain) price for an outstanding business with a long runway — a conservative path to superior returns. |

## Criticisms and Limitations

- **Subjective and hard to systematize.** The 15 points and scuttlebutt resist quantification; two skilled analysts can reach opposite conclusions on "management quality."
- **Survivorship and hindsight bias.** Fisher's celebrated wins (Motorola, Texas Instruments) are vivid, but the book offers little systematic accounting of misses; great-company investing can mask expensive mistakes.
- **Valuation discipline is light.** Critics in the value-investing camp note Fisher pays comparatively little attention to entry price and margin-of-safety, risking overpayment for "story" stocks. This is precisely the tension Buffett resolved by blending Fisher with Graham.
- **Access asymmetry.** Effective scuttlebutt is far easier for well-connected professionals than ordinary investors — though modern [[alternative-data|alternative data]] narrows the gap.
- **EMH challenge.** As with all stock-picking, the [[efficient-market-hypothesis]] (see [[a-random-walk-down-wall-street]]) questions whether qualitative edge survives net of costs.

## Who Should Read This

Investors who want to understand qualitative analysis and growth-investing at its source. Anyone who has read [[the-intelligent-investor]] and wants to understand the other half of Warren Buffett's intellectual DNA. Portfolio managers and analysts who want a systematic framework for evaluating management quality and competitive advantage.

## How It Applies to AI Trading

Fisher's scuttlebutt method is a precursor to modern [[alternative-data|alternative data]] analysis. AI systems that analyze customer reviews, employee satisfaction scores (Glassdoor data), supply chain relationships, patent filings, and social media sentiment are performing automated scuttlebutt at scale. Natural language processing applied to earnings call transcripts can assess management candor and confidence — a systematic version of Fisher's emphasis on management integrity. Fisher's 15-point checklist can be partially quantified: R&D spending as a percentage of revenue, employee retention rates, customer concentration metrics, and management tenure are all computable features that capture qualitative factors Fisher evaluated through personal investigation.

## Rating

**8/10** — A foundational text that permanently expanded the definition of investment analysis beyond financial statements. Fisher's qualitative framework complements Graham's quantitative approach perfectly. Some of the specific company examples are dated, but the analytical principles are eternal. Required reading for anyone who wants to understand why Buffett pays up for quality.
