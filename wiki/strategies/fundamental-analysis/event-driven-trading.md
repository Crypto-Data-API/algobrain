# Event-Driven Trading

## Overview

Event-Driven Trading is a fundamental-analysis strategy that profits from predictable price patterns around specific corporate events: **spinoffs**, **bankruptcies and restructurings**, **stock buybacks**, **index additions/removals**, **activist investor campaigns**, and **tender offers**. Each event type creates a temporary mispricing because of forced selling, institutional constraints, or information complexity. The approach was popularized by **Joel Greenblatt** in his book *You Can Be a Stock Market Genius* and is a core strategy at event-driven hedge funds like Elliott Management, Third Point, and Baupost Group.

The key insight is that corporate events create **mechanical supply/demand imbalances** that have nothing to do with intrinsic value. When a company spins off a small subsidiary, index funds and institutions that cannot hold the new entity sell it indiscriminately, pushing the price below fair value. When a stock is added to the S&P 500, index funds must buy it regardless of valuation. These imbalances are temporary and predictable, creating opportunities for prepared investors.

## Rules

### Entry -- By Event Type

**Spinoffs:**
1. When a parent company announces a spinoff, research the new entity's standalone fundamentals. Wait for the separation to complete.
2. Buy the spinoff 1-5 days after it begins trading independently (when forced selling from institutions peaks). Look for spinoffs that are too small for the parent's institutional holders.
3. Target spinoffs where insiders receive significant equity stakes (alignment of interests).

**Index Additions/Removals:**
1. When a stock is announced for addition to a major index (S&P 500, Russell 1000), buy immediately. Index funds must purchase before the effective date.
2. Conversely, buy stocks removed from indices after the forced selling subsides (typically 1-2 weeks post-removal).

**Buyback Announcements:**
1. Enter when a company announces a buyback of 5%+ of shares outstanding, especially if the stock is trading at low valuation multiples.
2. Confirm the company has the balance sheet to execute (low debt, strong free cash flow).

**Activist Campaigns:**
1. Buy when a credible activist investor (Icahn, Elliott, Pershing Square) files a 13D disclosing a 5%+ stake.
2. The stock typically re-rates as the market anticipates operational improvements, board changes, or strategic actions.

### Exit
1. **Spinoffs:** Hold 6-18 months until the market recognizes standalone value. Sell when the stock reaches estimated fair value or analyst coverage initiates with consensus price targets near current prices.
2. **Index events:** Exit within 1-5 days of the effective date. The supply/demand imbalance resolves quickly.
3. **Buybacks:** Hold as long as the company is actively repurchasing. Exit if buyback pace slows or debt levels become concerning.
4. **Activist campaigns:** Exit when the activist achieves their objectives (board seats, asset sale, etc.) or when the activist exits their position.
5. **Universal stop-loss:** 10-15% below entry for all event types.

### Position Sizing
5-10% of portfolio per event. Diversify across event types (do not load up on only spinoffs or only index changes). Hold 8-15 positions across different catalysts.

## Indicators Used
- SEC filings: Form 10-12B (spinoffs), SC 13D (activist stakes), 8-K (buyback announcements)
- Index reconstitution announcements (S&P, Russell, MSCI)
- [[volume]] -- forced selling creates abnormal volume patterns
- Insider transaction filings (Form 4) -- insider buying in spinoffs is a strong signal
- Free cash flow and balance sheet analysis for buyback sustainability
- Historical precedent: average returns for similar past events

## Example Trade
**Event:** S&P 500 addition of a mid-cap tech company
1. S&P announces the stock will be added to the S&P 500 index effective in 5 trading days. The stock is at $85.
2. Buy immediately at $86 (stock already popped $1 on the announcement). Index funds managing $7T+ in S&P 500 assets must buy before the effective date.
3. Over the next 4 days, sustained buying pressure from index funds pushes the stock to $93 (+8.1%).
4. Sell at market close on the day before the effective date at $93. By the effective date, demand normalizes and the stock often drifts sideways or gives back 1-2%.
5. **Result:** +$7 per share (8.1% gain in 4 days). The trade is purely mechanical -- the gain came from forced index-fund buying, not any change in business fundamentals.

## Performance Characteristics
- **Win Rate:** Varies by event type. Spinoffs: 60-70%. Index additions: 70-80%. Activist campaigns: 55-65%. Buybacks: 50-60%.
- **Profit Factor:** 1.5-3.0 depending on event type. Spinoffs and activist campaigns produce the largest winners.
- **Best Market Conditions:** Active corporate finance environment with high M&A, IPO, and restructuring activity. Bull markets encourage spinoffs and buybacks.
- **Worst Market Conditions:** Economic recessions (fewer corporate events), credit freezes (buybacks and M&A slow), or periods of low activist activity.
- **Annual Returns:** Event-driven hedge funds historically return 8-15% with lower volatility than equities.
- **Correlation:** Low to moderate correlation with equity markets since returns are driven by idiosyncratic events.

## Advantages
- Each event has a well-studied historical playbook with documented average returns and timelines
- Returns are driven by structural forces (forced buying/selling) rather than speculative forecasts
- Low correlation to market direction -- profits from corporate actions regardless of bull or bear markets
- Can be combined with value-investing-strategy principles (buy spinoffs at depressed valuations for a double catalyst)
- The complexity barrier deters casual participants, preserving the edge for dedicated researchers

## Disadvantages
- Requires significant legal and financial expertise to analyze SEC filings, deal structures, and regulatory processes
- Event pipelines are lumpy -- some months have 10+ opportunities, others have almost none
- Information disadvantage vs. hedge funds with dedicated legal teams and direct company access
- Not all events play out as historical averages suggest -- individual outcomes can deviate significantly
- Spinoffs and restructurings often involve complex tax and accounting considerations
- Illiquidity in smaller spinoffs and removed index stocks can create wide bid-ask spreads and execution challenges
