# Harmonic Patterns

Harmonic patterns are **geometric price structures** whose every leg conforms to a precise [[fibonacci-trading|Fibonacci]] ratio. Each pattern is an **XABCD** shape — five pivots joined by four swings — that completes at a **Potential Reversal Zone (PRZ)**, a cluster where multiple Fibonacci projections converge. Because the entry (at the PRZ), the stop (just beyond it), and the targets (Fibonacci retracements of the final leg) are all mathematically defined, harmonic trading offers unusually exact trade parameters. The core patterns — **Gartley, Bat, Butterfly, Crab, Shark, Cypher** and the foundational **AB=CD** — differ only in their required ratios. In [[crypto]], clean 24/7 swings on [[bitcoin|BTC]] and [[ethereum|ETH]] and one-click TradingView scanners make harmonics popular, though the market's volatility routinely overshoots the PRZ, favoring the deeper patterns.

## Origins and History

- **H.M. Gartley** described the original five-point structure in ***Profits in the Stock Market*** (1935). The pattern's nickname, the **"Gartley 222,"** refers to the page on which it appeared.
- **Larry Pesavento** overlaid explicit **Fibonacci ratios** onto Gartley's shape in *Fibonacci Ratios with Pattern Recognition* (1997), turning a rough template into a measurable one.
- **Scott Carney** systematized the field in ***Harmonic Trading*** (Vols 1-2), coining the term **"Potential Reversal Zone"** and defining the **Bat, Crab, Shark** and **5-0** patterns. The **Cypher** is commonly attributed to Darren Oglesbee, and **Bryce Gilmore** contributed foundational geometric work.
- The discipline is thus an evolution of classical chart analysis fused with the Fibonacci ratios that also underpin [[elliott-wave]].

## The Fibonacci Ratios Behind the Patterns

Harmonics use a specific subset of [[fibonacci-trading|Fibonacci]] ratios, several derived by taking **roots** of the primary ratios — which is why harmonic levels (0.786, 0.886, 1.13) differ from the everyday retracement set:

- **0.618** = 1/φ, and **0.786** = √0.618 — the Gartley D and Butterfly B.
- **0.886** = √0.786 (= √√0.618) — the Bat and Deep Crab D; a very deep, "last-stand" retracement.
- **1.13** = √1.272 = √√1.618 — the Shark/5-0 ratio, a shallow extension.
- **1.272** = √1.618 and **1.618** = φ — the Butterfly D extensions.
- **1.618 / 2.24 / 2.618 / 3.618** = powers and roots of φ — the Crab's deep CD projections.

The square-root ratios (0.786, 0.886, 1.13) are the harmonic "signature" and are why the patterns complete at levels a plain retracement grid would miss.

## The XABCD Framework

Every harmonic pattern is defined by five points and four legs:

- **X** — the origin.
- **XA** — the initial impulse leg.
- **AB** — the first retracement of XA.
- **BC** — a counter-move retracing AB.
- **CD** — the final leg that terminates at **D**, the PRZ where the trade is taken.

What distinguishes each pattern is the **specific Fibonacci ratio at each leg**, above all the location of **D relative to XA**. The PRZ is where the D-point projections from different legs *cluster*; that convergence of support/resistance is the pattern's claimed edge.

## The Pattern Catalog (Ratio Requirements)

Ratios below are the widely used tolerances; small variations exist between authors.

- **Gartley** — the classic retracement pattern. **B = 0.618 of XA**; **BC = 0.382-0.886 of AB**; **CD = 1.13-1.618 of BC**; **D = 0.786 of XA**. D stays *inside* the XA range. The most common and reliable.
- **Bat** — a deeper retracement. **B = 0.382-0.500 of XA**; **BC = 0.382-0.886**; **CD = 1.618-2.618 of BC**; **D = 0.886 of XA**. Tight, high-quality PRZ near the origin.
- **Butterfly** — an *extension* pattern that tops/bottoms beyond X. **B = 0.786 of XA**; **BC = 0.382-0.886**; **CD = 1.618-2.24 of BC**; **D = 1.272-1.618 of XA** (past the X point). Forms at market extremes.
- **Crab** — the most extreme extension, with the tightest, best reward-to-risk PRZ. **B = 0.382-0.618 of XA**; **CD = 2.24-3.618 of BC**; **D = 1.618 of XA**. The **Deep Crab** uses **B = 0.886**.
- **Shark** — labeled 0-X-A-B-C; relies on the **0.886 and 1.13** ratios with a **1.618-2.24** extension; often a precursor to the **5-0**.
- **5-0** — a reciprocal AB=CD variant where **D = 0.5 of BC**; identifies the first pullback after a strong reversal.
- **Cypher** — **B = 0.382-0.618 of XA**; **C = 1.272-1.414 of XA**; **D = 0.786 of XC**. Note C is measured against XA, unusual among the patterns.
- **AB=CD** — the foundational building block underlying the others: **CD equals AB** in price and roughly in time, with C typically a 0.618-0.786 retrace of AB. Variants (1.27 or 1.618 AB=CD) extend the CD leg.
- **Three Drives** — three symmetric pushes to successive Fibonacci extensions (1.272/1.618), each separated by 0.618-0.786 retracements; a cousin of the harmonic family and of [[elliott-wave]] ending structures.

## Ratio Cheat Sheet

The defining measurement of each pattern is **D relative to XA**; the other legs must fall within the ranges below.

| Pattern | B (of XA) | BC (of AB) | CD (of BC) | D (final point) | Character |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Gartley** | 0.618 | 0.382-0.886 | 1.13-1.618 | **0.786 of XA** (inside) | continuation, shallow |
| **Bat** | 0.382-0.500 | 0.382-0.886 | 1.618-2.618 | **0.886 of XA** (inside) | best R:R, deep |
| **Butterfly** | 0.786 | 0.382-0.886 | 1.618-2.24 | **1.272-1.618 of XA** (beyond X) | reversal, extension |
| **Crab** | 0.382-0.618 | 0.382-0.886 | 2.24-3.618 | **1.618 of XA** (beyond X) | extreme, tightest PRZ |
| **Deep Crab** | 0.886 | 0.382-0.886 | 2.24-3.618 | **1.618 of XA** (beyond X) | deep + extreme |
| **Cypher** | 0.382-0.618 | C = 1.272-1.414 of XA | — | **0.786 of XC** | fast markets |
| **Shark** | — | C = 1.13-1.618 | — | **0.886 / 1.13** (5-0 precursor) | reaction, two-stage |
| **5-0** | — | — | — | **0.5 of BC** | first pullback after reversal |
| **AB=CD** | — | C = 0.618-0.786 of AB | CD = AB (or 1.27/1.618) | D completes AB=CD | building block |

**Reliability ranking (practitioner consensus, not statistical proof):** Gartley and Bat are considered the most dependable (shallow, high-participation zones); Butterfly and Crab offer superior reward-to-risk but lower hit rates because they bet on exhaustion at extremes; Cypher and Shark are powerful but easiest to mis-label. In crypto's overshoot-prone tape, the **deeper patterns (Bat, Deep Crab, Crab) tend to survive liquidation wicks better** than a plain Gartley.

## Rules and Signals

### Entry
1. **Spot the developing XABCD** as price unfolds; most crypto traders use TradingView's built-in harmonic tools or auto-scanners to flag candidates.
2. **Validate every leg** against the required ratios for that specific pattern. If any leg falls outside tolerance, the pattern is **invalid** — do not force it.
3. **Enter at the PRZ (D)** only after a **confirmation** — a reversal candle (pin bar, engulfing, hammer/shooting star) or a lower-timeframe structure break at the zone.
4. **Do not front-run the PRZ.** Wait for price to *reach* the zone and show rejection; premature entries are the main way harmonic accounts bleed.

### Stops
- Place the stop just **beyond D** — beyond the X point for Gartley/Bat, or beyond the extension level for Butterfly/Crab. The precise PRZ means stops are tight; if price closes decisively through the PRZ, the pattern has failed — exit at once.

### Targets
- **T1** = 0.382 retracement of the CD leg (conservative). **T2** = 0.618 of CD (standard). **T3** = the C point, then the A point (aggressive). Scale out, moving the stop to breakeven after T1.

## Why Harmonic Patterns May Work

The claimed edge rests on the same foundations as [[fibonacci-trading]], concentrated into a single zone:

- **Fibonacci confluence.** The PRZ is where several independent Fibonacci projections (D-of-XA, CD-of-BC, an internal AB=CD) land within a narrow band. If any single Fibonacci level attracts clustered orders, a *stack* of them attracts more — a denser pocket of resting liquidity.
- **Self-fulfilling recognition.** Automated scanners flag the same patterns for thousands of traders, who place orders at the same D — coordinated behavior that can make the reversal partly self-caused.
- **Liquidity and stop hunts.** In crypto, the extension patterns (Butterfly/Crab) complete at new extremes *precisely where stops sit*; a [[liquidation|liquidation]] sweep fills large resting orders and then reverses — the microstructure reason a "failed" breakout snaps back through the PRZ.
- **The skeptic's null.** Critics counter that with wide ratio tolerances and multiple patterns, *some* XABCD can be drawn on almost any swing after the fact; the honest position is that harmonics have a plausible reflexive/liquidity rationale but thin independent statistical proof, so they earn their keep only with confirmation and confluence.

## Applying Harmonics to Crypto Charts

- **24/7 tape produces clean swings.** Continuous trading on [[bitcoin]] and [[ethereum]] draws well-formed XABCD legs, and scanners run around the clock.
- **Volatility favors deep patterns.** Crypto frequently overshoots shallow PRZs, so the **Bat, Deep Crab and Crab** (which complete at 0.886 / 1.618 of XA) tend to hold better than a plain Gartley, because the extra depth absorbs a liquidation flush.
- **Liquidation sweeps often *are* the D point.** A long/short [[liquidation|liquidation]] wick that tags the PRZ and reverses is a textbook confirmation; pair it with a [[funding-rate]] that has run to an extreme and is resetting.
- **Confluence lifts the odds.** A PRZ that coincides with a [[fibonacci-trading|golden pocket]] (0.618-0.65), a [[supply-demand-zones|supply/demand zone]], a [[smart-money-concepts|order block]], or a horizontal [[support-and-resistance]] level is far more trustworthy than a naked pattern.
- **Majors over alts.** BTC/ETH give reliable geometry; thin [[altcoins]] produce noisy, gappy swings where "patterns" are mostly curve-fitting.
- **Timeframe matters.** 4-hour, daily and weekly harmonics on majors attract more participants and hold better than sub-hourly patterns.

## Confluence With Other Tools

- **[[fibonacci-trading]]** — the mathematical foundation; the PRZ is itself a Fibonacci cluster.
- **[[elliott-wave]]** — harmonic patterns often complete at wave turning points (a Butterfly/Crab at a Wave 5 or Wave C extreme).
- **[[supply-demand-zones]]** and **[[smart-money-concepts]]** — resting institutional orders can explain *why* a PRZ reacts; alignment strengthens the setup.
- **[[support-and-resistance]]** — a horizontal level on the PRZ adds a non-Fibonacci confirmation.
- **[[price-action]] / [[rsi]] [[divergence]]** — a reversal candle and momentum divergence at D are the standard triggers.

## Strengths

- Delivers **precise, pre-defined** entry, stop and target levels — little ambiguity in trade planning.
- The **PRZ** concept concentrates several Fibonacci projections into one high-probability zone.
- Tight stops at a mathematically exact invalidation point produce **excellent reward-to-risk** when the pattern works.
- Automated recognition (TradingView, harmonic scanners) removes most manual scanning, useful across crypto's 24/7 sessions.
- Rule-based validation makes harmonics *more* falsifiable than many discretionary methods — a leg either meets its ratio or it does not.

## Criticisms and Limitations

- **Steep learning curve.** Memorizing each pattern's ratios and validating legs in real time takes considerable study.
- **X-point subjectivity.** Different chosen origins yield different patterns; the "objective" ratios sit atop a subjective anchor.
- **Failure in strong trends.** In a powerful crypto trend, patterns complete at the PRZ and immediately fail as momentum overrides the expected reversal — always trade *against* the pattern only with confirmation.
- **Tolerance bands invite fitting.** Ratio ranges (e.g., BC = 0.382-0.886) are wide enough that many structures can be labeled "close enough," enabling **hindsight fitting** and **over-optimization**.
- **Thin independent validation.** Rigorous, out-of-sample statistical evidence for a standalone harmonic edge is limited; most published results are illustrative, and edge (if present) leans on confirmation and confluence.
- **Constant monitoring.** Patterns complete without warning at the PRZ, demanding attention or alerting infrastructure.

## Worked Crypto Example

**Asset:** [[bitcoin|BTC]]/USD, 4-hour, bearish Gartley.

1. **XA:** BTC rises from **$60,000 (X)** to **$68,000 (A)** — an $8,000 impulse.
2. **AB:** price retraces to **$63,056 (B)** — a **0.618** retracement of XA. Valid.
3. **BC:** price rallies to **$66,000 (C)** — a ~59% retrace of AB (within 0.382-0.886). Valid.
4. **CD (developing):** compute the PRZ at **D = 0.786 of XA** = $68,000 − ($8,000 × 0.786) = **~$61,712**, checking that CD also falls in the 1.13-1.618-of-BC band. Confluence check: the PRZ sits just under the [[fibonacci-trading|golden pocket]] of the AB leg and on a prior [[support-and-resistance]] shelf.
5. Price reaches **$61,750** and prints a 4-hour bullish engulfing candle; a short-side [[liquidation]] wick tags the zone as [[funding-rate|funding]] flips from negative back toward neutral. **Enter long at $61,800**, stop at **$59,900** (below X). Risk ≈ $1,900.
6. **Targets** as retracements of the CD leg: **T1** 0.382 = ~$64,050 (+$2,250), **T2** 0.618 = ~$65,450 (+$3,650), **T3** the C point = ~$66,000 (+$4,200).
7. **Manage:** bank 50% at T1 (≈1.2:1), 30% at T2 (≈1.9:1), and let 20% run to T3 (≈2.2:1), trailing the stop to breakeven after T1.

**Outcome:** the trade worked because the D point was not just a Fibonacci number but a *cluster* — 0.786 of XA, a golden-pocket edge, prior support, and a liquidation-driven confirmation candle all at one price.

## Bullish and Bearish Construction

Every pattern has a mirror-image form:

- **Bullish (buy at D):** the structure completes at a swing **low**. XA is a down-leg, and D sits below C, forming a **W-shaped** or **M-then-drop** base where the trade is a long. Used to fade a downtrend into support.
- **Bearish (sell at D):** the structure completes at a swing **high**. XA is an up-leg, and D sits above C, forming an **M-shaped** top where the trade is a short. Used to fade an uptrend into resistance.

The ratio requirements are identical for both; only the direction of the legs flips. On a chart, a bullish Gartley and a bearish Gartley are vertical reflections of each other.

## Practical Notes by Pattern

- **Gartley** — the workhorse; best in a trend as a *continuation* pattern (the D-point retrace holds and the prior trend resumes). Relatively shallow, so it fits ordinary pullbacks on [[bitcoin]]/[[ethereum]] dailies.
- **Bat** — the highest reward-to-risk of the retracement group because the 0.886 D sits very close to X, allowing a tight stop just beyond the origin. A crypto favorite for fading deep flushes.
- **Butterfly / Crab** — *reversal* patterns that complete **beyond X**, i.e., at new local extremes. They shine at the *end* of a move, catching capitulation lows and blow-off highs — exactly where crypto liquidation cascades overshoot. The Crab's far D gives the tightest PRZ and the best R:R but the lowest hit rate.
- **Cypher** — needs C beyond A (1.272-1.414 of XA) and a 0.786 D of XC; strong in fast markets but easy to mis-label, so validate the unusual C measurement carefully.
- **Shark / 5-0** — best treated as a two-stage play: the Shark identifies the initial reaction, and the subsequent 5-0 offers a cleaner entry on the first pullback after the reversal.
- **AB=CD** — always check for it *inside* the larger pattern; a valid AB=CD landing in the PRZ is strong extra confirmation.

## Trade Management at the PRZ

Because the PRZ is a *zone* rather than a single price, execution matters as much as identification:

- **Scale in across the zone.** Split the intended size into two or three limit orders spanning the PRZ (e.g., at the D-of-XA level, and slightly deeper toward the extension band) so a normal crypto overshoot does not miss the fill or blow the stop.
- **Stop beyond the zone, not the first level.** Place the stop past the *far* edge of the PRZ (beyond X for retracement patterns; beyond the extension for Butterfly/Crab), sized so a liquidation wick that tags but does not close beyond it survives.
- **Confirmation before adding.** Take the first tranche on the reversal candle; add only after a lower-timeframe structure break confirms the turn.
- **Move to breakeven fast.** After Target 1 (0.382 of CD), shift the stop to entry — harmonic reversals that stall often round-trip, and giving back a winner is the pattern's most common way to disappoint.
- **Honor the invalidation.** A decisive close *beyond* the PRZ means the pattern failed; exit immediately rather than "giving it room."

## Harmonics vs Other Reversal Methods

- **vs [[support-and-resistance]]:** horizontal S/R marks *where* buyers/sellers appeared before; a harmonic adds a *forward-projected, ratio-defined* target with a built-in invalidation. They are strongest **together** — a PRZ on a prior S/R shelf.
- **vs [[supply-demand-zones]] / [[smart-money-concepts]]:** those frameworks explain the PRZ's reaction through resting institutional orders; harmonics supply the geometry, order-flow supplies the *why*.
- **vs [[elliott-wave]]:** Elliott gives the structural context (which wave), harmonics give the precise turn price; a Crab completing at a suspected Wave 5 extreme is a classic combined setup.
- **vs pure [[price-action]]:** price action confirms the turn candle-by-candle but offers no target; harmonics offer targets but need price-action confirmation to trigger.

## Common Mistakes in Crypto

- **Forcing the X point.** The single biggest error; if the origin is ambiguous, the pattern is not tradable — wait for a cleaner one.
- **Entering before confirmation.** Crypto overshoots PRZs on [[liquidation|liquidation]] wicks; a mechanical limit at D gets run over. Wait for a rejection candle or lower-timeframe structure break.
- **Trading harmonics against a strong trend without a stop discipline.** Reversal patterns fail repeatedly in parabolic advances; keep the beyond-D stop non-negotiable.
- **Ignoring the higher-timeframe context.** A bearish pattern completing at a daily [[support-and-resistance|support]] shelf, or into a bullish regime, is fighting the tape — demand confluence.
- **Over-tight tolerance shopping.** Widening the ratio bands to make a shape "fit" is curve-fitting; either the legs meet the standard ranges or the pattern is invalid.
