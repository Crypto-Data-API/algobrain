# Elliott Wave Theory

Elliott Wave Theory (EWT) is a form of [[technical-analysis]] which holds that market prices unfold in repetitive, self-similar patterns driven by the swings of collective investor psychology between optimism and pessimism. Its central claim is that trends advance in **five waves** in the direction of the larger trend (the *motive* phase) and then correct in **three waves** against it (the *corrective* phase), producing a complete **5-3 cycle**. The structure is **fractal**: every wave is itself built from smaller waves of the same form, so the same 5-3 signature repeats from decade-long "Grand Supercycles" down to one-minute charts. Because crypto trades continuously and is dominated by reflexive retail sentiment, [[crypto]] is one of the more actively wave-counted markets today, though EWT remains notorious for its subjectivity.

## Origins and History

- **Ralph Nelson Elliott** (1871-1948), an American accountant and management consultant, formulated the theory during a forced retirement recovering from illness in the 1930s. Studying 75 years of stock index data across multiple timeframes, he concluded that apparent market chaos was in fact structured.
- Elliott published ***The Wave Principle*** (1938), a series of articles in the *Financial World* magazine (1939), and his final synthesis ***Nature's Law: The Secret of the Universe*** (1946), in which he tied wave counts to the [[fibonacci-trading|Fibonacci sequence]] and the golden ratio.
- The theory lay dormant until **A.J. Frost** and **[[robert-prechter|Robert Prechter]]** revived it with ***Elliott Wave Principle: Key to Market Behavior*** (1978), still the canonical text. Prechter's *Elliott Wave Theorist* newsletter famously anticipated the 1982-1987 bull market, cementing EWT's popularity (Source: [[2026-04-20-comprehensive-guide-technical-trading-indicators]]).
- Prechter later developed **socionomics**, the hypothesis that social mood (measured by wave patterns) drives events, inverting the usual "news drives markets" assumption.
- Later refinements include **Glenn Neely's NEoWave** (a stricter, more rule-based counting method) and adaptations for 24/7 markets used by crypto analysts.

## Core Structure

A complete cycle is **eight waves**: a five-wave motive phase (labeled **1-2-3-4-5**) followed by a three-wave corrective phase (labeled **A-B-C**).

- In an uptrend, waves **1, 3, 5** advance (motive/impulse) and waves **2, 4** retrace (corrective).
- The **A-B-C** correction then unwinds part of the entire 1-5 advance.
- Each **motive** subwave (1, 3, 5) subdivides into **five** smaller waves; each **corrective** subwave (2, 4) subdivides into **three**. So a single impulse decomposes as **5-3-5-3-5**.
- This nesting is the fractal core: a wave 3 on the daily chart contains a full 1-2-3-4-5 on the 4-hour chart, which contains 1-2-3-4-5 on the 15-minute chart, and so on.

### The Degrees of Trend

Elliott named nine **degrees** so analysts can label which timeframe a wave belongs to. Largest to smallest:

1. Grand Supercycle (multi-decade / century)
2. Supercycle (decades)
3. Cycle (years)
4. Primary (months to years)
5. Intermediate (weeks to months)
6. Minor (weeks)
7. Minute (days)
8. Minuette (hours)
9. Subminuette (minutes)

Labels are conventionally distinguished by typography (Roman numerals, letters, circled numbers). In crypto, many analysts count Bitcoin's entire post-2009 history as a single **Supercycle** or **Grand Supercycle** impulse, with the 2013, 2017 and 2021 tops marking waves of Cycle degree.

## The Three Inviolable Rules

These three rules are absolute. If a count breaks one, the count is wrong and must be relabeled.

1. **Wave 2 never retraces more than 100% of Wave 1.** Price cannot fall below the start of Wave 1.
2. **Wave 3 is never the shortest** of the three motive waves (1, 3, 5). It need not be the longest, but it cannot be the shortest.
3. **Wave 4 never overlaps the price territory of Wave 1** (its price range does not enter Wave 1's range) — *except* inside diagonals (see below), which are the sanctioned exception. Highly leveraged, fast markets like crypto throw more diagonals and truncations, so overlap appears more often here than in equities.

## Guidelines (Strong Tendencies, Not Laws)

Guidelines refine a count between competing valid options:

- **Alternation:** if Wave 2 is a sharp, deep correction (a zigzag), Wave 4 is likely a shallow, sideways one (a flat or triangle), and vice versa. Corrections rarely take the same form twice in a row.
- **Extension:** one of the three motive waves is usually *extended* (elongated, subdividing conspicuously). In crypto bull runs, **Wave 3 is the most common extension**, producing the parabolic advance. When Wave 3 extends, Waves 1 and 5 tend toward equality.
- **Equality:** two of the three motive waves tend to be equal in length or time, most often Waves 1 and 5.
- **Depth of Wave 4:** Wave 4 frequently bottoms near the territory of the **fourth wave of one lesser degree** — a useful target zone.
- **Channeling:** draw a trendline through the ends of Waves 2 and 4; a parallel from the end of Wave 3 projects where Wave 5 may terminate.
- **Truncation:** Wave 5 sometimes fails to exceed the end of Wave 3 (a "truncated fifth" / "failure"), signaling underlying weakness — common after a blow-off Wave 3.

## Wave Personality (Mass Psychology)

Elliott's deeper claim is that each wave carries a characteristic sentiment. This "wave personality" is how experienced counters sanity-check a label:

- **Wave 1** — tentative; few believe the low is in; often mistaken for a dead-cat bounce.
- **Wave 2** — fear of a retest; a deep, frightening pullback (often 50-78.6%); pessimism returns, yet the prior low holds.
- **Wave 3** — the strongest and usually longest; the crowd capitulates *into* the trend; breakouts, gaps, and (in crypto) parabolic funding; the news finally turns bullish.
- **Wave 4** — complacency and profit-taking; sideways, frustrating consolidation; alternates in form with Wave 2.
- **Wave 5** — euphoria; retail piles in; typically shows **momentum divergence** (e.g., lower [[rsi]] than at the Wave 3 peak) even as price makes new highs.
- **Wave A** — the first decline; still viewed as a healthy pullback by most.
- **Wave B** — the bull trap; a partial rally that convinces holders the top was not real; often on thin volume.
- **Wave C** — decisive capitulation; five waves down; the sentiment mirror image of Wave 3.

## Corrective Patterns (The Full Taxonomy)

Corrections are where EWT gets hard, because they take many forms. All corrections are one of the following or a combination of them.

- **Zigzag (5-3-5):** a sharp correction. Wave A subdivides into five, B into three, C into five. C often equals A or extends to 1.618x A. Double and triple zigzags exist (joined by X waves).
- **Flat (3-3-5):** a sideways correction. A and B each subdivide into three; C into five. Variants:
  - *Regular flat* — B ends near the start of A; C ends near the end of A.
  - *Expanded flat* — B exceeds the start of A and C exceeds the end of A (very common in crypto, producing false breakouts in both directions).
  - *Running flat* — B exceeds the start of A but C fails to reach the end of A (strong underlying trend).
- **Triangle (3-3-3-3-3):** five overlapping legs labeled A-B-C-D-E within converging or diverging trendlines. Subtypes: *contracting* (symmetrical, ascending, descending) and *expanding*. Triangles appear almost exclusively in **Wave 4** or **Wave B** positions and precede the final thrust.
- **Combinations (double three / triple three):** two or three simple corrections strung together and connected by intervening **X waves**, labeled **W-X-Y** or **W-X-Y-X-Z**. These produce the long, messy consolidations common in crypto bear markets.

### Corrective Pattern Quick Reference

| Pattern | Subdivision | Usual position | Signature |
|---|---|---|---|
| Zigzag | 5-3-5 | Wave 2, Wave A/C | sharp, deep, directional |
| Regular flat | 3-3-5 | Wave 4, Wave B | sideways; B ≈ start of A |
| Expanded flat | 3-3-5 | Wave 4, Wave B | B overshoots start of A; C overshoots end of A (double fake-out) |
| Running flat | 3-3-5 | Wave 4, Wave B | B overshoots but C falls short (strong trend) |
| Triangle | 3-3-3-3-3 | Wave 4, Wave B | five overlapping legs A-B-C-D-E; precedes final thrust |
| Double/triple three | W-X-Y (-X-Z) | complex corrections | simple patterns joined by X waves; long, messy |

Corrections are the hardest part of counting precisely because so many valid forms exist — which is also the crux of the "unfalsifiability" criticism below. The **alternation** guideline (a sharp Wave 2 implies a sideways Wave 4, and vice versa) is the single most useful tool for narrowing the possibilities.

## Motive Variants: Diagonals

Besides the standard impulse, the other motive form is the **diagonal** (a wedge):

- **Leading diagonal** — appears in the Wave 1 or Wave A position; subwaves are 5-3-5-3-5 or 3-3-3-3-3.
- **Ending diagonal** — appears in the Wave 5 or Wave C position; subwaves are 3-3-3-3-3; signals exhaustion and usually precedes a sharp reversal.
- In diagonals, **Wave 4 overlaps Wave 1** — the only place the overlap rule is waived. Crypto's leverage-driven exhaustion tops frequently print ending diagonals.

## The Fibonacci Mathematics

EWT is inseparable from [[fibonacci-trading]] ratios, which quantify wave relationships:

| Relationship | Typical ratio |
|---|---|
| Wave 2 retrace of Wave 1 | 50%, 61.8%, or 78.6% |
| Wave 3 vs Wave 1 | 1.618x, 2.618x, or 4.236x (when extended) |
| Wave 4 retrace of Wave 3 | 23.6% or 38.2% |
| Wave 5 vs Wave 1 | 0.618x, 1.0x, or 1.618x |
| Wave 5 vs Waves 1-3 net | 0.618x |
| Zigzag Wave C vs Wave A | 1.0x or 1.618x |
| Flat Wave B vs Wave A | 0.9x-1.0x (more in expanded flats) |

These ratios turn the theory from pure description into a source of concrete price targets: project a Wave 3 target as 1.618x Wave 1 from the end of Wave 2, or a Wave 5 target as Wave-1 length added to the end of Wave 4.

## Applying Elliott Wave to Crypto Charts

- **24/7, gapless tape.** Crypto never closes, so wave counts are not interrupted by weekend gaps. This removes one distortion equities suffer, but it also means overnight liquidation cascades can complete an entire subwave in minutes.
- **Extended third waves are dramatic.** Crypto's reflexivity and leverage produce textbook parabolic Wave 3s — the 2017 and 2020-2021 [[bitcoin]] advances are frequently labeled as extended thirds of Primary or Cycle degree.
- **Higher timeframes are more reliable.** [[bitcoin]] and [[ethereum]] on the weekly/daily give cleaner counts. Low-cap [[altcoins]] have short, noisy histories that rarely produce trustworthy counts; force-fitting them is a classic error.
- **Halving cycles as a wave clock.** Many analysts align Bitcoin's ~4-year halving rhythm with Cycle-degree waves, treating each cycle top as a wave peak and each bear market as the ensuing A-B-C.
- **Funding and liquidation context.** Wave 3 and Wave 5 euphoria coincide with extreme positive [[funding-rate|funding]] and stacked longs; the sharp Wave A and Wave C legs are where long [[liquidation|liquidations]] cascade. A [[funding-rate]] spike into a suspected Wave 5 amid [[rsi]] divergence is a high-confidence exhaustion tell. Use [[open-interest]] alongside: rising OI into Wave 3 confirms conviction; OI unwinding through a correction confirms the A-B-C.
- **Log scale.** Because BTC has risen by orders of magnitude, count Cycle- and Supercycle-degree waves on a **logarithmic** chart so proportions and Fibonacci projections stay meaningful.

## Elliott Wave in Bitcoin's Price History

[[bitcoin|BTC]] is a favorite EWT subject because its ~15-year history plausibly forms a single large-degree impulse, and its halving rhythm gives the count an external clock:

- Many analysts label **2011, 2013, 2017 and 2021 cycle tops** as successive impulse waves (or wave peaks) of **Cycle or Supercycle degree**, with the intervening ~80% bear markets as the corrective A-B-C legs.
- Under this framing, each bull market is a smaller five-wave impulse nested inside the larger structure, and each cycle low (2015, 2018/19, 2022) is a Wave 2/Wave 4 or A-B-C completion.
- The **ongoing debate** is where BTC currently sits — whether the post-2009 advance is an early- or late-stage Supercycle impulse, and whether a given rally is a Wave 3 (more upside) or a Wave 5 (exhaustion). This is unresolved and count-dependent, which is precisely the theory's strength (a framework for the debate) and weakness (no unique answer).
- **[[altcoins]]** rarely support Supercycle counts — most lack the history — but individual bull runs on liquid alts can show clean five-wave impulses on the daily.

This history is also a caution: the same data supports multiple credible counts, so treat any single "BTC is in Wave X" claim as one hypothesis with an explicit invalidation, not a forecast.

## Confluence With Other Tools

- **[[fibonacci-trading]]** — the mathematical backbone; wave targets are Fibonacci projections and retracements.
- **[[wyckoff-method]]** — Wyckoff accumulation/distribution schematics often coincide with Wave 2/Wave 4 bases and Wave 5 tops, giving structural corroboration.
- **[[harmonic-patterns]]** — harmonic XABCD structures frequently complete at wave turning points, sharing the Fibonacci grammar.
- **[[rsi]] / [[macd]] divergence** — the single most useful confirmation: momentum divergence into a suspected Wave 5 supports a top; a lack of divergence argues the impulse is still extending.
- **[[volume]]** — should be heaviest in Wave 3 and lighter in Wave 5; a volume climax in Wave 5 warns of a truncation/blow-off.
- **[[support-and-resistance]]** and **[[market-cycles]]** — horizontal levels and cyclic timing that reinforce a count.

## Strengths

- A single framework that describes market structure at *any* timeframe and in any liquid market.
- The fractal model connects short-term trades to the long-term context, helping locate where a trend sits in its lifecycle (early Wave 1, powerful Wave 3, exhausted Wave 5).
- Deep integration with Fibonacci yields objective, falsifiable price targets and invalidation levels.
- When a count is correct, **Wave 3 entries** (buying the end of Wave 2) offer among the best risk-reward setups in discretionary trading.
- A large literature and active analyst community means abundant shared reference points — which, as with any popular tool, can become partly self-fulfilling.

## Criticisms and Limitations

- **Subjectivity.** The oldest joke in the field: give the same chart to ten Elliotticians and get ten counts. The rules constrain but do not uniquely determine a labeling.
- **Alternate counts and unfalsifiability.** The corrective taxonomy is flexible enough (flats, combinations, X waves, extensions, truncations) that almost any price path can be fitted *after the fact*. Critics argue this makes real-time prediction little better than chance.
- **Hindsight bias.** Retrospective counts look inevitable; real-time counting is far harder and constantly revised.
- **Mixed forecasting record.** Even Prechter, EWT's foremost popularizer, issued prominent deflation and top calls in the 1990s-2000s that markets did not honor — a reminder that a compelling structure is not a guarantee.
- **Not cleanly backtestable.** Because labeling is discretionary, systematic backtests are hard to construct, so claims of edge rest largely on anecdote.
- **Conviction trap.** Belief in "my wave" can keep a trader in a losing position waiting for a count to play out. Always trade with the invalidation level (a broken rule) as a hard stop.

## Worked Crypto Example

**Asset:** [[bitcoin|BTC]]/USD, weekly, log scale, bull phase.

1. **Wave 1:** BTC advances from a cycle low of $16,000 to $31,000 (+$15,000) on expanding [[volume]]; most of the market still calls it a bear-market bounce.
2. **Wave 2:** a frightening pullback to $22,700 — a 55% retracement of Wave 1, inside the 50-61.8% Fibonacci zone. Sentiment turns bearish; the prior low at $16,000 holds (Rule 1 intact). A bullish reversal candle prints.
3. **Entry:** go long at ~$23,000. Place the stop at $15,800, below the Wave 1 origin — a break there voids the count.
4. **Wave 3 target:** 1.618x Wave 1 projected from the Wave 2 low: $22,700 + ($15,000 x 1.618) = **~$46,970**. Confirm with rising [[open-interest]] and positive-but-not-extreme [[funding-rate|funding]].
5. **Wave 3 delivers:** BTC runs to ~$48,000 in a powerful, gap-and-go advance as the news turns bullish. Bank 60-70% of the position near the target.
6. **Wave 4:** a sideways/flat correction to ~$38,500 (a 38.2% retrace of Wave 3). Because Wave 2 was sharp, alternation argues Wave 4 will be shallow and sideways — which it is.
7. **Wave 5:** re-enter for the final push toward Wave-1 equality: $38,500 + $15,000 = **~$53,500**. Watch for [[rsi]] divergence and a [[funding-rate]] blow-off; exit into euphoria rather than waiting for confirmation.
8. **Correction:** as Wave 5 tops with divergence and stacked longs, prepare for the A-B-C down. The initial Wave A drop is where over-leveraged longs [[liquidation|liquidate]].

**Outcome:** the Wave 3 leg alone yielded roughly +$25,000/BTC on a $23,000 entry (~3.5:1 reward-to-risk), with the count's inviolable-rule stop keeping risk defined throughout.

## A Practical Counting Workflow

1. **Start high, work down.** Fix the largest visible degree first (weekly BTC), then subdivide into lower degrees. Counting bottom-up invites getting lost in noise.
2. **Find a clean five.** Locate an unambiguous five-wave move; it defines the trend direction and anchors everything else. If you cannot see a clean five, do not force a count.
3. **Check the three rules** on every candidate label. A rule break means relabel immediately — no exceptions outside diagonals.
4. **Apply the guidelines** to choose among valid options: alternation between Waves 2 and 4, which wave is extended, and Fibonacci proportionality.
5. **Draw the channel** through Waves 2 and 4 to project Wave 5; a Wave 5 that overshoots or undershoots the channel warns of a blow-off or truncation.
6. **Keep an alternate count.** Always maintain a primary and a backup labeling with explicit invalidation levels. When the primary's rule breaks, the alternate is often already correct — this is the discipline that separates usable EWT from wishful thinking.
7. **Trade only high-confidence junctures.** The end of Wave 2 (to catch Wave 3) is the highest-reward, most-identifiable entry; be far more cautious labeling Wave 5 tops in real time.

## Common Counting Mistakes

- **Skipping degrees / mixing timeframes** — labeling a minute-degree wiggle as a Cycle-degree wave. Keep degrees consistent.
- **Forcing a count on illiquid alts** — short, gappy histories rarely produce valid impulses; reserve EWT for [[bitcoin|BTC]]/[[ethereum|ETH]] and higher timeframes.
- **Ignoring alternation** — expecting Wave 4 to look like Wave 2; they almost always differ in form.
- **Mislabeling a third as a fifth** — Wave 3 (strong, with confirming news and rising [[open-interest]]) is often confused with Wave 5 (euphoric, with [[divergence]]); the momentum and funding signature distinguishes them.
- **Marrying a count** — refusing to relabel after a rule break. The three rules are the theory's only hard guardrails; honor them.

## Relationship to Other Methods

Elliott Wave is best treated as a *structural overlay* rather than a standalone system. It answers **where in the cycle** price sits; [[fibonacci-trading]] answers **at what price** a wave should turn; [[wyckoff-method]] describes the **accumulation/distribution mechanics** inside Wave 2/4 bases and Wave 5 tops; and momentum tools ([[rsi]], [[macd]]) confirm or deny a suspected fifth. Practitioners who combine a wave count with Fibonacci targets, a Wyckoff read, and momentum [[divergence]] extract far more from EWT than those who rely on wave labels alone.
