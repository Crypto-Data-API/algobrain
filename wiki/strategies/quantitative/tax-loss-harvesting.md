# Tax-Loss Harvesting

Tax-loss harvesting (TLH) is the practice of deliberately selling investments at a loss to realize a [[capital-gains-tax|capital loss]] that offsets realized capital gains (or, within limits, ordinary income), thereby reducing the current tax bill. The proceeds are then reinvested in a similar -- but not substantially identical -- asset so the portfolio's market exposure and risk profile are preserved. TLH is the canonical source of **[[tax-alpha]]**: incremental after-tax return generated by tax-aware portfolio management rather than by security selection.

## Overview

The core mechanism: sell a losing position, apply the realized loss against realized gains (and up to $3,000/yr of ordinary income in the US), reinvest the proceeds to stay invested, and let any excess loss carry forward indefinitely. The central constraint in the US is the IRS **[[wash-sale]] rule**: if you buy the same or a "substantially identical" security within 30 days *before or after* the loss sale, the loss is disallowed and instead added to the cost basis of the replacement shares. Crypto historically enjoyed a major advantage -- prior to 2026, digital assets were not subject to the wash-sale rule, so traders could sell at a loss and *immediately* repurchase the identical token. That loophole is closing as legislation extends wash-sale treatment to digital assets starting in 2026. Other jurisdictions differ sharply (see [[tax-loss-harvesting-australia]], which has no wash-sale rule but a 50% CGT discount and 30 June year-end).

## Edge source

Per [[edge-taxonomy]], TLH is a purely **structural** edge -- arguably the cleanest one available to a taxable investor. It does not require a market view, predictive skill, or being right about price direction. The "alpha" is extracted from the tax system's asymmetric treatment of gains and losses, not from another trader. This is why crowding_risk is **low**: one investor's harvesting does not degrade another's, and the IRS code does not become "arbitraged away."

## Why this edge exists

The counterparty is the **tax authority**, and the edge is the time value of deferred tax. When you harvest a $10,000 loss and offset a $10,000 short-term gain at a 35% rate, you keep $3,500 today that you would otherwise have remitted. TLH rarely *eliminates* the tax (the replacement asset carries a lower basis, so a larger gain accrues later) -- it **defers** it. Deferral has real value: the $3,500 stays invested and compounds, and the eventual gain may be taxed at a lower long-term rate, or stepped up at death, or offset by future harvests. The edge persists because tax deferral is positive-sum for the investor and the government cannot prevent legitimate loss realization.

## Null hypothesis

Under no edge, harvesting would be a wash: the present value of taxes saved today would exactly equal the present value of extra taxes owed later on the lower-basis replacement asset. The edge is positive only because of (1) the discount rate / time value applied to the deferral, (2) the **rate arbitrage** between short-term ordinary rates (harvested now) and long-term rates (paid later), (3) the chance the gain is never realized at full rate (basis step-up at death, charitable donation of appreciated shares), and (4) the $3,000 ordinary-income deduction, which converts capital losses into a higher-value offset. If none of these hold and rates are flat with a zero discount rate, TLH adds nothing.

## Rules

- **Identify** lots with unrealized losses, prioritizing **short-term** losses (offset ordinary-rate short-term gains, up to ~37%) over long-term losses (offset 15-20% rate gains). Lot-level [[cost-basis-lots|cost-basis]] tracking with specific-identification is essential.
- **Sell** to realize the loss.
- **Reinvest immediately** in a correlated but *not substantially identical* security to dodge the [[wash-sale]] rule (e.g., sell an S&P 500 fund, buy a total-market or large-cap-blend fund from a different index provider).
- **Offset** in IRS order: short-term losses → short-term gains, then long-term gains; then up to $3,000 against ordinary income; carry the rest forward.
- **Wait out the window:** do not rebuy the original security within the ±30-day [[wash-sale]] window across *all* accounts, including IRAs and a spouse's accounts.

## Implementation pseudocode

```python
WASH_WINDOW_DAYS = 30
for lot in portfolio.lots:
    unrealized = lot.market_value - lot.cost_basis
    if unrealized < -loss_threshold(lot):          # material loss
        if not bought_substantially_identical(lot.security, WASH_WINDOW_DAYS):
            sell(lot)                               # realize loss
            replacement = correlated_not_identical(lot.security)
            buy(replacement, lot.proceeds)          # keep market exposure
            record_loss(lot, holding_period(lot))   # ST first, then LT
            schedule_no_repurchase(lot.security, days=WASH_WINDOW_DAYS)

apply_losses(order=["ST_gains","LT_gains","ordinary_income<=3000","carryforward"])
```

## Indicators / data used

No market indicators. Required data: lot-level [[cost-basis-lots|cost basis]], acquisition dates ([[holding-period]] for ST/LT classification), current prices ([[ohlcv-daily]]), and a cross-account purchase ledger to enforce [[wash-sale]] compliance.

## Example trade

A trader has **$50,000 in realized short-term gains** this year (taxed at 35% = $17,500 owed). They also hold 2 ETH bought at $4,000 each, now $3,000 each -- a $2,000 unrealized loss. They sell both ETH, realizing a $2,000 short-term loss. **Tax offset: $2,000 × 35% = $700 saved now.** In a pre-2026 crypto context (no wash-sale rule), they immediately rebuy 2 ETH at $3,000, keeping the position. New basis: $3,000/ETH. If ETH later rises to $4,000 and they sell, they owe tax on the $1,000/ETH gain -- but the tax was *deferred*, and the $700 compounded in the interim. *(From 2026, the wash-sale rule applies to crypto, so the immediate identical repurchase would disallow the loss.)*

## Performance characteristics

TLH's benefit is **certain in direction** (you either harvest a real loss or you don't) but **modest and cost-sensitive in magnitude**. Industry estimates of [[tax-alpha]] from systematic, direct-indexed TLH commonly cite roughly 0.5-2% of incremental after-tax return per year, concentrated in volatile, dispersed years and early in a portfolio's life (before most positions are deeply in the green). Frame this qualitatively: the more dispersion across holdings and the higher the marginal tax rate, the larger the harvest opportunity.

Realistic cost overlay -- the benefit is **net of** these drags:

| Cost / friction | Effect on net tax-alpha |
|-----------------|-------------------------|
| [[bid-ask-spread]] + [[slippage]] on sell/rebuy | Direct drag; matters most in illiquid small caps / altcoins |
| Commissions | Near-zero for equities today; nontrivial for some crypto venues |
| Tracking error vs. original holding | Replacement asset's performance can diverge during the 30-day window |
| Lower replacement basis | Inflates *future* tax; reduces benefit to a *deferral*, not elimination |
| Administrative / software cost | Lot tracking, wash-sale monitoring across accounts |
| Short-term → long-term conversion | If you must hold the replacement, you may convert ST loss potential into LT |

Net rule of thumb: harvest only when the tax saved exceeds round-trip [[transaction-costs]] plus the expected cost of 30 days of tracking error.

## Capacity limits

Effectively unlimited at the individual and even institutional/direct-indexing scale -- TLH does not move markets and one investor's harvesting does not crowd out another's. The practical limit is **opportunity, not capacity**: a portfolio with few losing lots (e.g., after a long bull run) simply has little to harvest. Capacity is bounded by available losses, not by [[market-impact]].

## What kills this strategy

See [[failure-modes]]. The realistic risks:

- **[[wash-sale]] violation** -- the single biggest operational risk; disallows the loss if the same/identical security is bought within ±30 days in *any* account (including IRAs, spouse). Crypto loses its exemption from 2026.
- **"Substantially identical" ambiguity** -- aggressive replacement choices (e.g., two near-identical index funds) can be challenged.
- **Tax-law change** -- rate changes, deduction-cap changes, or extension of wash-sale scope alter the math.
- **Cost erosion** -- harvesting illiquid positions where spread/[[slippage]] exceeds tax saved.
- **Behavioral over-trading** -- harvesting becomes an excuse to churn, incurring costs and tracking error.

## Kill criteria

See [[when-to-retire-a-strategy]]. Stop or pause harvesting a given position/program when:

- Estimated round-trip [[transaction-costs]] for the lot exceed the tax saved.
- No material losing lots remain (opportunity exhausted).
- A wash-sale conflict cannot be avoided without unacceptable tracking error.
- A tax-law change removes the deferral/rate-arbitrage benefit.

## Advantages

- **Near-certain, quantifiable benefit** -- realized losses directly reduce the current tax bill.
- **Maintains market exposure** -- reinvesting in correlated assets keeps you invested.
- **Compounds** -- annual [[tax-alpha]] reinvested compounds over a portfolio's life.
- **Loss carryforward** -- excess losses carry forward indefinitely (US).
- **Low crowding** -- structurally non-competitive edge.

## Disadvantages

- **Deferral, not elimination** -- lower replacement basis means higher future tax.
- **[[wash-sale]] complexity** -- ±30-day window and "substantially identical" tests across all accounts.
- **Crypto wash-sale from 2026** -- erodes crypto's historical TLH edge.
- **Transaction costs & tracking error** -- erode benefit, especially in illiquid assets.
- **Administrative burden** -- meticulous [[cost-basis-lots|cost-basis]] and window tracking required.
