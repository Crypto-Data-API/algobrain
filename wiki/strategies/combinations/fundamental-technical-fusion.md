# Fundamental-Technical Fusion

## Overview

The oldest debate in trading is fundamentals vs. technicals. Fundamental-technical fusion ends that debate by using both. Fundamentals answer the question **"What should I buy?"** — identifying high-quality companies with strong earnings, reasonable valuations, and competitive advantages. Technicals answer the question **"When should I buy it?"** — timing entries at optimal price levels using chart patterns, indicators, and momentum signals.

Neither approach is complete alone. Pure fundamental investors often buy too early ("the stock is cheap" — then it gets cheaper for two years). Pure technical traders often buy garbage companies that look good on a chart but collapse on bad earnings. Fusion traders get the best of both: they own quality and they buy it at the right price at the right time.

## The Synergy

The synergy is rooted in the different information each approach captures:

**Fundamentals provide conviction.** When you know a company is growing earnings at 25% annually, has a fortress balance sheet, and trades at a reasonable [[price-to-earnings-ratio]], you can hold through volatility. You are not just trading a pattern — you are owning a business. This conviction prevents panic selling during normal pullbacks.

**Technicals provide timing and risk management.** Even the best company can be a terrible trade if you buy at the wrong price. Technical analysis tells you where institutional buyers are likely stepping in ([[support-and-resistance]]), when momentum is shifting in your favor ([[macd]], [[rsi]]), and where to place your stop loss to limit downside. You buy the great company, but only when the chart says "now."

**The compounding effect:** Fundamental quality means your winners tend to keep winning (strong businesses compound). Technical timing means your losers are cut quickly (stops are hit before damage compounds). Over time, this asymmetry — big winners, small losers — compounds into substantial outperformance.

## Implementation

**Phase 1 — Fundamental Screening.** Use a stock screener to filter the universe down to quality. Key filters:
- Earnings per share growth > 15% year-over-year (last 3 quarters)
- Revenue growth > 10% year-over-year
- [[return-on-equity]] > 15%
- [[price-to-earnings-ratio]] below the industry average or below the stock's 5-year average
- Debt-to-equity < 1.0 (balance sheet health)
- Institutional ownership between 20-70% (smart money involved, but not overcrowded)

This should yield 20-50 candidates depending on market conditions.

**Phase 2 — Chart Review.** For each fundamental candidate, examine the daily and weekly chart:
- Is the stock in a long-term uptrend (above 200-day [[moving-averages]])?
- Are there identifiable [[chart-patterns]] forming — [[cup-and-handle]], [[flat-base]], [[ascending-triangle]]?
- Is price approaching a clear [[support-and-resistance]] level from above (pullback to support)?
- Is [[rsi]] coming off oversold or trending upward from neutral?

Narrow to 5-10 stocks with both fundamental quality AND constructive chart setups.

**Phase 3 — Entry Trigger.** Wait for a specific technical event:
- **Breakout entry:** Price breaks above a [[cup-and-handle]] pivot or [[consolidation]] range on volume 50%+ above average. This is the [[can-slim]] approach.
- **Pullback entry:** Price pulls back to the 50-day MA or a key support level. [[rsi]] reaches 40-50 range and turns up. Buy on the first green candle with increasing volume.
- **Oversold bounce:** After a market-wide selloff, fundamentally strong stocks with [[rsi]] below 30 are bought with the expectation of mean reversion. The fundamental quality provides confidence that the selloff is temporary.

**Phase 4 — Risk Management.** Stop loss 7-8% below entry (O'Neil's rule), or just below the support level used for entry. Take partial profits at 20-25% gain. Let the remainder run with a trailing stop based on the 10-week MA.

## Example Setup

**CAN SLIM in action:**

A company reports earnings growth of 40% last quarter, 35% the quarter before. Revenue up 22%. ROE is 28%. Institutional sponsorship increasing (new fund positions reported in 13F filings). The stock has formed a 7-week [[cup-and-handle]] pattern on the daily chart. Volume has dried up during the handle (low supply). On the breakout day, the stock gaps above the pivot point on 3x average volume. Entry is taken at the pivot. Stop is set at 7% below. The stock runs 45% over the next 3 months as earnings continue to beat expectations.

## When It Excels

- **Bull markets** where fundamentally strong companies lead. Growth stocks with great earnings are the biggest winners in bull runs.
- **Earnings season** — fusion traders can position before earnings in stocks with strong fundamental trends, using technical levels for risk management.
- **Recovery phases** after bear markets, when quality companies are deeply oversold. Fundamentals identify the survivors, technicals identify the bottom.
- When the investor has a **medium-term time horizon** (weeks to months) that allows both fundamental thesis and technical setup to play out.

## When It Fails

- **Bear markets** driven by macro forces (recession, rate hikes). Even great companies go down when the tide goes out. The technical stop loss is essential here.
- **Speculative bubbles** where the best-performing stocks have terrible fundamentals (meme stocks, SPACs). Pure fundamental screening will miss these moves entirely.
- **Fast-moving markets** where fundamental screening is too slow. Day trading and scalping require pure technical approaches.
- When fundamentals are **fraudulent or misleading** (Enron, Wirecard). Technical analysis can sometimes flag these via distribution patterns, but it is not foolproof.

## Real-World Usage

**William O'Neil's [[can-slim]]** is the most systematic implementation of fundamental-technical fusion. The CAN SLIM acronym encodes both fundamental criteria (Current earnings, Annual earnings, New products) and technical criteria (Supply/demand, Leader, Institutional sponsorship, Market direction). O'Neil's research showed that the biggest stock market winners of the past century almost always had both strong fundamentals AND specific chart patterns before their major advances.

**Warren Buffett picks the company, the chart picks the price.** While Buffett himself does not use technical analysis, many Buffett-inspired investors use his fundamental framework for stock selection and then overlay basic [[technical-analysis]] for entry timing. Buying Berkshire-quality companies at technically oversold levels has historically been extremely effective.

**Peter Lynch's approach** also combined fundamental insight (invest in what you know, check the numbers) with awareness of valuation timing — his [[peg-ratio]] essentially adds a timing element to fundamental valuation.
