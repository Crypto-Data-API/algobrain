Brexit — the United Kingdom's withdrawal from the European Union — was triggered by a referendum on June 23, 2016, in which 51.9% voted to leave. The result shocked financial markets: the British pound fell 8% overnight (the largest single-day decline since the 1985 Plaza Accord), FTSE 100 futures dropped 9%, and approximately $2 trillion in global equity value was erased in two trading sessions. Brexit created a multi-year regime of uncertainty that affected currency markets, trade policy, and UK financial regulation.

## Market Impact: Referendum Night (June 23-24, 2016)

### The Setup

Polls and betting markets gave Remain a ~70-75% probability of winning on the day of the vote. Accordingly:
- GBP/USD had rallied to $1.50 in the days before the vote (up from $1.40 in February)
- Implied volatility in GBP options was at extreme levels — the market expected a large move but was positioned for Remain
- FTSE 100 had rallied 3.5% in the week before the vote

### The Result

| Time (London) | Event | GBP/USD |
|---------------|-------|---------|
| 10:00 PM | Polls close; bookmakers give Remain 85% probability | $1.50 |
| 11:30 PM | Sunderland result: Leave wins by larger margin than expected | $1.46 |
| 12:30 AM | Newcastle result: Remain wins but by thin margin (expected a large win) | $1.43 |
| 2:00 AM | Leave takes clear lead in running total | $1.37 |
| 4:40 AM | BBC calls the vote for Leave | $1.33 |
| 8:00 AM | London open | $1.36 |
| Close | End of June 24 | $1.37 |

**GBP/USD**: $1.50 → $1.33 (11.3% intraday range, closing down ~8%)
**EUR/GBP**: Jumped 7%+ overnight
**FTSE 100**: Opened down 8%, recovered to close down 3.1%
**FTSE 250** (more UK-domestic): Closed down 7.2%
**Gold**: +$60/oz as a safe-haven bid

### Volatility

GBP 1-month implied volatility had hit ~25% before the vote (vs. normal ~8%) and spiked further after the result. Traders who were long GBP vol — regardless of directional view — profited enormously. Those who sold options premium into the event faced catastrophic losses on the GBP leg.

## Longer-Term Market Effects

### Sterling

GBP/USD never returned to pre-referendum levels. It traded in a $1.20-1.40 range for most of 2016-2023, punctuated by the [[flash-crash-2016-gbp|October 2016 GBP flash crash]] when sterling briefly hit $1.1841. The permanent devaluation reflected:
- Reduced foreign investment flows into the UK
- Persistent uncertainty about trade arrangements
- Inflationary pressure from a weaker pound raising import costs

### UK Equities

FTSE 100 (multinational-heavy) actually outperformed post-Brexit as the weaker pound boosted overseas earnings. FTSE 250 (UK-domestic) underperformed as domestic economic outlook deteriorated. The FTSE 100 / FTSE 250 ratio became a widely-watched "Brexit sentiment" indicator.

### Bond Markets

UK gilts rallied (yields fell) as the Bank of England cut rates and resumed QE in August 2016 to cushion the economic impact. Gilt yields reached historic lows.

## Trading Lessons

1. **Event risk can overcome consensus positioning.** The market was positioned for Remain with ~75% confidence. When the consensus was wrong, the unwind was violent because everyone was on the same side.

2. **The overnight session is where binary event risk materializes.** GBP moved 17 big figures in 6 hours while most retail traders in London and New York were asleep. Stops could not protect positions against gap risk of this magnitude.

3. **Volatility is the best expression of event uncertainty.** Long straddles or strangles on GBP captured the move regardless of direction. The extreme implied vol was expensive, but the realized move exceeded it.

4. **Second-order effects persist longer than first-order.** The initial GBP crash was a one-day event, but the structural shift in UK-EU trade relationships created a multi-year regime change in GBP, UK equities, and UK rates.

5. **Betting markets and polls can be wrong.** Prediction markets gave Remain high probability — and were wrong. Traders who treated the vote as a genuine coin flip (50/50 with a slight lean) rather than trusting consensus odds were better positioned.
