# GameStop Short Squeeze

The GameStop (GME) short squeeze of January 2021 was one of the most extraordinary events in modern market history. A loosely coordinated group of retail traders on the [[reddit]] forum r/WallStreetBets drove the stock of a struggling video game retailer up over 2,000% in a matter of weeks, inflicting catastrophic losses on several [[hedge-funds]] and igniting a nationwide debate about the fairness of financial markets, the power of [[social-media]] in trading, and the mechanics of [[short-selling]].

## Background: Why GameStop Was Heavily Shorted

GameStop, a brick-and-mortar video game retailer, had been in secular decline for years as the industry shifted toward digital downloads and streaming. By late 2020, multiple institutional investors had taken large [[short-positions]] against GME, betting the stock would continue falling. [[short-interest]] exceeded 140% of the available [[float]] — meaning more shares had been sold short than actually existed in public circulation, a situation made possible by the mechanics of [[securities-lending]] and [[rehypothecation]].

This extreme level of [[short-interest]] created the conditions for a potential [[short-squeeze]]: if the stock price rose, short sellers would be forced to buy shares to cover their positions, driving the price even higher in a self-reinforcing feedback loop.

## The Catalyst: Keith Gill and r/WallStreetBets

The story of the GameStop squeeze is inseparable from Keith Gill, a financial educator and trader from Massachusetts who posted on [[reddit]]'s r/WallStreetBets under the username "DeepFuckingValue" (DFV) and on YouTube as "Roaring Kitty." Starting in mid-2019, Gill built a position in GME based on a fundamental value-investing thesis: he believed the stock was deeply undervalued, that the short sellers were overextended, and that new management (including incoming board member Ryan Cohen, co-founder of Chewy) could turn the company around.

Gill regularly posted screenshots of his portfolio showing his GME [[call-options]] and shares, which initially drew ridicule but increasingly attracted attention as the position grew in value. His conviction and transparency inspired a growing community of retail traders to research and buy GME.

## The Squeeze: Timeline of Events

- **December 2020 - Early January 2021**: GME began climbing from around $20, driven by increasing retail interest, Ryan Cohen joining the board, and growing awareness of the extreme [[short-interest]].
- **January 11-12, 2021**: Cohen was appointed to GameStop's board. The stock surged past $30.
- **January 13, 2021**: GME crossed $30, triggering initial [[short-covering]] and a momentum surge.
- **January 22, 2021**: GME hit $65, more than tripling in a week. Short sellers were under immense pressure.
- **January 25-26, 2021**: The stock exploded, reaching $148 and then $347. [[melvin-capital]], a prominent hedge fund with a massive short position, received a $2.75 billion emergency investment from [[citadel]] and Point72 to stave off collapse. Melvin ultimately lost approximately $6.8 billion, roughly 53% of its assets under management.
- **January 27, 2021**: GME hit an intraday high of $483 in pre-market trading. [[trading-volume]] was extraordinary, with hundreds of millions of shares changing hands.
- **January 28, 2021 — "The Buy Button Incident"**: [[robinhood]] and several other brokerages restricted buying of GME and other heavily shorted "[[meme-stocks]]" including AMC, BB, BBBY, and NOK. Users could only sell, not buy. The stock crashed from $483 to $112 in a single day. This decision provoked outrage from traders across the political spectrum, who accused [[robinhood]] of protecting [[hedge-funds]] at the expense of retail investors.
- **Late January - February 2021**: GME declined to approximately $40 before experiencing additional volatile surges throughout 2021.

## Why Robinhood Restricted Trading

[[robinhood]] stated that the trading restrictions were necessary due to increased deposit requirements from the DTCC (Depository Trust & Clearing Corporation), which demanded approximately $3 billion in additional collateral due to the extreme [[volatility]] and settlement risk. Robinhood CEO Vlad Tenev later testified before Congress that the firm faced a [[liquidity]] crisis, not pressure from [[hedge-funds]].

However, the fact that [[citadel]] Securities (the [[market-maker]] arm of Citadel, which is separate from — but related to — the hedge fund that bailed out [[melvin-capital]]) was Robinhood's largest source of revenue through [[payment-for-order-flow]] arrangements fueled widespread suspicion of a conflict of interest. The controversy brought intense scrutiny to the [[payment-for-order-flow]] business model that underpins "free" trading platforms.

## Short Squeeze Mechanics Explained

A [[short-squeeze]] occurs when a heavily shorted stock's price rises, forcing [[short-sellers]] to buy back shares to close their positions and limit losses. This forced buying creates additional upward pressure, which triggers more short covering in a cascading feedback loop.

Key factors that made GME's squeeze historically extreme:
- **[[short-interest]] above 100% of [[float]]**: More shares were sold short than existed, creating an almost mathematically guaranteed squeeze if enough buying pressure materialized.
- **[[gamma-squeeze]]**: As GME's price rose, [[market-makers]] who had sold [[call-options]] were forced to buy shares to [[delta-hedge]] their exposure, amplifying the upward move.
- **Coordinated retail buying**: Thousands of individual traders buying shares and call options simultaneously created concentrated demand.
- **Low [[float]]**: GameStop had a relatively small number of shares available for trading, making the price more sensitive to buying pressure.

## Congressional Hearings and Regulatory Response

The GameStop saga prompted multiple Congressional hearings in February 2021. Keith Gill, Vlad Tenev (Robinhood CEO), Ken Griffin (Citadel CEO), and Gabe Plotkin (Melvin Capital) all testified. The hearings addressed [[short-selling]] practices, [[payment-for-order-flow]], [[market-structure]], and the T+2 settlement cycle. The SEC later published a staff report in October 2021 concluding that the events were driven primarily by positive sentiment from retail investors rather than a traditional short squeeze, a characterization many retail traders disputed.

## Lasting Impact

The GameStop short squeeze left a permanent mark on market culture and structure:

1. **"[[meme-stocks]]" culture**: GME spawned an entire category of stocks driven by social media sentiment. AMC Entertainment, BlackBerry, Bed Bath & Beyond, and others experienced similar (if less extreme) squeezes.
2. **[[democratization-of-markets]]**: The event crystallized a narrative about retail traders gaining power in markets historically dominated by institutions.
3. **[[payment-for-order-flow]] debate**: Regulators began seriously examining whether PFOF creates conflicts of interest that harm retail traders.
4. **T+1 settlement**: The liquidity problems exposed by the GME event accelerated the SEC's move to shorten the settlement cycle from T+2 to T+1, implemented in May 2024.
5. **[[short-selling]] transparency**: Calls for greater disclosure of [[short-positions]] intensified, though comprehensive reforms remain limited.
6. **Keith Gill's legacy**: Gill became a folk hero in retail trading circles. His return to social media in mid-2024 triggered another GME surge, demonstrating the lasting cultural power of the movement.

The GameStop saga demonstrated that in the age of [[social-media]], zero-commission trading, and [[options]] accessibility, retail traders can exert market-moving force — and that the infrastructure of modern markets was not designed for such events. It remains essential reading for any trader seeking to understand [[market-microstructure]], [[short-squeeze]] dynamics, and the evolving relationship between retail and institutional participants.
