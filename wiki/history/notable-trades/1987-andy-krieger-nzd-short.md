# Andy Krieger — NZD Short (October 1987)

In the immediate aftermath of **Black Monday (October 19, 1987)**, **Andy Krieger** — a 32-year-old options trader on the Bankers Trust FX desk — built a short position in the New Zealand dollar reportedly larger than the entire **NZD money supply**. Over a few weeks beginning late October, the trade made an estimated **$300M for Bankers Trust** — the single largest profit ever generated by a Bankers Trust trader at that point and a foundational story in modern macro trading.

The trade is widely cited as the textbook example of using options-based leverage to magnify a triangular-arbitrage view: Krieger combined a directional view on NZD with cross-rate analysis (NZD-USD, NZD-AUD, NZD-JPY) and executed primarily through OTM options with built-in convexity.

## Background

**Black Monday** (October 19, 1987) crashed global equity markets by 22%+ in a single session. Investors scrambled into "safe" currencies — the US dollar initially, then the Japanese yen and Swiss franc. Smaller currencies that had benefited from the pre-crash carry-trade environment now faced a "flight from quality" — the New Zealand dollar in particular, which had been a high-yield destination for Japanese and European savers.

Krieger ran the **Bankers Trust options desk**, then the most aggressive FX options operation on Wall Street. He observed:

1. NZD spot was holding remarkably steady despite the crash — supported by central-bank intervention and slow repositioning by Asian carry-trade investors.
2. NZD options pricing implied a 1-month vol of ~12% — far below realized vol expectations for a small open economy in a global risk-off shock.
3. Cross-rates (NZD/AUD, NZD/USD, NZD/JPY) were consistent with each other, but inconsistent with the implied probability of repricing.

## The Trade

Krieger built a massive short NZD position via:

1. **Spot short** of NZD vs USD — the directional core.
2. **OTM put options** on NZD/USD — the convexity layer.
3. **Cross hedges** via NZD/AUD and NZD/JPY to diversify the move source.
4. **Reportedly leverage of 100-400x** on the options book, using the bank's balance sheet.

**Reported peak short position:** $1B+ in notional NZD short — described later as "more NZD than there is in circulation," though this figure is contested (probably refers to non-domestic-bank NZD float).

## The Move

NZD/USD declined approximately **5% over the following weeks**, accelerating as the bank's massive short pushed price further down (effectively a self-reinforcing loop given thin NZ market depth). Krieger's options position generated outsized P&L because:

- The 5% spot move triggered the gamma-positive options to print full notional.
- Vol re-priced from ~12% implied to ~25% realized over the period.
- Cross-rate dislocations (NZD/AUD widened more than NZD/USD) added a triangular component.

**Reported P&L: ~$300M** for Bankers Trust — booked over October-November 1987 and into early 1988. Krieger received a $3M bonus (he had asked for $10M+); resigned in 1988 over the dispute and went to Soros Fund Management.

## How the Triangulation Worked

The pure-spot trade alone wouldn't have generated $300M — Krieger needed the options layer for convexity *and* the triangular cross-rates to confirm the directional thesis.

**Triangle 1: NZD-USD-JPY.** Yen was rallying as global risk-off bid. NZD/JPY decompressed faster than NZD/USD because the yen leg moved both directions. Cross-rate broke parity intra-day.

**Triangle 2: NZD-AUD-USD.** AUD and NZD typically correlate >0.85; in the Black Monday aftermath they decoupled briefly because Australian commodity exports were less exposed than NZ agricultural exports to the global slowdown. Cross-rate dislocations of 50-100 bp were tradable.

**Triangle 3: NZD options vs Forward NZD.** Implied vol on the options market was inconsistent with the cross-currency basis embedded in the forward market. Krieger's options were systematically cheap relative to the synthetic alternative.

## Aftermath

- Bankers Trust's reported FX profits in 1987 jumped 80%+ vs 1986; Krieger's trade was the dominant driver.
- New Zealand authorities reportedly contacted Bankers Trust to complain about the position size and resulting NZD move — without policy effect.
- Krieger left Bankers Trust in early 1988 over the bonus dispute. Joined Soros Fund Management briefly; later founded Krieger & Associates.
- The trade became a Wall Street legend and a recruiting case study at hedge funds.
- Bankers Trust later became infamous for derivatives losses (Procter & Gamble, Gibson Greetings 1994), and was acquired by Deutsche Bank in 1999.

## Lessons for Traders

1. **Convexity is the leverage multiplier.** A directional view sized via options can generate 10-100x the P&L of pure-spot exposure if vol is mispriced going in.
2. **Crisis triangular dislocations are real.** Even in the most-traded G10 currencies, 50-100 bp cross-rate inconsistencies emerge during regime shifts.
3. **Position size relative to free float matters.** Krieger's $1B NZD short was reportedly larger than the actively-traded NZD float; this generated additional self-reinforcing alpha.
4. **Compensation politics ends careers.** The $7M gap between Krieger's request and bonus paid drove him out.
5. **Bank balance sheet enables the trade.** A pure prop trader without Bankers Trust's leverage capacity could not have replicated the size.
