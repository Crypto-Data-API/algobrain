# Hunt Brothers Silver Corner (1979-1980)

The Hunt brothers' attempt to corner the silver market in 1979-1980 remains one of the most dramatic episodes of commodity market manipulation in history. Nelson Bunker Hunt and William Herbert Hunt — sons of Texas oil billionaire H.L. Hunt — accumulated an estimated 100-200 million ounces of [[silver]], roughly one-third of the world's privately held supply, driving prices to an all-time high before the position collapsed in spectacular fashion.

## The Mechanics

The Hunts accumulated silver through multiple channels: physical bullion, COMEX and CBOT futures contracts, and silver held offshore. Their buying created a self-reinforcing cycle — as they bought, prices rose, drawing in more speculative interest, further tightening supply. At their peak, the Hunts and their associates controlled an estimated 200+ million ounces across physical and paper positions.

The squeeze was amplified by the Hunts' insistence on **taking physical delivery** of futures contracts rather than rolling or cash-settling — removing metal from exchange warehouses and tightening deliverable supply. COMEX registered silver stocks fell from ~120 million ounces to ~30 million ounces during the run-up.

## The Collapse

The collapse was triggered by **exchange rule changes**, not market forces:

1. **COMEX imposed "liquidation only" trading** on January 21, 1980 — meaning traders could only sell existing positions, not initiate new longs. This eliminated buy-side demand.
2. **Margin requirements were raised dramatically**, forcing leveraged longs (including the Hunts) to post massive additional collateral.
3. With buy-side demand eliminated and margin calls mounting, the price collapsed.
4. On **Silver Thursday** (March 27, 1980), silver fell to ~$10/oz. The Hunts failed to meet an estimated $100 million margin call from Bache Group.
5. The potential default by the Hunts threatened to cascade through the brokerage system, prompting a consortium of banks to arrange a $1.1 billion bailout.

## Lessons for Traders

- **Exchange rule changes can destroy a position**: Rules are not static. When a position grows large enough to threaten market integrity, exchanges will change the rules — mid-trade if necessary. See also [[2022-lme-nickel-squeeze]].
- **Concentrated positions create systemic risk**: A position large enough to move the market is large enough to be targeted by regulators and exchanges.
- **Margin requirements can change without warning**: Risk management must account for the possibility of margin increases at the worst possible time.
- **Physical delivery squeezes have limits**: Regulators and exchanges will intervene before a true corner succeeds in modern markets.
- **The crime is the corner, not the loss**: The Hunts were ultimately convicted not because they lost money but because the coordinated accumulation constituted market manipulation.
