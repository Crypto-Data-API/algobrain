# Notable Trades

Famous trades, legendary positions, and pivotal moments in trading history. Each page breaks down the **thesis**, the **execution**, the **risk management**, and the **aftermath** — so traders can learn from both the winners and the losers. The recurring lessons across these episodes are remarkably consistent: asymmetric risk/reward, conviction sizing into a structural mispricing, and the brutal role of liquidity, leverage, and rule changes in deciding who survives.

## The big themes

- **Breaking a policy peg.** The most studied macro trades attack a government commitment that markets no longer believe — sterling in 1992 ([[1992-black-wednesday-erm-crisis]]), the kiwi in the 1987 crash aftermath ([[1987-andy-krieger-nzd-short]]). When a peg or defended level cracks, the payoff is wildly asymmetric: a small carry cost if you are wrong, a multi-percent gap in days if you are right.
- **Cornering a market.** Concentrating a position large enough to move price invites the exchange to change the rules mid-trade — the [[hunt-brothers-silver-corner|Hunt brothers' silver corner]] (1979-80) and the [[2022-lme-nickel-squeeze|LME nickel squeeze]] (2022) both ended not with a market top but with a forced rule change.
- **Squeezing the shorts.** Crowded short positions in low-float names can be reflexively squeezed by coordinated buying — the [[gamestop-short-squeeze|GameStop short squeeze]] (2021) turned retail into a market-moving force.
- **Convexity as leverage.** Sizing a directional view through options rather than spot can multiply the payoff 10-100x when implied volatility is mispriced going in — the defining feature of Krieger's NZD trade.
