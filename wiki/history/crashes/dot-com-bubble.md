# Dot-Com Bubble

The dot-com bubble (approximately 1995-2000) was a speculative mania centered on internet and technology stocks that drove the nasdaq Composite up over 400% before it crashed 78% from its March 10, 2000 peak. It remains one of the most studied episodes of market excess and a reference point for evaluating every subsequent technology-driven rally.

## The Bubble

The commercialization of the internet in the mid-1990s triggered a wave of euphoria. Venture capital flooded into internet startups, many with no revenue, no path to profitability, and business models summarized as "get big fast." Companies like Pets.com, Webvan, eToys, and Kozmo.com raised hundreds of millions in IPOs. Traditional valuation metrics (P/E ratios, cash flow) were dismissed as irrelevant to the "New Economy."

The nasdaq rose from approximately 1,000 in mid-1995 to 5,048 on March 10, 2000. Day trading became a cultural phenomenon. Taxi drivers and dentists quit their jobs to trade stocks. Media celebrated paper millionaires. warren-buffett, who refused to invest in technology stocks he did not understand, was widely mocked as out of touch.

### Anatomy of the valuation excess

The defining feature of the bubble was the abandonment of cash-flow-based valuation in favour of invented metrics:

- **"Eyeballs," page views, and CPM** replaced earnings. Companies were valued on traffic that was never monetized.
- **Price-to-sales (not P/E)** because there were no earnings to divide by — and even revenue was sometimes booked aggressively (gross "barter" deals).
- **"Burn rate" reframed as ambition.** Losing money fast was celebrated as land-grab investment rather than a warning sign.
- **The "New Economy" narrative** held that the internet repealed the business cycle and made traditional valuation obsolete — a textbook "[[this-time-is-different]]" rationalization.

At the peak the nasdaq Composite traded at a trailing P/E above 175× — a level with no historical precedent and no plausible earnings-growth justification.

### Causes → Consequences

| Cause | Mechanism | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Internet commercialization (1995) | Genuine transformative technology | Real opportunity attracts real and speculative capital |
| Easy venture + IPO capital | "Get big fast"; profitless firms go public | Capital allocated to unviable business models |
| Narrative over numbers ("New Economy") | Valuation discipline abandoned | Prices detach from any fundamental anchor |
| Retail mania + [[behavioral-finance|herding]] | Day-trading culture, FOMO | Bubble inflates on momentum, not value |
| [[federal-reserve|Fed]] tightening (1999–2000) | Liquidity drains; discount rate rises | Long-duration "story stocks" repriced hardest |
| Earnings disappointments + fraud (Enron, WorldCom) | Narrative cracks; no fundamental floor | −78% nasdaq collapse; ~$5T in value erased |

## The Crash

The bubble burst in stages. The [[federal-reserve]] raised [[interest-rates]] six times between June 1999 and May 2000. Several high-profile internet companies reported disappointing earnings. The Microsoft antitrust ruling in April 2000 added negative sentiment. By October 2002, the nasdaq had fallen to 1,114 — a 78% decline from its peak. Trillions of dollars in market capitalization were destroyed. The index did not recover its 2000 high until 2015.

Notable casualties included Pets.com (liquidated), WorldCom (fraud, bankruptcy), and Enron (fraud, bankruptcy). Even survivors like Amazon fell from $107 to $7 before eventually recovering.

### Survivors vs casualties

The technology thesis was *right*; most of the equities expressing it were *wrong*. The crash separated durable businesses from financed narratives.

| Company | Outcome | Lesson |
|---|---|---|
| **Amazon** | Survived; fell ~95% ($107→$7) then compounded into a trillion-dollar company | Real (if thin) revenue + a defensible model + access to capital = survival |
| **eBay** | Survived; profitable network-effect marketplace | Genuine unit economics beat "eyeballs" |
| **Priceline / Booking** | Survived a near-death drop (~99%), restructured, became dominant | Even good businesses can be priced for catastrophe |
| **Google** | Founded 1998, IPO'd 2004 *after* the bust | Timing: the best business of the era never participated in the mania |
| **Pets.com** | Liquidated 2000, ~9 months after IPO | Cash burn with no path to profit; mascot ≠ moat |
| **Webvan** | Bankrupt 2001 after burning ~$800M | Capital-intensive expansion ahead of demand |
| **WorldCom / Enron** | Bankrupt via accounting fraud | The mania's incentives bred fraud at the periphery |

## Key Lessons

- **Valuation matters eventually**: Companies with no earnings and speculative business models were priced as if success were guaranteed.
- **"This time is different" is dangerous**: Every bubble produces a narrative explaining why traditional valuation is obsolete.
- **Survivors win big**: Amazon, eBay, and Google survived the crash and went on to dominate. The technology thesis was correct — the valuations were not.
- **The Fed matters**: Rate hikes were a key catalyst for the bubble's deflation, as they are in virtually every market cycle.

## Trading Relevance

The dot-com bubble is essential context for evaluating the current AI-driven market, where [[nvidia]] and other AI beneficiaries have seen parabolic price appreciation. The parallels (and differences) between the dot-com era and the AI era are actively debated — see ai-capex-vs-cash-flow-divergence. A key distinction often raised: today's mega-cap AI leaders generate enormous real cash flow, unlike the revenue-less dot-coms of 1999, while the bears point to circular vendor financing and capex running ahead of demonstrated demand. [[george-soros]]' [[quantum-fund]] lost approximately $3 billion when the bubble burst, illustrating that even the greatest traders can be caught. The episode reinforced warren-buffett's principle of staying within one's circle of competence and [[nassim-taleb]]'s warnings about the fragility of consensus narratives.

## Trader Takeaways

1. **Price the cash flows, not the story.** Companies with no earnings were valued on "eyeballs" and "page views." When the narrative cracked, there was no fundamental floor.
2. **Survivorship is not the base rate.** Amazon, eBay and Priceline survived and compounded; the median dot-com IPO went to zero. Indexing the survivors after the fact overstates how easy it was to pick winners in 1999.
3. **The Fed sets the clock.** Tightening cycles end speculative manias more reliably than valuation alone.
4. **Recovery can take 15 years.** The nasdaq Composite did not reclaim its March 2000 high until 2015 — a reminder that drawdown duration, not just depth, defines the damage to a buy-and-hold position.
