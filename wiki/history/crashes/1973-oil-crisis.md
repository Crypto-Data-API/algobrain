# 1973 Oil Crisis (OPEC Embargo)

On October 17, 1973, the Organization of Arab Petroleum Exporting Countries (OAPEC — the Arab subset of [[opec]]) imposed an oil embargo against the United States, Canada, Japan, the Netherlands, and the United Kingdom in retaliation for their support of Israel during the Yom Kippur War. The embargo and accompanying production cuts caused [[crude-oil]] prices to quadruple from approximately **$3 to $12 per barrel** in a matter of months, triggering the first modern energy crisis and reshaping global energy policy for decades. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Impact

### Economic Consequences

- **Oil prices**: Quadrupled from ~$3 to ~$12/barrel (a 300% increase in under six months)
- **U.S. inflation**: CPI inflation jumped from 3.4% (1972) to 12.3% (1974)
- **Recession**: The U.S. and most developed economies entered recession; U.S. GDP fell 3.2% (1973-1975)
- **Stock market**: The Dow Jones fell approximately 45% from January 1973 to December 1974
- **Stagflation**: The combination of rising inflation and economic contraction created "stagflation" — a previously theoretical phenomenon that confounded Keynesian economics
- **Global GDP**: Estimated $350 billion in economic losses across OECD nations (in 1970s dollars)

### Visible Effects

- Long lines at gas stations across the United States, sometimes stretching for miles
- Odd/even rationing: Cars could buy gas only on days matching the last digit of their license plate
- Speed limits reduced to 55 mph (National Maximum Speed Law, 1974)
- Daylight saving time extended year-round (1974-1975)
- Nixon's "Project Independence" — declared goal of U.S. energy self-sufficiency by 1980 (not achieved)

### Structural Changes

The 1973 crisis permanently altered global energy markets and geopolitics:

1. **International Energy Agency (IEA)**: Created in 1974 as a counterweight to OPEC, coordinating strategic petroleum reserves and emergency response among oil-consuming nations
2. **U.S. Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR)**: Established in 1975 (Energy Policy and Conservation Act), eventually growing to ~700 million barrels — the world's largest emergency oil stockpile
3. **Energy conservation**: Corporate Average Fuel Economy (CAFE) standards for automobiles (1975), building insulation codes, industrial energy efficiency programs
4. **Diversification of supply**: Accelerated development of North Sea oil (UK, Norway), Alaskan North Slope (Trans-Alaska Pipeline, completed 1977), and non-OPEC producers
5. **Nuclear power expansion**: Major expansion of nuclear power programs in France, Japan, and other import-dependent nations
6. **Petrodollar system**: OPEC revenues recycled into Western financial markets and U.S. Treasury debt, reinforcing the dollar's role as global reserve currency

## Lessons for Traders

- **[[geopolitical-risk-premium]]** can dwarf fundamental supply-demand dynamics — political decisions moved prices 4x in months
- **Supply concentration risk**: Dependence on a concentrated group of suppliers creates systemic vulnerability
- **Commodity supercycles**: The 1973 crisis initiated a commodity supercycle that lasted through the late 1970s
- **Regime change in markets**: Pre-1973, oil was stable and cheap; post-1973, oil became volatile and geopolitically charged. Markets that appear stable can undergo permanent regime shifts.
- **Inflation transmission**: Energy price shocks transmit rapidly through the entire economy — energy is an input to everything

## Second Oil Shock (1979)

The Iranian Revolution and subsequent Iran-Iraq War caused a second oil shock in 1979-1980, with prices rising from ~$14 to ~$40/barrel. Combined with the 1973 experience, this decade of oil shocks permanently established energy security as a core national security concern for oil-importing nations.
