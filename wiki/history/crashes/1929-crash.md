# Wall Street Crash of 1929

The Wall Street Crash of 1929 was the most devastating stock market collapse in American history, ending the speculative mania of the Roaring Twenties and ushering in the [[great-depression]]. The crash unfolded over several days in late October 1929, destroying tens of billions of dollars in nominal wealth and fundamentally reshaping U.S. financial regulation. The Dow Jones Industrial Average ultimately fell roughly **89% from its September 1929 peak (381.17) to its July 1932 trough (41.22)** — the deepest drawdown in the index's history — and did not recover its pre-crash level until **November 1954**, twenty-five years later.

## Background

Throughout the 1920s, U.S. stock prices soared on the back of post-war prosperity, the spread of new technologies (autos, radio, electrification), easy credit, and a culture of mass speculation. The Dow rose roughly fivefold between 1921 and its September 1929 peak. Two structural features made the market dangerously fragile:

- **Buying on margin.** Investors could buy stocks with as little as **10% down**, borrowing the remaining 90% from brokers ("call loans"). [[margin]] debt ballooned to roughly $8.5 billion by late 1929 — more than the total currency in circulation. This [[leverage]] meant a 10% price decline could wipe out an investor's entire equity and trigger forced selling.
- **Investment trusts and pyramiding.** Highly leveraged investment trusts (early closed-end funds) and holding-company pyramids amplified gains on the way up and losses on the way down.

The Federal Reserve had raised the discount rate to 6% in August 1929 in an attempt to curb speculation, tightening credit just as the [[bubble]] reached its peak.

| Mid-November 1929 | Dow down ~48% from its September peak (to ~198) |
| July 8, 1932 | Dow bottoms at **41.22**, down ~89% from peak |
| November 23, 1954 | Dow finally recaptures its 1929 high |

## Why It Happened

The crash was not caused by a single trigger but by the collapse of a leveraged speculative bubble:

1. **Margin call cascade.** As prices fell, brokers issued margin calls. Investors who could not meet them were sold out at market, adding supply, pushing prices lower, triggering more margin calls — a self-reinforcing [[deleveraging]] spiral identical in mechanism (if not in technology) to modern liquidation cascades.
2. **Stretched valuations and slowing economy.** Industrial production had already peaked in mid-1929; the real economy was softening even as stock prices climbed.
3. **No circuit breakers, no deposit insurance, no lender-of-last-resort doctrine.** Unlike 1987 or 2008, there was no institutional backstop. The Fed, constrained by gold-standard orthodoxy and internal disagreement, allowed the money supply to contract sharply (Friedman & Schwartz's central critique).

## Aftermath

The crash itself did not *cause* the Great Depression on its own, but it was the catalyst that interacted with monetary contraction, bank failures, and the gold standard to produce a decade-long slump:

- **Bank failures.** Roughly 9,000 U.S. banks failed between 1930 and 1933 as depositors ran and loan losses mounted.
- **Wealth destruction and unemployment.** U.S. unemployment reached ~25% by 1933; real GDP fell roughly 30% from 1929 to 1933.
- **Regulatory revolution.** Congress responded with landmark legislation:
  - **Securities Act of 1933** — disclosure requirements for new securities issues
  - **Securities Exchange Act of 1934** — created the **[[sec|Securities and Exchange Commission]]**, regulated secondary trading, and gave the Fed authority to set margin requirements (Regulation T)
  - **Glass-Steagall Act (1933)** — separated commercial and investment banking and created the FDIC deposit insurance

## Lessons for Traders

- **Leverage amplifies both directions.** 10%-margin buying turned a sharp correction into a wealth-destroying cascade. Every subsequent crash — [[black-monday|1987]], [[long-term-capital-management|LTCM 1998]], [[2008-global-financial-crisis|2008]], [[2025-10-crypto-liquidation-cascade|crypto 2025]] — repeats the deleveraging-cascade pattern.
- **Recovery time can be brutally long.** The 25-year nominal recovery (and the ~89% drawdown) is the canonical reminder that "buy the dip" assumes survival; an over-leveraged buyer in 1929 did not get the chance to be vindicated in 1954.
- **Policy response matters as much as the crash.** The depth of the Depression is widely attributed to monetary contraction, not the crash itself — the inverse of 1987, where decisive Fed liquidity prevented a recession.
- **Bubbles look like prosperity from the inside.** The mania of 1928-29 was rationalised as a "new era" of permanent prosperity — the same narrative that recurs in every bubble.
