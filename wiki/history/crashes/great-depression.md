# Great Depression

The Great Depression (1929-1939) was the longest and most severe economic downturn in modern history, triggered by the [[1929-crash|stock market crash of 1929]] and deepened by cascading bank failures, deflationary monetary policy, and protectionist trade policies. It reshaped the role of government in economic management and remains the benchmark against which all subsequent crises are measured.

## Scale of the Crisis

- U.S. unemployment peaked at approximately 25% in 1933.
- U.S. GDP fell roughly 30% between 1929 and 1933.
- Over 9,000 banks failed between 1930 and 1933, wiping out depositors' savings.
- Global trade collapsed by approximately 65% as countries erected tariff barriers (notably the Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act of 1930).
- The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 89% from its 1929 peak to its 1932 trough — it did not reclaim the 1929 high until **1954**.

## Causes → Consequences

The Depression resulted from a convergence of reinforcing factors. The clearest way to read it is as a chain in which each cause produced a consequence that became the next cause:

| Cause | Mechanism | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Speculative [[bubble]] + margin [[leverage]] (1920s) | Stocks bought on 10% margin; credit expansion | Crash wiped out leveraged investors and the brokers who financed them |
| [[1929-crash]] | Wealth destruction, collapse in confidence | Spending and investment froze |
| Banking panics ([[bank-run]]) | Depositors rush to withdraw; banks call loans and fail | Money supply contracted ~⅓; credit vanished |
| Federal Reserve passivity | Fed failed to act as **lender of last resort** | An ordinary recession became a depression |
| [[deflation]] | Falling prices raise the real value of debt | Debtors defaulted; deleveraging spiral self-reinforced |
| Smoot-Hawley + gold standard | Tariffs and rigid currency pegs | International contagion; global trade collapse |

The single most-cited analytical point: the **crash did not have to become the Depression**. The policy errors that followed — monetary contraction, the absence of deposit insurance, and protectionism — converted a severe market break into a decade-long catastrophe.

## Recovery and Legacy

Recovery began with Franklin Roosevelt's New Deal programs (1933 onward). The institutional architecture built in response still governs markets today:

- **FDIC deposit insurance** (1933, via [[glass-steagall|Glass-Steagall]]) — ended the dynamic where rumor alone could destroy a solvent bank through a [[bank-run]].
- **Glass-Steagall separation** of commercial and investment banking (1933) — repealed in 1999, a repeal later blamed by some for the [[2008-global-financial-crisis]].
- **The SEC** (1934) — federal disclosure and anti-manipulation regulation.
- **Social Security** (1935) — automatic stabilizer for consumer demand.

Full recovery did not arrive until wartime mobilization in the early 1940s.

## Why It Matters for Traders

The Great Depression established the intellectual foundation for modern [[central-bank]] intervention, deposit insurance, and financial regulation. Every major policy response since -- from [[quantitative-easing]] to [[too-big-to-fail]] bailouts during the [[2008-global-financial-crisis]] -- has been shaped by the determination to prevent a repeat. Understanding the Depression is essential context for evaluating government responses to market crises.

## Crash vs. Depression

The [[1929-crash]] (the October 1929 stock-market collapse, Black Thursday through Black Tuesday — see [[stock-market-crash-of-1929]]) was the *trigger event*; the Great Depression was the *decade-long economic downturn* that followed. The crash alone did not cause the Depression — the policy errors that followed did. Milton Friedman and Anna Schwartz's *A Monetary History of the United States* argued that the [[federal-reserve|Federal Reserve]]'s failure to act as lender of last resort, allowing the money supply to contract by roughly a third, turned an ordinary recession into a catastrophe. Ben Bernanke (a Depression scholar before becoming Fed chair) explicitly invoked this lesson when designing the aggressive 2008-2009 response — the direct intellectual link between the two crises.

## Lessons for Traders

1. **Policy response shapes the outcome more than the initial shock.** The same equity crash with a 2008-style [[central-bank]] response would not have produced a 25%-unemployment, 89%-drawdown decade. When evaluating a crisis, watch the policy reaction function as closely as the headline shock.
2. **[[deflation|Deflation]] is the tail risk that breaks leveraged longs.** Falling prices raise the real burden of debt; deleveraging feeds on itself. This is why modern central banks fear deflation more than moderate inflation — and why [[leverage]] is most dangerous precisely when asset prices are falling.
3. **An 89% drawdown takes 25 years to recover.** The Dow did not reclaim its 1929 peak until 1954. A 50% loss requires a 100% gain to break even; an 89% loss requires an ~810% gain. Depth and duration of drawdown, not just the crash, define the damage — the core argument for capital preservation and [[position-sizing|sizing]] that survives the worst case.
4. **Bank-run dynamics are reflexive and self-fulfilling.** A [[bank-run]] can destroy a solvent institution because the fear of failure causes the failure. The institutional fix — deposit insurance under [[glass-steagall|Glass-Steagall]] — worked by breaking that feedback loop, not by making banks individually stronger.
5. **Protectionism amplifies a downturn.** Smoot-Hawley and the resulting trade war turned a domestic crisis into a global one. Trade and capital-flow policy is a macro risk factor, not background noise.
6. **The crowd that bought the 1929 top "couldn't lose" was using the same logic as every bubble since.** Margin-fueled certainty in a new era is the recurring antecedent of a [[bubble]] top.
