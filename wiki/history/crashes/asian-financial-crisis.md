# Asian Financial Crisis

The Asian Financial Crisis of 1997-1998 was a severe financial and economic meltdown that swept through East and Southeast Asia, beginning with the collapse of the Thai baht on **July 2, 1997**. The crisis exposed the fragility of pegged exchange rates, excessive foreign-currency borrowing, and crony capitalism across the region. It is one of the most-studied examples of **financial contagion** — the rapid spread of distress across borders — and a foundational case study in [[global-macro]] currency trading.

## Origins

Through the early-to-mid 1990s, Thailand, South Korea, Indonesia, Malaysia, and the Philippines attracted massive foreign capital inflows, fuelled by high growth ("the Asian Miracle"), liberalised capital accounts, and currency pegs to the U.S. dollar. The pegs created a dangerous illusion of stability:

- **Currency mismatch.** Banks and corporations borrowed cheaply in dollars and yen but invested in local-currency assets (real estate, infrastructure, equities). A stable peg made this look free of FX risk.
- **Maturity mismatch.** Much of the foreign borrowing was short-term, while the investments were long-term and illiquid.
- **Asset bubbles.** Capital flooded into property and stock markets, inflating a regional [[bubble]]. By 1996 export growth was slowing, current-account deficits were widening, and non-performing loans were rising.

When the U.S. dollar strengthened against the yen in 1996-97, the dollar-pegged Asian currencies became overvalued, exports lost competitiveness, and speculators began testing the pegs.

## Timeline

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| May-June 1997 | Speculators attack the Thai baht; the Bank of Thailand burns most of its FX reserves defending the peg |
| **July 2, 1997** | Thailand floats the baht after exhausting reserves; the baht falls ~20% immediately, eventually losing **>50%** of its value |
| July-August 1997 | Contagion spreads to the Philippine peso, Malaysian ringgit, and Indonesian rupiah |
| August-October 1997 | Indonesian rupiah collapses (eventually down ~80%); regional equity markets crash |
| October 1997 | Hong Kong defends its dollar peg by spiking interbank rates; the Hang Seng falls sharply; spillover hits global markets (the Dow fell ~7% on Oct 27, 1997) |
| November 1997 | South Korea's won collapses; Korea accepts a **~$58 billion IMF bailout** — the largest in history at that time |
| January 1998 | Indonesia's economy implodes amid IMF austerity; food riots erupt |
| May 1998 | President Suharto resigns after 31 years amid the economic and political crisis |
| September 1998 | Malaysia imposes **capital controls** under PM Mahathir, rejecting the IMF model; the crisis contributes to the Russian default (Aug 1998) and the [[long-term-capital-management|LTCM]] collapse |

## Why It Happened

1. **Pegs that masked risk.** Fixed exchange rates suppressed visible FX volatility, encouraging unhedged foreign borrowing. When the peg broke, the currency mismatch turned into instant insolvency for borrowers.
2. **Self-fulfilling speculative attack.** Once reserves were visibly depleted, speculators (and then everyone) front-ran the inevitable devaluation — a classic [[currency-peg|second-generation currency-crisis]] dynamic.
3. **Contagion via correlation and leverage.** Investors who lost money in Thailand sold across the region to raise cash and reduce risk, transmitting the shock to economies with similar (or merely *perceived* similar) vulnerabilities — even relatively sound ones.
4. **IMF conditionality controversy.** IMF bailouts came with austerity and high interest rates that many economists (notably Joseph Stiglitz) later argued deepened the contractions rather than stabilising them.

## Impact

- **Currencies:** baht -50%+, rupiah ~-80%, won ~-50%, ringgit and peso sharp declines
- **Equities:** regional stock markets fell 40-60%
- **GDP (1998):** Indonesia ~-13%, Thailand ~-10%, South Korea ~-6%, Malaysia ~-7%
- **Global spillover:** contributed to the 1998 Russian default and the [[long-term-capital-management|LTCM]] blow-up, prompting an emergency Fed-coordinated rescue of LTCM
- **Reforms:** Asian economies subsequently accumulated massive FX reserves as self-insurance — a structural shift that helped fund the 2000s U.S. current-account deficit ("global savings glut")

## Trading Lessons

- **Pegs create asymmetric trades.** A credible currency peg offers limited upside but enormous downside when it breaks — a favourite setup for [[global-macro]] traders. The payoff is convex: bounded loss (the peg holds) versus a 30-80% move (the peg breaks). See [[black-wednesday]] (1992 GBP) for the same template.
- **Reserves are a countdown clock.** Watching a central bank burn FX reserves to defend a peg is watching a deadline approach. When reserves run low, the break is near-certain.
- **Contagion ignores fundamentals.** Sound economies were sold alongside unsound ones simply because they were in the same "bucket." Cross-asset correlation spikes toward 1 in a crisis.
- **Carry trades reverse violently.** The yen [[carry-trade]] and dollar-funded Asian carry unwound abruptly, a pattern that recurred in 2008 and the August 2024 yen-carry unwind.

[[george-soros]] and other [[global-macro]] traders profited from the crisis; Soros was publicly (and unfairly) blamed by Malaysian PM Mahathir Mohamad for "attacking" the ringgit, though the underlying vulnerabilities were domestic.
