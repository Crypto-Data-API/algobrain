# 2020 WTI Negative Oil Price

On April 20, 2020, the May 2020 WTI crude oil futures contract on [[cme-group|NYMEX]] settled at **-$37.63 per barrel** — the first time in history that a major commodity futures contract traded at a negative price. This meant sellers were effectively paying buyers to take physical oil off their hands. The event exposed the physical constraints underlying paper markets and stands as one of the most extraordinary episodes in commodity market history. ## What Caused It

The negative price event resulted from the convergence of three factors:

### 1. COVID-19 Demand Destruction

Global oil demand fell by approximately 30 million barrels per day (from ~100M bpd to ~70M bpd) virtually overnight as countries imposed lockdowns. This was the largest demand shock in petroleum history — orders of magnitude larger than any previous recession.

### 2. OPEC-Russia Price War

In early March, Russia refused Saudi Arabia's proposed production cuts. Saudi Arabia retaliated by flooding the market with discounted crude, adding supply into the worst demand environment in history. While OPEC+ eventually agreed to record cuts on April 12, the damage to near-term supply/demand balance was done.

### 3. Cushing Storage Saturation

WTI futures are physically delivered at Cushing, Oklahoma, which has approximately 90 million barrels of storage capacity. By mid-April, Cushing was over 80% full and filling rapidly. Traders holding expiring May contracts faced a stark reality:

- **Physical delivery required**: If you hold a WTI futures contract at expiration, you must take delivery of 1,000 barrels of crude oil at Cushing
- **No storage available**: With Cushing nearly full, taking delivery meant having nowhere to put the oil
- **Massive carrying costs**: Even if storage could be found, the cost of storage + insurance + financing exceeded the value of the oil itself

This created a **forced seller** dynamic: any speculator still holding May contracts on expiration day had to sell at any price to avoid physical delivery obligations they could not fulfill.

## How the Price Went Negative

The mechanics were straightforward:

1. The May WTI contract was expiring on April 21
2. Any holder at expiration would be obligated to take physical delivery at Cushing
3. With Cushing nearly full, there was effectively no storage capacity for incoming deliveries
4. Traders who had not rolled their positions to June panicked
5. Sellers outnumbered buyers so overwhelmingly that the price went below zero
6. At -$37.63, sellers were paying buyers $37.63 per barrel to take the oil — reflecting the estimated cost of acquiring emergency storage

The CME had modified its systems just days earlier to allow negative pricing (changing from a price floor of $0.01 to allowing negative prices), which enabled the move to occur technically.

## Who Got Hurt

- **Retail investors in USO (United States Oil Fund)**: USO held massive front-month WTI positions; losses were catastrophic. The fund was forced to restructure its portfolio and spread holdings across multiple contract months.
- **Small speculators**: Retail traders on interactive brokers and other platforms who held expiring contracts faced margin calls for more than their account balances (Interactive Brokers absorbed $104 million in client losses)
- **Oil-producing nations**: Countries dependent on oil revenue (Venezuela, Nigeria, Iraq) faced fiscal crises
- **Shale producers**: U.S. shale companies shut in wells and filed for bankruptcy at elevated rates
- **Banks**: Commodity-lending facilities faced collateral problems

## Lessons for Traders

### 1. [[storage-economics]] Can Overwhelm Price

When physical storage constraints bind, the paper market (futures) must adjust to physical market reality. No amount of "fundamental value" matters if there is literally nowhere to put the commodity.

### 2. Futures Markets Can Decouple from "Reality"

The May WTI contract went to -$37.63, but actual oil in the ground still had positive value. The futures price reflected the cost of *taking delivery at a specific location on a specific date* — not the long-term value of crude oil. The June contract was trading around $20 at the same time.

### 3. Position Management Before Expiry Is Critical

The traders who lost money were those still holding the May contract on expiration day. Professional commodity traders routinely roll positions 5-10 days before expiry. The lesson: understand the delivery mechanism of any physically-settled futures contract you trade.

### 4. [[contango]] Can Be Extreme

The May-June WTI spread reached approximately $58/barrel — the largest contango in petroleum history. This [[contango]] signaled the severity of the storage crisis and created extraordinary opportunities for those with access to storage (storage arbitrage: buy cheap near-term oil, sell expensive deferred contracts, store the physical oil in between).

### 5. Exchange Rule Changes Matter

The CME's decision to allow negative pricing (implemented April 15, just days before the crash) was a prerequisite for the event. Without this change, the contract would have been stuck at $0.01 with a massive queue of unfilled sell orders.

## Regulatory and Market Structure Aftermath

- CME implemented enhanced position management requirements for expiring energy contracts
- USO restructured to hold a diversified portfolio of futures months (no longer concentrated in front month)
- Increased focus on physically-settled vs cash-settled contract structures
- Renewed interest in Brent crude (cash-settled, no physical delivery risk) as a benchmark
- Several brokers changed their margin models for commodity futures to account for negative prices
- CFTC investigated whether market manipulation contributed to the price action (no charges filed)
