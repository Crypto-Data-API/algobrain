Market crashes are severe, rapid declines in asset prices that historically have provided the greatest wealth-building opportunities for investors willing to buy during peak fear. A conviction shared by veterans of the [[crash-of-1987]], the [[global-financial-crisis]], and the COVID-19 crash is that buying during crashes is among the most significant wealth makers over a lifetime. The prerequisite for capitalising on crashes is maintaining adequate [[cash-as-asset|cash reserves]] before the crash occurs, which means accepting lower returns during bull markets in exchange for dry powder during panics. See [[crashes-overview]] for a detailed catalogue of historical market crashes and their recoveries.

## Why Crashes Create Opportunity

### Forced Sellers Are Not Making Valuation Judgments

During crashes, most selling is *forced*, not voluntary. [[deleveraging|Forced deleveraging]] — margin calls, fund redemptions, risk limit breaches — means participants sell *whatever they can*, not *what they should*. This creates indiscriminate selling where high-quality assets fall alongside junk. The buyer who purchases during forced selling is acquiring assets from counterparties who don't care about price — only about raising cash.

### Historical Recovery Rates

| Crash | Peak-to-Trough | Time to Recover | 5-Year Return from Bottom |
|-------|---------------|----------------|--------------------------|
| [[1929-crash|1929 Crash]] | -86% (1929-1932) | 25 years (nominal) | +234% |
| [[crash-of-1987|Black Monday 1987]] | -22% (1 day) | 2 years | +102% |
| Dot-com (2000-2002) | -49% | 7 years | +101% |
| [[2008-global-financial-crisis|2008 GFC]] | -57% | 5.5 years | +178% |
| COVID-19 (2020) | -34% | 5 months | +107% |

Every major crash in the history of the US stock market has been fully recovered. The investors who bought at peak fear generated exceptional returns. But the key challenge is having capital to deploy — which requires maintaining [[cash-as-asset|cash reserves]] during the preceding bull market.

## The "Buy the Crash" Framework

### Prerequisites

1. **Cash reserves** — maintain 10-30% of portfolio in cash or short-term bonds during late-cycle markets (when valuations are stretched, leverage is high, and complacency reigns)
2. **Conviction** — buying during a crash means buying when every headline screams catastrophe. This requires having studied prior crashes and understanding that forced selling creates temporary, not permanent, value destruction
3. **Position sizing** — deploy capital in tranches, not all at once. The bottom is unknowable in real time

### What to Buy

- **Broad index exposure** (S&P 500, total market) — the simplest and most reliable approach
- **High-quality companies** trading at fire-sale prices due to indiscriminate selling
- **NOT** failing companies, over-levered sectors, or the specific assets at the epicenter of the crisis (those may not recover)

### When NOT to Buy

- When the crash reflects a genuine structural change (e.g., buying Kodak during the digital camera transition was not a "crash buying" opportunity — it was a permanent impairment)
- When you don't have a multi-year time horizon
- When buying would require leverage (adding leverage during a crash amplifies risk, not opportunity)

## Common Crash Mechanics

Every crash has unique triggers but shares common dynamics:

1. **Leverage buildup** during the preceding boom — [[margin]] debt, leveraged ETFs, shadow lending
2. **Trigger event** — can be anything (Lehman, COVID, rate hikes, geopolitics)
3. **[[deleveraging]] cascade** — forced selling → price decline → more margin calls → more forced selling
4. **[[crowding-risk|Crowded trade]] unwind** — positions that looked diversified turn out to be identical
5. **Liquidity evaporation** — market makers widen spreads or withdraw entirely
6. **Capitulation** — the final, high-volume wave of selling that typically marks the bottom (or near it)
7. **Recovery** — initially driven by short covering and value buyers, then broadening as confidence returns
