# 2022 LME Nickel Squeeze

On March 8, 2022, nickel prices on the [[london-metal-exchange|London Metal Exchange (LME)]] more than doubled in a single session, rising from approximately $30,000/tonne to over $100,000/tonne — a move unprecedented in the 145-year history of the LME. The squeeze was triggered by the Russia-Ukraine conflict, centered on one massive short position, and culminated in the LME's controversial decision to cancel billions of dollars in executed trades.

## Background

Chinese tycoon Xiang Guangda, founder of Tsingshan Holding Group (the world's largest nickel and stainless steel producer), had built a massive short position in [[nickel]] futures on the LME. Tsingshan is a physical nickel producer — the short position was ostensibly a hedge against its physical inventory. However, the size of the position far exceeded normal hedging requirements, effectively becoming a speculative bet on lower nickel prices.

## The Mechanics

The squeeze was a classic example of a **negative convexity short position** in a physical commodity market:

1. **Russia supply shock**: Russia's invasion of Ukraine created genuine concern about disruption to Russian nickel exports (~10% of global supply, but a much larger share of LME-deliverable Class 1 nickel)
2. **Concentrated short**: Tsingshan's short position was enormous relative to LME open interest and deliverable stocks
3. **Margin spiral**: As prices rose, Tsingshan faced margin calls it could not immediately meet, while other shorts also scrambled to cover
4. **Thin liquidity**: LME nickel is not a deeply liquid market. The concentrated buying (short covering) overwhelmed available sellers
5. **Reflexive loop**: Rising prices triggered more margin calls, forced more covering, pushed prices higher — a textbook short squeeze feedback loop

## The Controversy

The LME's decision to cancel executed trades was extraordinary and deeply controversial:

- **Winners wiped out**: Traders who had bought nickel (or were long) during the March 8 session had their profitable trades cancelled. Some had taken large positions specifically to profit from the squeeze.
- **Protecting Tsingshan**: Critics alleged that the LME effectively bailed out Tsingshan — its largest market participant — at the expense of other traders. The LME is owned by Hong Kong Exchanges and Clearing (HKEX), and Tsingshan is a major Chinese company, fueling allegations of political influence.
- **Legal challenges**: Multiple hedge funds and trading firms filed lawsuits against the LME, arguing the trade cancellations were unlawful. Elliott Management and Jane Street were among the firms that sued.
- **Court ruling (2023)**: A UK High Court ruled in November 2023 that the LME's decision to cancel trades was lawful, though the court acknowledged it was "highly unusual."
- **Loss of confidence**: The episode damaged the LME's credibility as a neutral marketplace. Some market participants shifted nickel trading to CME and other venues.

## Regulatory Aftermath

- The UK Financial Conduct Authority (FCA) and Bank of England launched reviews of LME's risk management
- LME introduced daily price limits for all metals
- LME proposed enhanced position reporting and concentration limits
- Broader debate about whether OTC commodity derivatives (which are less transparent than exchange-traded contracts) contributed to the buildup of concentrated risk

## Lessons for Traders

- **Concentrated short positions in physical commodity markets carry existential risk**: Unlike financial assets, physical commodities have finite deliverable supply. A short squeeze in a physical market can be more violent and harder to escape than in equities.
- **Exchange intervention is unpredictable**: The LME's decision to cancel trades shows that exchange rules can be applied retroactively in extreme situations. See also [[hunt-brothers-silver-corner]] for a historical parallel.
- **Geopolitical events can trigger cascading market failures**: The Russia-Ukraine conflict was the catalyst, but the underlying vulnerability was Tsingshan's concentrated short.
- **Counterparty and clearing risk are real**: The near-failure of a major market participant threatened the entire LME clearing system.
- **Market structure matters**: The LME's unique market structure (date-based rather than monthly contracts, phone-based "ring" trading, limited transparency) contributed to the opacity that allowed the position to grow unchecked.
