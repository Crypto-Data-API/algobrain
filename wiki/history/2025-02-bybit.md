The Bybit hack of **February 21, 2025** was the largest cryptocurrency theft in history: North Korea's [[lazarus-group|Lazarus Group]] drained approximately **$1.46 billion** of Ethereum-based assets from a [[bybit]] cold wallet during a routine transfer. The attack did not exploit a smart-contract bug or break Bybit's own systems — instead, the attackers compromised the signing interface of the multisig wallet provider (Safe, formerly Gnosis Safe), tricking Bybit's signers into approving a transaction that handed control of the cold wallet to the attacker. It is the canonical modern example of a **front-end / supply-chain attack** rather than an on-chain exploit, and a landmark case in [[counterparty-risk|exchange counterparty risk]].

## What Happened

On February 21, 2025, Bybit was executing a scheduled transfer of ETH from one of its Ethereum multisig **cold wallets** to a warm wallet. The wallet was managed through Safe, a widely used multisignature smart-contract wallet. The attackers had compromised the Safe front-end / signing flow so that what the Bybit signers saw in their interface (a benign, expected transfer) did not match the transaction they were actually authorizing.

The malicious transaction was a **`delegatecall` that replaced the wallet's implementation logic**, effectively transferring control of the cold wallet's contract to the attacker. Once the signers approved it, the attacker drained:

- ~**401,000+ ETH** and large amounts of staked-ETH derivatives (stETH, mETH, cmETH and related liquid-staking tokens)
- Total value approximately **$1.46 billion** at prices on the day of the theft

The funds were then rapidly laundered: swapped out of staking derivatives into native ETH, dispersed across thousands of intermediary wallets, and funneled through mixers and cross-chain bridges. On-chain investigators (Arkham, ZachXBT, Chainalysis, Elliptic) attributed the attack to Lazarus / TraderTraitor within hours based on wallet-clustering and behavioral fingerprints matching prior DPRK operations.

## Mechanism / Why It Happened

The defining feature of the Bybit hack is that **nothing on Bybit's exchange infrastructure was technically "broken."** The vulnerability lived in the operational seam between human signers and the wallet front-end:

1. **Front-end / UI compromise** — the Safe signing interface displayed a transaction different from the one being signed (a "blind signing" / what-you-see-is-not-what-you-sign failure). Signers physically confirming on hardware wallets cannot meaningfully verify a complex `delegatecall` payload.
2. **`delegatecall` privilege escalation** — instead of moving funds directly, the malicious payload swapped the wallet's logic contract, giving the attacker full control without needing every signature for every subsequent drain.
3. **Supply-chain leverage** — by attacking the wallet tooling rather than the exchange, the attackers bypassed Bybit's own (substantial) security controls entirely. This is the same template as Badger DAO (Cloudflare front-end injection, $120M) and Radiant Capital (hardware-wallet malware + UI spoof, $53M).

See [[smart-contract-vulnerability-taxonomy]] for the broader classification of `delegatecall` and front-end attack vectors.

## Market Impact

- **ETH price** fell roughly 6-8% in the 24 hours after the disclosure, amplified by fears the attacker would dump 400k+ ETH onto the market.
- **Bybit deposit/withdrawal surge** — Bybit faced a partial bank-run dynamic, processing a reported ~350,000+ withdrawal requests as users tested solvency.
- **Bybit remained solvent.** CEO Ben Zhou stated within hours that Bybit would cover the loss, that all other cold wallets were secure, and that withdrawals would be honored 1:1. Bybit secured bridge loans and OTC ETH purchases (reportedly ~$700M+ acquired within days) to replenish reserves, and published a Merkle-tree proof-of-reserves update. Withdrawals were fully restored within ~72 hours.
- **Liquid-staking depeg pressure** — the forced unwinding of stolen stETH/mETH created transient depeg/discount pressure in those markets, a tradeable dislocation of the type catalogued in [[post-hack-incident-response-arb]].

## Aftermath

- **Bounty / recovery campaign** — Bybit launched a bounty portal offering up to 10% of recovered funds; a small fraction was frozen by cooperating exchanges and stablecoin issuers (Tether froze USDT touched by the laundering chain). The overwhelming majority of the ETH was successfully laundered by Lazarus.
- **Attribution to DPRK** — the FBI formally attributed the theft to North Korea ("TraderTraitor"), consistent with the regime's use of crypto theft to fund weapons programs. Lazarus is also tied to the Ronin Bridge ($625M, 2022), DMM Bitcoin ($305M, 2024), and WazirX ($230M, 2024).
- **Industry response** — the hack drove a sharp re-examination of multisig signing hygiene: hardware-enforced transaction simulation, "clear signing" standards (so signers see decoded human-readable transactions), and reduced reliance on blind hardware-wallet confirmation.

## Trading Lessons

1. **Exchange risk is operational, not just solvency.** Bybit was solvent and well-run, yet still lost $1.46B. [[counterparty-risk]] includes the security of the custody stack, not just the balance sheet.
2. **Front-end and tooling are the soft underbelly.** As smart contracts hardened, attackers moved up the stack to UIs, signing flows, and supply chains. The most expensive 2024-2025 hacks were operational compromises, not Solidity bugs.
3. **Blind signing is a structural hazard.** Approving transactions you cannot fully read is the recurring failure mode (Bybit, Radiant, WazirX). Trust the decoded payload, not the dashboard.
4. **Post-hack dislocations are tradeable but dangerous.** Stolen-asset overhang (here, 400k ETH) and staking-derivative depegs create short-lived, high-variance opportunities — see [[post-hack-incident-response-arb]].
5. **A credible, fast solvency response can stop a run.** Bybit's immediate guarantee plus visible reserve replenishment prevented an FTX-style collapse despite a loss larger than FTX's hole.
