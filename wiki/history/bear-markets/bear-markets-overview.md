# Bear Markets

Extended market downturns, recessions, and periods of declining prices.

A **bear market** is conventionally defined as a decline of **20% or more** from a recent high in a broad index, sustained over weeks or months (distinct from a [[market-crashes|crash]], which is a sudden violent drop, and a *correction*, which is a 10-20% pullback). Bear markets test discipline and [[risk-management]]: they feature declining confidence, **rising cross-asset correlations** (diversification fails when it is most needed), [[liquidity]] withdrawal, and a final **capitulation** phase of high-volume forced selling that often marks the low.

## Anatomy of a Bear Market

| Phase | Behaviour |
|---|---|
| **Distribution / topping** | Breadth narrows; leadership concentrates in a few names; smart money sells into strength |
| **Initial decline** | First 10-20% drop; "buy the dip" still works briefly and traps dip-buyers |
| **Capitulation** | Forced [[deleveraging]], margin calls, panic; correlations spike to ~1; the low is usually made here on max volume and max fear |
| **Bottoming / accumulation** | Volatility high but lower lows stop; value buyers and short-covering provide a floor |
| **Recovery** | New [[bull-markets-overview|bull market]] begins; the first leg up is often the most violent (bear-market rallies and the real bottom look alike in real time) |

A characteristic trap is the **bear-market rally** (a sharp counter-trend bounce of 10-20%+ inside a downtrend) — every major bear had several, and each drew in buyers before resuming lower.

## Catalogue of Major Bear Markets

| Bear market | Peak-to-trough | Duration | Trigger |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Great Depression (1929-1932)** | S&P precursor -86% | ~3 years | 1929 crash, banking collapse, deflation |
| **1973-1974** | S&P 500 -48% | ~2 years | Oil embargo, stagflation, Bretton Woods end |
| **Dot-com (2000-2002)** | S&P 500 -49%, Nasdaq -78% | ~2.5 years | Tech valuation bust; see [[bull-markets-overview]] 1990s rally |
| **[[global-financial-crisis\|GFC]] (2007-2009)** | S&P 500 -57% | ~1.5 years | Subprime, leverage, Lehman; deepest post-war bear |
| **[[covid-crash\|COVID (2020)]]** | S&P 500 -34% | 23 sessions | Pandemic shutdown; fastest 30%+ drop, fastest recovery |
| **2022 bear** | S&P 500 -25%, Nasdaq -35% | ~10 months | Inflation, Fed hiking from zero, valuation reset |
| **Crypto winters** | BTC -80% to -85% (2014, 2018, 2022) | 1-2 years each | Bubble unwinds; see [[crypto-winter]] |

Crypto bear markets ("[[crypto-winter|crypto winters]]") are far deeper than equity bears — Bitcoin routinely draws down 80-85% from cycle highs, and altcoins frequently fall 90-99%. The 2022 winter combined a macro tightening cycle with idiosyncratic crypto blow-ups ([[terra-luna]], [[three-arrows-capital]], [[ftx]]).

## Trading and Defensive Playbook

- **Reduce gross exposure and leverage early** — the surest way to survive a bear is to enter it with dry powder ([[cash-as-asset|cash as an asset]]).
- **Hedge** with index puts, [[long-vol-vs-short-vol|long-vol overlays]], or inverse products — cheapest before volatility spikes.
- **Expect correlations to converge** — "diversified" books can all fall together in capitulation; the only reliable diversifiers are cash, long [[volatility]], and (sometimes) trend-following [[crisis-alpha]].
- **The short side carries its own risks** — squeezes and bear rallies punish over-leveraged shorts; size accordingly.
- **Crashes are buying opportunities for the prepared** — every US equity bear has fully recovered; see [[market-crashes]]. The constraint is having capital and conviction at the low.
