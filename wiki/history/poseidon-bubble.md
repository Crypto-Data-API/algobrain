The Poseidon bubble was a speculative mania in Australian mining stocks in 1969-1970, triggered by a nickel discovery at Windarra, Western Australia by Poseidon NL. The stock price surged from around $0.80 to over $280 before collapsing, taking much of the Australian speculative mining sector with it. Often described as Australia's equivalent of the South Sea Bubble, the Poseidon episode remains one of the most extreme examples of speculative excess in modern market [[history]] and a formative event in the development of Australian securities regulation.

## The Nickel Discovery

In September 1969, Poseidon NL — a small, obscure exploration company — announced the discovery of high-grade [[nickel]] sulfide mineralization at Windarra in the Mount Margaret region of Western Australia. The discovery came at a time when global nickel prices were elevated due to a prolonged strike at Inco's Sudbury mines in Canada, which had constrained supply of a metal essential for stainless steel production. The timing was perfect for speculation: a genuine supply shortage combined with a dramatic discovery story created the narrative fuel for a mania.

Poseidon's share price began its ascent immediately. From around $0.80 before the announcement, the stock rose to $12 by October 1969, then to $112 by January 1970, and peaked at approximately $280 in February 1970. At its peak valuation, Poseidon was priced as though it had discovered one of the largest nickel deposits in the world — despite the fact that only limited drilling had been completed and the full extent of the orebody was not yet known.

## The Broader Mania

The Poseidon discovery ignited speculation across the entire Australian mining sector. Hundreds of exploration companies saw their share prices surge on the slimmest of connections to nickel or Western Australian mining. Companies with no significant assets, no revenue, and no realistic prospect of a commercially viable discovery traded at wildly inflated prices. The Sydney and Melbourne stock exchanges (which would later merge to form the [[asx-limited|ASX]]) were overwhelmed by trading volumes, with settlement backlogs and administrative chaos. Retail investors who had never previously traded shares opened brokerage accounts to participate, drawn by media coverage of extraordinary overnight gains.

The mania was not limited to mining stocks. Banks, brokerages, and property companies all rose as speculative enthusiasm spilled across the broader market. Margin lending expanded as investors borrowed to increase their exposure. The classic hallmarks of a [[behavioral-finance|bubble]] were all present: a compelling narrative (Australia as a resource powerhouse), a genuine catalyst (real nickel at Windarra), extrapolation of early gains into unlimited future returns, and the social proof of seeing friends and colleagues getting rich.

## The Collapse

The bubble burst in early 1970 and the unwinding was brutal. Poseidon's share price collapsed from $280 to under $10 within months. Subsequent drilling at Windarra revealed that while the nickel deposit was real, it was far smaller and lower-grade than the market had priced in. Poseidon eventually developed a mine at the site, but it was never particularly profitable and was plagued by technical and economic challenges. The company was eventually delisted.

The broader market carnage was severe. The All Ordinaries index (the predecessor to the ASX 200) declined sharply, wiping out enormous amounts of retail investor wealth. Many of the speculative mining stocks that had risen on no fundamental basis whatsoever went to zero. Investors who had borrowed to buy — a common behavior during the mania — lost more than their initial investment. Brokerage firms that had extended credit liberally found themselves with uncollectable debts.

## Regulatory Aftermath

The Poseidon bubble exposed serious deficiencies in Australian securities regulation and market structure. At the time, securities regulation was fragmented across state-based exchanges with inconsistent rules. The crash accelerated reform efforts that eventually led to the establishment of national securities regulation, culminating in the creation of the Australian Securities Commission (later the Australian Securities and Investments Commission, ASIC) and the consolidation of state exchanges into the [[asx-limited|Australian Stock Exchange]] in 1987. Disclosure requirements for mining companies were tightened, and rules around continuous disclosure — requiring companies to promptly inform the market of material information — were strengthened.

## Key Lessons (per Fred)

[[fred-mcnaught|Fred McNaught]] references this event as a cautionary tale about speculative manias and the disconnect between valuations and reality. It exemplifies his principle that "in every boom, a dominant theme emerges that provides scope for valuations to disengage with reality — and they are always false."

- Speculative manias are recurrent and follow similar patterns
- The dominant narrative always sounds compelling at the time
- Prices overshoot to excessive levels because people cannot be prevented from making excessively optimistic assumptions
- The pattern repeats: dot-com boom, various commodity booms, crypto manias

## How Traders Use This (Lessons for Today)

The Poseidon episode is a template for recognising and surviving resource/narrative bubbles, and the same playbook applies to subsequent [[market-bubbles|bubbles]] (dot-com, the 2017/2021 crypto manias, and individual [[meme-stocks|meme-stock]] runs):

- **A real catalyst is the most dangerous kind.** Windarra nickel *was* real — that genuine kernel of truth is what let valuations "disengage with reality." The most seductive bubbles are not pure fraud; they are real stories extrapolated to absurdity.
- **Watch the breadth of the mania, not just the leader.** The tell was not Poseidon alone but hundreds of assetless shells rising on a "nickel connection." When low-quality names with no fundamentals rally in sympathy, the move is being driven by [[behavioral-finance|crowd psychology]], not analysis.
- **Leverage is the wealth-destroyer.** Investors who borrowed (margin lending) lost *more than their stake* on the way down. In a bubble, position sizing and avoidance of leverage matter more than being right about the long-term story.
- **Liquidity vanishes on the exit.** Settlement backlogs and chaos on the way up became a stampede with no buyers on the way down — exits are crowded precisely when you need them.

## Pitfalls and Risks Illustrated

- **No fundamental floor once the narrative breaks** — Poseidon fell from ~A$280 to under A$10; many peer shells went to zero.
- **Information asymmetry** — limited drilling meant the market priced a deposit whose size and grade were unknown; the eventual mine was never very profitable and Poseidon was delisted.
- **Regulatory risk is a *lagging* protection** — disclosure rules and national regulation (→ ASC/ASIC) were tightened *after* the wealth was already destroyed, not before.
