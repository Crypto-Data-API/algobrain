The Global Financial Crisis (GFC) was the worst US financial crisis since the Great Depression. From its **October 9 2007** SPX peak of 1565.15 to its **March 9 2009** SPX intraday low of 666.79, the S&P 500 fell **-57.4%** over 17 months. The defining event was the **Lehman Brothers bankruptcy on September 15 2008**, which triggered a global cross-asset deleveraging cascade and the most rapid escalation of central-bank and fiscal interventions in postwar history. The GFC remains the largest peak-to-trough US equity drawdown of the modern era and is one of the formative reference events in [[long-vol-vs-short-vol|long-vol versus short-vol]] book performance, alongside [[black-monday|1987]] and [[covid-crash|2020]].

## Overview

The crisis had a long fundamental fuse and a short violent climax. From 2003 to 2007, US household leverage rose sharply on a credit boom in subprime and Alt-A mortgages, securitised into mortgage-backed securities (MBS), repackaged into collateralised debt obligations (CDOs), and distributed globally via the structured-credit complex. Bank-balance-sheet leverage (especially at investment banks) rose to 30:1 or higher; the [[credit-default-swap|CDS]] market expanded to a notional ~$60 trillion by mid-2008. When US house prices peaked in 2006 and began falling, the underlying default assumptions of subprime MBS broke and the value of the structured-credit pyramid collapsed. The crisis ran in stages from the first cracks in early 2007 through the climactic collapse of September-October 2008, the deepening recession of late 2008-early 2009, and the eventual stabilisation in spring 2009.

## What Happened

**February 2007.** HSBC issues the first major subprime warning, takes a $10.5B writedown. New Century Financial files Chapter 11 in April.

**June-August 2007.** Two Bear Stearns hedge funds (the "High-Grade Structured Credit Strategies Enhanced Leverage Fund") collapse on subprime exposure. BNP Paribas freezes redemptions on three funds on August 9. The European Central Bank injects €95B of overnight liquidity. Money-market spreads (LIBOR-OIS) blow out -- the start of the structural funding stress.

**October 9 2007.** SPX makes all-time closing high at 1565.15. VIX 16.

**January 2008.** Société Générale unwinds rogue-trader Jérôme Kerviel positions in extreme volatility; the Fed cuts 75bp inter-meeting on January 22.

**March 16 2008.** Bear Stearns collapses; Fed-brokered acquisition by JPMorgan at $2/share (later raised to $10), backed by a $29B Fed loan.

**Summer 2008.** Oil peaks above $147/barrel in July. Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac come under stress; placed in conservatorship on September 7.

**Monday, September 15 2008.**
- **Lehman Brothers files for Chapter 11 bankruptcy** at 1:45am ET, with $639B in assets -- the largest bankruptcy in US history.
- Bank of America announces purchase of Merrill Lynch over the weekend.
- AIG -- the largest insurer in the world, having sold roughly $440B of CDS protection on subprime CDOs -- comes under acute liquidity stress.
- SPX falls -4.7% on the day; VIX rises from 25 to 31.

**Tuesday, September 16 2008.** Federal Reserve announces an **$85B bridge loan to AIG** in exchange for a 79.9% equity stake; without this, AIG's CDS counterparty obligations would have cascaded across the global financial system. Reserve Primary Fund (a money-market fund) "breaks the buck" -- the first MMF to do so in a generation -- triggering a US institutional-MMF run.

**Wednesday-Friday, September 17-19 2008.** US Treasury and Fed announce a $700B troubled-asset relief plan ([[tarp|TARP]]). SEC bans short-selling on 799 financial stocks. SPX rallies +4.0% on Sep 18 and +4.0% on Sep 19 on the announcement.

**Monday, September 29 2008.**
- US House of Representatives **rejects the TARP** legislation 228-205. Markets crash.
- DJIA falls 777.68 points (-7.0%); SPX falls **-8.8%**, the largest one-day drop since [[black-monday|1987]] until that date. VIX closes 46.7.
- House passes revised TARP on October 3.

**October 6-10 2008.** SPX falls 18.2% over the week, the worst week in SPX history at that time. **VIX intraday peaks at 89.53 on October 24** and **closes at 80.86 on November 20 2008**, the highest VIX close until [[covid-crash|March 16 2020]].

**October-December 2008.** Coordinated global rate cuts; G7 communiqué Oct 10 commits to no major financial-institution failures. Fed cuts to ~0% on December 16; first round of QE announced.

**Q4 2008-Q1 2009.** SPX continues to grind lower as the recession deepens. Citigroup falls below $1/share in March 2009. Bank stress tests are conducted in early 2009.

**Monday, March 9 2009.** SPX makes intraday low at **666.79**, closing low at **676.53**. From this date the bull market resumes; SPX would not revisit 666 again.

**June 2009.** NBER officially dates the US recession as ending in June 2009 (begun December 2007). The bull market continues for the next 11 years until the [[covid-crash|February 2020 peak]].

The **peak-to-trough was -57.4% over 17 months**. The full round-trip back to the 2007 high took until April 2013 -- 5.5 years.

## Mechanism / Why It Happened

| Mechanism | What broke | Why it amplified |
|---|---|---|
| Subprime default cascade | House prices fell ~30% (2006-2012); subprime defaults ran 5-10x model assumptions | Diversification assumptions failed in a *national* housing decline |
| Securitisation pyramid | [[mortgage-backed-securities|MBS]] -> CDOs -> CDOs-of-CDOs | A 5% pool default could wipe 100% of mezzanine/subordinate tranches |
| Counterparty contagion | [[aig|AIG]]'s ~$440B [[credit-default-swap|CDS]] on subprime CDOs | Opaque bilateral derivatives: nobody knew counterparties' counterparties |
| Funding-market freeze | Repo, commercial paper, interbank lending seized post-Lehman | Even IG firms could not roll CP; Fed had to fund non-banks |

Four compounding mechanisms:

1. **Subprime mortgage default cascade.** US house prices fell ~30% from 2006 peak to 2012 trough. Subprime MBS were rated investment-grade based on diversification assumptions that failed in a national housing decline; the underlying default rates ran 5-10x what the rating-agency models assumed.
2. **Securitisation pyramid collapse.** CDOs and CDOs-of-CDOs amplified the loss. A 5% default in the underlying mortgage pool could produce 100% loss in mezzanine and subordinate CDO tranches. The downstream structured-credit complex was many multiples of the underlying mortgage market.
3. **Counterparty contagion via CDS and bilateral derivatives.** AIG's $440B of CDS protection on subprime CDOs meant that the failure of *any* large structured-credit issuer would cascade through AIG and its counterparties (Goldman Sachs, Société Générale, Deutsche Bank, etc.). The opacity of bilateral derivatives meant no participant knew the true counterparty exposure of their counterparties' counterparties.
4. **Funding-market freeze.** Repo, commercial paper, and inter-bank lending froze for weeks at a time post-Lehman. Banks could not fund themselves at any rate; even highly-rated investment-grade corporations could not roll commercial paper. The Fed's response had to include direct funding to non-banks (commercial paper, money-market funds, asset-backed securities) -- a substantial expansion of the lender-of-last-resort doctrine.

The intellectual breakdown -- the moment the structured-credit pyramid was understood to be illusory -- was approximately the AIG rescue weekend (September 13-15 2008). After that, the question was the size and speed of policy intervention, not the underlying solvency analysis.

## Casualties / Survivors

**Casualties.**
- **Lehman Brothers** -- bankrupt September 15 2008. ~26,000 employees laid off; bondholders eventually received ~21 cents on the dollar.
- **Bear Stearns** -- absorbed by JPMorgan March 2008.
- **Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac** -- placed in federal conservatorship September 7 2008; common equity essentially wiped out.
- **AIG** -- 79.9% government-owned after September 16 2008; the company was eventually broken up.
- **Washington Mutual** -- seized September 25 2008, the largest bank failure in US history.
- **Wachovia** -- absorbed by Wells Fargo October 2008.
- **Merrill Lynch** -- absorbed by Bank of America September 14 2008.
- **Hedge funds.** Several large funds closed (Citadel Kensington fund -55% in 2008, ultimately recovered; multiple credit-arbitrage funds wound down). Funds-of-funds sold in 2009 to meet redemptions.
- **Madoff Ponzi scheme** -- exposed in December 2008 when redemption pressure unmasked a $65B fraud. Bernie Madoff sentenced to 150 years.
- **Naked short-vol and credit-vol books.** Wiped out in 2008 in size. Variance-swap dealers carrying short-variance positions on equity indices suffered some of the largest credit-derivative-trading losses in history.

**Survivors and beneficiaries.**
- **[[universa-investments]]** -- founded January 2007 by [[mark-spitznagel]]; reportedly returned ~+100%+ in 2008 on the long-vol/long-tail book. The fund's track record was made by the GFC.
- **[[michael-burry]] / Scion Capital** -- the canonical subprime-CDS short. See [[2007-2008-burry-subprime-cds-trade]] for the trade chronicled in *The Big Short*. Burry and a small cohort (John Paulson, Steve Eisman/FrontPoint, Greg Lippmann at Deutsche Bank, Jamie Mai/Charlie Ledley/Cornwall Capital) made multi-billion-dollar profits on subprime CDS protection bought at 1-3% running.
- **John Paulson / Paulson & Co.** -- earned ~$15B in 2007-2008 across his Credit Opportunities funds via subprime CDS shorts.
- **CTAs and managed-futures.** Trend-following CTAs (Winton, Man AHL, Millburn, etc.) had a strong 2008 short-equity / long-bond / short-commodity programme; the year is a banner result on the trend-following composite index.
- **Government-backstopped survivors.** Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, and the major US commercial banks all received TARP capital injections, Fed funding access, and (in October 2008) the federal-bank-debt guarantee. None failed; equity was diluted but franchises were preserved.

## Impact on Vol Books

For the long-vol-vs-short-vol framework, the GFC is the canonical *deep-cycle* monetization event, distinct from [[volmageddon|Feb 2018]] (microstructural ETP unwind) or [[vix-august-2024-spike|Aug 2024]] (positioning unwind). The crash was:

- **Slow:** 17 months from peak to trough, with multiple intra-cycle rallies (e.g., +24% from late November 2008 to early January 2009 before resuming the decline).
- **Deep:** -57% peak-to-trough.
- **Multi-asset:** Equities, credit, and structured products all collapsed; only Treasuries and gold provided positive carry.
- **Vol-rich:** Spot VIX above 60 from October 6 2008 through end of October; closed above 80 in mid-November.

Implications for vol books:

- **Naked short-vol books** (where they existed in 2008) were eliminated. Short-variance and short-volatility books at major dealer banks were a substantial driver of bank losses in late 2008 (variance swaps were the canonical "selling the [[variance-risk-premium|VRP]] in size" instrument, and they marked at par-vol levels of 50-70 vs. premiums collected at 15-20).
- **[[universa-investments|Universa]]'s 2008 result** is one of the two banner monetizations of the modern long-vol track record (the other being March 2020). The fund was barely two years old at year-end 2008; the result anchored Spitznagel's career and the broader [[tail-risk-hedging]] thesis.
- **The skew got steeper.** Post-GFC, the SPX OTM-put skew widened further from the post-1987 level. The structural premium for crash protection -- and the reciprocal income for selling it -- has been visibly higher in the 2009-present regime than the 1990-2007 regime.
- **[[long-vol-overlay]]** strategies -- the synthesis described in [[long-vol-vs-short-vol]] -- emerged as institutional best practice during 2009-2012 in direct response to the GFC. Allocators that had run naked short-vol or naked-equity books rebuilt with explicit overlays.
- **CDS as tail hedge.** The GFC also legitimised CDS protection on credit indices (CDX IG, CDX HY) as a portfolio tail hedge. The same logic was deployed by [[bill-ackman]] in the [[2020-03-ackman-pandemic-cds-trade|March 2020 trade]].

## Cross-Asset Impact and Policy Response

Peak-to-trough damage was broad; only the highest-quality safe havens carried positive returns through the worst of the cascade.

## Lessons / Takeaways

1. **The largest equity drawdowns are slow.** 1987 was a one-day event; 2020 was a one-month event; the GFC was a 17-month event. Long-vol overlays must remain funded across multi-year regimes, not just gap days. This is the single hardest behavioural test of the strategy.
2. **Credit and equity vol move together in real crises.** Structured products, CDS spreads, and equity vol all spiked simultaneously in 2008. A "diversified" book that owned only equity puts missed the credit leg; books that owned both monetized fully.
3. **Counterparty risk is a hidden short-vol position.** Owning a put bought from a dealer that fails is worth nothing. The post-GFC migration of OTC derivatives to central clearing was a direct response. Long-vol books should monitor counterparty solvency as part of risk-management.
4. **Government intervention defines the recovery shape.** TARP, the Fed's emergency facilities, and the eventual QE programmes set the bottom. Strategies betting on a multi-year continuation of the GFC bear market underperformed the 2009-2020 bull run.
5. **The post-1987 skew was insufficient pricing of GFC-magnitude tails.** Even after 1987's lesson, OTM-put pricing did not fully price for a GFC event. The post-GFC skew remains structurally steeper, suggesting markets have learned -- but each generation of vol-sellers will be tempted to think the new pricing is "rich" until the next event.
