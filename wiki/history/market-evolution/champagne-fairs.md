# Champagne Fairs

The **Champagne Fairs** (*foires de Champagne*) were a cycle of six annual trade fairs held in four towns of the County of Champagne, north-eastern France, that dominated European long-distance commerce and finance between approximately **1180 and 1320**. Their decline marked a shift to permanent commercial centres (Bruges, then Antwerp, Lyon, Geneva) but they were the foundational venue for [[medieval-bill-of-exchange-arbitrage|medieval bill-of-exchange arbitrage]] and the first European market with a multi-week settlement cycle that traders could exploit.

## The Six-Fair Cycle

| Sequence | Town | Approximate Calendar |
|----------|------|---------------------|
| 1 | Lagny-sur-Marne | January-February |
| 2 | Bar-sur-Aube | Lent-Easter |
| 3 | Provins (Foire de Mai / "Hot Fair") | May-June |
| 4 | Troyes (Foire de Saint-Jean / "Hot Fair") | July-August |
| 5 | Provins (Foire de Saint-Ayoul) | September-October |
| 6 | Troyes (Foire de Saint-Remi / "Cold Fair") | November-December |

Each fair lasted ~6 weeks and was structured into successive phases: drapery (cloth), leather, weighed goods (spices, wax), avoirdupois, and finally **payments** — a settlement window during which all bills of exchange and credit obligations from the prior phases came due.

## Why They Dominated

The geography (mid-way between Italian banking houses and Flemish wool-and-cloth producers) was supplemented by political and legal advantages:

- **Counts of Champagne** (especially Henry the Liberal, r. 1152-1181, and Thibaut IV, r. 1201-1253) granted *safe-conducts*, debtor-protection courts (*conservatori*), and tax exemptions.
- A specialized fair court (*juges des foires*) enforced contracts using the *lex mercatoria* — merchant law that crossed national boundaries.
- Standardised weights, measures, and currency (the *denier de Provins*).
- A continuous information flow: the next fair's prospects shaped the rate-setting at the current fair.

## Banking and Bills of Exchange

The financial breakthrough at Champagne was the **bill of exchange** (*lettera di cambio*) — a commercial paper instrument allowing payment to be made in one currency at one fair and redeemed in another currency at another fair. Florentine, Sienese, Lucchese, Genoese, and Venetian banking houses operated *banchi* at each fair phase to:

1. Collect payments owed to merchant clients in foreign currency.
2. Issue bills payable at the next fair, denominated in another currency.
3. Net positions on internal books across branches.
4. Ship physical specie only when net imbalances exceeded the cost of insurance + transport (the medieval analogue of [[gold-point-arbitrage|gold points]]).

The fair *conto* — a collectively negotiated session-end exchange rate table — set "official" rates but actual transactions could deviate by 1-5%. This persistent gap funded both arbitrage profit and disguised interest (*cambium et recambium* / "dry exchange") that skirted canonical usury prohibitions.

## Decline

Multiple causes from ~1280:

- **Genoese galley fleet** (1278+): direct sea route from Italy to Bruges and Southampton bypassed land fairs.
- **Capetian absorption:** Philip the Fair (r. 1285-1314) inherited Champagne via marriage to Joan I of Navarre (1284); royal taxation increased.
- **War and plague:** Hundred Years' War from 1337; Black Death 1347-1351 collapsed merchant networks.
- **Italian super-company collapses:** Bardi and Peruzzi failed 1343-1346 after Edward III's English-throne default.

By 1350 the fairs were largely a regional cloth market. Their financial function migrated to:

- **Bruges** (continuous cloth + bill-of-exchange centre).
- **[[lyon-fairs|Lyon]]** (4 fairs/year from 1420, dominated by Florentines and Genoese).
- **Geneva** (4 fairs/year, peak ~1450-1470).
- **Besançon** (16th-c centre after Genoese moved from Lyon).
- **Medina del Campo** (Castilian cycle, 16th c).

## Legacy

The Champagne fair cycle established multiple practices still recognizable in modern finance:

- **Settlement cycle / netting:** the fair "payments" phase is the direct ancestor of T+2 settlement and bilateral netting.
- **Standardized cross-rates:** the *conto* table is the ancestor of fixings (LIBOR, Bloomberg BFIX).
- **Triangular arbitrage:** explicitly performed across fair locations.
- **Multi-branch banking:** the Florentine houses' network model survives in modern global banking.
- **Mercantile law:** modern commercial-arbitration rules trace to *lex mercatoria*.
