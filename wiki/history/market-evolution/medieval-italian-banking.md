# Medieval Italian Banking

The **Italian merchant-bankers** of the 13th-16th centuries built the first recognizably modern multinational financial firms. They invented the bill of exchange, double-entry bookkeeping, branch-network banking, and disguised interest (*cambium et recambium*) — all in service of cross-border commerce and arbitrage at the [[champagne-fairs|Champagne]] and [[lyon-fairs|Lyon]] fairs. Their three great waves — the **Bardi/Peruzzi/Acciaioli super-companies** of the 14th century, the **Medici Bank** of the 15th, and the **Genoese-dominated Lyon and Besançon** networks of the 16th — established practices that persist in modern global finance.

## Wave 1: The Florentine Super-Companies (1250-1346)

The three largest Florentine banking houses of the early 14th century:

- **Compagnia dei Bardi** — peak ~1310-1340, branches in 25+ cities including Avignon, Barcelona, Bruges, Constantinople, Cyprus, Jerusalem, London, Majorca, Naples, Paris, Pisa, Rhodes, Seville, Tunis, Venice.
- **Compagnia dei Peruzzi** — second-largest, similar branch footprint.
- **Compagnia degli Acciaioli** — smaller but parallel structure.

They functioned as both commercial firms (wool, grain, spices) and bankers (lending to popes, kings, princes). Capital came from partnership shares (*soprariccorsi* and *fattori*) — an early proto-corporate structure.

**Collapse 1343-1346:** **Edward III of England** defaulted on war loans (Hundred Years' War financing), wiping out the Bardi (lost ~900,000 gold florins) and Peruzzi (~600,000). Acciaioli followed. Florentine commercial chronicler Giovanni Villani — who lost personally — described it as "an irreparable disaster."

The collapse established a permanent lesson: sovereign concentration risk eats the largest banking houses.

## Wave 2: The Medici Bank (1397-1494)

**Giovanni di Bicci de' Medici** (1360-1429) founded the Medici Bank in 1397. His son **Cosimo "il Vecchio"** (1389-1464) expanded it into the dominant European bank by ~1450. Grandson **Lorenzo "il Magnifico"** (1449-1492) presided over its slow decline.

**Branch structure ~1450 (peak):**

| Branch | Function |
|--------|----------|
| Florence (HQ) | Tabula — central books, capital allocation |
| Rome | Apostolic Camera — papal banking, indulgence collection |
| Venice | Levant trade, alum monopoly |
| Geneva → Lyon (1466) | Fair banking, French royal account |
| Bruges | Wool, cloth, English crown |
| London | Italian wool exports, English crown loans |
| Milan | Sforza ducal court |
| Avignon | Anti-pope and curial banking |
| Pisa | Mediterranean trade |

**Innovations:**

- **Holding-company structure:** Each branch was a separate partnership (*ragione*) with the Medici as senior partner. Limited the Florence HQ's liability for branch failures.
- **Double-entry bookkeeping:** Pacioli's 1494 *Summa* codified what Florentine bankers had practiced for 50+ years.
- **Bill of exchange standardization.**
- **Dry exchange** for disguised interest (round-trip FX yielding 8-15% annual without canonical usury).
- **Letter of credit** for traveling merchants and clergy.

**Decline:** The Medici Bank failed under Lorenzo (over-extended branch lending, especially London branch's loans to Edward IV) and was effectively dissolved when Charles VIII of France invaded Florence in 1494. The Medici family rose later to Florentine dukes and popes (Leo X, Clement VII), but the bank itself did not survive.

## Wave 3: Genoese Dominance (1530-1620)

After the Sack of Rome (1527) and the Italian Wars, Genoese bankers rose to dominate European finance via:

- **Loans to the Spanish crown** funded by silver from the New World (the *asientos* system).
- **Besançon fairs** (later Piacenza) — settlement venue for Habsburg-debt-driven flows.
- **Bills of exchange in écus de marc** — a unit of account specific to fair settlement.

Banking houses: **Spinola, Doria, Grimaldi, Pallavicini, Centurione**. The "Age of the Genoese" ended with Spanish bankruptcy 1627, after which the centre of European finance migrated to Amsterdam.

| Multi-branch banking | Bardi/Peruzzi 14th c | Global banks |

| Letter of credit | Templars + Italians 13-14th c | Trade finance |
| Foreign-exchange arbitrage | Champagne fairs 13th c | FX desks |

## Lessons for Traders

Two durable lessons survive from the medieval Italian houses into modern markets:

1. **Sovereign concentration risk is the recurring killer.** The Bardi and Peruzzi were destroyed by Edward III's default (1343-46), the Medici London branch by loans to Edward IV, and the Age of the Genoese by the Spanish bankruptcy of 1627. The largest, most prestigious counterparty — a crown lending its name as much as borrowing money — is the one whose default takes down the whole firm. The same pattern recurs in modern sovereign-debt crises and in any book over-concentrated in a single "too important to fail" counterparty.
2. **Edge comes from infrastructure and information asymmetry, not just price.** The Italians' real advantage was the branch network, double-entry books, and the bill of exchange — instruments that let them settle, finance, and arbitrage across fragmented currency zones faster than rivals. The disguised-interest "dry exchange" was effectively an early cross-currency basis trade dressed up to evade usury law. Whoever owns the better settlement rails and the better books extracts the spread — a principle that still describes FX desks and crypto market-makers today.
