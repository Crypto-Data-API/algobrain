# Physical vs Paper Commodity Exposure

Physical commodity ownership means holding the actual material — gold bars in a vault, silver coins in a safe, barrels of oil in a tank. Paper (financial) commodity exposure means holding a derivative or fund that tracks the commodity's price — [[futures-overview|futures contracts]], ETFs, options, or CFDs. The choice between them involves trade-offs in counterparty risk, storage costs, liquidity, tax treatment, and practicality that vary dramatically by commodity.

## Overview

For most [[commodities]], physical ownership is impractical. Nobody stores 1,000 barrels of [[crude-oil]] in their garage or warehouses 5,000 bushels of [[corn]] in their apartment. Physical exposure is primarily relevant for **precious metals** — [[gold]], [[silver]], [[platinum]], and [[palladium]] — where the commodity is small, high-value, non-perishable, and storable.

For energy, agriculture, and industrial metals, financial instruments are the only realistic option for most market participants. Even professional commodity traders at banks and funds rarely take physical delivery — they close or roll positions before expiration.

## Head-to-Head Comparison

| Dimension | Physical Ownership | Paper/Financial Exposure |
|---|---|---|
| **Counterparty risk** | None (you hold the asset) | Exchange clearinghouse (futures), fund sponsor (ETFs), broker (CFDs) |
| **Storage cost** | Vault fees (0.1-0.5%/year for gold), insurance, security | None (embedded in instrument pricing) |
| **Transaction cost** | 3-8% bid-ask spread (coins); 0.5-1% (bars via dealer) | 0.01-0.1% bid-ask (liquid futures/ETFs) |
| **Liquidity** | Low — physical market is OTC, settlement in days | High — seconds to execute on-exchange |
| **Divisibility** | Limited (can't sell half a gold bar) | Arbitrary (buy $100 of GLD) |
| **Short selling** | Impossible | Easy (futures, inverse ETFs, options) |
| **Leverage** | None (unless borrowing against physical) | Built-in (futures margin 5-15%) |
| **Tracking error** | None (you own spot) | Zero for futures; 0.1-15%/year for ETFs depending on structure |
| **Tax (US)** | Collectibles rate (28% long-term for precious metals) | Varies: 60/40 for futures, collectibles for physical-backed ETFs |
| **Seizure/confiscation risk** | Subject to government confiscation (US precedent: 1933 Executive Order 6102) | Subject to account freezes, exchange halts |
| **Insurance** | Your responsibility | Brokerage/SIPC coverage applies to account |
| **Portability** | Can move across jurisdictions physically | Tied to brokerage/exchange jurisdiction |
| **Bankruptcy protection** | Outside financial system | Depends on brokerage segregation rules |

## The Core Trade-off

Neither form eliminates risk — each substitutes one risk profile for another. The decision is really about *which* risk you would rather hold:

| You are choosing... | Physical | Paper |
|---------------------|----------|-------|
| Risk you accept | Storage, theft, loss, illiquidity, wide spreads | Counterparty, tracking error, [[contango|roll/contango]], account freeze |
| Risk you remove | Issuer/counterparty default | Storage, insurance, physical illiquidity |
| You optimise for | Crisis insurance / sovereignty | Trading P&L, leverage, short access |

## Quick Decision Reference

| Goal | Choose | Vehicle |
|------|--------|---------|
| Crisis/insurance hedge, outside the system | Physical | Coins or allocated vault |
| Strategic portfolio allocation (5–15%) | Paper | [[gold]] ETF (GLD/IAU) |
| Active trading / tactical | Paper | [[futures-overview|Futures]] (GC/MGC) or options |
| Leverage / short exposure | Paper | Futures margin, inverse/LEAPS |
| Tax-advantaged account (IRA) | Paper | GLD/IAU |
| Energy / ags / industrial metals | Paper (only option) | Futures / ETFs |

## The Gold Case Study

[[gold]] is the commodity where physical-vs-paper matters most, because both options are viable:

### Physical Gold Options

- **Gold bars (1oz, 10oz, 1kg):** Lowest premium over spot (~1-3% for major refiners like PAMP, Valcambi, Perth Mint). Requires vault storage or home safe. [[lbma]] Good Delivery bars (400oz, ~$300K) are the institutional standard.
- **Gold coins (American Eagle, Krugerrand, Maple Leaf):** Higher premiums (3-8% over spot) but more recognizable, divisible, and liquid in the retail market. Legal tender status in some jurisdictions.
- **Allocated vault storage (BullionVault, Perth Mint, Brink's):** Professional vault storage with specific bar serial numbers assigned to you. Fees ~0.12-0.4%/year. Removes home storage risk but introduces custody counterparty risk.
- **Unallocated accounts:** Cheaper (~0.1%/year) but your gold is pooled — you have a claim on gold, not specific bars. In a bank failure, you are an unsecured creditor.

### Paper Gold Options

- **GLD/IAU ETFs:** Physically backed by gold in London vaults (HSBC for GLD, JP Morgan for IAU). Track spot gold closely. GLD expense ratio 0.40%, IAU 0.25%. Accessible in any brokerage. No K-1 forms.
- **GC futures (COMEX):** Direct exposure with 5-10% margin. Standard contract = 100 troy oz (~$230K notional at $2,300/oz). Micro gold (MGC) = 10 oz. No storage costs but requires rolling.
- **Gold mining stocks/ETFs (GDX, GDXJ):** Indirect exposure through equity ownership. Amplified moves (leveraged to gold price) but introduces management risk, cost inflation, geopolitical risk, and equity market correlation.
- **Gold options:** Options on GC futures or GLD. Allow defined-risk exposure with asymmetric payoffs.

### Which Gold Exposure for Which Purpose?

| Purpose | Best Vehicle |
|---|---|
| Long-term wealth preservation / insurance | Physical (allocated vault or coins at home) |
| Portfolio allocation (5-15%) | GLD or IAU in brokerage account |
| Active trading / tactical positioning | GC futures or GLD options |
| Leveraged gold bet | GC futures (margin) or LEAPS on GLD |
| Tax-advantaged account (IRA) | GLD or IAU |
| Outside-the-system hedge (extreme scenario) | Physical coins in personal custody |

## The 2020 Physical-Paper Disconnect

During the COVID-19 pandemic in March-April 2020, the physical and paper gold markets temporarily disconnected in an instructive way:

- **Major gold refineries shut down** (Valcambi, PAMP, Argor-Heraeus in Switzerland) due to lockdowns
- **Logistics froze** — air freight capacity collapsed, armored transport disrupted
- **Physical gold premiums spiked to 10-15%** over spot COMEX futures prices for retail products (coins, small bars)
- **COMEX futures briefly traded at $70+/oz premium** over London spot (normally <$1) because the standard 400oz London bar couldn't be reliably delivered to COMEX's 100oz contract specification
- The [[cme-group|CME]] rushed to create a new 400oz futures contract to bridge the London-New York delivery gap

This episode demonstrated that physical and paper gold are **not perfectly fungible** — in a logistics crisis, the physical premium can spike dramatically. For investors who hold physical gold as a genuine crisis hedge, this was validation. For traders, it created rare arbitrage opportunities.

Similar but smaller disconnections have occurred in [[silver]] (2020, 2021 Reddit squeeze attempt) and [[platinum]] (periodic sponge/ingot shortages).

## Practicality by Commodity

| Commodity | Physical Ownership Practical? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| [[gold]] | Yes | Small, high-value, non-perishable, established retail market |
| [[silver]] | Somewhat | Bulkier per dollar (density/value ratio ~70x worse than gold); $50K in silver = ~110 lbs |
| [[platinum]], [[palladium]] | Somewhat | Smaller retail market; fewer dealers; coins exist but less liquid |
| [[copper]] | No | $10K of copper = ~4 metric tonnes; warehouse required |
| [[crude-oil]] | No | Hazardous, perishable, requires tank farm; never practical for individuals |
| [[natural-gas]] | No | Requires cryogenic storage or pipeline; impossible for individuals |
| [[corn]], [[wheat]], [[soybeans]] | No | Perishable, requires grain elevators, pest control, insurance |
| [[coffee]], [[cocoa]], [[sugar]] | No | Perishable, temperature-sensitive, massive volume |
| [[live-cattle]], [[lean-hogs]] | No | Living animals; requires ranch/feedlot infrastructure |
| [[uranium]] | No | Regulated nuclear material; requires NRC licensing |

For all commodities below the precious metals line, paper/financial exposure is the only realistic option for individual traders and most institutional investors.

## The "Sound Money" Perspective

A meaningful subset of gold and silver buyers are motivated by distrust of the financial system itself — central bank money printing, fractional reserve banking, counterparty chains. For these buyers, the entire point of physical metal is that it exists **outside the financial system**:

- No counterparty can default on a gold bar in your possession
- No exchange can halt trading on coins in your safe
- No government can freeze a vault in a friendly jurisdiction (though confiscation is a historical precedent — US 1933, UK 1966 restrictions)

This perspective values physical metal even though it is more expensive to buy, sell, store, and insure. The premium is the cost of eliminating counterparty risk entirely.

For traders focused on profit and loss from commodity price movements, paper instruments are almost always superior due to tighter spreads, instant liquidity, leverage, and the ability to go short.

## Pros and Cons

### Physical Ownership

**Pros**
- No counterparty or issuer risk — you hold the asset directly, outside the financial system
- Cannot be halted, frozen, or defaulted on while in personal custody; portable across jurisdictions
- A genuine crisis/insurance hedge — value demonstrated when physical and paper markets disconnect

**Cons**
- Wide bid-ask spreads and dealer premiums; storage, insurance, and security costs
- Poor liquidity and divisibility; no leverage and no practical way to go short
- Confiscation/seizure precedent exists historically; only practical for high-value, non-perishable goods (chiefly precious metals)

### Paper / Financial Exposure

**Pros**
- Tight spreads, instant on-exchange liquidity, and arbitrary dollar sizing
- Built-in leverage (futures) and easy short selling; no storage or insurance burden
- Works inside ordinary and tax-advantaged brokerage accounts

**Cons**
- Introduces counterparty/issuer risk (clearinghouse, fund sponsor, broker, note issuer)
- Tracking error and roll/[[contango]] drag for futures-based funds; potential K-1 / collectibles tax frictions
- Exposed to account freezes, exchange halts, and brokerage-segregation rules in a crisis

## Risks

- **Counterparty and custody risk** dominate the paper side (and unallocated physical accounts); allocated physical in personal custody minimizes it but shifts risk to storage, theft, and loss.
- **Physical-paper basis risk** — the two are not perfectly fungible; in a logistics or delivery crisis the physical premium can spike sharply (as in 2020).
- **Liquidity and spread risk** are high for physical retail products; selling at a fair price can be slow.
- **Roll/contango and tracking risk** apply to most paper vehicles (see [[contango]], [[roll-yield]], [[commodity-futures-vs-etfs]]).
- This page is educational and structural, not tax or investment advice; tax and confiscation rules are jurisdiction-specific and change over time.
