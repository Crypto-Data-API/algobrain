# Long vs Short Positions

## Overview

Going long and going short are the two fundamental directional positions in trading. A long position profits when prices rise; a short position profits when prices fall. While "buy low, sell high" is intuitive to everyone, [[short-selling]] -- selling borrowed assets to buy them back cheaper -- is mechanically more complex, riskier, more expensive, and psychologically harder. Understanding both sides is essential for any trader who wants to profit in all market conditions.

## Comparison Table

| Dimension | Long | Short |
|---|---|---|
| **Mechanics** | Buy to open, sell to close | Sell borrowed to open, buy to close |
| **Profit When** | Price rises | Price falls |
| **Max Profit** | Unlimited (price can rise forever) | Capped at 100% (price goes to zero) |
| **Max Loss** | Limited to investment (price to zero) | Theoretically unlimited |
| **Holding Cost** | None (or margin interest) | Borrow fees + margin interest |
| **Margin Requirement** | 50% initial (Reg T) | 50% initial + maintenance |
| **Psychological Difficulty** | Natural, intuitive | Counterintuitive, stressful |
| **Regulatory Restrictions** | None | Uptick rule, short-sale bans |
| **Crypto (Perpetuals)** | Pay funding when positive | Receive funding when positive |
| **Dividends** | Received | Must pay to lender |
| **Time Pressure** | None | Ongoing costs erode profit |

## Key Differences

**Risk Asymmetry** is the most critical distinction. A long position can lose at most 100% of the invested capital (stock goes to zero). A short position has theoretically unlimited loss -- a stock can rise 200%, 500%, or more. This asymmetry is not theoretical: short squeezes like GameStop (2021) bankrupted short sellers when the stock rose over 1,500% in weeks.

**Cost Structure** penalizes shorts. Holding a long position in a cash account costs nothing beyond the purchase price. Shorting requires borrowing shares, which incurs borrow fees ranging from minimal (0.3% for liquid stocks) to astronomical (50%+ for hard-to-borrow names). In crypto, perpetual futures funding rates create variable costs that can swing for or against either direction.

**Regulatory Environment** restricts shorting specifically. The SEC's alternative uptick rule (Rule 201) limits short selling during sharp declines. Countries have historically banned short selling during market crises. There are no equivalent restrictions on buying. These rules exist because regulators view unchecked short selling as potentially destabilizing.

**Psychology** makes shorting harder in practice. Markets have an upward long-term bias, so shorts fight the prevailing trend. Losses on shorts grow faster (a stock rising 50% on a short hurts more than a stock falling 50% on a long, due to compounding). Short sellers also face social stigma and management hostility.

**In Crypto Markets**, shorting primarily occurs through perpetual futures rather than borrowing spot assets. Funding rates on perps create a dynamic cost: when the market is bullish and most traders are long, shorts receive funding payments. When bearish, shorts pay funding. This mechanism is unique to crypto and can make shorting profitable even in sideways markets.

## When to Use Each

**Go long when** you believe an asset's price will rise, have bullish conviction, and want straightforward risk exposure. Longs are the default for portfolio building, trend following, and buy-and-hold strategies.

**Go short when** you have specific bearish conviction, see overvaluation, want to hedge existing long exposure, or are implementing a market-neutral strategy. Use tight [[stop-loss]] orders given the unlimited risk profile. Consider defined-risk alternatives like buying put options.

**Pair both when** running long/short strategies: long undervalued assets, short overvalued ones. This hedges market-wide risk and isolates relative value. Many hedge funds operate this way, targeting alpha from the spread rather than directional market moves.

## Verdict

Longing is simpler, cheaper, and aligns with the long-term upward drift of most markets. [[short-selling]] is a powerful tool but demands more skill, carries higher costs, and exposes traders to unlimited loss. Most traders should master long positions before attempting shorts. When shorting, use strict risk management, prefer liquid assets with low borrow costs, and consider options as a defined-risk alternative. Both directions are essential tools -- but they are not equally forgiving.
