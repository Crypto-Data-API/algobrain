## Overview

[[bitcoin]] and [[ethereum]] are the two dominant cryptocurrencies by every measure: market cap, institutional adoption, developer activity, and cultural significance. But they serve fundamentally different purposes. Bitcoin is digital gold, a store of value with a fixed supply and maximum simplicity. Ethereum is a programmable platform, hosting thousands of [[smart-contracts]], DeFi protocols, and [[layer-2]] networks. Understanding their differences is essential for any crypto portfolio allocation. Live prices from our data: BTC ~$69K, ETH ~$2.1K.

## Key Differences

**Store of value vs utility platform.** [[bitcoin]]'s value proposition is simplicity and scarcity. 21 million coins, no changes to monetary policy, [[proof-of-work]] security that costs real energy. [[ethereum]]'s value comes from being useful: every DeFi protocol, NFT marketplace, and [[layer-2]] chain pays fees in ETH, creating organic demand.

**Supply dynamics diverge.** Bitcoin's 21M cap is absolute and has never changed. New BTC enters circulation via mining rewards that halve every four years ([[bitcoin-halving]]). Ethereum has no hard cap, but since EIP-1559 and the Merge, ETH is burned with every transaction. During high network usage, more ETH is burned than issued, making it deflationary. During low usage, it is mildly inflationary.

**Energy and consensus are politically charged.** Bitcoin's [[proof-of-work]] consumes significant energy, which critics call wasteful and proponents call the most secure consensus ever built. Ethereum's move to [[proof-of-stake]] reduced energy use by ~99.95% but introduced different centralization concerns around large staking providers like Lido.

**Institutional adoption favors Bitcoin so far.** The spot [[btc-etf]] approvals in January 2024 were a watershed moment, with BlackRock's IBIT alone attracting tens of billions. The spot [[eth-etf]] approvals followed but with lower inflows. Bitcoin's narrative as digital gold is simpler for traditional finance to underwrite.

**The L2 ecosystem gives Ethereum leverage.** [[arbitrum]], Optimism, Base, and zk-rollups all settle on Ethereum and pay fees in ETH. This creates a scalability path that Bitcoin lacks. The Lightning Network exists for payments but has not achieved comparable adoption.

## When to Use Each

**Allocate to [[bitcoin]] when:**
- You want a macro hedge against fiat currency debasement
- You prioritize the simplest, most battle-tested crypto asset
- You want the deepest institutional [[liquidity]] via [[btc-etf]]
- You believe in the store of value / digital gold thesis

**Allocate to [[ethereum]] when:**
- You want exposure to the DeFi and [[smart-contracts]] ecosystem
- You want to earn [[staking]] yield (~3.5-4.5% APR)
- You believe in the [[layer-2]] scaling roadmap
- You want exposure to the broadest developer ecosystem in crypto

**Consider holding both when:**
- You want diversified crypto exposure across different use cases
- You believe both the store of value and smart contract theses will play out
- You want to balance BTC's lower volatility with ETH's higher beta
- You recognize that [[bitcoin]] and [[ethereum]] often move together but diverge during narrative shifts

## Verdict

This is not an either/or decision for most portfolios. [[bitcoin]] and [[ethereum]] serve different functions and have different risk profiles. BTC is the macro asset: simpler thesis, deeper liquidity, stronger institutional backing. ETH is the technology bet: higher upside if crypto adoption grows, but more complex risks. A common allocation is 60-70% BTC and 30-40% ETH for crypto portfolios, adjusted based on conviction about DeFi growth vs sound money narrative.

The ETH/BTC ratio is itself a tradeable metric that reflects market sentiment: when ETH outperforms BTC, it signals risk appetite and DeFi enthusiasm. When BTC outperforms ETH, it signals flight to safety within crypto. Watch this ratio alongside [[funding-rates]] and [[open-interest]] for a complete picture of crypto market positioning.
