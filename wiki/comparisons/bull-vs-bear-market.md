# Bull Market vs Bear Market

## Overview

Bull and bear markets are the two dominant phases of market cycles. A bull market is a sustained period of rising prices driven by optimism and economic expansion. A bear market is a sustained decline driven by fear and contraction. The conventional definitions use 20% thresholds, but the practical implications run far deeper: strategies that thrive in one regime can be destroyed in the other. Recognizing which environment you are in -- and when the [[trend]] is shifting -- is among the most valuable skills in trading.

## Comparison Table

| Dimension | Bull Market | Bear Market |
|---|---|---|
| **Definition** | 20%+ rise from recent low | 20%+ decline from recent high |
| **Average Duration** | ~4-5 years (equities) | ~1-1.5 years (equities) |
| **Dominant Psychology** | Greed, optimism, FOMO | Fear, capitulation, panic |
| **Winning Strategies** | Momentum, growth, buy-the-dip | Defensive, short-selling, hedging |
| **Sector Leaders** | Tech, growth, speculative | Utilities, healthcare, consumer staples |
| **Volume Pattern** | Rising volume on up days | Rising volume on down days |
| **Volatility** | Low to moderate | High, spiky |
| **Credit Conditions** | Easy, spreads tightening | Tight, spreads widening |
| **Retail Participation** | High, increasing | Low, decreasing |
| **IPO/Fundraising** | Active, frothy | Frozen |

## Key Differences

**Duration Asymmetry** is important for planning. Bull markets historically last three to four times longer than bear markets. The S&P 500 bull market from 2009 to 2020 ran eleven years. Most bear markets resolve within 12-18 months. This means long-biased strategies have a structural time advantage, but the speed and severity of bear markets can erase years of gains in months if not hedged.

**Strategy Selection** must adapt to the regime. In bull markets, momentum and growth strategies outperform: buying breakouts, adding to winners, and buying dips are rewarded. In bear markets, these same strategies lead to catching falling knives. Bear market strategies include [[short-selling]], rotating into defensive sectors, raising cash, and using options for protection. The traders who suffer most are those who refuse to adapt.

**Investor Psychology** swings between extremes. Late-stage bull markets produce euphoria, excessive leverage, and the belief that prices only go up. Late-stage bear markets produce despair, capitulation selling, and the belief that prices will never recover. Both extremes create opportunity for contrarian traders. Fear-greed indicators, put-call ratios, and VIX levels help quantify these emotional shifts.

**Sector Rotation** follows a predictable pattern. Early bull markets favor financials and cyclicals. Mid-bull favors technology and growth. Late bull sees speculation in low-quality names. Bear markets rotate into defensive sectors: utilities, healthcare, consumer staples, and precious metals. Understanding this rotation helps traders position ahead of regime changes.

**Identifying the Transition** is the hardest and most valuable skill. Key signals that a bull may be ending include: narrowing market breadth (fewer stocks making highs), yield curve inversion, credit spread widening, divergences between price and momentum indicators, and excessive retail speculation. Bear-to-bull transitions often feature a capitulation volume spike, extreme pessimism readings, and breadth expansion from a low base.

## When to Use Each (Strategy Adaptation)

**In a bull market**, lean into [[trend]]-following strategies: buy breakouts, ride momentum, overweight growth sectors, and use dips as entry points. Keep position sizes normal and accept that drawdowns are buying opportunities until proven otherwise.

**In a bear market**, shift to capital preservation: reduce position sizes, raise cash allocations, use stop-losses aggressively, consider [[short-selling]] or put options for hedging, and rotate into defensive sectors. Avoid the temptation to buy every dip -- in a true bear, the dips keep dipping.

**During transitions**, reduce conviction and size. When you are unsure of the regime, that uncertainty is information. Tighten stops, diversify across strategies, and wait for confirmation before committing heavily to a new direction.

## Verdict

Bull and bear markets are not just price labels -- they are fundamentally different operating environments that demand different strategies, risk tolerances, and psychological discipline. The biggest risk is not being in the wrong market; it is applying the wrong playbook. Successful long-term traders build systems that detect regime changes and adapt accordingly, rather than assuming today's [[trend]] will last forever.
