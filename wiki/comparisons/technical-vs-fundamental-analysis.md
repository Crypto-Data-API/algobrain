# Technical vs Fundamental Analysis

## Overview

[[technical-analysis]] and fundamental-analysis are the two pillars of market analysis. Technical analysis studies price charts, volume, and indicators to forecast future price movements based on historical patterns. Fundamental analysis evaluates a company's financial health, earnings, and intrinsic value to determine whether a security is fairly priced. The debate between them has raged for decades, but the most successful market participants often use both -- see [[fundamental-technical-fusion]].

## Comparison Table

| Dimension | Technical Analysis | Fundamental Analysis |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **What It Tells You** | Where price is likely to go next | What the asset is actually worth |
| **Data Used** | Price, volume, open interest (OHLCV) | Financial statements, earnings, macro data |
| **Timeframe** | Short to medium term (minutes to months) | Medium to long term (months to years) |
| **Subjectivity** | Moderate (pattern interpretation varies) | High (valuation models require assumptions) |
| **Learning Curve** | Moderate (patterns and indicators) | Steep (accounting, finance, economics) |
| **Applicability** | Any traded asset with price data | Primarily stocks, bonds, currencies |
| **Entry Timing** | Excellent (precise levels and signals) | Poor (knows value but not timing) |
| **Works Best For** | Active trading, swing trades, derivatives | Long-term investing, value investing |
| **Key Criticism** | "Reading tea leaves" - past does not predict future | "The market can stay irrational longer than you can stay solvent" |
| **Automation** | Easily automated and backtested | Difficult to fully automate |

## Key Differences

**Price vs value.** Technical analysis assumes all known information is already reflected in the price, so studying price action is sufficient. Fundamental analysis assumes the market frequently misprices assets, and studying the underlying value reveals profitable discrepancies. These are fundamentally different beliefs about market efficiency.

**Timing vs selection.** Fundamental analysis excels at telling you what to buy -- undervalued assets with strong financials. Technical analysis excels at telling you when to buy -- precise entry points with defined risk. This is why they combine so naturally: fundamentals select the asset, technicals time the entry.

**Universal vs specific.** Technical analysis works on anything with a price chart: stocks, forex, commodities, crypto, even NFTs. The same patterns and indicators apply everywhere. Fundamental analysis requires domain-specific knowledge. Analyzing a bank's balance sheet is completely different from valuing a tech startup or assessing a commodity's supply dynamics.

**Speed of information.** Technical analysis reflects new information instantly because price adjusts in real time. Fundamental data arrives in batches -- quarterly earnings, annual reports, economic releases. This gives technical analysis an advantage in fast-moving markets and fundamental analysis an advantage in identifying long-term mispricings.

## When to Use Each

**Use technical analysis when:**
- You are an active trader (day trading or swing trading)
- You need precise entry and exit points with defined risk
- Trading derivatives, forex, or commodities
- The asset lacks clear fundamental data (crypto, for example)
- You want a systematic, backtestable approach

**Use fundamental analysis when:**
- You are a long-term investor with a multi-year horizon
- You want to understand what drives a business's value
- Selecting stocks for a portfolio or evaluating company quality
- You are analyzing bonds, real estate, or private investments
- You want to understand macro themes and sector rotations

**Use both together -- the [[fundamental-technical-fusion]] approach.** Screen stocks using fundamental criteria (strong earnings, reasonable valuation), then time entries using technical signals (breakouts from bases, support bounces). This captures the strengths of both and mitigates the weaknesses of each.

## Verdict

The "technical vs fundamental" debate is a false dichotomy. Both frameworks have proven track records and clear limitations. Technical analysis provides actionable timing signals but says nothing about intrinsic value. Fundamental analysis identifies genuine value but offers no help with timing. The pragmatic approach is to use both: let fundamentals tell you what to trade and technicals tell you when to trade it. If your timeframe is short (intraday to weeks), lean technical. If your timeframe is long (months to years), lean fundamental. In between, blend them.
