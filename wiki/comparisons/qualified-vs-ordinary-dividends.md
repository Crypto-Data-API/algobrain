# Qualified vs Ordinary Dividends

For income investors in the United States, the single biggest determinant of *after-tax* dividend income is whether a payment is classified as a **qualified dividend** or an **ordinary (non-qualified) dividend**. The two are taxed under completely different schedules, so the same headline [[dividend-yield|yield]] can leave very different amounts of cash in your pocket. This page explains the distinction generically; it is not tax advice, tax law changes, and rules differ outside the US (e.g. Australia's [[franking-credits|franking credit]] system works differently).

## The core difference

- **Qualified dividends** are taxed at the lower **long-term capital-gains rates** (in the US, the 0% / 15% / 20% brackets, depending on taxable income).
- **Ordinary (non-qualified) dividends** are taxed at your ordinary **income-tax rate** — the same marginal rate as wages, which is higher than the capital-gains rate for most investors.

All taxable dividends are reported to a US investor on **Form 1099-DIV**: Box 1a shows *total* ordinary dividends, and Box 1b shows the portion that is *qualified*. Box 1b is a subset of Box 1a, not an addition to it.

## Comparison table

| Dimension | Qualified Dividend | Ordinary (Non-Qualified) Dividend |
|-----------|--------------------|-----------------------------------|
| **US tax rate** | Long-term capital-gains rates (0/15/20%) | Ordinary income rates (can be much higher) |
| **Holding-period test** | Must satisfy it (see below) | Not applicable |
| **Typical sources** | Common/preferred shares of US corporations and qualified foreign corporations held long enough | [[reits|REIT]] distributions, most MLP distributions, money-market and most bond-fund "dividends," dividends on shares held too briefly |
| **1099-DIV box** | Box 1b (subset of 1a) | The remainder of Box 1a |
| **Tax-deferred account** | Distinction is moot — no annual tax inside an IRA/401(k) | Distinction is moot inside a tax-sheltered account |

## What makes a dividend "qualified"

Two main conditions generally must both be met:

1. **Paid by a qualifying payer** — a US corporation, or a qualified foreign corporation (e.g. one trading on a US exchange or covered by a tax treaty).
2. **Holding-period test** — the investor must hold the shares for **more than 60 days during the 121-day window** that begins 60 days *before* the [[ex-dividend-date|ex-dividend date]]. The idea is to deny the lower rate to traders who flip a stock just to grab one payment (the [[dividend-capture|dividend-capture]] pattern). Days where your downside risk is hedged (e.g. via certain options) do not count toward the holding period.

If either condition fails, the dividend is taxed as ordinary income.

## Common things that are usually NOT qualified

- **[[reits|REIT]] distributions** — generally taxed as ordinary income, because the REIT itself typically pays little or no corporate tax (the income is passed through). A separate deduction has at times applied to the pass-through portion, but the base character is ordinary.
- **MLP / partnership distributions** — pass-through income, reported on a K-1, not a 1099-DIV.
- **Interest dressed up as "dividends"** — money-market funds and most bond funds pay distributions that are really interest, taxed as ordinary income.
- **Dividends on shares held too briefly** — even an ordinary US blue-chip dividend becomes non-qualified if you fail the holding-period test.
- **Employer-plan and certain preferred situations** — special rules can disqualify the lower rate.

## Why it matters for an income strategy

A naive comparison of two stocks on [[dividend-yield|dividend yield]] alone can be misleading: a 4% qualified yield and a 4% ordinary yield are *not* equivalent after tax. This is one reason a [[reits|REIT]]-heavy income portfolio held in a **taxable** account can be less efficient than it looks, and why income investors often place ordinary-dividend payers inside **tax-sheltered accounts** (IRAs, 401(k)s) where the distinction disappears, while keeping qualified payers in taxable accounts. The same logic favours [[shareholder-yield|buyback-heavy total-return]] names for high earners, because buybacks defer tax into capital gains entirely.

## Clearly-hypothetical example

Suppose an investor in a 32% ordinary bracket (and a 15% long-term capital-gains bracket) receives **$10,000** in dividends:

- If fully **qualified**: tax ≈ $1,500, leaving **$8,500**.
- If fully **ordinary**: tax ≈ $3,200, leaving **$6,800**.

Same gross income, a ~$1,700 difference — driven entirely by classification. (Illustrative round numbers only; actual brackets, surtaxes such as the net investment income tax, and state taxes are ignored.)

## Limitations and caveats

- This is a **US framing**. Other jurisdictions tax dividends differently — Australia uses [[franking-credits|imputation/franking credits]], and many countries withhold tax at source on foreign dividends.
- Tax brackets, thresholds, and special deductions **change with legislation**; treat any specific rate as time-sensitive.
- Classification can be **partial** — a single distribution may be split into qualified, ordinary, and return-of-capital portions on the 1099-DIV.
- None of this is personalised tax advice; confirm with a professional or current IRS guidance.
