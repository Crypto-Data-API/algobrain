# Trend Following vs Mean Reversion

## Overview

[[trend-following]] and [[mean-reversion]] represent the two fundamental philosophies of trading. Every strategy, at its core, is either riding a trend or fading an extreme. Trend followers believe "the trend is your friend" and seek to capture large directional moves. Mean reversion traders believe "what goes up must come down" and profit when stretched prices snap back to equilibrium. The two approaches have opposite performance profiles, making them remarkably powerful when combined in a portfolio -- see [[regime-adaptive-strategy]].

## Comparison Table

| Dimension | Trend Following | Mean Reversion |
|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| **Philosophy** | Ride trends, let winners run | Fade extremes, prices revert to mean |
| **Win Rate** | Low (30-45%) | High (55-75%) |
| **Reward/Risk per Trade** | High (2:1 to 10:1+) | Low (0.5:1 to 1.5:1) |
| **Best Market Condition** | Strong trending markets | Range-bound, choppy markets |
| **Worst Market Condition** | Choppy, whipsaw markets | Strong trends (fighting the trend) |
| **Holding Period** | Days to months | Hours to days |
| **Drawdown Profile** | Long, grinding drawdowns from whipsaws | Sharp, deep drawdowns from trend continuation |
| **Psychological Challenge** | Enduring many small losses | Holding against the crowd, sizing discipline |
| **Edge Source** | Fat-tailed returns, positive skew | High frequency of small gains |
| **Correlation** | Negatively correlated with mean reversion | Negatively correlated with trend following |
| **Indicators Used** | Moving averages, breakouts, [[macd]] | [[rsi]], [[bollinger-bands]], [[stochastic]] |

## Key Differences

**Win rate vs payoff.** Trend following strategies lose more often than they win, but the winners are much larger than the losers. A single trend trade can pay for ten losing trades. Mean reversion strategies win frequently but each win is small. A single trend that does not revert can erase many small gains. Both can be profitable -- they just get there differently.

**Psychological demands.** Trend following requires the discipline to take loss after loss while waiting for the big winner. Most traders abandon trend systems during inevitable losing streaks. Mean reversion requires the nerve to buy falling prices and sell rising prices, which feels deeply wrong in the moment. It also demands strict position sizing because the rare catastrophic loss must not wipe out accumulated gains.

**Market regime dependency.** Markets alternate between trending and ranging regimes. Trend following profits during trends and bleeds during ranges. Mean reversion profits during ranges and gets crushed during trends. Neither approach works in all conditions, which is why the concept of [[regime-adaptive-strategy]] has gained traction.

**The negative correlation advantage.** Because these strategies suffer in opposite market conditions, combining them in a portfolio produces smoother equity curves. When trend following draws down during choppy periods, mean reversion often profits, and vice versa. This natural hedge is one of the strongest arguments for running both.

## When to Use Each

**Use trend following when:**
- Markets are displaying clear directional movement
- Volatility is expanding (breakouts from consolidation)
- You have the patience for low win rates and long drawdowns
- Trading futures, forex, or commodities (which trend well)
- You prefer set-and-forget systems with mechanical rules

**Use mean reversion when:**
- Markets are range-bound with clear support/resistance
- Volatility is elevated but directionless
- You prefer frequent wins and high activity
- Trading equities (which mean-revert on shorter timeframes)
- You have the discipline for strict stop-losses on every trade

**Combine them for portfolio resilience.** Allocate capital to both trend and mean-reversion strategies. The negative correlation between them reduces portfolio drawdowns significantly. Many professional trading firms run both styles simultaneously for exactly this reason.

## Verdict

Neither trend following nor mean reversion is inherently superior. Trend following captures the biggest market moves and has produced legendary returns (think managed futures), but it demands extraordinary psychological endurance. Mean reversion offers more consistent daily results but carries catastrophic tail risk if not managed carefully. The wisest approach is to run both, sized appropriately, and let their negative correlation smooth your equity curve. If you must choose one, match it to your psychology: trend following for the patient and detached, mean reversion for the active and disciplined.
