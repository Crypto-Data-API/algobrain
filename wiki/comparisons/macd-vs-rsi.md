# MACD vs RSI

## Overview

[[macd]] and [[rsi]] are arguably the two most popular technical indicators in trading. They both fall under the momentum umbrella but measure fundamentally different things. MACD tracks the relationship between two moving averages to identify momentum shifts and trend direction. RSI measures the relative strength of upward versus downward price changes to gauge whether a security is overbought or oversold. Together they form one of the most effective indicator combinations available.

## Comparison Table

| Dimension | MACD | RSI |
|-----------|------|-----|
| **What It Measures** | Momentum via moving average convergence/divergence | Relative strength of gains vs losses |
| **Type** | Unbounded momentum oscillator | Bounded oscillator (0-100) |
| **Signal Types** | Signal line crossover, zero line cross, histogram | OB/OS levels (70/30), centerline, divergence |
| **Leading/Lagging** | Lagging (based on moving averages) | Slightly leading (reacts to price velocity) |
| **Trend Identification** | Strong (above/below zero line) | Moderate (above/below 50) |
| **OB/OS Detection** | Not designed for it | Primary purpose |
| **Divergence Signals** | Excellent with histogram | Excellent with price levels |
| **Best Market** | Trending markets | Both trending and ranging |
| **Default Settings** | 12, 26, 9 (fast EMA, slow EMA, signal) | 14-period |
| **Visual Complexity** | Higher (MACD line, signal line, histogram) | Lower (single line with levels) |

## Key Differences

**Bounded vs unbounded.** RSI is locked between 0 and 100, giving clear overbought (70) and oversold (30) thresholds. MACD has no fixed boundaries, so it cannot tell you whether price is "too high" or "too low" -- only whether momentum is accelerating or decelerating. This makes RSI better for identifying exhaustion and MACD better for identifying momentum direction.

**Signal mechanics.** MACD generates signals through crossovers: when the MACD line crosses the signal line, or when either crosses zero. RSI generates signals when it enters or exits overbought/oversold zones. These are fundamentally different signal types, which is exactly why they complement each other so well.

**Trend vs range.** MACD thrives in trending markets where moving averages separate cleanly. In choppy, range-bound conditions, MACD produces constant whipsaw crossovers. RSI handles ranging markets better because it can identify overbought/oversold extremes for mean-reversion entries.

**Divergence application.** Both indicators excel at [[divergence]] signals. MACD histogram divergence (price makes new high, histogram makes lower high) is a powerful early warning of trend exhaustion. RSI divergence works similarly but tends to fire earlier, sometimes too early.

## When to Use Each

**Use MACD when:**
- You need to identify the direction and strength of a trend
- Trading breakouts and momentum continuation setups
- Looking for trend reversal warnings via histogram divergence
- You want a visual read on whether momentum is expanding or contracting
- Trading on daily or weekly timeframes

**Use RSI when:**
- You need to identify overbought or oversold conditions
- Trading mean-reversion or pullback strategies
- You want a quick read on whether buyers or sellers are in control
- Looking for divergence signals with clear reference levels
- Trading any timeframe from intraday to monthly

**Use both together (highly recommended).** The classic combination uses MACD for trend direction and RSI for entry timing. Example: MACD above zero confirms bullish momentum, then wait for RSI to dip below 30 and reverse upward for the entry. This filters out many false signals that either indicator produces alone.

## Verdict

Neither MACD nor RSI is superior -- they answer different questions. MACD tells you which direction momentum is moving and how strongly. RSI tells you whether the market has pushed too far in one direction. The real power is in combining them: use MACD to establish the trend bias and RSI to time entries. If forced to choose only one, RSI is more versatile because it works in both trending and ranging markets, but you would be leaving significant edge on the table by not using both.
