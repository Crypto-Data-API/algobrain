# Value vs Growth Investing

## Overview

[[value-investing]] and [[growth-investing]] are the two dominant equity investment styles. Value investors seek stocks trading below their intrinsic worth, buying companies the market has overlooked or unfairly punished. Growth investors seek companies with above-average revenue and earnings growth, willing to pay premium valuations for superior expansion. The two styles cycle in and out of favor, and understanding which environment suits each is essential for portfolio construction. See also [[momentum-value-combination]] for blending approaches.

## Comparison Table

| Dimension | Value Investing | Growth Investing |
|-----------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Core Metric** | Low P/E, low P/B, high dividend yield | High revenue growth, high earnings growth |
| **Valuation Tolerance** | Demands a discount to intrinsic value | Pays premium for growth potential |
| **Risk Profile** | Lower volatility, margin of safety | Higher volatility, valuation risk |
| **Typical Sectors** | Financials, energy, utilities, industrials | Technology, healthcare, consumer discretionary |
| **Income** | Often pays dividends | Rarely pays dividends (reinvests profits) |
| **Holding Period** | Years (waiting for re-rating) | Months to years (riding growth trajectory) |
| **Biggest Risk** | Value trap (cheap for a reason) | Multiple compression (growth slows, stock crashes) |
| **Recovery Performance** | Outperforms early in economic recovery | Outperforms during economic expansion |
| **Rate Sensitivity** | Benefits from rising rates (financials) | Hurt by rising rates (longer duration) |
| **Famous Practitioners** | Warren Buffett, Benjamin Graham, Seth Klarman | Cathie Wood, Peter Lynch, Philip Fisher |

## Key Differences

**What you are paying for.** Value investors pay for what a company is worth today, ideally at a discount. They want assets, cash flows, and earnings at a bargain price. Growth investors pay for what a company will be worth tomorrow, accepting a premium for the trajectory. They want revenue acceleration, expanding margins, and market share gains.

**The margin of safety concept.** Value investing is built on Benjamin Graham's concept of margin of safety -- buying a dollar for fifty cents. If your analysis is wrong, the discount protects you. Growth investing has no such buffer. If growth decelerates, the stock can lose 50-80% as the premium valuation evaporates.

**Market cycle behavior.** Value and growth trade leadership in roughly decade-long cycles. Value dominated 2000-2007 after the dot-com bubble burst. Growth dominated 2010-2021 driven by tech and low interest rates. Value staged a comeback in 2022 as rates rose sharply. Understanding where you are in this cycle matters enormously for style allocation.

**Interest rate impact.** Growth stocks are long-duration assets because their value depends heavily on future earnings discounted back to today. When interest rates rise, the discount rate increases and those future earnings are worth less in present-value terms. Value stocks, particularly financials, often benefit from higher rates through improved lending margins.

## When to Use Each

**Lean toward value when:**
- Interest rates are rising or elevated
- The economy is in early recovery from recession
- Growth stock valuations are at historical extremes
- You want dividend income and lower portfolio volatility
- Market sentiment is shifting from speculation to fundamentals

**Lean toward growth when:**
- Interest rates are falling or low
- The economy is in mid-cycle expansion
- Innovation is accelerating in key sectors
- You have a long time horizon and can tolerate volatility
- Earnings growth is scarce, making it worth a premium

**Blend both styles.** A portfolio that includes both value and growth exposure benefits from style diversification. Some of the best investors, like Peter Lynch, ignored the style labels entirely and simply bought "growth at a reasonable price" (GARP) -- a natural fusion of both philosophies.

## Verdict

Over very long periods (50+ years), value has historically outperformed growth by a small margin, driven by the value premium identified in academic research. However, the last fifteen years have challenged that record as technology-driven growth companies have dominated. The practical answer is to hold both, tilting toward whichever style is favored by the current rate environment and economic cycle. Pure value investors risk missing the next great growth company. Pure growth investors risk catastrophic losses when sentiment shifts. Balance is the answer, and [[momentum-value-combination]] offers a systematic framework for achieving it.
