A resistance level is a price zone where selling pressure historically exceeds buying pressure, causing an uptrend to stall or reverse. Resistance forms at prices where prior buyers look to take profits, where short sellers initiate positions, or where previous bag-holders seek to exit at breakeven. It is the upside mirror of [[support]]: where support is a floor of latent demand, resistance is a ceiling of latent [[supply-and-demand|supply]]. Like support, resistance levels gain significance through repeated tests and high-[[volume]] reactions, and the two are studied together as [[support-and-resistance]].

## Why Resistance Forms

Resistance is, at root, a [[supply-and-demand]] phenomenon expressed on a chart. Three overlapping groups create selling pressure at a level:

- **Profit-takers:** earlier buyers who are now sitting on gains and sell into strength.
- **Breakeven sellers:** traders who bought higher, got trapped, and finally exit "to get their money back" when price returns to their entry.
- **Fresh short sellers:** traders who view the level as expensive and initiate new short positions.

Together these create a cluster of resting sell orders that can absorb buying demand and cap the advance. The level becomes a self-reinforcing reference point because so many participants are watching the same price.

## Types of Resistance

| Type | How it is drawn | Strength signal |
|------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Horizontal | Fixed price of prior swing highs / consolidation ceiling / all-time high | More touches, higher volume |
| Trendline | Connects a series of descending highs in a downtrend | More touches along the line |
| [[moving-average]] | A declining/flat 50-day or 200-day MA acting as a ceiling | Confluence with horizontal level |
| Psychological | Round numbers ($100, $50,000 for [[bitcoin\|BTC]]) | Visible cluster in [[order-book]] |
| Fibonacci / measured-move | Extension levels projected from a prior swing | Confluence with other types |
| Volume profile | High-volume nodes / point of control above price | Large traded volume at the price |

An asset trading below its 200-day [[moving-average]] will often struggle to reclaim it without a significant catalyst, which is why that line is one of the most-watched dynamic resistance levels.

## Breakouts and Volume Confirmation

A [[breakout]] above resistance is generally interpreted as a bullish signal, indicating that buyers have overwhelmed the supply zone. However, not all breakouts are genuine. **Volume confirmation** is critical: a breakout on above-average [[volume]] suggests real conviction, while a breakout on thin volume is more likely to fail and reverse back below resistance -- a "fakeout" or **bull trap**. Many [[breakout-trading|breakout traders]] require a *close* above resistance (not merely an intraday wick) and a volume surge before entering. Once resistance is decisively broken, it frequently becomes [[support]] on subsequent pullbacks -- the **polarity principle** (also called role reversal).

## Worked Example

A stock has rejected three times near **$50** over six months -- a clear horizontal resistance zone, roughly $49.50-$50.50.

- **Range-fade play:** a trader shorts near $50 with a stop at $51 (just above the zone) targeting a return to range support at $44. Risk ≈ $1; reward ≈ $6 -- a ~6:1 [[risk-reward-ratio|risk/reward]] if it holds.
- **Breakout play:** price closes at $52 on double the average [[volume]]. A breakout trader buys the close, placing a stop back below $49.50 (former resistance, now expected [[support]]), targeting a measured move equal to the prior range height (~$6) for a ~$58 objective.
- **Failed breakout (bull trap):** price pokes to $50.80 intraday on thin volume, then closes back at $49. Breakout buyers are trapped; their stops below the range can fuel a sharp move down.

The same $50 level offers three different trades depending on *how* price interacts with it -- the essence of reading resistance.

## How Traders Use Resistance

- **Target placement:** book profits on longs into resistance, where supply is expected.
- **Short entries:** initiate or add to shorts near resistance in a range or downtrend.
- **Stop placement:** for shorts, park stops just above resistance, where a break invalidates the thesis.
- **Breakout triggers:** enter longs on a confirmed close above resistance with volume.
- **Confluence filtering:** prefer levels where multiple resistance types stack up (see below).

## Practical Considerations and Pitfalls

Resistance levels are **zones, not precise prices** -- "resistance at $50" may mean selling between $49.50 and $50.50; treat them as areas, not lines. Multiple forms of resistance converging at one price (a horizontal level, a declining trendline, and a Fibonacci extension all near $50) create **confluence**, which strengthens the zone. Resistance analysis is used in [[swing-trading]], [[day-trading]], and [[position-trading]] alike.

Common pitfalls:

- **Trading wicks, not closes:** an intraday spike through resistance that closes back inside is not a breakout.
- **Ignoring volume:** breakouts without a volume surge fail disproportionately often (bull traps).
- **Over-precise lines:** demanding an exact price ignores the zonal nature of the level and leads to whipsawed stops.
- **Stale levels:** a months-old high in a changed regime may no longer matter; recent, repeatedly tested levels carry more weight.
- **Hindsight bias:** it is easy to "see" resistance after the fact; the discipline is acting on it in real time with predefined rules.
