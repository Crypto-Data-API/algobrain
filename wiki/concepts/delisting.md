**Delisting** is the removal of a stock from a public exchange such as the [[nasdaq|Nasdaq]] or [[nyse|NYSE]]. After delisting, the shares no longer trade on that exchange — but this does **not automatically** mean they are worthless. The crucial distinction is **why** the stock was delisted: a **voluntary** delisting (a takeover or going-private deal) usually pays shareholders out, whereas an **involuntary** delisting (failing the exchange's listing standards) is typically a distress signal that pushes the stock into the murkier over-the-counter (OTC) market.

## Why a stock gets delisted

**Voluntary delisting** happens for benign or strategic reasons:

- **Acquisition / merger.** When a company is bought (see [[mergers-and-acquisitions]] and [[tender-offer]]), it delists because it is now a subsidiary — shareholders received cash or acquirer stock.
- **Going private.** A buyout (often private-equity led) takes the company off the public market; remaining holders are cashed out at the deal price.
- **Switching venues or cost.** A company may move to another exchange, or "go dark" to escape the cost and disclosure burden of a listing.

**Involuntary delisting** is enforced by the exchange when a company breaches **continued-listing standards**:

- **Minimum bid price.** The major US exchanges require a stock to stay above **$1.00**; trading below it (commonly for 30 consecutive trading days) triggers a deficiency notice. A [[stock-split|reverse split]] is the standard tool to cure this — see [[stock-split]].
- **Market value, float, or shareholder count** falling below thresholds.
- **Bankruptcy, prolonged losses, or failure to file** financial reports on time.

The company usually gets a **cure period** (often 180 days, sometimes extendable) to regain compliance before it is actually removed.

## The delisting process (mechanics)

Involuntary delisting is not instant — it runs through a defined sequence, using Nasdaq as the illustrative example (NYSE is similar):

1. **Deficiency notice.** The exchange sends the company a formal notice when it breaches a continued-listing standard (e.g., closing bid below **$1.00 for 30 consecutive business days**). Nasdaq lists its issuers on three tiers — **Global Select Market**, **Global Market**, and **Capital Market** — with progressively lighter standards; the bid-price rule applies across all three.
2. **Cure / compliance period.** For a bid-price deficiency, Nasdaq grants **180 calendar days** to regain compliance, and a company on the Capital Market that meets other criteria can get a **second 180 days**. Compliance is regained when the bid closes at **$1.00 or above for a minimum of 10 consecutive business days**.
3. **Reverse split cure.** The near-universal fix is a [[stock-split|reverse split]] (e.g., 1-for-10) to lift the nominal price back over $1 — cosmetic unless the underlying business also recovers.
4. **Delisting determination and appeal.** If the company cannot cure, the exchange issues a delisting determination. The company may **appeal to a Hearings Panel**, which can grant an extension (up to ~180 more days) if it presents a credible compliance plan.
5. **Form 25 and deregistration.** The exchange (or company) files **Form 25** with the SEC to remove the security from listing. The company may also file **Form 15** to deregister and suspend SEC reporting duties — the formal act of "**going dark**."

## What it means for you as a shareholder

- **You still own the shares.** Delisting changes *where* (and how easily) a stock trades, not who owns it.
- **It may keep trading OTC.** Many involuntarily delisted stocks move to OTC markets / "pink sheets," where liquidity is thin, spreads are wide, disclosure is weaker, and many brokers restrict or charge more to trade them (see [[liquidity]]).
- **In a takeover or going-private deal, you get paid.** Cash deals pay the agreed price; stock deals convert your shares into the acquirer's.
- **In bankruptcy, equity is usually last in line.** If delisting accompanies a Chapter 11 / insolvency, common shareholders are typically wiped out or recover little — debt holders rank ahead of equity.
- **Index and fund effects.** A delisted name is dropped from indices, forcing passive funds to sell — which can add downward pressure around the event.

## Hypothetical example

*Illustrative only.* Suppose NanoCorp has bled lower for a year and now trades at $0.40. After 30 trading days below $1.00, Nasdaq issues a deficiency notice with a 180-day cure window. NanoCorp does a **1-for-10 reverse split**, lifting the price to ~$4.00 and regaining compliance — but the business is unchanged, and within a year it drifts back under $1.00. This time it fails to cure, is delisted, and the shares migrate to OTC pink sheets, where the bid-ask spread widens to 10%+ and your broker flags the position as "no longer marginable." You still own the shares, but exiting now costs far more and finding a buyer is harder. Contrast this with a *voluntary* delisting: if instead a private-equity firm had bought NanoCorp at $6.00 cash, you would simply have received $6.00 per share and the listing would close cleanly.

## Notable real-world delistings

- **Lehman Brothers (2008).** After filing the largest bankruptcy in US history on September 15, 2008, Lehman was delisted from the NYSE; the stock collapsed from double digits to pennies on the pink sheets and common holders were effectively wiped out — the archetype of a distress delisting.
- **Hertz (2020).** Hertz filed Chapter 11 in May 2020 and was delisted from the NYSE to OTC. Unusually, a rebound in used-car values let the reorganized company pay equity holders in 2021 — a reminder that delisting via bankruptcy *usually*, but not always, means a wipeout.
- **Luckin Coffee (2020).** The Chinese coffee chain was delisted from Nasdaq in mid-2020 after disclosing that roughly **$300 million** of sales had been fabricated — a fraud-driven involuntary delisting. It later continued trading OTC and rebuilt.
- **DiDi Global (2021–2022).** The ride-hailing giant voluntarily delisted from the NYSE within months of its June 2021 IPO under pressure from Chinese regulators — a geopolitical, not financial, delisting.
- **Chinese ADRs and the HFCAA.** The **Holding Foreign Companies Accountable Act (2020)** threatened to delist US-listed Chinese firms (Alibaba, JD.com, etc.) if the PCAOB could not inspect their auditors for a period of years. A December 2022 US–China audit-inspection agreement averted mass delisting, showing how listing status can hinge on cross-border regulation rather than company fundamentals.
- **SPAC liquidations (2022–2023).** Hundreds of blank-check SPACs that failed to complete a merger by their deadline delisted and returned trust cash (~$10/share) to holders — a clean, benign form of delisting where investors are paid out.

## What to watch for

- **Read the reason.** A delisting tied to a buyout is a payout event; one tied to a $1 breach or late filings is a red flag.
- **Deficiency notice + cure period.** A notice is a warning, not a verdict — many companies cure it (often with a reverse split). Watch whether the *underlying* problem is fixed or just the symptom.
- **Where it will trade next.** Confirm whether the stock continues OTC and whether your broker still supports it.
- **Bankruptcy vs. operations.** Delisting alongside insolvency usually means equity is impaired; delisting from a healthy take-private means a cash exit.
- **Liquidity tax.** OTC names carry wide spreads, sparse data, and weaker disclosure — size and exit accordingly.
