Corporate governance is the system of rules, practices, processes, and incentives by which a company is directed and controlled. It governs the relationships between a firm's management, its board of directors, its shareholders, and other stakeholders, and exists primarily to resolve the **agency problem** — the conflict that arises when the people who run a company (managers) are not the people who own it (shareholders). For investors, governance quality is a first-order risk factor: weak governance reliably precedes value destruction through self-dealing, dilutive [[capital-raise|capital raises]], empire-building acquisitions, and accounting fraud.

## The Agency Problem

The intellectual foundation of corporate governance is the principal–agent problem (Jensen & Meckling, 1976). Shareholders (principals) delegate control to managers (agents), but managers have their own incentives — job security, compensation, prestige, perks — that do not always align with maximising shareholder value. Governance mechanisms are the set of tools that constrain managers and re-align their incentives:

- **Internal mechanisms** — the board of directors, audit committee, executive compensation design, internal controls, and shareholder voting rights.
- **External mechanisms** — the market for corporate control (takeovers), activist investors, regulators, auditors, and the financial press.

## Key Components

### Board of Directors
The board is the primary internal check on management. Investors assess:
- **Independence** — the proportion of directors with no material ties to management. Independent directors are more willing to fire a CEO or block a bad deal.
- **Separation of Chair and CEO** — combining the roles concentrates power and weakens oversight.
- **Board skills and tenure** — relevant expertise, but also turnover (very long-tenured boards become captured by management).

### Executive Compensation
Pay should reward long-term value creation, not short-term metrics that can be gamed. Red flags include compensation tied to revenue or EPS (easily inflated by [[share-buybacks]] or acquisitions) rather than return on capital, and option grants repriced after the stock falls.

### Shareholder Rights
- **One-share-one-vote** vs. **dual-class structures** (founders keep super-voting shares — common in tech: Meta, Alphabet). Dual-class entrenches insiders and removes the takeover threat.
- **Anti-takeover defences** — poison pills, staggered boards, and golden parachutes insulate management from accountability.
- **Disclosure and transparency** — timely, complete, and honest reporting. Selective disclosure is both a governance failure and, in many jurisdictions, illegal ([[insider-trading]]).

### Audit and Controls
An independent external auditor and a functioning audit committee guard against accounting manipulation. Auditor changes, restatements, and material weaknesses in internal controls are serious warning signs.

## Trading Relevance

Governance is the "G" in ESG and a recurring theme in [[fundamental-analysis]]. A trader or investor uses governance assessment to:

- **Avoid value traps** — a cheap stock with predatory management may stay cheap or get cheaper as insiders extract value. Fred McNaught emphasises that "management quality always matters," citing the EVN [[capital-raise]] where negative information was withheld from shareholders until after the raise closed — a governance failure that directly harmed existing holders (Source: [[fred-sam-session-2023-12-05]]).
- **Detect fraud risk early** — Enron, Wirecard, Luckin Coffee, and FTX were all governance failures before they were accounting or solvency failures. Concentrated control, weak boards, related-party transactions, and resistance to scrutiny are common antecedents.
- **Anticipate dilution** — boards that repeatedly approve dilutive equity issuance or generous option pools transfer value from existing shareholders to insiders.
- **Price the activist option** — poorly governed but operationally sound companies attract activist investors, whose campaigns to replace boards or split companies can unlock value.

Empirical research (Gompers, Ishii & Metrick, 2003) found that firms with stronger shareholder rights ("democracies") historically outperformed those with weak rights ("dictatorships") on a risk-adjusted basis, though the effect has been debated and may have weakened as governance practices converged.

## Regulatory Framework

Governance standards are codified in securities law and stock-exchange listing rules. The **Sarbanes-Oxley Act (2002)**, passed after Enron and WorldCom, mandated CEO/CFO certification of financials, independent audit committees, and stronger internal controls. The **Dodd-Frank Act (2010)** added "say-on-pay" shareholder votes and clawback provisions. National codes (e.g., the UK Corporate Governance Code's "comply or explain" model) operate alongside formal [[regulation]].
