# Commodity Super Cycle

Long-duration (15-25 year) cycles in broad commodity prices driven by structural shifts in demand and supply. Super cycles are characterized by a sustained period of above-trend price increases followed by an equally prolonged period of below-trend prices. Unlike shorter business cycle fluctuations (3-5 years), super cycles reflect fundamental transformations in the global economy -- industrialization of major economies, wars, infrastructure buildouts -- that overwhelm existing supply capacity for extended periods before the inevitable supply response catches up (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]]).

## Overview

The concept of commodity super cycles was formalized by Bilge Erten and José Antonio Ocampo (2013), who identified four complete super cycles since the late 1800s using a long-run commodity price index. Each cycle shares a common pattern:

1. **Demand shock**: A new, large source of demand emerges (industrializing economy, wartime mobilization, infrastructure boom).
2. **Supply lag**: Existing commodity production capacity cannot meet new demand. [[capex-cycle|Capex]] ramps up, but new mines, wells, and farms take 5-15 years to reach production.
3. **Price boom**: Prices rise above long-run trend as demand outpaces supply. The boom phase typically lasts 10-15 years.
4. **Overinvestment**: High prices incentivize massive capital expenditure. Eventually, new supply floods the market.
5. **Supply glut and bust**: New capacity comes online just as the demand shock fades or is satisfied. Prices collapse below trend. Capex is cut. The bust phase lasts 10-15 years.
6. **Underinvestment**: Low prices discourage new investment, setting the stage for the next supply deficit (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]]).

## Historical Super Cycles

### Cycle 1: Late 1800s-1910s (US Industrialization + Urbanization)
- **Demand driver**: Rapid US industrialization, railroad construction, urbanization
- **Key commodities**: Steel, copper, coal, lumber
- **Peak**: ~1910-1917 (amplified by WWI demand)
- **Bust**: 1920s commodities deflation

### Cycle 2: 1930s-1950s (Rearmament + Post-War Reconstruction)
- **Demand driver**: WWII military production, followed by European and Japanese reconstruction, Marshall Plan
- **Key commodities**: Steel, copper, oil, rubber, agricultural goods
- **Peak**: ~1950-1951 (Korean War spike)
- **Bust**: 1950s-1960s (reconstruction complete, new capacity online)

### Cycle 3: 1960s-1980s (Oil Shocks + Inflation)
- **Demand driver**: Rapid global GDP growth, energy-intensive industrialization, OPEC supply restrictions
- **Key commodities**: [[crude-oil]] (10x price increase 1970-1980), gold (25x), silver, agricultural goods
- **Peak**: 1980 (oil at $40/bbl, gold at $850/oz -- both in nominal terms)
- **Bust**: 1980s-1990s (Volcker rate hikes crushed inflation, North Sea/Alaska oil came online, agricultural green revolution increased yields)

### Cycle 4: 2000-2011 (China Industrialization)
- **Demand driver**: China's entry into the WTO (2001), urbanization of 300+ million people, infrastructure buildout (housing, roads, high-speed rail, power grid)
- **Key commodities**: Iron ore (10x), [[copper]] (6x), [[crude-oil]] (10x from 1998 low to 2008 peak), coal, nickel, zinc, soybeans
- **Peak**: 2008 (pre-GFC) and 2011 (post-stimulus secondary peak)
- **Bust**: 2011-2020 (China growth deceleration, massive supply from Australia/Brazil/US shale, commodity capex cycle peaked ~2012-2013)
(Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## The Current Debate: Cycle 5?

Several analysts and commodity bulls argue that a fifth super cycle began around 2020, driven by:

### Bull Case (New Super Cycle Underway)
1. **Energy transition demand**: [[copper]], nickel, lithium, cobalt, rare earths required in massive quantities for EVs, batteries, solar panels, wind turbines, grid upgrades. The IEA estimates copper demand for clean energy must double by 2040.
2. **Underinvestment legacy**: Commodity capex was slashed 2015-2020 following the bust. Mining capex fell ~40% from 2012 peaks. Oil & gas E&P spending fell ~50% from 2014 peaks. This created a structural supply deficit that will take years to address.
3. **Deglobalization/reshoring**: Supply chain restructuring post-COVID and post-Russia/Ukraine requires building new infrastructure (semiconductor fabs, LNG terminals, defense industry) -- all commodity-intensive.
4. **Fiscal expansion**: Government spending on infrastructure (US IRA, EU Green Deal, China stimulus) directly drives commodity demand.

### Bear Case (No Super Cycle)
1. **Super cycles are only visible in hindsight** -- what looks like a cycle in progress may be a temporary price shock.
2. **Technology disrupts cycles**: Shale revolution (2014-2016) broke the oil super cycle well ahead of schedule. Similar technological shifts (substitution, recycling, efficiency) could limit the upside.
3. **China is decelerating**: The demand impulse from China's industrialization is fading; no comparable demand source is emerging (India's commodity intensity is lower due to a services-oriented economy).
4. **Financialization effects**: Post-2004 commodity financialization (passive index funds, CTAs) may distort price dynamics, making cycle analysis less applicable.
5. **Demand destruction from high prices**: Unlike the 2000s, governments and corporations are actively pursuing efficiency and substitution to reduce commodity intensity (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]]).

## Trading Implications

### For Long-Term Investors
- **During the upswing** (demand > supply response): long bias to commodities, commodity equities (miners, E&P), commodity-exporting country currencies/bonds
- **Identifying the peak**: watch for capital expenditure peaking, new supply announcements accumulating, demand growth decelerating
- **During the downswing** (oversupply): avoid long-only commodity exposure, favor commodity consumers over producers, underweight commodity-linked economies

### For Systematic Strategies
- [[commodity-momentum]] strategies tend to perform well during super cycle phases (strong trending behavior)
- [[commodity-value-strategy]] signals can be misleading during super cycles -- a commodity may appear "expensive" on a 5-year basis but still have further to run
- [[commodity-carry-strategy]] tends to work well during the tight-supply phase of the cycle (persistent backwardation)

### Key Indicators to Monitor
- **[[capex-cycle|Mining/E&P capex]]** relative to revenue: low capex-to-revenue ratio = underinvestment = bullish for supply deficit
- **Stocks-to-use ratios** across commodities ([[supply-demand-balance]])
- **China fixed asset investment** and infrastructure spending
- **Global manufacturing PMIs** -- leading indicator of industrial commodity demand
- **New mine/well approvals** -- long lead-time indicator of future supply

## Limitations of Super Cycle Analysis

- **Narrative bias**: It is easy to construct a compelling story for a new super cycle at any point (and analysts have incentive to do so for client engagement).
- **Timing is impossible**: Even if a super cycle is underway, the upswing can last 10-15 years -- you cannot time the exact peak or trough.
- **Not all commodities participate equally**: Each cycle favors different commodities (Cycle 4 was dominated by iron ore and copper; Cycle 5, if real, may be dominated by battery metals).
- **Policy can break the cycle**: Government intervention (strategic reserve releases, export controls, price caps) can distort natural cycle dynamics (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]]).
