A share buyback occurs when a company repurchases its own shares on the open market, reducing the total shares outstanding. This mechanically increases [[earnings-per-share]] even without earnings growth, and can signal management confidence in the company's intrinsic value. Buybacks are an alternative to [[dividend-yield|dividends]] as a method of returning capital to shareholders, with different tax implications depending on jurisdiction. Fred McNaught views buybacks positively when funded from [[free-cash-flow]] but critically when funded by debt, and notes they can mask underlying earnings stagnation by artificially boosting per-share metrics.

## Mechanics and Scale

Companies typically execute buybacks through open-market purchases over an extended period, though accelerated share repurchase (ASR) programs allow immediate execution via an investment bank intermediary. In the US, share buybacks have exceeded $800 billion annually in recent years, surpassing dividends as the primary method of shareholder returns among S&P 500 companies. Buybacks reduce shares outstanding, which increases [[earnings-per-share]], return on equity, and other per-share metrics even if total earnings are flat. This makes buybacks an important adjustment factor when analyzing a company's fundamental growth -- analysts should distinguish between organic earnings growth and per-share growth driven by share count reduction.

## When Buybacks Create Value

A buyback creates value when the company repurchases shares below their intrinsic value -- effectively, each remaining shareholder's claim on the business grows at a discount. Warren Buffett has articulated this framework clearly: buybacks at or below intrinsic value are beneficial, while buybacks above intrinsic value destroy shareholder wealth. The best buybacks are funded from [[free-cash-flow]], executed when the stock is trading at a significant discount to fair value, and undertaken by companies that lack higher-return reinvestment opportunities. The worst buybacks are funded by debt, timed at peak valuations (often coinciding with management stock option vesting), and used as a substitute for genuine capital investment.

## Criticism and Risks

Critics argue that the modern buyback wave represents financial engineering rather than genuine value creation. Companies that load up on debt to fund buybacks can weaken their balance sheets and become vulnerable in downturns -- several airlines and retailers that aggressively bought back shares in 2018-2019 required government bailouts during COVID-19. The US introduced a 1% excise tax on buybacks in 2023 (Inflation Reduction Act) as a partial policy response. Additionally, buybacks can be used to offset [[share-dilution]] from employee stock compensation, meaning that much of the reported buyback activity simply prevents shares outstanding from rising rather than actually reducing them. Investors should examine both gross and net buyback figures to understand the true impact.
