An Initial Public Offering (IPO) is the process by which a private company lists its shares on a public stock exchange for the first time. The company issues a prospectus, sets an offer price (often with the help of underwriters), and sells shares to institutional and retail investors. Fred McNaught contrasts the IPO environment of the 1980s, when he experienced an 80%+ success rate on IPO allocations, with the modern market where IPOs are considerably riskier and often priced to benefit insiders rather than new investors. He advises careful scrutiny of the prospectus, the use-of-funds breakdown, and whether the listing is primarily a [[capital-raise]] or an exit opportunity for existing shareholders.

## The IPO Process

The traditional IPO process follows a structured path. The company files a registration statement (S-1 in the US, a prospectus in Australia) disclosing financials, risks, management, and use of proceeds. **Underwriters** -- typically investment banks -- manage the offering: they advise on pricing, conduct a **roadshow** to market the deal to institutional investors, and run the **bookbuilding** process to gauge demand and set the final offer price. The underwriters often purchase the shares from the company and resell them to investors, earning the **spread** (typically 5-7% of proceeds). On listing day, shares begin trading on the exchange. The underwriter may exercise a **greenshoe option** (overallotment) to stabilize the stock price in early trading.

## IPO Pricing and the "Pop"

IPOs are frequently underpriced -- the offer price is set below what the market is willing to pay, resulting in a first-day price "pop" that benefits institutional investors who received allocations. Research by Jay Ritter shows average first-day returns of 15-20% for US IPOs, representing a significant wealth transfer from the issuing company (which received less capital than the market would have paid) to well-connected investors. Retail investors typically receive small or no allocations in hot IPOs and often buy on the open market after the pop, facing worse odds. **Lock-up periods** (typically 90-180 days) prevent insiders from selling immediately after listing; the expiration of lock-ups often creates selling pressure and is a known [[catalyst]] for price declines.

## Alternatives to Traditional IPOs

Several alternatives have emerged. **Direct listings** (used by Spotify in 2018, Coinbase in 2021) skip the underwriting process -- existing shares are simply listed on the exchange with no new capital raised, no lock-up, and no underwriter spread. **SPACs** (Special Purpose Acquisition Companies) create a publicly listed blank-check company that subsequently merges with a private target, effectively taking it public through a reverse merger. SPACs surged in 2020-2021 but most have underperformed, with academic studies showing average post-merger losses of 30-50% within a year. In Australia, **back-door listings** (reverse takeovers of dormant shell companies) have historically been common in the mining sector, though the [[asx|ASX]] has tightened rules around these.

## Trading Relevance

IPOs create a series of recurring, structurally-driven tradeable events rather than a single clean signal:

- **First-day pop and allocation asymmetry.** The 15-20% average first-day return (Ritter) accrues almost entirely to allocated institutions, not to retail buying in the open market. Buying a hot IPO *after* the pop has historically been a poor expected-value bet.
- **Lock-up expiry.** The 90-180 day [[catalyst]] when insiders can first sell is a well-documented source of supply pressure and negative drift; it is one of the cleanest calendar-driven short setups, though increasingly arbitraged.
- **Long-run underperformance.** IPOs as a class tend to underperform seasoned peers over 3-5 years — a robust anomaly covered in [[ipo-underperformance]] — consistent with issuers timing offerings to windows of high sentiment.
- **Float dynamics.** A small post-IPO [[float]] amplifies volatility and borrow costs, making early shorting expensive and squeeze-prone.
- **Structure matters.** Direct listings (no lock-up, no fresh capital) and SPACs (post-merger underperformance of 30-50% in academic studies) carry different risk profiles than traditional underwritten IPOs.
