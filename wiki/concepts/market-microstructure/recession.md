A recession is a significant decline in economic activity spread across the economy, lasting more than a few months. The popular rule of thumb is two consecutive quarters of negative [[gdp|GDP]] growth, but the official US arbiter — the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) — uses a broader set of criteria including [[employment]], industrial production, real personal income, and wholesale/retail sales. For traders, recessions are the most consequential macro events: they destroy corporate earnings, trigger [[fed-policy|Fed]] easing cycles, and produce the largest equity drawdowns — but also create the best buying opportunities of the decade.

## How Recessions Start

Recessions don't appear from nowhere. They are typically triggered by one or more of:

- **[[fed-policy|Monetary tightening]]** — The Fed raises [[interest-rates]] to fight [[inflation]], credit conditions tighten, borrowing slows, and the economy eventually cools too much. This is the most common trigger (e.g., 1980, 1981-82, 2007-08 cycles).
- **[[credit-cycle|Credit crunch]]** — Excessive lending during expansion leads to rising defaults, bank losses, and a sudden contraction in credit availability. The [[2008-global-financial-crisis]] is the canonical example.
- **External shock** — Oil price spikes (1973, 1979), pandemics (2020), financial contagion, or geopolitical events that disrupt economic activity.
- **Asset bubble collapse** — When inflated asset prices correct violently, wealth destruction reduces spending and investment (e.g., [[dot-com-bubble]], 2008 housing).
- **Fiscal contraction** — Government austerity (spending cuts or tax hikes) at the wrong time can tip a fragile economy into recession, as European austerity demonstrated in 2011-2012.

In practice, recessions usually involve multiple reinforcing factors. A rate-hike cycle that exposes [[credit-cycle|credit excesses]] while an external shock hits is the classic recipe.

## Leading Indicators

The most reliable recession predictors, ranked by historical track record:

| Indicator | Lead Time | Track Record |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| **[[yield-curve]] inversion** (2s/10s) | 6-24 months | Preceded every US recession since 1955 with one false signal |
| **Initial jobless claims** (rising trend) | 3-9 months | Weekly frequency makes it the most timely labor signal |
| **[[ism-pmi|ISM Manufacturing PMI]]** below 50 | 1-6 months | Signals manufacturing contraction |
| **Conference Board LEI** declining 3+ months | 3-12 months | Composite of 10 leading indicators |
| **Credit spreads** (HY OAS > 600bps) | 3-12 months | Has preceded 85% of recessions within 12 months |
| **Corporate profit margins** rolling over | 6-12 months | Profits peak before revenue because costs rise first |
| **[[money-supply|M2]]** contraction | 12-18 months | Rare but powerful signal (occurred in 2022-2023) |

No single indicator is perfect. The most robust approach is monitoring a dashboard of multiple signals and looking for convergence.

## Recession Anatomy

Post-WWII US recessions have averaged about 10-11 months in duration, though they range from 2 months (COVID, 2020) to 18 months (2007-2009). The typical sequence:

1. **Pre-recession** — [[yield-curve]] inverts, leading indicators turn, stocks may still be rising on momentum
2. **Early recession** — GDP turns negative, [[employment|layoffs]] begin, consumer confidence drops, the Fed may still be tightening (policy operates with a lag)
3. **Mid-recession** — Unemployment rising sharply, earnings declining, credit tightening, Fed pivots to cutting rates aggressively
4. **Late recession / early recovery** — Maximum pessimism, but leading indicators bottom, Fed is fully accommodative, stocks begin to rally before the economy does

## Market Behavior During Recessions

**Equities** decline an average of 30-35% from peak to trough in recession-associated bear markets. However, markets are forward-looking — the S&P 500 has bottomed an average of 4-5 months *before* the recession ends. The worst time to sell is when the recession is already obvious.

**Bonds** rally as the Fed cuts rates and investors flee to safety. The 10-year Treasury yield typically falls 200-400bps during a recession.

**[[sector-rotation]]** shifts to defensive sectors: Utilities (XLU), Healthcare (XLV), and Consumer Staples (XLP) outperform, while Financials, Consumer Discretionary, and Technology underperform.

**Commodities** generally decline as demand falls, though [[gold]] often rallies as a safe haven.

**Credit spreads** widen dramatically — high yield spreads can blow out from 300bps to 800-1000+bps, creating opportunities for credit investors willing to buy at the peak of fear.

## Trading Recessions

Fred views recessions as buying opportunities — his principle of keeping [[cash-as-asset|cash as an asset]] ensures capital is available to buy quality stocks at depressed prices during downturns. The key insights for trading around recessions:

1. **Don't try to time the exact bottom** — markets bottom on maximum fear, not on good news. Dollar-cost averaging into high-quality names during the recession works better than trying to catch the knife.
2. **The first rally is the strongest** — the initial recovery rally off a recession bottom is typically 30-50% in the first 12 months. Missing the first few weeks can cost half the recovery.
3. **Quality over quantity** — companies with strong balance sheets, low debt, and essential products survive recessions. Leveraged, cyclical businesses can go bankrupt.
4. **Watch the Fed, not GDP** — the Fed pivot from tightening to easing is the most reliable buy signal. When the Fed starts cutting aggressively, the market bottom is usually near.
