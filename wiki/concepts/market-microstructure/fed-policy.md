Federal Reserve policy refers to the monetary policy decisions made by the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) that shape [[interest-rates]], credit conditions, and liquidity across all financial markets. The Fed's dual mandate is to promote maximum employment and stable prices (targeting approximately 2% inflation). For traders, few forces are more powerful -- the maxim "don't fight the Fed" reflects decades of evidence that monetary policy direction dominates asset class returns over multi-month timeframes.

## Primary Policy Tools

### Federal Funds Rate

The Fed's benchmark interest rate -- the overnight rate at which depository institutions lend reserves to each other. Changes to this rate ripple through the entire economy: mortgage rates, corporate borrowing costs, savings yields, and [[currency]] valuations all adjust. Rate hikes are contractionary (slow the economy, strengthen the dollar, pressure equity valuations), while rate cuts are expansionary (stimulate activity, weaken the dollar, boost equities and [[bond-market|bonds]]).

### Quantitative Easing / Tightening

When the federal funds rate reaches the zero lower bound, the Fed turns to [[quantitative-easing]] (QE) -- purchasing Treasury bonds and mortgage-backed securities to inject liquidity and suppress long-term yields. Quantitative tightening (QT) is the reverse: allowing bonds to mature or actively selling them, draining reserves from the system. QE tends to inflate asset prices across the board; QT creates headwinds, particularly for rate-sensitive sectors and growth stocks.

### Forward Guidance

The Fed uses public communication -- press conferences, FOMC meeting minutes, speeches by Fed governors -- to shape market expectations about future policy. This "open mouth operations" approach can move markets as powerfully as actual rate changes, because asset prices reflect *expected* future rates, not just current ones.

### The Discount Window and Emergency Facilities

The discount window provides short-term lending to banks at a penalty rate. In crises, the Fed can create emergency lending facilities (as it did in 2008 and 2020) to backstop specific markets -- corporate bonds, money markets, municipal securities -- acting as lender of last resort.

## FOMC Meeting Structure

The FOMC meets **8 times per year** on a pre-announced schedule (roughly every 6-7 weeks). Key outputs from each meeting:

- **Rate decision** -- the target range for the federal funds rate
- **Policy statement** -- language shifts ("considerable time," "patient," "data-dependent") are parsed by traders for directional clues
- **Dot plot** (quarterly) -- each FOMC member's anonymous projection for future rates; the median dot signals the committee's expected path
- **Summary of Economic Projections (SEP)** -- GDP growth, unemployment, inflation, and rate forecasts
- **Press conference** -- the Chair takes questions; often the most market-moving part of the event

## Hawkish vs. Dovish

| Stance | Meaning | Market Impact |
|--------|---------|---------------|
| **Hawkish** | Prioritizes fighting inflation; favors higher rates, tighter policy | Bearish for equities and bonds; bullish for USD |
| **Dovish** | Prioritizes supporting growth/employment; favors lower rates, easier policy | Bullish for equities and bonds; bearish for USD |

Market participants track not just the current stance but the *direction* of change. A hawkish Fed turning less hawkish (without cutting rates) can be enough to rally risk assets.

## CME FedWatch and Rate Expectations

The CME FedWatch tool derives implied probabilities of future rate changes from federal funds futures contracts. Markets reprice these probabilities in real time after every [[economic-indicators|economic data release]], Fed speech, or geopolitical event. Traders often say "the market is pricing in X cuts/hikes" -- this language comes directly from FedWatch. When actual decisions deviate from market pricing, volatility spikes.

## Trading Implications

Fed policy is central to [[sector-rotation]] and [[business-cycle]] positioning. Rate-sensitive sectors (Financials, Real Estate, Utilities) react most directly to rate changes. The [[yield-curve]] shape reflects market expectations about the Fed's future path. [[quantitative-easing|QE]] environments historically compress [[volatility]] and favor growth over value, while tightening cycles favor value, quality, and defensive sectors.
