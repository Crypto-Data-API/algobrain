The credit cycle is the expansion and contraction of access to credit over time. It amplifies the [[business-cycle]] — easy credit fuels booms beyond fundamentals, and credit contraction deepens downturns. Credit makes up the vast majority of economic transactions in the United States, with roughly $60 trillion in credit-based transactions compared to approximately $3 trillion in cash. Understanding where we are in the credit cycle is arguably more important for traders than GDP or employment data, because credit conditions determine the availability of leverage and the appetite for risk across every asset class.

## The Four Phases

The credit cycle moves through four distinct phases, each with characteristic market behavior:

### 1. Expansion

Banks loosen lending standards, credit growth accelerates, asset prices rise, and defaults are low. Confidence builds as borrowers and lenders both become more optimistic. Spreads tighten, leverage increases, and new financial products emerge to meet demand for yield. This phase can last years and tends to feed on itself — rising collateral values justify more lending, which pushes collateral values higher.

### 2. Peak

Lending standards are at their loosest. Leverage is at its highest. Risk premiums are compressed to historically tight levels. The "this time is different" narrative dominates financial media. Covenant-lite loans proliferate, underwriting quality deteriorates, and marginal borrowers gain access to credit they could not previously obtain. The peak is only identifiable in retrospect, but warning signs include record-low [[credit-default-swaps|CDS]] spreads, surging issuance of high-yield debt, and declining lending standards in the Fed's Senior Loan Officer Survey.

### 3. Contraction

Defaults begin rising, banks tighten lending standards, and credit becomes scarce. Asset prices fall as forced selling emerges. Borrowers who relied on refinancing discover they cannot roll over debt. The contraction phase is self-reinforcing: falling collateral values trigger margin calls and covenant breaches, which force more selling, which pushes collateral values lower. [[contagion]] spreads from the weakest borrowers to stronger ones as lenders pull back indiscriminately.

### 4. Trough

Maximum pessimism. Lending is effectively frozen for all but the highest-quality borrowers. Defaults peak. Distressed assets are available at deep discounts, but few have the capital or willingness to buy. This is where the greatest opportunities exist for investors with available capital and the conviction to deploy it. Credit troughs have historically been the best entry points for high-yield bonds, bank stocks, and distressed debt strategies.

## Minsky's Financial Instability Hypothesis

The most important framework for understanding credit cycles comes from economist Hyman Minsky, who argued that **stability itself breeds instability**. During prolonged periods of economic calm, borrowers, lenders, and regulators all become more complacent, leading to progressively more fragile financial structures.

Minsky identified three stages of financing that characterize the progression toward crisis:

- **Hedge finance** — Borrowers can repay both principal and interest from operating cash flows. This is the most stable form of finance. Early in a recovery, most borrowing falls into this category.

- **Speculative finance** — Borrowers can service interest payments from cash flows but must roll over (refinance) the principal at maturity. This structure is fragile because it depends on continued access to credit markets. As a credit expansion matures, more borrowing shifts to this category.

- **Ponzi finance** — Borrowers cannot even cover interest payments without rising asset prices or access to additional borrowing. They depend entirely on capital gains to service their obligations. By the peak of a credit cycle, a significant share of borrowing has become Ponzi finance.

The **Minsky Moment** is the point where over-leveraged borrowers are forced to begin selling assets to meet their obligations, triggering a cascade of forced liquidation. The term was popularized during the 2008 financial crisis but applies to every credit cycle peak. Once the Minsky Moment arrives, the transition from complacency to panic can be shockingly fast.

## Credit Spreads as Trading Signals

Credit spreads are the difference in yield between corporate bonds and risk-free government bonds. They are among the most reliable real-time indicators of credit cycle positioning.

| Indicator | What It Measures | Expansion Signal | Stress Signal | Data Source |
|-----------|-----------------|------------------|---------------|-------------|
| IG OAS (Investment Grade Option-Adjusted Spread) | Credit risk of high-quality corporates | Tightening below 100bps | Widening above 200bps | FRED: BAMLC0A0CM |
| HY OAS (High Yield Option-Adjusted Spread) | Credit risk of below-investment-grade borrowers | Below 350bps | Above 600bps (preceded 85% of recessions within 12 months) | FRED: BAMLH0A0HYM2 |
| TED Spread (LIBOR minus T-bill) | Interbank lending stress | Below 25bps | Above 100bps indicates banking system stress | FRED: TEDRATE |
| Senior Loan Officer Survey (SLOOS) | Bank lending standards | Net easing | Net tightening (leads recessions by 2-4 quarters) | Federal Reserve |

High yield spreads deserve particular attention. When the HY OAS exceeds 600 basis points, it has preceded roughly 85% of recessions within the following 12 months. Conversely, when HY spreads are below 300bps, it signals maximum complacency and is often a contrarian warning of excessive risk-taking.

## Ray Dalio's Debt Cycles

[[ray-dalio]] frames credit cycles as occurring on two timescales:

- **Short-term debt cycle** (5-8 years) — Maps roughly to the [[business-cycle]]. Credit expands, the economy overheats, the [[central-bank]] raises [[interest-rates]], credit contracts, the economy slows, the central bank cuts rates, and the cycle begins again.

- **Long-term debt cycle** (50-75 years) — Across multiple short-term cycles, total debt-to-GDP ratchets higher because each downturn is met with easier monetary policy and more debt. Eventually, debt burdens become so large that interest rates hit zero and conventional [[monetary-policy]] is exhausted. This is the "beautiful deleveraging" problem — resolving it requires some combination of austerity, debt restructuring, wealth transfers, and money printing.

Dalio argues the US entered the late stage of a long-term debt cycle after 2008, which explains why the [[federal-reserve]] has been unable to normalize interest rates or its balance sheet without triggering market disruptions.

## Credit Cycle and Asset Classes

Different asset classes perform best at different phases of the credit cycle:

| Phase | Favored Assets | Avoided Assets |
|-------|---------------|----------------|
| Early expansion | High yield bonds, financials, real estate, small caps | Treasuries, gold, cash |
| Late expansion | Quality stocks, short-duration credit | High yield, leveraged loans, speculative growth |
| Contraction | Treasuries, cash, gold, defensive equities | Credit, cyclicals, real estate |
| Trough / early recovery | Distressed debt, bank stocks, deep value | Treasuries (rates about to rise) |

The key trading insight is that credit conditions **lead** equity markets. Widening credit spreads typically precede equity market declines by weeks to months, making credit markets a valuable early warning system for equity traders.
