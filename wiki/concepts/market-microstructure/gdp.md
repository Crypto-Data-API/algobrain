Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is the total monetary value of all finished goods and services produced within a country's borders in a specific time period. It is the broadest single measure of economic health and serves as the anchor of the [[business-cycle]] — expansions are periods of rising real GDP, contractions are periods of falling real GDP. For traders, GDP data shapes the macro backdrop against which every asset class is priced.

## Components

The standard expenditure approach breaks GDP into four components:

**GDP = C + I + G + (X - M)**

| Component | Description | Share of US GDP |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| **C** — Consumer spending | Retail sales, services, durable goods (autos, appliances) | ~68% |
| **I** — Business investment | Capital expenditure, inventory changes, residential construction | ~18% |
| **G** — Government spending | Federal, state, and local expenditures (see [[fiscal-policy]]) | ~17% |
| **X - M** — Net exports | Exports minus imports; the US runs a persistent [[trade-balance|trade deficit]] | ~-3% |

Because consumer spending dominates, traders watch proxies like [[retail-sales]], consumer confidence, and [[employment]] data closely between GDP releases. A slowdown in consumer spending almost always drags GDP lower.

## How GDP Is Reported

The Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA) publishes US GDP on a staggered schedule each quarter:

1. **Advance estimate** — released ~1 month after the quarter ends. Most market-moving but least accurate.
2. **Second estimate** — released ~2 months after. Incorporates more complete data.
3. **Third estimate** — released ~3 months after. Considered the final reading for that quarter (though annual revisions follow).

Key reporting details:

- GDP growth is reported as an **annualized quarter-over-quarter rate**. A reported 2.0% means the economy grew at a pace that, if sustained for a full year, would produce 2.0% annual growth.
- **Revisions can be large** — the advance estimate has been revised by more than 1 percentage point in several recent cycles. Traders who overreact to the advance number sometimes give back gains on revisions.
- **Real GDP vs. nominal GDP** — real GDP strips out [[inflation]] using the GDP deflator. Nominal GDP can rise purely from price increases; real GDP isolates actual output growth.

## GDP and Markets

Markets do not wait for GDP prints to move — they are forward-looking. Several patterns matter for traders:

- **Stocks bottom before GDP troughs** and peak before GDP peaks. By the time GDP confirms a [[recession]], equity markets have often already priced in the downturn and begun recovering.
- **GDP surprise** — the difference between the actual print and the consensus forecast — drives short-term price action across equities, [[bonds]], [[forex]], and [[commodities]]. A beat lifts risk assets; a miss pressures them.
- **Growth rate vs. level** — a positive but decelerating growth rate (e.g., 3.5% to 2.0% to 1.0%) is often a bearish signal for equities, even though the economy is still expanding. Markets price the second derivative.
- **GDPNow and nowcasting** — the Atlanta Fed's GDPNow model and similar [[nowcasting]] tools provide real-time GDP estimates updated as component data arrives. These models have become essential for macro traders who need to position before the official release.

## Alternative Output Measures

GDP is published quarterly with a long lag. Traders supplement it with higher-frequency output gauges:

- **GDI (Gross Domestic Income)** — theoretically equal to GDP, since every dollar spent is income to someone. In practice, a persistent GDP-GDI divergence signals measurement problems and can revise in either direction.
- **Industrial production** — published monthly by the Federal Reserve. Covers manufacturing, mining, and utilities. More timely but narrower than GDP.
- **Real personal income** — a monthly proxy for consumer health, excluding [[transfer-payments|government transfer payments]].
- **PMI surveys** — ISM Manufacturing and Services indices provide diffusion-style readings of economic activity well ahead of GDP (see [[purchasing-managers-index]]).

## GDP and the Business Cycle

GDP is the primary metric used to map the phases of the [[business-cycle]]:

- **Expansion** — real GDP rising, typically accompanied by falling [[unemployment]] and rising corporate earnings.
- **Peak** — GDP growth rate tops out; leading indicators roll over.
- **Contraction** — real GDP falls. The rule-of-thumb definition of a [[recession]] is two consecutive quarters of negative real GDP growth, though the NBER Business Cycle Dating Committee uses broader criteria including [[employment]], industrial production, and real personal income.
- **Trough** — GDP bottoms; policy stimulus ([[monetary-policy]], [[fiscal-policy]]) and inventory restocking drive recovery.

Understanding where the economy sits in this cycle is central to [[macro-trading]] and [[sector-rotation]] strategies.

## Limitations

GDP is a powerful summary statistic but has well-known blind spots:

- **Inequality** — GDP can rise while median household income stagnates if gains concentrate at the top.
- **Debt sustainability** — high GDP growth funded by [[sovereign-debt|unsustainable government or private debt]] is fragile and can precede crises.
- **Quality of growth** — GDP counts disaster rebuilding and wasteful spending the same as productive investment.
- **Environmental costs** — resource depletion and pollution are not subtracted from GDP.
- **Lag** — by the time GDP is published, the data is 1-3 months old. Traders must rely on [[leading-indicators]] and nowcasting models to stay ahead.

Despite these limitations, GDP remains the single most referenced measure of economic output and is embedded in central bank reaction functions, fiscal policy decisions, and asset-pricing models worldwide.
