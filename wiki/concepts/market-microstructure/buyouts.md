A buyout is the acquisition of a company or a controlling interest in a company, often at a premium to the current market price. Buyouts can be friendly (board-recommended) or hostile, and may be funded through cash, shares, or a combination. Fred McNaught discusses tactics for shareholders when receiving buyout offers: evaluate whether the offer price reflects fair value, consider the acquirer's track record, and be wary of accepting the first offer as initial bids are often below final terms. He notes that [[sophisticated-investor|sophisticated investors]] sometimes have access to pre-bid information or off-market deals not available to retail shareholders.

## Leveraged Buyouts (LBOs)

A leveraged buyout is a specific type of acquisition where a [[private-equity]] firm acquires a company using a significant amount of borrowed money (typically 60-80% debt), with the target company's own assets and cash flows serving as collateral. The PE firm contributes a relatively small equity portion and uses the target's free cash flow to service and repay the debt over a 3-7 year holding period. The LBO model works best with companies that have stable, predictable cash flows, low existing debt, hard assets for collateral, and opportunities for operational improvement. Famous LBOs include KKR's $25 billion takeover of RJR Nabisco (1989), which was the largest LBO in history at the time and was chronicled in "Barbarians at the Gate," and Michael Dell's $24.4 billion take-private of Dell Technologies (2013).

## Types of Buyouts

**Management buyouts (MBOs)** occur when the existing management team acquires the company, often with PE backing. MBOs can present conflicts of interest since management may have an incentive to depress the share price before making their offer. **Take-private transactions** remove a publicly listed company from the stock exchange, eliminating public reporting obligations and the pressure of quarterly earnings. This can benefit companies that need time to restructure or invest in long-term initiatives without market scrutiny. **Strategic acquisitions** involve an operating company (not a financial buyer) acquiring a target for synergies, market share, or technology. These typically command higher premiums than financial buyouts because the acquirer can justify the price through cost savings or revenue synergies.

## Shareholder Considerations

For shareholders of a target company, a buyout offer requires careful evaluation. The initial bid premium (typically 20-40% above the pre-announcement price) may seem attractive but can still undervalue the business. Shareholders should assess: whether the offer price reflects the company's standalone value including future growth, whether competing bids are likely, whether the acquirer has a track record of completed deals or withdrawn offers, and the tax implications of accepting cash versus scrip. In Australia, the Corporations Act provides protections including independent expert reports and minimum acceptance conditions. Holding out for a higher bid can be rewarding but carries the risk that the offer lapses entirely.

## Trading Relevance

For traders, buyouts are a core [[special-situations|special situation]] and the basis of **merger arbitrage**: once a deal is announced, the target's share price typically jumps to just below the offer price, leaving a small spread to the deal price that closes at completion. The arbitrageur captures that spread as compensation for **deal risk** — the chance the deal breaks (regulatory block, financing failure, due-diligence shock, or a collapse in the acquirer's stock in a share deal). Wider spreads signal higher perceived break risk. Cash deals carry pure timing/break risk; stock-for-stock deals require shorting the acquirer to hedge the exchange ratio. The asymmetry is unattractive — modest upside if the deal closes, large downside if it breaks — so position sizing and a read on antitrust/regulatory risk are everything. Announced buyouts also drive volatility and volume spikes in the target and its sector peers, who may be revalued as potential next targets.
