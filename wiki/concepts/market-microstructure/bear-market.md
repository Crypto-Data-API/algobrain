A bear market is conventionally defined as a decline of 20% or more from a recent high in a broad market index (e.g. the S&P 500), sustained over weeks or months. It is the macro counterpart to a [[bubble]] — a regime of falling prices, deteriorating sentiment, and contracting valuations. Bear markets are distinct from short, sharp [[crashes]]: a crash is an event, a bear market is a regime that can persist for months or years.

## How It Works

The 20% threshold is a market convention, not a scientific cutoff. Smaller declines are graded as a **correction** (a fall of 10-20% from highs) or a **pullback** (under 10%). What separates a bear market from a correction is usually not just depth but **breadth and duration** — a broad-based, multi-month repricing driven by a regime shift rather than a single shock.

Two broad categories are commonly distinguished:

- **Cyclical bear markets** — tied to the [[business-cycle]], typically triggered by [[fed-policy|monetary tightening]], a [[recession]], or an earnings contraction. These tend to last 9-18 months and are followed by recovery as the cycle turns.
- **Secular bear markets** — multi-year regimes of flat-to-falling real returns, often after a major [[bubble]] bursts (e.g. 1929-1949, 1966-1982, and arguably 2000-2013 in inflation-adjusted terms).

Common triggers:

- **Monetary tightening** — rising [[interest-rates]] raise the discount rate on future cash flows and compress valuation multiples (the 2022 bear is a textbook case: rates up, P/E down).
- **Recession / earnings collapse** — falling corporate profits remove the "E" in P/E (2007-2009).
- **Bubble unwind** — speculative excess reverses as [[leverage]] forces cascading selling (2000-2002 dot-com bust).
- **Exogenous shock** — pandemics, wars, oil shocks (the brief but violent 2020 COVID bear).

Bear markets typically progress through phases mirroring [[bubble]] psychology in reverse: complacency, then denial ("buy the dip"), then fear, then capitulation (panic selling on heavy volume), and finally despair — the point of maximum pessimism that often marks the bottom. Counter-trend **bear-market rallies** (sharp 10-20% bounces inside the downtrend) are characteristic and frequently trap buyers who believe the bottom is in.

## Trading Relevance

Bear markets invert the dynamics traders rely on in bull regimes:

- **[[trend-following|Trend]] and [[momentum]] strategies** flip short or move to cash; buy-the-dip strategies bleed.
- **[[volatility]] spikes** — the [[vix|VIX]] typically rises sharply; option premiums expand, favouring premium sellers but punishing naked long calls.
- **Correlations rise toward 1** — diversification fails when "everything sells off together," stressing [[portfolio-theory|portfolio]] construction.
- **Defensive [[sector-rotation]]** — utilities, consumer staples, and healthcare outperform; cyclicals and high-multiple growth underperform.
- **[[cash-as-asset|Cash becomes an asset]]** — Bear markets can be the best wealth-building opportunity of a lifetime: holding dry powder lets a disciplined investor buy quality companies at depressed valuations during the despair phase.

The hard part is timing. "The market can remain irrational longer than you can remain solvent" applies equally to shorting near a bottom and to catching a falling knife. Robust practice favours [[risk-management|defined risk]], staged entries ([[dollar-cost-averaging]]), and waiting for confirmation of a regime change rather than predicting the exact turn.
