**Macroeconomics** is the study of economy-wide forces — output, [[employment]], [[inflation]], [[interest-rates]], credit, and capital flows — that determine the environment in which all financial assets trade. For traders, macro is not optional background reading; it is the single largest driver of cross-asset returns over multi-month timeframes. [[fed-policy|Federal Reserve policy]], [[fiscal-policy|government spending and taxation]], and the [[credit-cycle]] set the tide that lifts or sinks all boats. Even a pure technical trader operating on 5-minute charts is trading inside a macro regime whether they acknowledge it or not.

This page serves as the central hub for all macroeconomic concepts in the wiki. Each section links to dedicated deep-dive pages.

---

## Why Macro Matters for Trading

Three core reasons every trader must understand macroeconomics:

1. **Regime determines strategy performance.** A [[trend-following]] system that prints money in a trending rate-hike cycle will get chopped apart in a range-bound, QE-suppressed volatility regime. The [[regime-matrix]] maps which strategies work in which macro conditions.
2. **Macro moves are the largest moves.** The biggest drawdowns and rallies in equities, bonds, currencies, and commodities are driven by macro forces — [[recession|recessions]], [[central-bank]] pivots, [[inflation]] shocks, [[sovereign-debt]] crises. Missing the macro context means being blindsided by the biggest moves.
3. **Macro sets the discount rate.** All asset valuations ultimately depend on [[interest-rates]] and growth expectations. When the 10-year Treasury yield moves 100bps, the entire equity market re-prices. [[top-down-macro-analysis|Top-down analysis]] starts here for a reason.

## The Macro Map

The macroeconomic system can be organized into seven interconnected domains. Each domain has its own dedicated pages; this section provides the map.

### 1. Monetary Policy

[[monetary-policy|Monetary policy]] is the management of [[interest-rates]], [[money-supply]], and credit conditions by [[central-bank|central banks]]. It is the single most powerful macro force for financial markets.

**Key pages:**
- [[monetary-policy]] — Framework: tools, transmission channels, and market impact
- [[fed-policy]] — The Federal Reserve's specific tools: federal funds rate, QE/QT, forward guidance, dot plot
- [[central-bank]] — Major central banks (Fed, ECB, BOJ, BOE, PBOC) and how they differ
- [[interest-rates]] — Policy rates vs. market rates, real vs. nominal, yield curve mechanics
- [[quantitative-easing]] — QE/QT: how balance sheet policy moves every asset class

**The core insight:** Monetary policy works through multiple transmission channels — the interest rate channel (cost of borrowing), the credit channel (availability of lending), the asset price channel (wealth effects), and the exchange rate channel (currency competitiveness). When the Fed raises rates, it doesn't just make bonds cheaper; it tightens the entire financial ecosystem. The maxim "don't fight the Fed" is the most reliable rule in macro trading.

### 2. Fiscal Policy

[[fiscal-policy|Fiscal policy]] is government spending and taxation — the other major lever of macroeconomic management, controlled by legislatures rather than central banks.

**Key pages:**
- [[fiscal-policy]] — Expansionary vs. contractionary, multipliers, crowding out, automatic stabilizers
- [[sovereign-debt]] — Government debt levels, debt-to-GDP ratios, and why they matter for bond markets and currencies

**The core insight:** Fiscal policy affects markets through aggregate demand (government spending directly adds to GDP), taxation (disposable income and corporate profits), and debt issuance (bond supply affects yields). Fiscal and monetary policy can work together (both stimulative) or at cross-purposes (loose fiscal + tight monetary = higher rates for longer). The current US fiscal deficit exceeding 6% of GDP while the economy is near full employment is historically unusual and creates persistent upward pressure on [[interest-rates]].

### 3. Economic Output & Growth

[[gdp|Gross Domestic Product]] is the broadest measure of economic activity and the anchor of the [[business-cycle]].

**Key pages:**
- [[gdp]] — Components (C + I + G + NX), measurement, revisions, and what GDP signals for markets
- [[business-cycle]] — The four phases (expansion, peak, contraction, trough), sector performance by phase, transition indicators
- [[recession]] — Definition, historical patterns, leading indicators, and trading implications
- [[sector-rotation]] — Rotating equity exposure based on the business cycle phase

**The core insight:** Markets are forward-looking and typically move 6-9 months ahead of GDP. The stock market bottoms before the recession ends and peaks before the expansion ends. Traders who wait for GDP confirmation are always late. The [[business-cycle]] framework combined with [[economic-indicators|leading indicators]] gives a probabilistic edge in timing regime transitions.

### 4. Employment & the Labor Market

[[employment|Employment]] is one half of the Fed's dual mandate and produces the single most market-moving data release each month — Nonfarm Payrolls.

**Key pages:**
- [[employment]] — NFP, unemployment rate, wage growth, labor force participation, JOLTS, jobless claims
- [[economic-indicators]] — Where employment fits in the leading/coincident/lagging framework

**The core insight:** The labor market is a lagging indicator — unemployment peaks well after the recession has begun. But *initial jobless claims* are a leading indicator, and the trend in claims is one of the earliest recession signals. Wage growth feeds directly into [[inflation]] expectations, which drive [[fed-policy]] decisions. The Phillips Curve relationship — the trade-off between unemployment and inflation — is the theoretical backbone of central bank decision-making, even though the relationship is unstable in practice.

### 5. Inflation & Deflation

[[inflation|Inflation]] is the sustained rise in the general price level; [[deflation]] is the opposite. Both create distinct trading regimes with different winners and losers.

**Key pages:**
- [[inflation]] — Types (demand-pull, cost-push, monetary), measurement (CPI, PCE, PPI), market impact
- [[deflation]] — Causes, the debt-deflation spiral, Japan's lost decades, liquidity traps
- [[stagflation]] — The worst of both worlds: stagnant growth + high inflation. The 1970s playbook.

**The core insight:** Moderate inflation (2-3%) is the goldilocks zone for equities. Above that, [[central-bank|central banks]] tighten, discount rates rise, and multiples compress. Deflation is arguably worse — it increases the real burden of debt, triggers a [[credit-cycle|debt-deflation spiral]], and pushes the economy toward a liquidity trap where [[monetary-policy]] loses effectiveness. The 2021-2023 inflation cycle demonstrated how quickly an inflation shock can re-price every asset class.

### 6. Money & Credit

[[money-supply|Money supply]] and the [[credit-cycle]] are the plumbing of the financial system. Credit creation is the mechanism through which monetary policy actually reaches the real economy.

**Key pages:**
- [[money-supply]] — M1, M2, the equation of exchange (MV = PQ), velocity of money, Fed balance sheet
- [[credit-cycle]] — The four phases of credit, Minsky's Financial Instability Hypothesis, credit spreads as trading signals
- [[financial-conditions]] — Financial conditions indices, what they measure, and their predictive power

**The core insight:** Credit makes up the vast majority of transactions — total credit in the US is roughly $60 trillion vs. $3 trillion in physical cash. The [[credit-cycle]] amplifies the [[business-cycle]]: easy credit fuels expansion beyond what fundamentals justify, and credit contraction deepens downturns. Hyman Minsky's insight — that stability itself breeds instability by encouraging excessive risk-taking — is the most important idea in macro-finance. Credit spreads (the gap between corporate bond yields and Treasuries) are one of the best real-time signals of financial stress.

### 7. Currency & International

[[currency-dynamics|Currency dynamics]] and [[trade-balance|international trade]] connect domestic macro to the global economy. No country's macro exists in isolation.

**Key pages:**
- [[currency-dynamics]] — Exchange rate determination, interest rate differentials, purchasing power parity, carry trades
- [[trade-balance]] — Current account, capital account, twin deficits, reserve currency dynamics

**The core insight:** Currency movements are the purest expression of relative macro fundamentals between countries. When the Fed tightens while the ECB holds, the dollar strengthens — attracting global capital but hurting US exporters and emerging markets with dollar-denominated debt. The US dollar's reserve currency status gives it unique properties: the US can run persistent [[trade-balance|trade deficits]] because global demand for dollars as a reserve asset finances the gap. This "exorbitant privilege" shapes the entire global macro landscape.

## Key Macro Relationships

The macro system is connected by transmission mechanisms. Understanding these linkages is what separates macro literacy from macro trading:

| Cause | → Transmission | → Effect |
|-------|---------------|----------|
| Fed raises rates | → Cost of borrowing rises | → Equity valuations compress, dollar strengthens |
| Government increases spending | → Aggregate demand rises | → GDP growth accelerates, bond supply increases |
| [[inflation]] surprises to upside | → Rate expectations shift higher | → Bonds sell off, growth stocks underperform |
| [[credit-cycle|Credit]] tightens | → Banks reduce lending | → Business investment falls, defaults rise |
| Dollar strengthens | → Exports become more expensive | → Multinational earnings fall, EM debt stress rises |
| Unemployment rises | → Consumer spending falls | → Corporate earnings decline, Fed turns dovish |
| [[money-supply]] expands (QE) | → Liquidity floods financial system | → Asset prices inflate, volatility compresses |
| [[yield-curve]] inverts | → Banks' net interest margins compress | → Lending slows, recession signal activated |

These relationships are not mechanical — they operate with variable lags, interact with each other, and depend on the starting conditions. But they are the skeletal structure of macro analysis.

## Macro Regimes and Asset Class Performance

Different macro environments produce radically different asset class returns:

| Regime | Equities | Bonds | Commodities | Gold | Cash |
|--------|----------|-------|-------------|------|------|
| **Growth + Low Inflation** | Strong | Moderate | Neutral | Weak | Low yield |
| **Growth + Rising Inflation** | Mixed (value > growth) | Weak | Strong | Mixed | Improving yield |
| **[[recession|Recession]] + Low Inflation** | Weak → recovery | Strong (flight to safety) | Weak | Strong | Fed cutting |
| **[[stagflation]]** | Weak | Weak | Strong | Strong | Only safe haven |
| **[[quantitative-easing|QE]] regime** | Strong (artificially) | Strong (suppressed yields) | Mixed | Strong | Zero yield |

The [[regime-matrix]] provides strategy-level mapping against these macro conditions.

|---------|-----------|---------------------|

## Macro Trading Approaches

Several trading methodologies are built directly on macro analysis:

- **[[global-macro]]** — The broadest approach: taking positions across [[forex]], [[bond-market|bonds]], equities, and [[commodities]] based on macro trends. Practitioners include [[george-soros]], [[ray-dalio]], [[stanley-druckenmiller]], and [[paul-tudor-jones]].
- **[[top-down-macro-analysis]]** — The institutional framework: global macro → sectors → stock selection → technical timing. Start with the macro regime, filter to favored sectors, then pick individual names.
- **[[sector-rotation]]** — Rotating equity exposure based on the [[business-cycle]] phase. Early expansion favors technology and consumer discretionary; late expansion favors energy and materials; contraction favors utilities and staples.
- **[[carry-trade|Carry trades]]** — Exploiting [[interest-rates|interest rate]] differentials between currencies, which are directly determined by relative [[monetary-policy]].
- **[[risk-parity]]** — Balancing risk across asset classes that respond to different macro environments (growth, inflation, both, neither).

## Historical Macro Episodes

Major market events are almost always macro events:

- **[[great-depression]]** (1929-1939) — Deflationary collapse, bank failures, monetary policy failure, fiscal austerity that deepened the crisis
- **1970s [[stagflation]]** — Oil shocks + loose [[monetary-policy]] = inflation above 10%, stagnant growth, equities lost a decade in real terms
- **Volcker Shock** (1979-1982) — Fed Chair Paul Volcker raised the federal funds rate to 20% to break inflation, triggering a severe [[recession]] but restoring price stability
- **[[dot-com-bubble]]** (2000) — Irrational exuberance in equities collided with Fed tightening
- **[[2008-global-financial-crisis]]** — [[credit-cycle]] excess, [[subprime-mortgage|subprime]] collapse, systemic bank failures, birth of modern [[quantitative-easing]]
- **COVID crash & response** (2020) — Fastest bear market in history, followed by the most aggressive combined fiscal + monetary stimulus ever deployed
- **2021-2023 Inflation Cycle** — Supply chain disruptions + fiscal stimulus + QE → highest [[inflation]] in 40 years → fastest Fed tightening cycle since the 1980s
- **[[2025-tariff-market-volatility]]** — Tariff-driven supply shocks intersecting with fiscal expansion and uncertain [[monetary-policy]]
