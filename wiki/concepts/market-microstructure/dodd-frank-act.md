# Dodd-Frank Act

The Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act (2010) is the most comprehensive financial regulation enacted since the 1930s, passed in direct response to the [[2008-global-financial-crisis]]. It fundamentally reshaped oversight of banks, [[derivatives]], and consumer financial products in the United States.

## Key Provisions

- **Volcker Rule**: Prohibits banks from engaging in proprietary trading and limits their investments in [[hedge-funds]] and private equity -- separating risk-taking from deposit-taking.
- **Derivatives regulation**: Required most [[derivatives]] (including [[credit-default-swaps]]) to be cleared through central counterparties and traded on exchanges, increasing transparency.
- **Systemically Important Financial Institutions (SIFIs)**: Designated certain institutions as [[too-big-to-fail]], subjecting them to enhanced capital requirements, stress tests, and "living wills" (resolution plans).
- **Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)**: Created a new agency to regulate consumer financial products like mortgages and credit cards.
- **Orderly Liquidation Authority**: Established a mechanism for winding down failing financial firms without taxpayer bailouts.

## Impact on Traders

Dodd-Frank reduced bank proprietary trading desks (a significant source of market [[liquidity]]), increased the cost of trading [[derivatives]], and shifted much of the risk-taking activity from banks to [[hedge-funds]] and non-bank financial institutions. Position limits on commodity [[futures]] also affected commodity traders. The act remains a live political issue, with periodic efforts to roll back provisions.

## Why It Matters

Understanding Dodd-Frank is essential for any trader operating in regulated U.S. markets. The act's restrictions on [[leverage]], [[derivatives]], and proprietary trading continue to shape [[market-microstructure]] and [[liquidity]] conditions.
