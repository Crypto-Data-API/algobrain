# TARP

The **Troubled Asset Relief Program (TARP)** was a $700 billion U.S. government bailout program enacted in October 2008 to stabilize the financial system during the [[2008-global-financial-crisis]].

TARP purchased toxic assets and equity from financial institutions, including major banks and AIG. While it ultimately recovered most funds, TARP intensified debates about [[moral-hazard]] and [[too-big-to-fail]] policies.

## How TARP Worked

The U.S. Treasury, under Secretary Hank Paulson, initially proposed buying toxic mortgage-backed securities from banks to remove them from balance sheets. The plan quickly pivoted to the **Capital Purchase Program**, which directly injected capital into banks by purchasing preferred stock. Major recipients included:

- Citigroup: $45 billion
- Bank of America: $45 billion
- AIG: $67.8 billion (through multiple interventions)
- JPMorgan Chase, Wells Fargo, Goldman Sachs, and Morgan Stanley: $10-25 billion each

The Treasury also used TARP funds to bail out the auto industry (GM and Chrysler) and support mortgage modification programs.

## Controversy and Public Backlash

TARP was deeply unpopular. Critics on the left argued it bailed out Wall Street while Main Street suffered through foreclosures and unemployment. Critics on the right opposed the government picking winners and creating [[moral-hazard]] -- rewarding reckless behavior with taxpayer money. The Tea Party movement and Occupy Wall Street both drew energy from anti-bailout sentiment.

## TARP Repaid with Profit

Despite the controversy, TARP was ultimately a financial success for taxpayers. Banks repaid their TARP funds with interest and dividends, and the Treasury's equity stakes appreciated. By the time the program was wound down, the government recovered approximately $441 billion on $426 billion disbursed -- a net profit of roughly $15 billion. The auto bailout portion was the only component that lost money.

## Legacy

TARP demonstrated that government intervention could stabilize a financial crisis, but it left lasting questions about [[too-big-to-fail]] institutions and led directly to the [[dodd-frank-act]] reforms of 2010.

## Trading Relevance

TARP is the canonical reference case for *policy put* dynamics — the market's expectation that authorities will intervene to backstop systemic institutions in a crisis. Traders use the TARP/2008 playbook to anticipate how governments and central banks respond to acute financial stress (capital injections, asset purchases, guarantees), which shapes how to position around bank-sector tail risk and crisis-era [[liquidity]] flows. The eventual *profitability* of TARP is also a reminder that forced selling and panic during a crisis often create the deepest value, and that recapitalized survivors can rally sharply once solvency is restored.
