Employment is one half of the Federal Reserve's dual mandate (the other being price stability, i.e., [[inflation]]). The monthly jobs report — Nonfarm Payrolls (NFP) — is widely regarded as the single most market-moving scheduled data release. Labor market conditions drive [[fed-policy]] decisions, shape [[inflation]] expectations, and signal the health of the [[business-cycle]].

## Key Employment Indicators

| Indicator | Frequency | Type | What It Measures |
|-----------|-----------|------|------------------|
| **Nonfarm Payrolls (NFP)** | Monthly (first Friday) | Coincident | Net new jobs created in the non-farm economy. The headline number traders watch most closely. |
| **Unemployment Rate (U-3)** | Monthly | Lagging | Percentage of the labor force actively seeking work but unemployed. The "headline" rate cited in media. |
| **U-6 (Broad Unemployment)** | Monthly | Lagging | Includes discouraged workers who stopped looking and those working part-time for economic reasons. A fuller picture of labor market slack. |
| **Average Hourly Earnings (AHE)** | Monthly | Coincident | Year-over-year and month-over-month wage growth. A direct proxy for wage [[inflation]] and feeds into inflation expectations. |
| **Labor Force Participation Rate** | Monthly | Structural | Share of the working-age population either employed or actively looking. Affected by demographics, retirement trends, and discouraged-worker effects. |
| **Initial Jobless Claims** | Weekly (Thursdays) | Leading | New filings for unemployment insurance. One of the best high-frequency [[recession]] indicators available. |
| **Continuing Claims** | Weekly (one-week lag) | Coincident | Total number of people receiving unemployment benefits. Shows duration of unemployment spells. |
| **JOLTS** | Monthly (~2 month lag) | Coincident | Job openings, quits rate, hires rate, and layoffs. The quits rate signals worker confidence; openings signal labor demand. |
| **ADP Employment Report** | Monthly (2 days before NFP) | Coincident | Private-sector payroll estimate from ADP payroll processing data. Treated as a preview of NFP, though correlation is imperfect. |

## Employment and the Business Cycle

The labor market is fundamentally a **lagging indicator**. Unemployment peaks well after a [[recession]] has already begun and troughs well after recovery is underway. Employers are slow to fire (due to hiring and training costs) and slow to hire (due to uncertainty about demand sustainability).

However, not all employment data lags equally. The deterioration sequence typically unfolds as follows:

1. **Initial jobless claims rise** — the earliest warning signal, often leading recession by 3-6 months
2. **Job openings decline** (visible in JOLTS) — firms pull postings before cutting staff
3. **Hiring slows** — payroll gains decelerate from, say, +250k/month to +100k/month
4. **Hours worked decline** — employers cut overtime and hours before headcount
5. **Layoffs increase** — NFP prints turn negative
6. **Unemployment rate rises** — lags the actual onset of recession by months
7. **Consumer spending falls** — unemployed workers cut discretionary spending
8. **Recession deepens** — the feedback loop between job losses and reduced demand accelerates

This sequence matters for [[sector-rotation]]. Defensive sectors (utilities, staples, healthcare) outperform once claims begin rising, well before the official recession call.

## The Phillips Curve

The Phillips Curve describes the theoretical inverse relationship between unemployment and [[inflation]]. When unemployment is low:

- Workers have bargaining power and demand higher wages
- Wage pressures feed into service-sector inflation (labor is 60-70% of service costs)
- The [[fed-policy|Fed]] responds by tightening monetary policy ([[interest-rates]] rise)

The concept of **NAIRU** (Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment) represents the theoretical unemployment rate at which inflation neither accelerates nor decelerates. The Fed's long-run estimate of NAIRU has typically been in the 4.0-4.5% range, though this shifts over time.

**Important caveats for traders:** The Phillips Curve relationship is unstable. It appeared to break down in the 2010s (low unemployment with low inflation) and reasserted itself in 2021-2023 (tight labor market with high inflation). The Fed still uses this framework as a guide, but markets increasingly focus on realized wage data (Average Hourly Earnings, Employment Cost Index) rather than the unemployment rate alone.

## How Markets React to Jobs Data

NFP release day (typically the first Friday of each month at 8:30 AM ET) is one of the highest-volatility scheduled events. Market reaction depends heavily on the phase of the [[business-cycle]]:

**Early/Mid Cycle (expansion)**
- Above-consensus NFP = bullish equities, bearish bonds (stronger growth expectations)
- Below-consensus NFP = bearish equities, bullish bonds

**Late Cycle (near peak)**
- Above-consensus NFP = potentially **bearish** equities if it signals "higher for longer" [[fed-policy]]
- The wage component (AHE) becomes the dominant driver because it feeds [[inflation]] expectations
- A "Goldilocks" report — moderate job gains with tame wages — is the most bullish outcome

**Recession / Early Recovery**
- Any positive surprise is aggressively bullish as it signals the turn
- Claims data matters more than NFP at this stage

**Practical NFP trading notes:**
- The initial move in the first 30 seconds is often reversed or extended by the time details are digested (revisions to prior months, household survey, participation rate)
- The two-month revision to prior NFP prints can be as market-moving as the headline number
- Weekly initial claims data, released every Thursday, provides a real-time pulse between NFP releases
