# Capital Expenditure Cycle

The multi-year investment cycle in commodity production capacity. New mines take 5-10 years from discovery to first production. Oil and gas projects take 3-7 years. Agricultural acreage expansion takes 1-3 years. This lag between investment decisions and production output creates cyclical over- and under-supply that drives commodity price cycles -- the fundamental supply-side mechanism underlying [[commodity-super-cycle|commodity super cycles]] and shorter-duration price trends (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]]).

## Overview

The capex cycle is the most important supply-side driver in commodity markets. Unlike financial assets, where "supply" can adjust instantly (issuing new shares, bonds, or derivative contracts), physical commodity supply is constrained by geology, engineering, regulation, and time. This structural rigidity means that supply consistently overshoots and undershoots demand, creating the cyclical pattern (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]]).

## The Cycle

### Phase 1: Underinvestment (Trough)
- **Conditions**: Low commodity prices, producer margins compressed or negative, capex slashed to survival levels.
- **Duration**: 3-7 years.
- **Examples**: Mining capex 2015-2020 (down ~40% from 2012 peak), oil & gas E&P spending 2015-2020 (down ~50% from 2014 peak).
- **Observable signals**: Declining Baker Hughes rig count (oil/gas), falling exploration budgets (mining), mine/project closures, producer bankruptcies.
- **What happens next**: Existing mines/wells deplete, no replacement capacity is being built, supply gradually declines.

### Phase 2: Supply Deficit (Tightening)
- **Conditions**: Demand growth (or supply decline from depletion) gradually creates a deficit. Inventories begin drawing. Prices start rising.
- **Duration**: 2-5 years.
- **Observable signals**: Declining inventories ([[eia]] petroleum stocks, LME warehouse stocks, USDA ending stocks), rising spot premiums, [[backwardation]] appearing in futures curves.
- **Producer response**: Boards of directors remain cautious (burned by the previous bust), initially using rising cash flows for debt reduction and shareholder returns rather than new capex. This "capital discipline" delays the supply response.

### Phase 3: Investment Boom (Peak)
- **Conditions**: Prices high enough that even marginal projects earn attractive returns. Producers compete for investment: new mines, new wells, exploration, expansion projects.
- **Duration**: 3-7 years.
- **Examples**: Mining capex boom 2005-2012, oil & gas E&P boom 2005-2014.
- **Observable signals**: Soaring rig counts, record exploration budgets, M&A frenzy in resource sector, commodity equity IPOs, mining/E&P companies raising capital.
- **The trap**: Projects approved at peak prices assume those prices persist. Cost inflation (equipment, labor, materials) erodes project economics. Many projects approved at cycle peak deliver disappointing returns.

### Phase 4: Oversupply (Glut)
- **Conditions**: New supply from Phase 3 investment floods the market just as demand growth slows (or was never as strong as assumed). Inventories build rapidly. Prices collapse.
- **Duration**: 2-5 years.
- **Examples**: Iron ore 2013-2016 (Rio Tinto, BHP, Vale expanded massively just as Chinese demand decelerated), US shale oil 2014-2016 (production growth from 2011-2014 investment overwhelmed demand).
- **Observable signals**: Rising inventories, [[contango]] deepening, cash costs approaching or exceeding spot prices for marginal producers.

Then the cycle returns to Phase 1 (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]]).

## Lead Times by Commodity

| Commodity | Discovery-to-Production | Key Constraints |
|-----------|------------------------|-----------------|
| Copper | 7-15 years | Permitting, remote locations, declining ore grades |
| Gold | 5-10 years | Permitting, environmental approvals |
| Iron ore | 5-8 years | Scale (billions of dollars), infrastructure (rail, port) |
| Crude oil (conventional) | 5-10 years | Exploration, development drilling, platform construction |
| Crude oil (shale) | 1-2 years | Shorter cycle, but higher decline rates |
| Natural gas (LNG) | 5-8 years | Liquefaction plant construction |
| Aluminum | 3-5 years | Smelter construction, power supply |
| Corn/soybeans | 1 growing season | Acreage can shift quickly, but yield is weather-dependent |
| Coffee | 3-5 years | Tree maturation time |
| Lithium | 3-7 years | Brine evaporation or hard-rock mine development |

The variation in lead times is critical: copper supply cannot respond to price signals for 7+ years, while US shale oil can respond in 12-18 months. This explains why copper prices tend to overshoot more dramatically than oil prices during demand shocks (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]]).

## Key Metrics to Track

### Mining Sector
- **Total mining capex** (published by BHP, Rio Tinto, Glencore, Vale, Freeport-McMoRan in annual reports)
- **Exploration budgets** (S&P Global Market Intelligence tracks global nonferrous exploration spending)
- **New project approvals / FIDs** (Final Investment Decisions)
- **Project pipeline**: announced, feasibility study, construction, commissioning
- **Ore grade trends**: declining ore grades at existing mines (a secular trend in copper, gold) mean more capex is needed per unit of output

### Oil & Gas Sector
- **Global E&P spending** (tracked by IHS Markit, Rystad Energy, Barclays annual survey)
- **Baker Hughes rig count** (weekly, leading indicator for US production growth)
- **Offshore rig utilization** (Clarkson Research)
- **Decline rates** on existing fields (IEA World Energy Outlook)
- **Shale breakeven costs** (per-basin, tracked by Dallas Fed)

### Agricultural Sector
- **Planted acreage** ([[usda]] Prospective Plantings report, published annually in March)
- **Yield trends** (USDA WASDE, FAO)
- **Fertilizer application rates and cost** (proxy for input investment)
- **Irrigation infrastructure** investment

## Trading Implications

### For Commodity Traders
1. **Identify where in the cycle each commodity sits**: Is capex rising or falling? Are new projects being approved or cancelled?
2. **Capex cuts = bullish (with lag)**: When miners/drillers slash budgets, supply will decline 3-10 years later. Position for the eventual deficit.
3. **Capex boom = bearish (with lag)**: When investment surges, new supply will arrive 3-10 years later. Be cautious of "this time is different" narratives at cycle peak.
4. **Capital discipline is bullish**: If producers resist capex growth despite high prices (as many mining companies did post-2020), the supply response is delayed, extending the deficit.

### For Equity Investors
- Commodity producers' stock prices are driven by cash flow, which depends on prices, which depends on the capex cycle. The best time to buy mining/E&P stocks is during the underinvestment phase (Phase 1) when valuations are depressed and the next deficit is building.
- Avoid buying at cycle peak (Phase 3) when earnings look great but new supply is about to crush margins.

### For Strategy Design
- [[commodity-value-strategy]] captures the mean-reversion component of the capex cycle: commodities that have crashed (underinvestment phase) eventually recover as supply contracts.
- [[commodity-momentum]] captures the trending component: the cycle phases last years, creating multi-year trends that momentum strategies ride.
- [[supply-demand-balance]] modeling is the analytical tool for tracking where in the cycle each commodity sits (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]]).

## The 2015-2020 Underinvestment Period

A particularly notable period that many analysts cite as setting the stage for potential supply deficits in the 2020s:

- **Mining capex**: Fell from ~$170B (2012) to ~$95B (2016) -- a 44% decline. Copper exploration budgets fell 70%.
- **Oil & gas E&P**: Fell from ~$780B (2014) to ~$340B (2016) -- a 56% decline. Upstream investment remained depressed through 2020 (COVID).
- **Consequences**: Fewer new mine approvals, fewer oil discoveries, declining production from existing assets. The copper supply pipeline in 2024 was the thinnest since 2008. Oil spare capacity (ex-OPEC+) fell to historical lows.
- **The "capital discipline" effect**: Post-2020, mining companies and E&P operators faced shareholder pressure (ESG, returns-focused investors) to maintain capital discipline rather than chase growth, further delaying the supply response despite high prices (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]]).

## Limitations

- **Timing is approximate**: The lag between capex and production varies widely. A capex cut today may not affect supply for 3, 5, or 10 years depending on the commodity.
- **Technology can shorten cycles**: US shale demonstrated that investment-to-production lead times can be compressed (1-2 years vs. 5-10 for conventional). This reduced the amplitude of oil price cycles.
- **Government-owned capacity**: State-owned enterprises (Saudi Aramco, Codelco, CNPC) may invest for strategic reasons regardless of market economics, distorting the cycle.
- **Substitution and demand destruction**: Persistently high prices can drive permanent demand reduction (e.g., aluminum replacing copper in some applications), changing the balance equation permanently.
