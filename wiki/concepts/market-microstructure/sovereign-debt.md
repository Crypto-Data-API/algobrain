Sovereign debt is money owed by a national government, typically issued as bonds in domestic or foreign capital markets. Debt levels, sustainability trajectories, and market confidence in repayment are among the most consequential macro variables for asset prices, [[interest-rates]], and [[currency-dynamics|exchange rates]].

## Debt-to-GDP Ratio

The debt-to-GDP ratio is the standard metric for assessing sovereign debt burdens. As of 2025:

| Country | Debt/GDP | Currency | Notes |
|---------|----------|----------|-------|
| Japan | ~250% | Own (JPY) | Mostly held domestically |
| US | ~120% | Own (USD) | Reserve currency issuer |
| Italy | ~140% | Shared (EUR) | Cannot print own currency |
| UK | ~100% | Own (GBP) | Post-COVID increase |
| China | ~80% | Own (CNY) | Excluding local government/SOE debt much higher |

What matters is not just the level but three critical factors:

1. **Trajectory** — Is the ratio rising or stabilizing? A country at 80% with a 5% annual deficit is more concerning than one at 120% with a balanced budget.
2. **Currency denomination** — Debt in your own currency is fundamentally different from debt in a foreign currency. This distinction separates manageable debt from crisis-prone debt.
3. **Who holds it** — Domestically-held debt (Japan) is more stable than foreign-held debt. Domestic holders are less likely to flee and more subject to financial repression.

## Why Sovereign Debt Matters for Markets

Sovereign debt levels affect nearly every asset class:

- **Bond yields** — Higher debt → more bond issuance → more supply → higher yields, all else equal. But this relationship is not mechanical — Japan has the highest debt/GDP and among the lowest yields because the Bank of Japan has been the dominant buyer.
- **Currency confidence** — Excessive debt growth can undermine confidence in the currency, particularly if markets expect eventual monetization ([[inflation]] to erode the real value of debt).
- **Inflation expectations** — Large debts create incentives for governments to tolerate or engineer higher [[inflation]], which reduces the real burden of nominal debt. This "inflation tax" is a form of soft default.
- **Fiscal space** — High debt limits a government's ability to respond to crises with fiscal stimulus. Countries that entered COVID with low debt (Germany, Australia) had more room to spend.
- **Crowding out** — Government borrowing competes with private borrowing for available capital, potentially raising costs for corporate and household borrowers.

## The Debt Sustainability Equation

The fundamental equation of debt sustainability:

**Change in Debt/GDP = (r - g) x Debt/GDP - Primary Balance/GDP**

Where:
- **r** = average interest rate on government debt
- **g** = nominal GDP growth rate
- **Primary balance** = government revenue minus spending, excluding interest payments

The critical variable is **r vs. g**:

- If **r < g** (interest rate below growth rate), the debt/GDP ratio naturally declines even without a primary surplus. This was the case for most advanced economies from 2010-2021, when central banks suppressed rates below growth.
- If **r > g** (interest rate above growth rate), the debt/GDP ratio rises unless the government runs a primary surplus large enough to offset the differential. This is the regime that creates sustainability pressure.

The 2022-2025 rate hiking cycle pushed r closer to g (or above it) for many countries, reigniting debt sustainability concerns that had been dormant during the zero-rate era.

## Sovereign Debt Crises

Sovereign debt crises occur when markets lose confidence in a government's ability or willingness to service its debt. The dynamics differ based on whether the country borrows in its own currency or a foreign one.

### Foreign-currency crises (the most severe)

When a country borrows in USD or EUR and its own currency depreciates, the real burden of debt increases — creating a reflexive spiral:

1. Economic weakness → currency depreciation
2. Depreciation → debt burden rises (in local terms)
3. Higher burden → more default risk → capital flight
4. Capital flight → more depreciation → back to step 2

Examples: Argentina (recurring), Asian financial crisis (1997), Russia (1998), many emerging market episodes. These crises tend to be sudden, violent, and require IMF intervention.

### Domestic-currency crises (slower, different mechanism)

Countries that borrow in their own currency (US, UK, Japan) face a different constraint: they can always print money to avoid technical default, but this risks [[inflation]]. The failure mode is not default but currency collapse or runaway inflation.

Examples: UK gilt crisis (2022, triggered by Truss mini-budget), periodic pressure on Japanese government bonds during BOJ policy shifts. These episodes tend to be market panics that force policy reversals rather than outright defaults.

### Eurozone — the hybrid case

Eurozone members (Greece, Italy, Spain, Portugal) borrow in euros but cannot print euros — that power belongs to the ECB. This creates a quasi-foreign-currency dynamic. The 2010-2012 European sovereign debt crisis (Greece, Ireland, Portugal, Spain, Italy) demonstrated this vulnerability. It was resolved only when the ECB effectively backstopped sovereign debt ("whatever it takes" — Mario Draghi, 2012).

## Fiscal Dominance

Fiscal dominance is the regime where sovereign debt is so large that the [[central-bank]] can no longer set [[monetary-policy]] independently. The mechanism:

1. Debt levels are very high
2. Central bank raises rates to fight [[inflation]]
3. Higher rates increase government interest expense
4. Higher interest expense widens the deficit
5. Wider deficit requires more borrowing
6. More borrowing at higher rates further increases interest expense
7. The math becomes unsustainable — the central bank must back off

In fiscal dominance, the central bank effectively becomes subordinate to fiscal needs. It must keep rates low enough to maintain debt sustainability, even if [[inflation]] is above target. Some analysts argue the US is approaching this threshold.

Historical precedent: many advanced economies practiced financial repression (keeping rates below inflation) after World War II to erode large war debts. Real rates were negative for over a decade. This is the most likely resolution for current debt levels — slow, sustained negative real rates.

## US Sovereign Debt — Current Situation

As of 2025, the US fiscal picture:

- **Total federal debt**: ~$35T+
- **Debt/GDP**: ~120%
- **Annual interest expense**: approaching $1T/year (largest line item, surpassing defense)
- **Fiscal deficit**: >6% of GDP (unusual outside of recessions)
- **CBO projections**: debt/GDP rising to 150%+ by 2035 under current law

The debate centers on whether the US can sustain this trajectory:

**Bull case (reserve currency privilege)**: The USD's reserve status creates structural demand for Treasuries. The US can run deficits that would be unsustainable for other countries. Interest rates will remain manageable because global savings need a safe asset to park in.

**Bear case (bond vigilantes)**: Interest expense is compounding. Deficit spending during full employment is unprecedented. Foreign holders may gradually diversify away from Treasuries (de-dollarization). At some point, the bond market forces discipline — either through a rate spike or a currency crisis.

**Most likely resolution**: Financial repression. The Fed keeps real rates low (potentially negative) for an extended period, gradually eroding the real value of the debt. This is a hidden tax on savers and bondholders, benefiting debtors (including the government).

## Trading Implications

- Sovereign credit default swap (CDS) spreads are the market's real-time assessment of default risk. Widening CDS spreads on a country signal deteriorating confidence.
- Yield curve shape in government bonds reflects growth and [[inflation]] expectations, not just debt levels.
- Sovereign debt stress in one country can trigger [[contagion]] — the 2010 Greek crisis spread to Portugal, Ireland, Spain, and Italy in sequence.
- Long-dated government bonds are the asset most exposed to sovereign debt sustainability concerns. Duration risk and credit risk compound.
