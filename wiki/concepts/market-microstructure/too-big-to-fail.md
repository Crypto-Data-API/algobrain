# Too Big to Fail

"Too big to fail" (TBTF) refers to financial institutions whose size, complexity, and interconnectedness are so great that their failure would trigger cascading damage across the entire financial system. Because governments are implicitly or explicitly compelled to bail out these institutions, TBTF creates a fundamental [[moral-hazard]]: the profits are privatized while the catastrophic losses are socialized.

## Origin and 2008 Crisis

The concept gained mainstream prominence during the [[2008-global-financial-crisis]]. The U.S. government bailed out Bear Stearns, AIG, Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, and major banks through [[tarp]] and Federal Reserve facilities -- but allowed [[lehman-brothers]] to fail, triggering a global panic that demonstrated exactly why regulators feared TBTF collapses. The inconsistency in treatment (who gets saved, who does not) amplified uncertainty and market chaos.

## Moral Hazard Problem

When institutions believe they will be rescued, they have incentive to take excessive risk and accumulate excessive [[leverage]]. If the bet works, they profit enormously. If it fails, taxpayers absorb the losses. This dynamic encourages the very behavior that makes institutions systemically dangerous in the first place.

## Regulatory Response

The [[dodd-frank-act]] attempted to address TBTF by designating Systemically Important Financial Institutions (SIFIs), imposing higher capital requirements, requiring "living wills" for orderly resolution, and creating the Orderly Liquidation Authority. Whether these measures have truly ended TBTF remains debated.

## Why It Matters for Traders

TBTF dynamics influence how markets price risk during crises. Traders who understand which institutions are systemically important can better anticipate government intervention (or its absence), [[central-bank]] responses, and the flow of [[liquidity]] during stress events. The concept reappeared during the 2023 regional banking crisis (the FDIC's invocation of the systemic-risk exception to backstop all Silicon Valley Bank and Signature Bank depositors) and echoes in crypto through events like the [[ftx-collapse]] — where, notably, no public backstop existed. The implicit TBTF subsidy also affects relative pricing: bonds and equity of perceived-systemic banks tend to trade at tighter spreads than their standalone risk would justify, a premium that compresses in calm and can reprice violently when the bailout assumption is questioned.
