The trade balance measures the difference between a country's exports and imports of goods and services. The broader balance of payments tracks all international transactions, including investment flows. Persistent imbalances are among the most important structural forces in global macro and have direct consequences for [[currency-dynamics|exchange rates]], [[interest-rates]], and asset prices.

## Current Account vs. Capital Account

The balance of payments has two main components that must, by accounting identity, sum to zero:

- **Current account** — Trade in goods and services, income on foreign investments (dividends, interest), and unilateral transfers (remittances, foreign aid). The trade balance in goods is the largest component for most countries.
- **Capital account (financial account)** — Foreign direct investment (FDI), portfolio investment (stocks, bonds), bank lending, and reserve asset changes. When a country runs a current account deficit, it must run a capital account surplus of equal size — it is receiving more goods than it sends, so it must be sending financial claims (IOUs, assets) in return.

This identity is crucial: a trade deficit is not "losing" — it is the flip side of capital inflows. The question is whether those inflows are productive (FDI, equity investment) or fragile (short-term hot money, debt).

## Trade Deficit vs. Surplus

- **Deficit countries** (US, UK) — Import more than they export. Funded by foreigners buying domestic assets (Treasuries, equities, real estate, corporate bonds). A trade deficit means the country is consuming more than it produces, borrowing the difference from abroad.
- **Surplus countries** (Germany, China, Japan historically) — Export more than they import. Accumulate foreign assets, particularly reserve currency bonds. A trade surplus means the country is producing more than it consumes, saving the difference abroad.

The US runs a persistent trade deficit of roughly $800B-$1T per year (as of 2025). This has been sustained for decades because of the unique role of the dollar as global reserve currency (see [[currency-dynamics#Reserve Currency Dynamics]]).

## Twin Deficits Hypothesis

The twin deficits hypothesis holds that fiscal deficits (government spending > revenue) and trade deficits tend to reinforce each other. The mechanism:

1. Government runs a fiscal deficit, borrowing to fund spending
2. Higher government borrowing pushes up [[interest-rates]]
3. Higher rates attract foreign capital, strengthening the currency
4. Stronger currency makes imports cheaper and exports more expensive
5. Trade deficit widens

The empirical evidence is mixed — the twin deficits relationship held in the 1980s (Reagan deficits) but broke down at other times. The relationship is complicated by other factors including private savings rates, global capital flows, and [[monetary-policy]].

## Tariffs as Trade Policy

Tariffs serve a dual role as both fiscal policy (they generate government revenue) and a supply shock (they raise prices for domestic consumers and businesses that rely on imported inputs).

The economic effects of tariffs are complex:

- **Direct effect** — Imported goods become more expensive, discouraging imports and theoretically narrowing the trade deficit.
- **Retaliation** — Trading partners impose counter-tariffs, reducing exports. The net effect on the trade balance can be neutral or even negative.
- **Currency offset** — Tariffs on a country's goods can cause that country's currency to depreciate, partially or fully offsetting the tariff's price effect. This happened with the Chinese yuan during the 2018-2019 trade war.
- **Supply chain disruption** — Modern supply chains are deeply integrated across borders. Tariffs on intermediate goods raise costs for domestic manufacturers, reducing their competitiveness.
- **Inflationary pressure** — Tariffs are a one-time price level increase that can feed into broader [[inflation]] if supply chains are disrupted broadly enough.

See [[2025-tariff-market-volatility]] for the market impact of the 2025 tariff escalation.

## Why the US Trade Deficit Persists

The US trade deficit is structural, not cyclical, driven by the dollar's reserve currency role:

- **Triffin dilemma** — To supply the world with the reserve currency it needs for trade and reserves, the US must run persistent current account deficits. If the US balanced its trade, there would be insufficient dollar liquidity globally. The privilege of issuing the reserve currency comes with the constraint of running deficits.
- **Savings-investment imbalance** — The US has a low domestic savings rate (both private and public). The gap between investment and savings is, by definition, the current account deficit.
- **Structural demand for US assets** — Foreign central banks, sovereign wealth funds, and private investors have structural demand for US Treasuries and US equities, providing the capital account surplus that funds the trade deficit.
- **Dollar strength** — Reserve currency demand keeps the dollar stronger than it would otherwise be, making US exports less competitive.

## Market Implications

**Currency effects**: Countries with improving trade balances tend to see currency appreciation; deteriorating balances tend to see depreciation. However, for the USD, the relationship is weak because capital flows dominate.

**Sector impacts**: A strong currency (from capital inflows) hurts exporters and benefits importers. Domestically-focused sectors are relatively insulated. Trade-sensitive sectors (manufacturing, agriculture, technology hardware) are most affected by tariff policy.

**Bond market**: Foreign demand for US Treasuries — driven by reserve accumulation and trade surplus recycling — has been a persistent force suppressing US yields. A reduction in this demand (de-dollarization, reduced trade surpluses) would put upward pressure on yields, all else equal.

**Equity market**: Trade policy shifts (tariff announcements, trade deals) create short-term volatility. The 2018-2019 trade war and 2025 tariff escalation demonstrated that trade policy is a first-order market driver, not just background noise.
