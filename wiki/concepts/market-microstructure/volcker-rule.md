# Volcker Rule

The **Volcker Rule** is a provision of the [[dodd-frank-act]] (2010) that prohibits banks from engaging in proprietary trading and limits their investments in hedge funds and private equity.

Named after former [[federal-reserve]] Chairman Paul Volcker, the rule was enacted to prevent banks from using depositor-backed funds for speculative trading, addressing [[moral-hazard]] concerns exposed by the [[2008-global-financial-crisis]].

## Key Provisions

The Volcker Rule, codified as Section 619 of the Dodd-Frank Act, contains two core prohibitions:

1. **Ban on proprietary trading**: Banks cannot engage in [[proprietary-trading]] — trading stocks, bonds, derivatives, or other instruments for their own profit (as opposed to on behalf of clients).
2. **Limits on fund investments**: Banks cannot own, sponsor, or have certain relationships with hedge funds or private equity funds beyond 3% of the fund's total ownership interests.

## Exemptions

The rule includes important exemptions that significantly affect its practical scope. Banks are still permitted to engage in:

- **[[market-making|Market making]]**: Providing liquidity to clients by holding inventory, though the line between market making and prop trading is notoriously blurry.
- **Hedging**: Taking positions that offset risk from client-facing activities.
- **Underwriting**: Holding securities temporarily as part of the underwriting process.
- **Government securities trading**: Trading U.S. Treasuries and agency debt is exempt.

## Impact on Wall Street Culture

Before the Volcker Rule, many major banks ran large [[proprietary-trading|proprietary trading]] desks that were significant profit centers. Goldman Sachs's principal strategies group, Morgan Stanley's Process Driven Trading desk, and JPMorgan's chief investment office were all scaled back or shut down. Talented prop traders migrated to hedge funds and independent trading firms, reshaping the industry landscape. The rule fundamentally changed the business model of universal banks, pushing them toward fee-based advisory and asset management revenues.

## 2019-2020 Amendments ("Volcker 2.0")

Regulators (the Fed, FDIC, OCC, SEC and CFTC) finalized amendments in 2019-2020 that simplified compliance and loosened several constraints. The changes tiered banks by trading activity (the strictest documentation requirements apply only to firms with large trading assets), removed the rebuttable presumption that positions held under 60 days were prohibited prop trades, and relaxed the "covered funds" restrictions on bank investments in venture capital and credit funds. Critics argued the amendments reopened doors the original rule was meant to close; defenders said they removed compliance burden without restoring depositor-funded speculation.

## Trading Relevance

- **Liquidity in stressed markets.** The most debated market-structure effect is whether shrinking bank dealer balance sheets reduced [[market-making|market-making]] capacity in [[market-microstructure|less-liquid]] markets — notably corporate bonds and Treasuries — making prices gap further during stress (the rule has been cited in discussions of the March 2020 Treasury-market dislocation). With banks holding less inventory, more market-making migrated to non-bank principal trading firms and [[high-frequency-trading|HFT]]-style electronic liquidity.
- **Industry restructuring as an edge source.** The exodus of prop talent from banks to hedge funds and proprietary trading firms reshaped where quantitative and discretionary [[edge-taxonomy|edge]] is concentrated — much of the risk-bearing and short-horizon liquidity provision that banks once did is now done by lightly regulated specialists.
- **The market-making / prop-trading boundary.** For a trader, the practical legacy is the blurry line between hedging/market-making (permitted) and directional speculation (banned). Understanding how a bank desk must justify inventory as client-facing explains why bank dealers may pull quotes or refuse to warehouse risk precisely when liquidity is most needed.
