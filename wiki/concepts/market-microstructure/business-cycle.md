The business cycle describes the recurring fluctuation between periods of economic expansion and contraction that characterize modern market economies. Understanding the current phase of the cycle is fundamental to [[sector-rotation]], asset allocation, and [[risk-management]] because each phase favors different sectors, asset classes, and trading strategies.

## The Four Phases

### 1. Early Expansion (Recovery)

The economy emerges from recession. [[fed-policy|The Federal Reserve]] typically holds [[interest-rates]] low or begins cutting, credit conditions ease, and consumer confidence starts to recover. [[economic-indicators|Leading indicators]] like the [[ism-pmi|ISM PMI]] cross back above 50, housing starts rise, and the [[yield-curve]] steepens. Sectors that lead in this phase include **Technology** (XLK), **Consumer Discretionary** (XLY), and **Financials** (XLF) -- companies most sensitive to improving credit and consumer spending. Historically this is the best time to be long equities, as markets bottom before the economy does.

### 2. Mid Expansion

Growth is broadly established. Employment is strong, corporate earnings are growing, and GDP growth is above trend. The Fed may begin tightening gradually. **Industrials** (XLI) and **Information Technology** (XLK) tend to perform well as capital expenditure increases. This is often the longest phase, lasting several years. [[relative-strength]] analysis across [[gics-sectors|GICS sectors]] helps identify which groups are maintaining leadership.

### 3. Late Expansion

The economy is running hot. Inflation pressures build, the Fed is actively raising rates, the [[yield-curve]] flattens or inverts, and commodity prices surge. **Energy** (XLE) and **Materials** (XLB) tend to outperform as input prices rise. Wage growth accelerates, profit margins get squeezed, and credit conditions tighten. The [[ism-pmi|ISM PMI]] may still be above 50 but trending lower. This is the most dangerous phase for buy-and-hold investors -- markets often peak during late expansion before the recession is officially recognized.

### 4. Contraction (Recession)

Economic output declines. The National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) defines a recession as "a significant decline in economic activity spread across the economy, lasting more than a few months." In practice, the old rule of thumb is two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth, though the NBER considers employment, industrial production, real personal income, and wholesale/retail sales. Defensive sectors lead: **Utilities** (XLU), **Health Care** (XLV), and **Consumer Staples** (XLP) -- companies selling essential goods and services with stable demand regardless of economic conditions.

## Typical Cycle Duration

Post-World War II business cycles in the United States have averaged roughly 5-6 years, though individual cycles have varied widely. The expansion from June 2009 to February 2020 lasted 128 months (nearly 11 years), the longest on record. Contractions have averaged about 10-11 months but can be as short as 2 months (the COVID recession of 2020) or as long as 18 months (2007-2009).

## Key Transition Indicators

Several [[economic-indicators]] are particularly useful for identifying cycle transitions:

- **[[yield-curve]] inversion** (2s/10s spread turning negative) -- has preceded every US recession since 1955 with only one false signal
- **[[ism-pmi]]** declining below 50 -- signals manufacturing contraction
- **Initial jobless claims** rising above their 4-week moving average trend
- **Conference Board Leading Economic Index** (LEI) declining for 3+ consecutive months
- **Corporate profit margins** peaking and rolling over
- **Credit spreads** (investment grade and high yield) widening sharply

## Trading Implications

The business cycle framework is the backbone of [[sector-rotation]] strategies. The [[sector-momentum-screen|sector rotation + momentum screening]] combination uses cycle identification as its first filter, selecting the top-performing [[gics-sectors|GICS sectors]] aligned with the current phase, then applying [[momentum-trading|momentum screening]] within those sectors. The maxim "don't fight the Fed" reflects how [[fed-policy|Federal Reserve policy]] both responds to and shapes the business cycle's trajectory.
