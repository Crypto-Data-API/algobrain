Fiscal policy refers to the use of government spending and taxation to influence the economy. Along with [[monetary-policy]], it is one of the two primary levers that shape [[macroeconomics|macroeconomic]] conditions. For traders, fiscal policy matters because it directly drives [[gdp|aggregate demand]], determines the supply of [[sovereign-debt|government bonds]], and sets the backdrop against which [[central-bank|central banks]] operate. Major fiscal announcements — budgets, stimulus packages, tax reforms, tariff changes — routinely move [[bond-market|bond]], equity, and currency markets.

## Types of Fiscal Policy

### Expansionary

Expansionary fiscal policy aims to stimulate the economy through increased government spending, tax cuts, or both. It widens the budget deficit and puts more money into the hands of consumers, businesses, or government contractors. Typical contexts include [[recession|recessions]], financial crises, and periods of high unemployment. Examples: the 2009 American Recovery and Reinvestment Act (~$800B), the 2020 CARES Act (~$2.2T), and the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act.

### Contractionary

Contractionary fiscal policy reduces demand through spending cuts, tax increases, or both. It shrinks the deficit (or builds a surplus) and is deployed — rarely, in practice — when the economy is overheating or [[inflation]] is running above target. Austerity programs in the European periphery (2010-2014) are the recent canonical example; they illustrate both the intended deficit reduction and the unintended deepening of [[recession]].

### Timing in the Business Cycle

In theory, governments run deficits during the trough of the [[business-cycle]] and surpluses during the peak. In practice, political incentives skew toward perpetual expansion, which is why structural deficits persist even at full employment — a key concern in the current macro environment.

## Key Mechanisms

### The Fiscal Multiplier

The fiscal multiplier measures how much [[gdp|GDP]] changes per dollar of government spending or tax adjustment. A multiplier above 1.0 means the policy generates more economic activity than it costs; below 1.0 means leakage (saving, imports) absorbs much of the impulse.

| Spending Type | Estimated Multiplier (recession) | Estimated Multiplier (expansion) |
|---|---|---|
| Direct government purchases (infrastructure, defense) | 1.5 - 2.0 | 0.5 - 1.0 |
| Transfer payments (unemployment benefits, stimulus checks) | 1.0 - 1.5 | 0.3 - 0.8 |
| Tax cuts (broad-based) | 0.8 - 1.2 | 0.3 - 0.6 |
| Tax cuts (high-income) | 0.3 - 0.6 | 0.1 - 0.3 |

Multipliers are consistently higher in recessions because idle labor and capital can be mobilized without bidding up prices. At full employment, the multiplier shrinks because new spending mostly displaces private activity.

### Crowding Out

When the government borrows to fund deficits, it competes with private borrowers for a finite pool of savings, pushing [[interest-rates]] higher. This "crowding out" can partially offset the stimulus — firms invest less because capital is more expensive. Crowding out is most relevant near full employment; during deep recessions with near-zero rates, the effect is negligible because private credit demand is weak.

### Automatic Stabilizers

Some fiscal mechanisms activate without any new legislation. Progressive income taxes automatically collect less revenue when incomes fall; unemployment insurance pays out more when layoffs spike. These stabilizers dampen the amplitude of the [[business-cycle]] and are a reason why modern recessions are generally milder than pre-WWII downturns. For traders, automatic stabilizers matter because they set a floor under consumer spending declines.

### Ricardian Equivalence

The theoretical argument, associated with Robert Barro, that deficit-financed tax cuts do not stimulate the economy because rational households anticipate future tax increases and save the windfall. In practice, Ricardian equivalence is rarely fully operative — liquidity-constrained households spend windfalls, and time horizons are shorter than the theory assumes — but it provides a useful framework for thinking about why some stimulus programs have weaker-than-expected effects.

## Fiscal Policy vs. Monetary Policy

| Dimension | Fiscal Policy | [[monetary-policy|Monetary Policy]] |
|---|---|---|
| Who controls it | Congress / Parliament (political) | [[federal-reserve|Central bank]] (technocratic) |
| Speed of implementation | Slow — requires legislation, appropriations | Fast — rate decisions take effect within weeks |
| Transmission mechanism | Direct spending + taxation | Interest rates, credit conditions, asset prices |
| Political constraints | High — subject to elections, lobbying, partisanship | Lower — independent mandate, but political pressure exists |
| Precision | Blunt — hard to target specific sectors cleanly | Moderate — can target credit channels, but broad impact |
| Limit | Debt sustainability / bond market vigilance | Zero lower bound (mitigated by [[quantitative-easing|QE]]) |

In practice the two interact constantly. Fiscal expansion alongside monetary tightening produces high real rates and a strong currency. Fiscal expansion alongside monetary accommodation is maximally stimulative but risks [[inflation]]. When fiscal and monetary authorities work at cross purposes, markets become volatile and the policy signal is ambiguous.

## Market Impact

### Bond Market

Fiscal expansion means more Treasury supply. All else equal, increased issuance pushes yields higher as the market demands a larger term premium to absorb the additional [[sovereign-debt]]. Traders watch CBO deficit projections, Treasury quarterly refunding announcements, and debt-ceiling negotiations as primary catalysts. A surprise increase in projected borrowing can sell off long-duration Treasuries within minutes.

### Equity Market

Fiscal stimulus generally boosts corporate earnings, but the effect is sector-dependent. Infrastructure spending benefits industrials and materials. Defense appropriations flow to aerospace and defense contractors. Healthcare expansion lifts hospital and pharma revenues. Tax cuts directly increase after-tax earnings. [[sector-rotation|Sector rotation]] around major fiscal legislation is a well-established pattern.

### Currency

The currency impact depends on the policy mix. Fiscal expansion combined with monetary tightening attracts foreign capital and strengthens the currency (the "Reagan dollar" of 1981-1985). Fiscal expansion combined with monetary accommodation (twin deficits) tends to weaken the currency over time, as happened with the dollar in 2002-2008.

### Fiscal Dominance

Fiscal dominance is the scenario where government debt levels become so large that the [[central-bank]] loses its ability to control [[inflation]] through [[interest-rates|interest rate]] policy. Raising rates increases debt-servicing costs, which widens the deficit, which requires more borrowing, which pushes rates higher — a self-reinforcing spiral. In this regime, the central bank is effectively forced to keep rates low or monetize the debt through [[quantitative-easing]]. Markets price fiscal dominance risk through the term premium on long-duration bonds and through inflation breakevens.

## Current Relevance (2025-2026)

The US is running a federal deficit exceeding 6% of GDP despite unemployment near historic lows — an unusual combination that normally occurs only during recessions or wars. Federal debt held by the public exceeds 120% of GDP. Net interest payments have surpassed defense spending. These dynamics create several trading-relevant pressures:

- **Bond supply**: Treasury issuance is structurally elevated, supporting higher long-term yields regardless of [[fed-policy|Fed policy]].
- **Fiscal sustainability questions**: Credit default swap spreads on US sovereign debt, while still low, have widened from historical norms.
- **Tariff policy as fiscal policy**: Tariffs function simultaneously as a revenue measure (fiscal) and a supply shock (inflationary). The 2025-2026 tariff escalation blurs the line between fiscal and trade policy, complicating the macro outlook for equities and currencies alike.
- **Political paralysis**: Debt ceiling standoffs and continuing resolutions inject episodic [[volatility]] into Treasury markets, particularly in short-dated bills.

Traders positioning around fiscal developments should monitor CBO scoring of proposed legislation, Treasury auction results (especially tail spreads), and the composition of deficit spending (transfers vs. investment) to gauge the likely multiplier effect.
