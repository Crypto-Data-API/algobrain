Quantitative easing (QE) is an unconventional [[monetary-policy]] tool in which a [[central-bank]] purchases large quantities of government bonds, mortgage-backed securities, or other financial assets to inject money directly into the financial system. QE is deployed when traditional interest rate cuts have reached the zero lower bound and the economy still needs stimulus.

## How It Works

The central bank creates new reserves electronically and uses them to buy assets (primarily Treasury bonds and MBS) from banks and financial institutions. This increases the money supply, lowers long-term [[interest-rates]], pushes investors into riskier assets (the "portfolio rebalancing" effect), and aims to stimulate lending, investment, and economic activity.

## QE in Practice

- **QE1 (Nov 2008)**: The Federal Reserve purchased $1.75 trillion in MBS and Treasuries in response to the [[2008-global-financial-crisis]].
- **QE2 (Nov 2010)**: An additional $600 billion in Treasury purchases to combat slow recovery.
- **QE3 (Sep 2012)**: Open-ended purchases of $85 billion/month until labor market improvement.
- **QE4/Pandemic QE (Mar 2020)**: Unlimited purchases to stabilize markets during COVID-19, expanding the Fed's balance sheet to nearly $9 trillion.

## Impact on Markets

QE is one of the most powerful forces in modern markets. It suppresses bond yields (pushing investors into stock-markets and riskier assets), compresses [[volatility]], inflates asset prices, and weakens the currency. The phrase "don't fight the Fed" reflects the reality that QE can override fundamental valuations for extended periods. Quantitative tightening (QT) -- the reversal of QE -- has the opposite effect and has contributed to market selloffs.

## Why It Matters for Traders

Every trader must understand QE because [[central-bank]] balance sheet policy is now the dominant driver of cross-asset correlations, [[liquidity]] conditions, and risk appetite. Ignoring [[monetary-policy]] is ignoring the single largest participant in global markets.
