# Moral Hazard

**Moral hazard** occurs when an entity takes on excessive risk because it knows that the negative consequences will be borne by someone else -- such as taxpayers or depositors. It is a specific consequence of [[information-asymmetry]]: once a party is insulated from the downside of its actions (and the insulating party cannot perfectly monitor that behavior), the protected party's incentives shift toward more risk than it would otherwise bear.

In finance, moral hazard is closely tied to the [[too-big-to-fail]] doctrine: banks that expect government bailouts have less incentive to manage risk prudently, as demonstrated during the [[2008-global-financial-crisis]].

## Examples in Financial Markets

- **Too-big-to-fail banks** -- large financial institutions that believe they will be rescued by the government in a crisis have an incentive to take outsized risks, since they capture the upside profits while taxpayers absorb catastrophic losses. The 2008 bailouts of AIG, Citigroup, and others reinforced this expectation. See [[too-big-to-fail]].
- **Deposit insurance (FDIC)** -- while deposit insurance prevents bank runs and stabilizes the banking system, it also reduces depositors' incentive to monitor their bank's risk-taking behavior, since their funds are guaranteed up to $250,000.
- **Central bank puts** -- the perception that the [[federal-reserve]] will intervene to support falling markets (the "Fed put," "Greenspan put," "Bernanke put") encourages risk-taking because investors expect a floor under asset prices. This arguably inflates asset valuations beyond fundamentals.
- **Executive compensation** -- bonus structures that reward short-term profits without clawback provisions for subsequent losses incentivize excessive risk-taking by bank executives and traders.

## How Moral Hazard Distorts Markets

When market participants expect to be protected from downside risk, risk premiums compress, leverage increases, and fragile structures proliferate. This creates a paradox: the very measures designed to stabilize the system (bailouts, deposit insurance, central bank intervention) can encourage the risk-taking that makes the next crisis more severe. The 2008 crisis bailouts -- while arguably necessary in the moment -- set a precedent that many argue has amplified moral hazard in the financial system.

The [[dodd-frank-act]] was enacted partly to address moral hazard by imposing stricter capital requirements, stress testing, and orderly liquidation authority for systemically important institutions.

## Trading Relevance

Traders interact with moral hazard primarily through the **"central bank put"** thesis. If a meaningful share of the market believes the [[federal-reserve|Fed]] will cut rates or inject [[liquidity]] whenever asset prices fall sharply, then implied [[volatility]] and credit spreads stay compressed, [[leverage]] builds, and tail-risk is systematically underpriced — until a regime where the put fails to appear (e.g., the 2022 inflation-driven tightening, when the Fed hiked into a falling market). Recognizing when a moral-hazard-supported risk premium is unusually thin is itself a signal: compressed spreads plus rising leverage plus a strong consensus that "they'll backstop it" describe exactly the fragile conditions that precede disorderly de-risking. Conversely, bailout/backstop announcements (TARP 2008, the 2023 Bank Term Funding Program) tend to mark sharp short-term reversals in the assets being backstopped.
