A catalyst is a specific event or development expected to move a stock's price -- earnings reports, management changes, regulatory decisions, capital raises, product launches, or macro events. Identifying catalysts before they're priced in is central to both fundamental and [[event-driven-trading|event-driven]] strategies.

## Types of Catalysts

Catalysts can be broadly categorized by their nature and predictability. **Scheduled catalysts** have known dates: earnings announcements, FDA decision dates, central bank meetings, shareholder votes, and index rebalancing dates. These are anticipated by the market, so the edge lies not in knowing the date but in correctly predicting the outcome and its magnitude. **Unscheduled catalysts** are surprises: management departures, activist investor involvement, M&A offers, fraud revelations, product failures, or geopolitical shocks. These create the largest price dislocations because the market has had no opportunity to price them in. **Structural catalysts** operate over longer timeframes: regulatory changes, industry consolidation, spin-offs, or demographic shifts. They are often slower-moving but can create sustained directional moves.

## Catalyst Investing as a Strategy

[[event-driven-trading|Catalyst investing]] focuses on taking positions ahead of identified catalysts with asymmetric risk/reward. The approach requires answering three questions: what is the catalyst, when will it occur, and what is the expected price impact? The ITPM framework treats catalysts as essential to trade construction -- a position without an identifiable catalyst is essentially a bet on valuation recognition, which can take indefinitely long. Catalysts compress the time horizon, which is important because carrying a position has costs (opportunity cost, margin, theta for options positions). Combining fundamental analysis (to identify mispricing) with catalyst identification (to determine when the mispricing will be resolved) is more powerful than either approach alone.

## Distinguishing Catalysts from Noise

Not every piece of news is a genuine catalyst. A catalyst must be material enough to change the consensus view of a company's value. Analyst upgrades/downgrades, minor product updates, or industry conferences rarely qualify unless they contain genuinely new information. The key test: does this event change the base case for the company's earnings, growth, or risk profile? If not, any price reaction is likely to be temporary noise rather than a lasting re-rating. Experienced catalyst investors also pay attention to **second-order catalysts** -- events that don't directly affect a company but change the environment (e.g., a competitor's failure creating market share opportunity, or a regulatory ruling on a peer that sets a precedent).
