A capital raise is the process by which a listed company raises additional funds from shareholders or investors, typically through rights issues, placements, or share purchase plans. Capital raises result in [[share-dilution]] as new shares are issued. Fred McNaught evaluates capital raises by assessing whether the offer price adequately compensates investors for the risk, what the funds will be used for (growth acquisition vs. balance sheet repair), and the company's track record with prior raises. He notes that mining companies like [[evolution-mining|Evolution Mining]] frequently raise capital for acquisitions, and the key question is whether the acquired assets will generate returns above the cost of the dilution.

## Types of Capital Raises

**Rights Issues** allow existing shareholders to purchase new shares in proportion to their current holdings, typically at a discount to the market price (often 10-30%). Rights may be renounceable (tradeable on the exchange, allowing shareholders who do not wish to participate to sell their entitlement) or non-renounceable (use it or lose it). Rights issues are considered the most shareholder-friendly form of capital raise because they give all shareholders equal opportunity to maintain their proportional ownership.

**Institutional Placements** involve issuing new shares directly to institutional investors, usually via an accelerated bookbuild that completes overnight or over a few days. Placements are faster and cheaper to execute than rights issues but are dilutive to retail shareholders who are excluded from participating. On the [[asx-limited|ASX]], companies can place up to 15% of their issued capital without shareholder approval under ASX Listing Rule 7.1, with an additional 10% capacity available under Rule 7.1A if approved at the annual general meeting.

**Share Purchase Plans (SPPs)** allow existing retail shareholders to buy a limited amount of new shares (up to A$30,000 per holder on the ASX) at a price equal to or below the placement price. SPPs are typically offered alongside institutional placements to give retail shareholders a chance to participate, though the amounts are capped and allocations may be scaled back if oversubscribed.

**[[ipo|Initial Public Offerings (IPOs)]]** are the original capital raise — a private company selling shares to the public for the first time. Secondary offerings refer to any subsequent equity issuance after the IPO.

## Dilution Impact and Pricing

Every capital raise creates [[share-dilution]]: existing shareholders own a smaller percentage of the company after new shares are issued. The key question is whether the capital raised will generate returns that exceed the cost of dilution. A company raising $500 million at a 15% discount to acquire an asset generating 20% returns on capital is value-accretive despite the dilution. A company raising the same amount to plug a balance sheet hole created by poor prior decisions is value-destructive — shareholders are paying to fix management's mistakes.

The discount to market price matters because it determines how much value transfers from existing shareholders to new participants. Deep discounts (20%+ below market) signal urgency or distress. Modest discounts (5-10%) suggest the company is negotiating from a position of relative strength. Pricing is also influenced by market conditions — companies raising capital during a market downturn may be forced to accept deeper discounts, which is why well-managed companies tend to raise capital before they need it, when their stock price is strong.

## Evaluating a Capital Raise

Investors should assess several factors when a portfolio company announces a capital raise: the stated purpose of the funds (acquisition, debt reduction, working capital, exploration), the discount to market price, whether the raise is structured to include retail shareholders (rights issue or SPP component), the company's history with prior raises (did past raises create value?), and the post-raise balance sheet metrics. Repeated capital raises with no corresponding improvement in returns on capital are a red flag that the business cannot self-fund its operations — a sign of structural weakness rather than healthy growth.
