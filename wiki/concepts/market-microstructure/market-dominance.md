**Market dominance** is a company's controlling position within its industry -- a level of [[market-share]] and structural advantage large enough that the firm sets, rather than takes, the terms of competition. Where market share is a number, dominance is what that number lets a company *do*: command [[pricing-power]], dictate terms to suppliers and distributors, and deter entrants. It is a core screen in quality-focused [[fundamental-analysis]].

## Overview

A dominant company is not merely the largest player; it is one whose position is durable and self-reinforcing. Dominance typically rests on one or more sources of [[economic-moat]]:

- **Network effects** -- each new user makes the product more valuable to others (exchanges, social platforms, marketplaces), creating a winner-take-most dynamic.
- **Economies of scale** -- the largest producer has the lowest per-unit cost, which funds price cuts or reinvestment that smaller rivals cannot match.
- **Brand and switching costs** -- entrenched customer habits and the cost/friction of switching lock in revenue.
- **Regulatory or distribution barriers** -- licenses, patents, or control of a critical channel keep competitors out.

Dominance is measured against the relevant *addressable* market, not a loosely defined sector -- a firm can dominate a niche while being small in a broader category. Antitrust regulators (US DOJ/FTC, EU Commission) use dominance tests built on market share thresholds combined with barriers to entry; in EU competition law, share above ~40% raises a presumption of dominance.

## The 10% Rule

A practical investing heuristic comes from Fred McNaught: a company should hold **more than 10% market share** in its sector to be considered a dominant player worth owning. The reasoning is that below ~10%, a firm is usually a price-taker with little structural advantage, whereas crossing that threshold tends to unlock the economies of scale and bargaining power that compound returns over time. Rising share is treated as a stronger signal than absolute level -- a company taking share from rivals is demonstrating a live [[competitive-advantage]] (Source: [[fred-sam-session-2024-02-09]]).

## Trading Relevance

For investors, dominance is a quality and durability filter rather than a timing signal:

- **Pricing power** -- dominant firms can raise prices ahead of inflation without losing volume, protecting margins through downturns.
- **Margin and return profile** -- dominance usually shows up as above-average gross margins, high returns on invested capital, and stable [[market-share]] over multiple years.
- **Valuation premium** -- the market pays up for durable dominance, so the risk is overpaying; the edge is in spotting dominance *before* it is fully recognized, or in firms whose share is quietly compounding.
- **Erosion risk** -- the key thing to monitor is whether dominance is widening or narrowing. Technological disruption, regulation (antitrust action against [[microsoft]], Google), or a new entrant with a different cost structure can erode a moat. Falling share even amid rising revenue is a warning.
