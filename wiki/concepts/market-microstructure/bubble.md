# Bubble

A bubble is a market condition in which the price of an asset inflates far beyond its intrinsic value, driven by speculative excess, herd behavior, and self-reinforcing feedback loops. Bubbles always eventually burst, often with devastating consequences for participants who bought near the peak.

## Anatomy of a Bubble

Economist Hyman Minsky identified five stages:

1. **Displacement**: A new technology, policy, or innovation creates genuine excitement and early profits.
2. **Boom**: Prices rise, attracting more participants. Media coverage increases. Early skeptics are proven "wrong."
3. **Euphoria**: Valuations detach from fundamentals entirely. "This time is different" becomes the consensus. [[leverage]] accelerates buying.
4. **Profit-taking**: Smart money begins to exit. Warning signs appear but are dismissed by the majority.
5. **Panic**: A trigger causes rapid selling. [[liquidity]] evaporates, prices collapse, and [[leverage]] forces cascading liquidations.

## Famous Bubbles

- **Dutch Tulip Mania (1637)**: Tulip bulb prices reached the equivalent of luxury homes before collapsing overnight.
- **South Sea Bubble (1720)**: British stock speculation ended in financial ruin for thousands, including Isaac Newton.
- **[[1929-crash]]**: The Roaring Twenties stock bubble led to the [[great-depression]].
- **Dot-com Bubble (1995-2000)**: Internet stocks reached absurd valuations before the NASDAQ fell 78%.
- **U.S. Housing Bubble (2003-2007)**: [[subprime-mortgage]] lending inflated housing prices, triggering the [[2008-global-financial-crisis]].

## Reflexivity and Bubbles

[[george-soros]]'s theory of [[reflexivity]] explains why bubbles form: rising prices improve fundamentals (easier capital raising, more confidence), which justifies further price increases in a self-reinforcing loop -- until the process exhausts itself and reverses.

## Why It Matters for Traders

Recognizing bubbles is one of the most valuable skills in trading, but also one of the hardest: bubbles can persist far longer than expected, and shorting a bubble prematurely has destroyed many traders. The key is [[risk-management]] -- participating in the upside while maintaining strict exit discipline. "The market can remain irrational longer than you can remain solvent."

The existence of bubbles is itself evidence against the strong form of the [[efficient-market-hypothesis]]: prices can and do detach from fundamentals for extended periods. When a bubble bursts, the unwind is often a [[crashes|crash]] followed by a prolonged [[bear-market]], as [[leverage]] forces selling and confidence takes years to rebuild.
