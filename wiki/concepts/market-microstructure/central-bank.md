# Central Bank

A **central bank** is a national institution responsible for monetary policy, [[interest-rates]], money supply, and financial system stability. Central bank decisions are among the most powerful drivers of financial markets.

Major central banks include the [[federal-reserve]] (U.S.), [[bank-of-england]] (U.K.), European Central Bank (ECB), and Bank of Japan (BOJ).

For the U.S. central bank, see [[federal-reserve]].

## Policy Tools

Central banks wield several instruments to influence economic conditions and financial markets:

- **Interest rate policy** -- setting benchmark short-term [[interest-rates]] (e.g., the Fed Funds rate) is the primary tool. Lower rates stimulate borrowing and risk-taking; higher rates cool the economy and tighten financial conditions.
- **Quantitative easing (QE)** -- purchasing government bonds and other assets to inject liquidity into the financial system and push down long-term yields. QE was used extensively after 2008 and during the 2020 COVID crisis.
- **Reserve requirements** -- mandating how much capital banks must hold in reserve, affecting the money multiplier and credit creation.
- **Forward guidance** -- communicating future policy intentions to shape market expectations. The Fed's "dot plot" (projections of future rate levels by FOMC members) is a key forward guidance tool.

## Major Central Banks

- **Federal Reserve (Fed)** -- United States. The most influential central bank globally; its decisions ripple across all asset classes worldwide.
- **European Central Bank (ECB)** -- Eurozone. Manages monetary policy for 20 member states.
- **[[bank-of-japan|Bank of Japan (BOJ)]]** -- known for ultra-loose policy including negative interest rates and yield curve control.
- **Bank of England (BOE)** -- United Kingdom.
- **People's Bank of China (PBOC)** -- manages the world's second-largest economy with unique tools including medium-term lending facilities.

## How Central Bank Decisions Move Markets

Rate decisions, policy statements, press conferences, and meeting minutes can trigger massive moves across equities, bonds, currencies, and commodities. Traders closely watch scheduled announcements (FOMC meetings are 8 times per year) and parse every word for hawkish or dovish shifts. Even subtle changes in language can move billions in asset values within seconds.
