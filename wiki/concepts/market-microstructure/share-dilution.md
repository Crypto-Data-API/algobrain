Share dilution occurs when a company increases its total shares outstanding, reducing existing shareholders' ownership percentage and typically lowering [[earnings-per-share]]. Common causes include [[capital-raise|capital raises]], exercise of employee stock options, and share-based compensation. Fred McNaught tracks dilution carefully, particularly in the mining sector where frequent capital raises can erode shareholder value significantly over time. He compares the dilution cost against the expected return on the capital raised -- if the new capital generates returns above the company's cost of equity, dilution may be justified; otherwise it destroys value.

## Sources of Dilution

Dilution occurs through several mechanisms. **Secondary offerings** (seasoned equity offerings) are the most direct: the company issues new shares and sells them on the market, typically at a discount to the current price. **Employee stock options and RSUs** create dilution when exercised or vested, adding shares to the count. In the technology sector, stock-based compensation (SBC) is a major and persistent source of dilution -- companies like Tesla, Amazon, and Meta issue billions of dollars in SBC annually, which investors often overlook because it is a non-cash expense. **Convertible bonds** and **convertible preferred shares** create contingent dilution: if the stock price rises above the conversion price, bondholders will convert to equity, increasing shares outstanding. **Warrants** function similarly, giving holders the right to purchase shares at a set price. Investors should always examine the **fully diluted share count**, which includes all shares that could be created from outstanding options, warrants, and convertible securities, rather than relying solely on the basic share count.

## Measuring and Tracking Dilution

The impact of dilution is best measured by comparing the growth in shares outstanding against the growth in earnings or book value. If shares outstanding grow at 5% per year but earnings grow at 10%, shareholders are still gaining value on a per-share basis. However, if shares grow at 5% and earnings grow at 3%, the dilution is eroding per-share value. Key metrics to monitor include: the fully diluted share count reported in financial statements (usually found in the notes), the ratio of SBC to revenue or net income, and the net effect after accounting for [[share-buybacks]] (which offset dilution). Some companies are chronic diluters -- particularly in mining, biotech, and early-stage technology -- while others maintain strict share count discipline.

## Anti-Dilution Protections

Certain investors, particularly venture capitalists and holders of preferred stock, negotiate **anti-dilution provisions** to protect their ownership percentage. The most common types are **full ratchet** (the conversion price is adjusted down to the new, lower issue price) and **weighted average** (the conversion price is adjusted based on the number of new shares issued and the price differential). These protections are standard in private company financing rounds and convertible security terms. Retail shareholders of public companies generally have no anti-dilution protections and must rely on [[share-buybacks]], management discipline, and shareholder voting rights to prevent excessive dilution.

## Trading Relevance

For a trader, dilution is a per-share value leak that headline market-cap and revenue numbers can hide. Two stocks with identical enterprise value can deliver very different shareholder returns if one steadily prints shares. Practical checks: compare the year-over-year change in **diluted shares outstanding** against EPS growth; treat persistent stock-based-compensation dilution as a real (non-cash) cost when valuing high-SBC tech names; and watch for overhang from convertibles, warrants, and ATM ("at-the-market") equity programs, which let a company issue into strength and cap upside. In small caps, biotech, and mining, serial dilution is a structural short or avoid signal; conversely, a credible [[share-buybacks|buyback]] that shrinks the count is a tailwind to per-share metrics. Event-wise, announced secondary offerings typically gap the stock down toward the (discounted) offer price — a recurring, tradeable pattern.
