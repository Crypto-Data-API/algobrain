Elasticity of demand (price elasticity of demand) measures how sensitive the quantity demanded of a good or service is to a change in its price. It is one of the most direct readouts of a company's **pricing power**, which in turn is among the strongest signals of a durable [[economic-moat]]. Inelastic demand means customers keep buying even when prices rise; elastic demand means they readily cut back or switch to substitutes.

## How It Works

Price elasticity of demand (PED) is defined as:

```
PED = (% change in quantity demanded) / (% change in price)
```

Because the two move in opposite directions, PED is normally negative; it is conventionally quoted as an absolute value:

- **Inelastic demand (|PED| < 1)** — quantity falls *less* than proportionally to a price rise. The seller has pricing power: raising price increases total revenue. Indicates strong [[pricing-power]] and a likely [[economic-moat]].
- **Unit elastic (|PED| = 1)** — quantity falls in exact proportion; revenue is unchanged.
- **Elastic demand (|PED| > 1)** — quantity falls *more* than proportionally; raising price *reduces* total revenue. Customers have alternatives or the purchase is discretionary.

Key determinants of elasticity: availability of substitutes, share of the buyer's budget the item consumes, whether it is a necessity or discretionary, switching costs, and time horizon (demand is usually more elastic over longer periods as buyers adapt).

| Inelastic (pricing power) | Elastic (price-taker) |
|---------------------------|------------------------|
| Essential medicines, insulin | Luxury goods, discretionary apparel |
| Tobacco, addictive products | Commoditised goods with many substitutes |
| Utilities (water, power) | Mid-tier consumer electronics |
| Subscription software with high switching costs (e.g. infrastructure SaaS) | Generic/private-label staples |

## Trading Relevance

Demand elasticity is a core lens in [[fundamental-analysis]] for judging the quality and durability of a business:

- **Pricing power = moat.** A company with inelastic demand can raise prices without losing customers, which directly supports [[gross-profit|gross-margin]] expansion and is one of the clearest markers of a durable [[economic-moat]]. ITPM-style analysis (as discussed by Fred McNaught) treats inelastic demand — companies like Cloudflare that can raise prices with minimal churn — as evidence that margin expansion is structurally supported rather than cyclical.
- **Inflation pass-through.** Firms with inelastic demand can pass input-cost [[inflation]] through to customers, protecting margins during inflationary periods; elastic-demand firms absorb cost shocks and see margins compress.
- **Revenue forecasting.** Elasticity governs whether a planned price increase grows or shrinks revenue — central to modelling forward revenue and to assessing management's pricing actions.
- **Sector behaviour.** Inelastic-demand sectors (utilities, staples, healthcare) are classic defensives that hold revenue through downturns; elastic-demand discretionary sectors are far more cyclical.
