# Subprime Mortgages

Subprime mortgages are home loans issued to borrowers with poor credit histories, low incomes, or insufficient documentation -- borrowers who do not qualify for conventional "prime" lending. The mass origination and securitization of subprime loans was the primary catalyst of the [[2008-global-financial-crisis]].

## How Subprime Fueled the Crisis

Lenders issued subprime loans with adjustable rates, teaser rates, and minimal documentation ("NINJA loans" -- no income, no job, no assets). These loans were bundled into **mortgage-backed securities (MBS)**, then further sliced and restructured into **collateralized debt obligations (CDOs)**. [[credit-rating-agencies]] gave AAA ratings to senior CDO tranches, allowing pension funds and banks worldwide to buy what was effectively junk credit packaged as safe investments.

## The Role of Credit Default Swaps

**Credit default swaps (CDS)** functioned as insurance contracts on MBS and CDOs. Firms like AIG sold massive quantities of CDS without adequate reserves, assuming housing prices would never decline nationally. When defaults surged, CDS payouts threatened to bankrupt AIG, requiring a $182 billion government bailout.

## The Chain of Failure

Subprime defaults -> MBS losses -> CDO implosions -> CDS payouts -> bank insolvency -> credit freeze -> global recession. The entire chain was amplified by excessive [[leverage]] at every level and by the failure of [[credit-rating-agencies]] to accurately assess risk.

## Why It Matters for Traders

The subprime crisis demonstrates how financial engineering can obscure risk, how [[leverage]] amplifies losses through the system, and how seemingly safe assets can become toxic. For traders, the lesson is to always understand the underlying collateral and to be skeptical of complexity that claims to eliminate risk. These dynamics recur in different forms -- including in crypto through overleveraged lending protocols and the 2022 collapse of Terra/Luna and Three Arrows Capital.
