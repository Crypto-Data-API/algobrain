The **Form 10-Q** is the quarterly report U.S. public companies file with the [[sec|SEC]] for each of their first three fiscal quarters. It is a condensed, **unaudited** update on the financial statements and the business between the comprehensive annual [[10-k|10-K]] filings. For active traders it is the periodic document most tightly coupled to short-term price action, because it lands during [[earnings-season]] and confirms (or contradicts) the headline numbers in the [[earnings|earnings press release]].

## Overview

A company files **three 10-Qs per year** — for Q1, Q2, and Q3. There is no fourth-quarter 10-Q; the fourth quarter is rolled into the audited annual [[10-k|10-K]]. The 10-Q is lighter than the 10-K in two important ways: its financial statements are **reviewed but not audited** by the independent accountant, and its narrative is condensed. It is filed electronically on [[edgar|SEC EDGAR]] and is free to read.

The 10-Q is distinct from the **earnings release**, which usually comes out first as a press release (often filed as an [[8-k|8-K]] under Item 2.02) and drives the immediate stock reaction. The 10-Q follows days to weeks later with the full statements and footnotes — and occasionally reveals details the upbeat release glossed over.

## What It Contains

The 10-Q has two Parts:

**Part I — Financial Information**
- **Item 1 — Financial Statements**: condensed, unaudited [[balance-sheet|balance sheet]], [[income-statement|income statement]], [[cash-flow-statement|cash-flow statement]], and statement of equity, with abbreviated footnotes. Numbers are typically shown for the quarter and the year-to-date, with prior-year comparatives.
- **Item 2 — Management's Discussion and Analysis (MD&A)**: management's narrative on the quarter's results, liquidity, and known trends.
- **Item 3 — Quantitative and Qualitative Disclosures About Market Risk**.
- **Item 4 — Controls and Procedures**: disclosure-control effectiveness and any changes to internal controls.

**Part II — Other Information**
- **Item 1 — Legal Proceedings** and **Item 1A — Risk Factors** (only *material changes* since the last 10-K).
- **Item 2 — Unregistered Sales / Use of Proceeds / [[share-buybacks|Repurchases]]**: monthly buyback activity.
- **Items 3–6 — Defaults, Mine Safety, Other Information, Exhibits**.

## What an Investor Looks For

- **Sequential and year-over-year trends** — revenue, margins, and cash flow versus both the prior quarter and the same quarter a year ago. One quarter is noise; the trajectory across several 10-Qs is signal.
- **The cash-flow statement** — operating cash flow that consistently lags reported net income is an early warning of earnings quality problems (see [[earnings]]).
- **Balance-sheet movement** — building inventory or ballooning receivables can foreshadow a future revenue miss; rising short-term debt signals liquidity stress.
- **Updated Risk Factors (Part II, Item 1A)** — companies only restate risks here when something *material* changed since the 10-K, so any new language is worth reading closely.
- **Buyback pace (Part II, Item 2)** — confirms whether management is actually executing an authorized repurchase, which flatters per-share [[earnings]].
- **Subsequent-events footnote** — events after quarter-end but before filing that holders need to know.

## Timing and Cadence

The deadline depends on filer category:
- **Large accelerated and accelerated filers**: within **40 days** of quarter-end.
- **Non-accelerated / smaller reporting companies**: within **45 days**.

Because most companies share calendar-aligned quarters, 10-Qs cluster into the same [[earnings-season|earnings-season]] weeks (roughly mid-April, mid-July, and mid-October in the U.S.), concentrating single-stock volatility and the [[post-earnings-announcement-drift|post-earnings drift]] window.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Trading the headline and ignoring the filing.** The press-release beat can mask a deteriorating balance sheet that only the 10-Q reveals; the [[post-earnings-announcement-drift|drift]] sometimes reflects the market slowly digesting the full filing.
- **Forgetting the statements are unaudited.** A 10-Q is *reviewed*, not audited; restatements are more likely to surface later at the 10-K.
- **Annualizing one quarter naively.** Seasonal businesses (retail holiday quarters, tax-season names) cannot be multiplied by four — compare like quarter to like quarter.
- **Missing the Form NT 10-Q.** A "Notification of Late Filing" is a yellow flag, often signalling an accounting review or auditor disagreement.
- **Reading only Part I.** Part II hides the repurchase pace, new legal proceedings, and changed risk factors.

## A Hypothetical Example

Imagine **XYZ Corp** (fictional) reports a Q2 earnings release showing EPS a few cents above consensus, and the stock gaps up. Ten days later the 10-Q lands. In Item 1, an analyst sees that accounts receivable jumped 35% quarter-over-quarter while revenue rose only 6%, and operating cash flow turned negative even though net income was positive. Part II adds a new risk factor about a delayed product launch. The audited reality in the filing tempers the headline beat — and the stock that initially gapped up drifts lower over the following weeks as the market reads past the press release. This gap between the release and the filing is a recurring reason to read the actual 10-Q.
