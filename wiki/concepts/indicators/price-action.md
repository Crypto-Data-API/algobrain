Price action is the study of raw price movements on a chart to make trading decisions, without reliance on lagging [[indicators]] such as [[moving-averages]], [[rsi]], or [[macd]]. Practitioners read [[candlestick-patterns]], [[support-and-resistance]] levels, [[chart-patterns]], and the rhythm of higher highs and lower lows directly from the price chart. It is considered the purist form of [[technical-analysis]].

## Overview

Price action analysis rests on the premise that price itself is the most timely and complete source of market information. All indicators are derived from price (and sometimes [[volume-analysis|volume]]), so they inherently lag. By reading price directly, a trader can react faster and with less noise.

The philosophical roots trace back to [[tape-reading]] in the early 20th century, as practiced by [[jesse-livermore]] and documented in [[book-reminiscences-of-a-stock-operator]]. Modern price action traders like Al Brooks, Bob Volman, and Lance Beggs have systematized the approach into teachable frameworks, but the core skill remains pattern recognition and contextual judgment.

Key principles:
- **Price discounts everything** -- all information (fundamental, sentiment, flow) is reflected in the price
- **Context over pattern** -- the same candlestick pattern means different things at [[support-and-resistance]], in a trend, or in a range
- **Simplicity** -- clean charts with minimal indicators force the trader to focus on what matters
- **Subjectivity is a feature** -- skilled discretionary judgment is the edge, not a formula

## How It Works

### Core Building Blocks

**1. Candlestick Analysis**
Individual and multi-bar [[candlestick-patterns]] reveal the battle between buyers and sellers. Key patterns include pin bars (rejection), engulfing bars (momentum shift), inside bars (consolidation), and doji (indecision). The size of bodies and wicks relative to context communicates urgency and conviction.

**2. Market Structure**
Reading the sequence of swing highs and swing lows to determine [[trend]] direction:
- **Uptrend**: higher highs and higher lows
- **Downtrend**: lower highs and lower lows
- **Range**: roughly equal highs and lows

Trend changes are identified when this sequence breaks -- e.g., a lower low in an uptrend signals potential reversal.

**3. Support and Resistance**
Horizontal price levels where buying or selling pressure has historically appeared. Price action traders watch for how price behaves at these levels -- does it reject sharply (continuation of the level) or slice through (breakout)? See [[support-and-resistance]] for detailed treatment.

**4. Chart Patterns**
Larger formations composed of multiple swings: [[head-and-shoulders]], double tops/bottoms, [[flags-and-pennants]], triangles, and [[cup-and-handle]]. These patterns encode the psychology of accumulation, distribution, and breakout. See [[chart-patterns]].

### Reading Context

The critical skill in price action trading is interpreting patterns within their broader context:
- A pin bar at a major [[support-and-resistance]] level after a prolonged downtrend is far more significant than one appearing mid-range
- An inside bar in a strong trend is a continuation signal; in a range, it is noise
- Volume confirmation (when used) adds conviction -- a breakout on high [[volume-analysis|volume]] is more reliable than one on thin volume

### Timeframe Considerations

Price action works on all timeframes but the signals carry different weight:
- **Higher timeframes** (daily, weekly) -- more reliable signals, represent larger participants
- **Lower timeframes** (1-minute, 5-minute) -- more noise, useful for entry timing within a higher-timeframe thesis
- **Multi-timeframe analysis** -- the most effective approach: establish direction on higher frames, time entries on lower frames

## Trading Applications

### Entry Techniques
- **Pullback entries** -- wait for a retracement to a key level within a trend, then enter on a rejection candle
- **Breakout entries** -- enter when price closes beyond a significant level with conviction (strong close, increasing volume)
- **Failed breakout entries** -- when a breakout reverses quickly, trade in the opposite direction (traps)

### Exit and Stop Placement
- **Stop-loss** -- placed beyond the signal bar's extreme or beyond the relevant structure level
- **Targets** -- set at the next significant [[support-and-resistance]] level or using measured moves from [[chart-patterns]]
- **Trailing stops** -- moved to lock in profits as price creates new structure (e.g., trail below each higher low in an uptrend)

### Advantages and Limitations

**Advantages**: Minimal lag, works across all markets and timeframes, develops deep market reading skills, requires no paid indicators or software.

**Limitations**: Highly subjective -- two traders can read the same chart differently. Requires significant screen time to develop competence. Difficult to backtest systematically due to discretionary elements.
