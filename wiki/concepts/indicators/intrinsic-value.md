**Intrinsic value** is the estimated "true" or "fair" value of a security based on its underlying fundamentals -- earnings, assets, cash flows, and growth prospects -- independent of its current market price. The concept is the cornerstone of [[value-investing]]: buy when the market price is significantly *below* intrinsic value (creating a [[margin-of-safety]]), and sell when the price exceeds it.

## Overview

The concept of intrinsic value was formalized by [[benjamin-graham|Benjamin Graham]] and [[david-dodd|David Dodd]] in *Security Analysis* (1934). They argued that while market prices fluctuate wildly due to emotion, speculation, and short-term news, the underlying value of a business changes slowly and can be estimated through rigorous analysis of [[financial-statement-analysis|financial statements]].

Graham's famous allegory of "Mr. Market" illustrates the relationship: the market is like a manic-depressive business partner who offers to buy or sell shares every day at wildly varying prices. The intelligent investor ignores Mr. Market's mood swings and focuses on whether the offered price is above or below the business's intrinsic value.

[[warren-buffett|Warren Buffett]] defined intrinsic value as "the discounted value of the cash that can be taken out of a business during its remaining life." This definition shifts emphasis from accounting earnings to actual cash generation -- a critical distinction because earnings can be manipulated through accounting choices while cash flow is harder to fake.

## How It Works

**Methods for Estimating Intrinsic Value:**

**1. Discounted Cash Flow (DCF):** The theoretically correct approach. Project future free cash flows, then discount them back to present value using an appropriate discount rate (typically the [[weighted-average-cost-of-capital|weighted average cost of capital]]). The sum of discounted future cash flows = intrinsic value.

- Strength: Directly values what the business generates
- Weakness: Highly sensitive to assumptions about growth rates and discount rates; small changes in inputs produce large changes in output

**2. Earnings-Based Valuation:** Multiply normalized earnings by an appropriate [[price-to-earnings-ratio|P/E multiple]]. Graham suggested intrinsic value = EPS x (8.5 + 2g), where g is the expected annual growth rate. This provides a quick estimate but is less rigorous than DCF.

**3. Asset-Based Valuation:** Sum the fair market value of all assets minus liabilities. Graham preferred this for defensive investing -- buying stocks trading below their net current asset value (NCAV = current assets - total liabilities) provided an extreme margin of safety. (Source: [[book-the-intelligent-investor]])

**4. Earnings Power Value (EPV):** Capitalize current normalized earnings at the cost of capital, assuming no growth. This isolates the value of the existing business without speculating on future growth. If EPV > asset value, the company has a franchise/moat.

**The Margin of Safety:**

Intrinsic value is inherently an *estimate*, not a precise number. Graham and Buffett emphasized that investors should only buy when the market price is substantially below intrinsic value -- typically 30-50% below. This "[[margin-of-safety]]" protects against:
- Errors in the intrinsic value estimate
- Unforeseen negative developments
- Market irrationality persisting longer than expected

## Trading Applications

**Stock Selection:** Value investors screen for stocks where the market price is well below estimated intrinsic value. This typically means:
- Low [[price-to-earnings-ratio|P/E ratios]] relative to earnings quality
- Price below book value or net asset value
- High free cash flow yield
- Depressed price due to temporary problems (not permanent impairment)

**Sell Discipline:** Intrinsic value also provides a sell signal. When a stock's price rises to or above estimated intrinsic value, the margin of safety disappears and the position should be trimmed or closed. This prevents the common mistake of holding winners too long.

**Avoiding Value Traps:** Not every cheap stock is undervalued. A low P/E may reflect genuinely deteriorating fundamentals rather than market mispricing. Distinguishing true undervaluation from "value traps" requires deep [[financial-statement-analysis]] -- examining whether earnings are sustainable, whether the balance sheet is sound, and whether competitive advantages are intact. (Source: [[book-security-analysis]])

**Buffett's Evolution:** While Graham focused on quantitative cheapness (statistical bargains), Buffett evolved the framework to pay fair prices for "wonderful companies" with durable competitive advantages ([[economic-moat|economic moats]]). In this view, intrinsic value includes the compounding value of reinvested earnings by an excellent business at high returns on capital.
