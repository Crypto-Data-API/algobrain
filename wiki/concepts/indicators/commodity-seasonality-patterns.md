**Commodity seasonality** refers to the systematic, calendar-based price patterns that recur in commodity markets due to **physical production and consumption cycles**. Unlike equity seasonality (which is more behavioral), commodity seasonality is anchored in real-world phenomena -- harvest schedules, weather patterns, heating/cooling demand, livestock biology, and industrial cycles. These patterns are among the most robust seasonal effects in financial markets because their underlying drivers are physical and persistent. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Why Commodity Seasonality Exists

Commodity seasonal patterns are driven by factors that are structural and recurring:

1. **Production cycles:** Crops are planted and harvested on a fixed calendar. Harvest floods the market with supply; pre-harvest periods create scarcity
2. **Weather-driven demand:** [[natural-gas]] demand peaks in winter (heating) and summer (cooling). [[gasoline]] demand peaks in summer (driving season)
3. **Biological cycles:** [[live-cattle]] have a 2+ year production cycle; [[lean-hogs]] have ~6 months. Breeding patterns create predictable supply waves
4. **Industrial demand cycles:** Metal demand follows construction and manufacturing seasons
5. **Cultural/religious events:** Gold demand peaks around Chinese New Year and Indian wedding season (Diwali)
6. **Policy calendars:** Ethanol mandates, export quotas, and government purchasing programs follow fiscal/calendar years

These patterns persist because **you cannot change when corn needs to be harvested** or when winter arrives, regardless of how many traders know about the pattern.

## Energy Commodity Seasonality

### Natural Gas

[[natural-gas]] has the most pronounced seasonality of any major commodity:

| Season | Pattern | Driver |
|--------|---------|--------|
| **Winter (Nov-Mar)** | Demand peak, prices tend higher | Heating demand (residential + commercial) |
| **Spring (Apr-May)** | Shoulder season, typically weakest | Heating ends, cooling not yet started |
| **Summer (Jun-Aug)** | Secondary demand peak | Air conditioning power generation demand |
| **Fall (Sep-Oct)** | Injection season, storage builds | Mild weather, supply builds ahead of winter |

Key metric: the **storage injection/withdrawal vs 5-year average** determines whether winter supply will be adequate. Below-average storage entering winter is the strongest seasonal bullish signal.

### Crude Oil and Refined Products

| Product | Peak Season | Driver |
|---------|-------------|--------|
| **[[gasoline]]** | May-September | Summer driving season; refiners switch to costlier summer-blend gasoline (RVP spec changes) |
| **[[heating-oil]]** (diesel) | October-March | Heating demand in the US Northeast; diesel demand from harvest-season trucking |
| **[[crude-oil]]** | Less pronounced seasonality | Partly offset by refinery maintenance schedules (turnarounds in spring and fall) |

The **gasoline crack spread** (gasoline minus crude) tends to widen from March to June and narrow from September to November. Refinery turnaround season (spring) temporarily reduces supply and supports product prices.

## Agricultural Commodity Seasonality

Agricultural commodities have the most intuitive seasonality because they are tied directly to planting and harvest calendars.

### Northern Hemisphere Grains (Corn, Soybeans, Wheat)

| Period | Pattern | Driver |
|--------|---------|--------|
| **Feb-Jun** | Prices tend to rally | "Weather premium" builds; uncertainty about planted acreage and growing conditions |
| **Jul** | Peak volatility | [[corn]] pollination; make-or-break weather window |
| **Aug** | [[soybeans]] critical period | Pod fill determines soybean yield |
| **Sep-Nov** | Prices tend to decline | Harvest pressure; new supply floods the market |
| **Dec-Jan** | Consolidation | Demand data and South American crop conditions dominate |

The **15-year seasonal average** for corn shows prices are typically:
- Lowest: late September / early October (harvest)
- Highest: late June / early July (pre-pollination weather premium)
- Average seasonal range: ~15-20% from low to high

### Southern Hemisphere (Brazilian Soybeans, Argentine Crops)

Brazil and Argentina harvest from February to May, creating a **second supply injection** that affects global prices:

| Period | Pattern | Driver |
|--------|---------|--------|
| **Oct-Dec** | South American planting | Weather during planting affects early crop estimates |
| **Jan-Feb** | La Nina/drought risk peak (Argentina) | Argentine drought risk is highest in January |
| **Feb-May** | South American harvest | Brazilian soybean harvest; supply surge depresses global prices |
| **May-Aug** | South American safrinha corn | Brazil's second-crop corn is now a major global supply factor |

### Soft Commodities

| Commodity | Key Seasonal | Driver |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| **[[coffee]]** | Jun-Aug: frost premium in Brazil | Southern Hemisphere winter frost risk; biennial bearing cycle |
| **[[sugar]]** | Apr-Nov: Brazilian crushing season | UNICA data on sugar/ethanol allocation drives near-term supply |
| **[[cocoa]]** | Oct-Mar: West African main crop harvest | Main crop season in Ivory Coast and Ghana |
| **[[cotton]]** | Jun-Jul: Texas drought risk peak | US cotton crop concentrated in drought-prone Texas |

### Livestock

| Commodity | Seasonal Pattern | Driver |
|-----------|-----------------|--------|
| **[[live-cattle]]** | Strongest: Apr-Aug | Grilling season demand; reduced cattle marketings in spring |
| **[[lean-hogs]]** | Peak: Jun-Jul; Trough: Nov-Jan | Summer grilling demand; fall oversupply from spring pig crop |

## Metals Seasonality

Metals have less pronounced seasonality than agriculture or energy, but some patterns are documented:

| Metal | Seasonal Tendency | Driver |
|-------|-------------------|--------|
| **[[gold]]** | Strong: Jan-Feb, Aug-Sep | Chinese New Year buying, Indian wedding season (Diwali), post-summer portfolio allocation |
| **[[silver]]** | Similar to gold, plus spring industrial demand | Overlaps gold safe-haven demand + spring industrial/solar panel manufacturing |
| **[[copper]]** | Tends stronger: Jan-Apr | Chinese restocking after Lunar New Year; spring construction season |
| **[[platinum]]/[[palladium]]** | Less pronounced | Auto manufacturing cycles provide some regularity |

## How to Use Seasonal Patterns

### The Overlay Method

1. **Calculate the 5/10/15/20-year seasonal average** for the commodity (use Barchart, MRCI, or custom calculation)
2. **Overlay** the current year's price action against the seasonal average
3. **Identify deviations:** If the current price is significantly below the seasonal average heading into a historically strong period, the seasonal tailwind may provide support
4. **Confirm with fundamentals:** Seasonal patterns work best when confirmed by fundamental conditions (e.g., low stocks heading into a strong demand season)

### Seasonal Spread Trading

See [[seasonal-spread-trading]] for detailed strategies. Key seasonal spreads:

- **Calendar spreads:** Buy the month before a seasonal demand peak, sell the month after (e.g., long July corn / short December corn)
- **Inter-commodity spreads:** Corn-wheat, corn-soybean, gold-silver ratios have their own seasonal patterns
- **Crack/crush spreads:** [[crush-spread]] (soybeans) and [[crack-spread]] (crude oil products) have seasonal components tied to demand cycles

### Entry/Exit Timing

Seasonality is best used as a **timing overlay** on a fundamental or technical thesis:

- Enter trades aligned with the seasonal direction during the seasonal window
- Avoid fighting the seasonal pattern unless there is a strong fundamental reason
- Use seasonal patterns to set trade duration expectations

### Worked Example — A Natural-Gas Winter Setup (qualitative, numbers illustrative)

The numbers below are **illustrative**, chosen to demonstrate the decision process rather than to report a real trade.

1. **Seasonal context.** [[natural-gas]] tends to rally into winter on heating demand. The 5-year seasonal average typically shows strength from late October through January.
2. **Fundamental confirmation.** Suppose EIA storage entering November sits roughly 8% below the 5-year average — the strongest seasonal-bullish signal (low stocks into a strong-demand season). Seasonality alone is weak; seasonality *plus* a confirming fundamental is the high-conviction case.
3. **Entry.** Enter long (or a long calendar/spread) in late October, aligned with the seasonal window, sized as a *timing overlay* on the storage thesis — not as a standalone bet on the calendar.
4. **Risk.** The seasonal edge is modest (historically a single-digit to mid-teens percentage move) and easily overridden by a warm-winter forecast. Position size and stops reflect that the pattern is a *tendency*, not a certainty.
5. **Exit.** Plan to exit into the seasonal-strength window (December–January) rather than holding into the spring shoulder season, when the pattern reverses.
6. **What would invalidate it.** A mild-winter forecast, an above-average storage surprise, or a demand shock would override the seasonal pattern entirely — the discipline is to *stand aside or cut* when the fundamental contradicts the season, never to fight it on the calendar alone.

The example generalises: **seasonality sets the direction and timing; fundamentals decide whether to take the trade; risk management decides the size.** A seasonal pattern with no fundamental confirmation is a low-conviction trade; a fundamental thesis with seasonal tailwind is the high-conviction case.

## Interaction With Other Factors

Seasonal patterns rarely operate in isolation. The strongest setups arise when the seasonal tendency aligns with other structural and macro forces; the weakest are when those forces point the other way.

| Interacting factor | Reinforces seasonality when… | Overrides seasonality when… |
|---|---|---|
| Inventory / storage vs 5-yr avg | Stocks low into a strong-demand season | Stocks abundant into demand season |
| Futures-curve shape ([[contango]] / [[backwardation]]) | Backwardation confirms tight near-term supply | Deep contango signals oversupply |
| Weather forecasts | Forecast matches the seasonal driver (cold winter, hot summer) | Forecast contradicts it (mild winter) |
| Macro regime ([[risk-off]] / USD) | Calm regime lets physical drivers dominate | Risk-off / strong-USD swamps the seasonal signal |
| Roll yield | Positive carry adds to the seasonal return | Negative roll erodes a modest seasonal edge |

The futures-curve interaction is especially important for traders who hold via futures: a modest seasonal price move can be entirely consumed by negative roll yield in a [[contango]] market. The realised return on a seasonal trade is the spot move *plus* the roll yield, not the spot move alone — which is one reason [[seasonal-spread-trading|seasonal spreads]] (which net out much of the carry) are often preferred over outright seasonal longs.

## Statistical Robustness

Not all seasonal patterns are created equal. Key considerations:

| Factor | Strong Seasonality | Weak Seasonality |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Win rate** | 65-80% across 15+ years | Below 60% |
| **Physical driver** | Clear, persistent (weather, harvest) | Behavioral only |
| **Consistency** | Pattern holds in most years | Pattern driven by a few extreme years |
| **Magnitude** | Average seasonal move > transaction costs | Small average moves easily eroded by costs |
| **Capacity** | High (grain, energy calendars) | Low (thin markets) |

### Known Limitations

- **Seasonality is backward-looking:** Past patterns do not guarantee future results. Climate change, policy changes, and structural shifts can alter seasonal patterns
- **Fundamental override:** In years with extreme fundamentals (severe drought, demand shock, geopolitical crisis), the seasonal pattern may be completely overridden
- **Crowding risk:** As more traders follow the same seasonal patterns, the edge may compress. However, physical commodity seasonality is harder to arbitrage away than equity seasonality because the underlying drivers are physical
- **Transaction costs:** Seasonal edges tend to be modest (5-15% average moves). High transaction costs can erode the edge, especially for frequent rotation strategies
- **Averaging effect:** Seasonal averages can be distorted by a few extreme years (e.g., a single 50% drought rally can inflate the average for that month)
