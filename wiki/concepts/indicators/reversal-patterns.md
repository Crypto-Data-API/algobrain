**Reversal patterns** are price formations that signal an existing [[trend]] is likely to *change direction* — an uptrend turning down, or a downtrend turning up. They are one of the two broad families of [[chart-patterns]] (the other being **continuation patterns**, which signal a pause before the trend resumes). Reversal patterns appear at both candlestick scale (one to a few bars) and chart scale (formations that build over weeks), and traders use them to time exits, take profits, or position for a new trend.

## Why reversal patterns matter

A trend reflects a sustained imbalance between buyers and sellers. A reversal pattern is the visual evidence that this imbalance is **exhausting** — the prevailing side is running out of new participants and the opposing side is starting to win at a key [[support-and-resistance]] level. Catching a reversal early offers an attractive reward-to-risk setup: a tight stop just beyond the pattern's extreme, against a potentially large move in the new direction. The trade-off is that reversals are inherently lower-probability than trend-continuation trades, because most of the time a trend continues rather than turns.

## Candlestick-scale reversal patterns

These form over one to a handful of [[candlestick-patterns|candles]] and signal a *short-term* shift in momentum:

- **[[doji]]** — a candle with a negligible real body, showing indecision after a directional run.
- **[[hammer]]** and hanging man — a long lower wick rejecting lower prices (bullish at a bottom) or signalling exhaustion at a top.
- **[[pin-bar]]** — a long-wicked bar rejecting a level, the price-action analogue of the hammer/shooting star.
- **[[bullish-engulfing]]** / bearish engulfing — a candle that fully engulfs the prior one, showing a decisive shift in control.
- **[[evening-star]]** and **[[morning-star]]** — three-candle clusters marking tops and bottoms respectively.

## Chart-scale reversal patterns

These build over longer periods and are generally considered higher-conviction:

- **[[head-and-shoulders]]** (and its inverse) — three peaks with a higher middle, confirmed when price breaks the **[[neckline]]**.
- **[[double-top]]** and double-bottom — two failed attempts at the same extreme.
- Triple tops/bottoms, rounding tops/bottoms, and rising/falling wedges that resolve against the trend.

## How traders use them

1. **Identify the prevailing trend** the pattern would reverse — a reversal pattern is meaningless without a trend to reverse.
2. **Wait for confirmation.** Most reversal patterns are only "complete" once a trigger occurs — a [[neckline]] break, a close beyond the signal candle, or a break of the prior swing. Acting before confirmation is a common source of [[false-signals]].
3. **Check [[trading-volume]] and context.** Reversals are more reliable when they form at a significant [[support-and-resistance]] level and on rising volume into the break.
4. **Define risk against the pattern's extreme.** The stop typically sits just beyond the high/low that formed the pattern; the measured target is often the pattern's height projected from the breakout (see [[head-and-shoulders]]).

## Worked example (hypothetical)

Suppose a stock has rallied for two months and then prints a **[[double-top]]** at $50 — two failed pushes to the same price with a trough (the "neckline") at $46 between them. A trader treats the pattern as confirmed only when price closes below **$46**. They enter short on the break, place a stop just above $50, and project a measured target of roughly the $4 pattern height below the neckline (~$42). If price instead reclaims $50, the reversal has failed and the stop limits the loss. These figures are illustrative, not a forecast.

## Limitations

- **Confirmation lag vs. reliability trade-off.** Waiting for confirmation reduces [[false-signals]] but gives up part of the move; acting early improves entry but raises the failure rate.
- **Subjectivity.** Where a "neckline" or "shoulder" sits is partly in the eye of the chartist; the same chart yields different patterns to different traders.
- **Base rates favour continuation.** Trends persist more often than they reverse, so reversal signals carry a meaningful false-positive rate — they are best used with confluence, not in isolation.
- **Timeframe dependence.** A reversal on a 5-minute chart may be noise within an intact daily uptrend. Always read the pattern in the context of the higher timeframe trend.
