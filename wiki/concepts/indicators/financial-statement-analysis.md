**Financial statement analysis** is the systematic evaluation of a company's income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement to assess its financial health, profitability, and [[intrinsic-value]]. It is the core skill of [[fundamental-analysis]] and the foundation upon which all earnings-based [[valuation]] rests. As [[benjamin-graham|Graham]] and [[david-dodd|Dodd]] argued, the intelligent investor must understand what the numbers say -- and what they hide. (Source: [[book-security-analysis]])

## Overview

Public companies report three primary financial statements each quarter and annually:

1. **Income Statement** (Profit & Loss): Shows revenues, expenses, and net income over a period. Answers: "How much did the company earn?"
2. **Balance Sheet**: Shows assets, liabilities, and shareholders' equity at a point in time. Answers: "What does the company own and owe?"
3. **Cash Flow Statement**: Shows actual cash inflows and outflows from operations, investing, and financing. Answers: "Where did the cash come from and go?"

Each statement reveals different aspects of the business. A profitable company (strong income statement) can still be in trouble if it is burning cash (weak cash flow statement) or drowning in debt (stressed balance sheet). Comprehensive analysis requires examining all three together.

## How It Works

**Income Statement Analysis:**

Key metrics derived from the income statement:
- **Revenue growth** -- top-line growth rate year-over-year and quarter-over-quarter
- **Gross margin** (gross profit / revenue) -- measures production efficiency and pricing power
- **Operating margin** (operating income / revenue) -- measures core business profitability
- **Net margin** (net income / revenue) -- bottom-line profitability after all expenses and taxes
- **[[earnings-per-share|EPS]]** -- net income divided by shares outstanding; the denominator of the [[price-to-earnings-ratio|P/E ratio]]

Trend analysis matters more than absolute levels. Expanding margins suggest improving competitive position; contracting margins may signal pricing pressure or rising costs.

**Balance Sheet Analysis:**

- **Current ratio** (current assets / current liabilities) -- measures short-term liquidity; above 2.0 is conservative
- **Debt-to-equity ratio** -- measures financial leverage; lower is generally safer
- **Book value per share** -- net asset value available to shareholders; compare to market price
- **Net current asset value (NCAV)** -- current assets minus *total* liabilities; Graham's most conservative valuation floor
- **Return on equity (ROE)** -- net income / shareholders' equity; measures how efficiently the company deploys capital

**Cash Flow Statement Analysis:**

- **Operating cash flow (OCF)** -- cash generated by core business operations; should consistently exceed net income
- **Free cash flow (FCF)** -- OCF minus capital expenditures; the cash available for dividends, buybacks, debt reduction, or reinvestment
- **Capex-to-depreciation ratio** -- if consistently above 1.0, the company is investing for growth; if below, it may be underinvesting
- **Quality of earnings** -- comparing net income to operating cash flow. If net income far exceeds OCF, earnings may be inflated by [[accrual-accounting|accrual accounting]] tricks

**Red Flags to Watch:**
- Revenue growing but cash flow declining
- Rising accounts receivable faster than revenue (may signal channel stuffing)
- Frequent "one-time" charges that aren't really one-time
- Off-balance-sheet obligations (operating leases, pension liabilities)
- Frequent changes in accounting methods

## Trading Applications

**Fundamental Screening:** Investors use financial statement ratios to screen for attractive stocks. Classic Graham-style screens include: low [[price-to-earnings-ratio|P/E]], price below book value, positive earnings for 10+ consecutive years, current ratio above 2.0, and long-term debt below net current assets. (Source: [[book-the-intelligent-investor]])

**Earnings Quality Assessment:** [[peter-lynch|Peter Lynch]] emphasized understanding *where* earnings come from. A company growing earnings by cutting costs has a finite runway; one growing by expanding into new markets has a longer one. Cash flow analysis separates genuine earnings from accounting artifacts. (Source: [[book-one-up-on-wall-street]])

**Competitive Position Analysis:** Comparing financial statements across competitors reveals relative strengths. Higher margins, better returns on capital, and stronger cash generation typically indicate a [[economic-moat|competitive moat]].

**Trend Analysis:** Multi-year analysis reveals whether a business is improving or deteriorating. Single-period snapshots can be misleading. Graham recommended examining at least 7-10 years of financial history before making investment decisions.

**Short Selling:** Detailed financial statement analysis can identify companies likely to disappoint -- those with deteriorating cash flows masked by aggressive accounting, excessive leverage, or unsustainable revenue recognition practices.
