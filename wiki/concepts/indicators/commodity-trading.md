Commodity trading involves buying and selling physical goods -- metals, energy, agriculture, and livestock -- primarily through [[futures|futures contracts]]. Commodity markets are driven by real-world supply and demand dynamics, making them fundamentally different from [[stocks-overview|equities]] and offering unique diversification benefits.

## Major Commodity Categories

### Energy
- **Crude Oil** ([[oil|WTI]], Brent): The world's most actively traded commodity. Price driven by OPEC production, geopolitics, global demand, and inventories
- **Natural Gas**: Seasonal demand (heating/cooling), storage levels, LNG exports
- **Gasoline, Heating Oil**: Refined products with their own supply/demand dynamics

### Metals
- **[[gold|Gold]]**: Safe haven, inflation hedge, central bank reserves. Inversely correlated with the US dollar and real interest rates
- **Silver**: Industrial and precious metal hybrid
- **Copper**: "Dr. Copper" -- considered a leading economic indicator due to broad industrial use
- **Platinum, Palladium**: Automotive catalytic converters, industrial use

### Agriculture
- **Grains**: Corn, wheat, soybeans -- affected by weather, planting reports, export demand
- **Softs**: Coffee, sugar, cocoa, cotton
- **Livestock**: Live cattle, lean hogs

## How Commodities Trade

### Futures Contracts
The primary instrument for commodity trading. Key exchanges:
- **CME Group** (COMEX, NYMEX): Gold, silver, crude oil, natural gas
- **ICE** (Intercontinental Exchange): Brent crude, coffee, cocoa, sugar
- **CBOT** (Chicago Board of Trade): Grains, soybeans

Each futures contract specifies the commodity, quantity, quality, delivery date, and delivery location. Most traders close positions before delivery -- physical delivery is rare for speculators.

### Contango and Backwardation
The shape of the futures term structure affects returns:
- **Contango**: Futures price > spot price. Rolling contracts forward costs money ("negative roll yield"). Common in well-supplied markets
- **Backwardation**: Futures price < spot price. Rolling contracts forward earns money ("positive roll yield"). Typical in tight supply situations

This dynamic is critical for commodity ETFs and [[trend-following]] strategies that hold rolling futures positions.

### Seasonality
Many commodities exhibit predictable seasonal patterns:
- **Natural gas**: Prices tend to rise into winter (heating demand) and summer (cooling demand)
- **Grains**: Prices often decline into harvest and rise into planting season (weather uncertainty)
- **Gasoline**: "Summer driving season" typically lifts gasoline prices April-June
- Seasonality is well-documented but not a standalone strategy -- it provides context, not guaranteed returns

## Trading Strategies

### Trend Following
Commodity markets tend to trend due to slow-moving supply/demand shifts. [[trend-following|Managed futures / CTA]] strategies allocate heavily to commodities:
- Long trends in oil during supply disruptions
- Short trends in agricultural products during bumper harvests
- Multi-asset trend following across commodity, equity, bond, and FX futures

### Fundamental/Supply-Demand Analysis
- Monitoring inventory reports (EIA crude oil inventories, USDA crop reports)
- Tracking production decisions (OPEC meetings, mine output)
- Weather models for agricultural commodities
- Import/export data and shipping rates

### Spread Trading
Trading the price difference between related commodities or contract months:
- **Calendar spreads**: Long one delivery month, short another (front-month vs. back-month)
- **Inter-commodity spreads**: Crack spread (crude oil vs. gasoline), crush spread (soybeans vs. soy meal/oil)
- **Geographic spreads**: WTI vs. Brent crude

### CTA (Commodity Trading Advisor) Strategies
Professional commodity fund managers registered with the CFTC. Predominantly systematic/quantitative, using [[trend-following]] and [[momentum]] signals across commodity futures.

## Risk Characteristics

- **High volatility**: Commodities can experience extreme price moves due to supply shocks, weather events, and geopolitical disruptions
- **No intrinsic cash flows**: Unlike stocks (dividends) or bonds (coupons), commodities generate no income -- returns come purely from price appreciation and roll yield
- **Storage costs**: Physical commodities incur storage, insurance, and transportation costs
- **Leverage**: Futures contracts are inherently leveraged (small margin for large notional exposure)
- **Correlation**: Commodities have historically low correlation with stocks and bonds, making them valuable for [[diversification]]
