A dividend is a cash payment distributed to shareholders from a company's profits, typically on a quarterly basis. Dividends represent a direct return of capital to investors and are one of only two ways stock investors are compensated — the other being capital appreciation. Dividend yield, calculated as annual dividend divided by current stock price, is a key metric for income-oriented investors.

## Overview

Companies that pay dividends are typically mature, profitable businesses generating more cash than they need for reinvestment. The decision to pay (or increase) a dividend signals management's confidence in future earnings stability. Conversely, a dividend cut is one of the strongest negative signals a company can send, often triggering sharp sell-offs.

[[benjamin-graham]] considered a long and unbroken dividend record as one of his primary screening criteria for defensive investors. In *[[book-the-intelligent-investor|The Intelligent Investor]]*, he required at least 20 years of uninterrupted dividend payments. Graham viewed dividends as tangible evidence that a company's reported [[earnings]] translated into real cash flow, not just accounting profits.

**Dividend Aristocrats** are S&P 500 companies that have increased their dividend for 25 or more consecutive years. This elite group — including names like Johnson & Johnson, Procter & Gamble, and Coca-Cola — is widely followed by income investors as a proxy for financial durability and shareholder-friendly management.

## The Dividend Timeline (Four Key Dates)

A dividend moves through four dates that every income investor must understand:

| Date | What happens | Why it matters |
|------|--------------|----------------|
| **Declaration date** | Board announces the amount and the schedule | The dividend becomes a legal liability of the company |
| **Ex-dividend date** | First day the stock trades *without* the right to the next dividend | You must own the stock *before* this date to be paid; price typically drops by ~the dividend amount at the open |
| **Record date** | Company checks its books for registered holders | Set by the company; under T+1 settlement the ex-date is the record date (formerly one day before) |
| **Payment date** | Cash is actually deposited into accounts | Usually a few weeks after the ex-date |

Worked example: a board declares a **$0.50** quarterly dividend on June 1, with an ex-dividend date of June 15 and a payment date of July 1. To collect the $0.50, you must own the shares at the close of June 14 (the day *before* the ex-date). On the morning of June 15, a stock that closed at $40.00 would, all else equal, open near **$39.50** — the dividend has left the company and the share price reflects it. See [[ex-dividend-date]] for the trading nuances (and why "dividend capture" rarely produces a free lunch after tax and price-drop effects).

## Key Formulas

**Dividend yield** — annual income relative to price:

```
Dividend Yield = Annual Dividend Per Share / Current Share Price
```

A stock paying $4.00/year at $100/share yields **4.0%**. Yields move inversely to price: if the same stock falls to $80, the yield rises to **5.0%** — which can mean a bargain *or* a market pricing in a coming cut (a "yield trap").

**Payout ratio** — share of profit paid out:

```
Payout Ratio = Dividends Per Share / Earnings Per Share
             = Total Dividends / Net Income
```

A company earning $5.00 EPS and paying $2.00 yields a **40%** payout ratio — comfortable, with room to grow. A payout ratio above **80-90%** may be unsustainable; above **100%** the company is paying out more than it earns, a red flag unless temporary. ([[reits]] are an exception — they are *required* to distribute 90%+ of taxable income, so high payout ratios are normal.) A more conservative version uses [[free-cash-flow|free cash flow]] in the denominator, since dividends are paid in cash, not accounting earnings.

**Dividend coverage ratio** — the inverse of the payout ratio (`EPS / DPS`); a coverage of 2.5× means earnings cover the dividend two-and-a-half times over.

## Trading Applications

- **Income investing**: Building a portfolio of reliable dividend payers to generate steady cash flow, particularly for retirees. [[reits]] are a specialized form of this, required by law to distribute 90%+ of income.
- **Dividend growth investing**: Focusing on companies with a track record of increasing dividends, which compounds the income stream over time. Total return (dividends + appreciation) often outperforms non-dividend strategies over long periods.
- **Value screening**: High dividend yield is a classic [[value-investing]] signal. Graham and [[david-dodd]] used dividend yield as a primary valuation metric in [[book-security-analysis|Security Analysis]]. However, unusually high yields require investigation — they may indicate a dividend cut is coming.
- **[[compounding]] power**: Reinvesting dividends via DRIPs / [[drp|Dividend Reinvestment Plans]] dramatically increases long-term returns by buying more shares each quarter, which then pay their own dividends. Historically, reinvested dividends account for roughly 40-50% of total S&P 500 returns over multi-decade periods.
- **Tax considerations**: Qualified dividends receive preferential tax treatment in many jurisdictions, making them more tax-efficient than interest income. However, in taxable accounts, forced dividend distributions create a tax drag that growth stocks avoid.

## DRIP Worked Example

Suppose you own **100 shares** of a stock at **$50** ($5,000) paying a **$2.00** annual dividend (a 4% yield) and you enrol in a [[drp|DRIP]]:

- Year-1 dividend = 100 × $2.00 = **$200**, which buys ~4 more shares at $50 → **104 shares**.
- Year 2, the same $2.00/share now pays 104 × $2.00 = **$208**, buying ~4.16 more shares.

Each year the share count grows, so each year the cash dividend grows even if the per-share payout never changes — the [[compounding]] flywheel. Add modest annual dividend *growth* (a [[dividend-aristocrats|Dividend Aristocrat]] raising the payout ~7%/year) and the income stream can double roughly every decade from growth alone, before any reinvestment.

## How Traders and Investors Use Dividends

- **Income vs. total return.** Income investors prize the *cash*; total-return investors note that a dividend is not "free" — the share price drops by the dividend on the ex-date, so a dividend is partly a return *of* capital, not purely a return *on* it.
- **Quality signal.** A long, unbroken, *rising* dividend record is hard to fake and screens for durable cash generation — the core of [[defensive-sectors|defensive]] and quality investing.
- **Cut as a warning.** A dividend cut or suspension is one of the strongest bearish signals a company can send and usually triggers a sharp selloff.
- **Yield-trap discipline.** Treat any yield far above the sector norm as a question, not a gift — check the [[free-cash-flow|cash-flow]] coverage and the payout ratio before buying.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Chasing yield.** The highest-yielding stocks are often the riskiest; an unsustainable payout precedes a cut, which costs both income *and* capital.
- **Ignoring the price drop.** "Dividend capture" (buying just before the ex-date to grab the dividend) is undercut by the ex-date price drop plus taxes and trading costs.
- **Confusing yield direction.** A *rising* yield often reflects a *falling* price — verify whether the business is healthy.
- **Overlooking tax drag.** Forced distributions are taxable events in taxable accounts even if you immediately reinvest them.
