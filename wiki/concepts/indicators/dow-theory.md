Dow Theory is the oldest and most fundamental framework in [[indicators-ta-primer|technical analysis]], originating from editorials by Charles Dow -- co-founder of Dow Jones & Company and The Wall Street Journal -- in the late 1890s. Later formalized by William Hamilton (*The Stock Market Barometer*, 1922) and Robert Rhea (*The Dow Theory*, 1932), its principles -- that markets move in trends, trends have three phases, and averages must confirm each other -- remain the philosophical foundation of modern TA. [[john-murphy]] devotes Chapter 2 of *[[technical-analysis-of-the-financial-markets]]* to Dow Theory, treating it as the bedrock upon which all subsequent technical tools are built. (Source: [[book-technical-analysis-of-the-financial-markets]])

## The Six Tenets

### 1. The Market Discounts Everything

All known information -- fundamentals, earnings expectations, political developments, natural disasters, market sentiment -- is already reflected in the price of the averages. This is the philosophical justification for studying price action rather than underlying economic data. The technician assumes that the market's collective judgment, expressed through price, is more efficient than any individual analysis of fundamentals.

This principle directly anticipates the Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) of the 1960s, though Dow Theory practitioners would argue that markets are efficient in discounting information but still exhibit exploitable trends. (Source: [[book-technical-analysis-of-the-financial-markets]])

### 2. The Market Has Three Trends

Dow identified three categories of trend operating simultaneously:

- **Primary trend** (months to years): The major bull or bear market. This is the tide -- the dominant direction. Murphy emphasizes that the primary trend is the most important to identify and trade with.
- **Secondary trend** (weeks to months): Corrections within the primary trend. Typically retraces 33% to 66% of the prior primary move. These are the waves within the tide.
- **Minor trend** (days to weeks): Short-term fluctuations within secondary movements. The ripples -- largely noise and not tradeable within the Dow Theory framework.

Murphy stresses that traders should always know the primary trend and trade in its direction, using secondary corrections as entry opportunities. (Source: [[book-technical-analysis-of-the-financial-markets]])

### 3. Primary Trends Have Three Phases

**Bull market phases:**
1. **Accumulation phase**: Smart money (informed investors, institutions) begins buying when sentiment is most bearish and valuations are depressed. The market has stopped going down, but the public sees no reason to buy. Volume is low.
2. **Public participation phase**: The trend becomes obvious. Economic data improves, earnings rise, and the broader public begins buying. This is the longest and most profitable phase, where [[trend-following]] strategies perform best.
3. **Excess/distribution phase**: Speculation reaches a peak. The financial media is euphoric, retail participation surges, IPOs and speculative assets proliferate. Smart money begins selling to the late-arriving public.

**Bear market phases:**
1. **Distribution phase**: Smart money exits while the public is still buying the dip. Prices may set marginal new highs on declining [[volume]] and breadth.
2. **Panic phase**: Selling accelerates. Prices drop sharply, volume is heavy, margin calls force liquidation. This is where the steepest declines occur.
3. **Despair phase**: Selling grinds on. The public exits in disgust. Headlines are uniformly negative. Valuations reach attractive levels -- setting up the next accumulation phase.

### 4. Averages Must Confirm Each Other

Originally: the Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA) and the Dow Jones Transportation Average (DJTA) must both make new highs (or new lows) to confirm a valid trend signal. The logic is that industrial production (Industrials) requires goods to be shipped (Transports) -- if only one average makes new highs while the other diverges, the signal is suspect.

**A divergence** -- one average making a new high while the other fails to confirm -- is one of the most important warning signals in Dow Theory. For example, the Industrials making a new all-time high while the Transports fail to surpass their prior peak warns that the uptrend may be weakening.

**Modern application**: While the specific Dow/Transport pair is less relevant in a services and technology-driven economy, the confirmation principle extends to using multiple market indices, sectors, or even asset classes for confirmation. This concept is the precursor to Murphy's [[intermarket-analysis]]. (Source: [[book-technical-analysis-of-the-financial-markets]])

### 5. Volume Confirms the Trend

Volume should expand in the direction of the primary trend:
- In a bull market: volume should be heavier on rallies and lighter on pullbacks
- In a bear market: volume should be heavier on declines and lighter on rallies

Declining volume on rallies in a bear market, or on selloffs in a bull market, confirms the underlying primary trend remains intact. Volume divergence (price making new highs on shrinking volume) is an early warning of potential trend exhaustion. (Source: [[book-technical-analysis-of-the-financial-markets]])

### 6. A Trend Is in Effect Until It Gives Definitive Reversal Signals

The trend is assumed to continue until proven otherwise by a clear pattern of reversal. For an uptrend, this means a sequence of lower highs and lower lows must be established -- not just a single pullback. For a downtrend, higher highs and higher lows must appear.

This tenet encourages patience and discourages trying to pick tops and bottoms. Murphy emphasizes that it is far more profitable to follow confirmed trends than to anticipate reversals. (Source: [[book-technical-analysis-of-the-financial-markets]])

## Practical Dow Theory Signals

### Bull Signal
A bull signal occurs when:
1. Both the DJIA and DJTA make secondary corrections (pullback from highs)
2. Both averages then rally above their prior highs
3. Volume expands on the breakout

### Bear Signal
A bear signal occurs when:
1. Both averages fail to make new highs (or set lower highs)
2. Both averages break below their prior secondary lows
3. Volume expands on the breakdown

### Non-Confirmation Warning
When one average makes a new high or low but the other does not follow within a reasonable time (weeks, not days), it warns of a potential trend change. This is not an immediate reversal signal, but it puts the current trend on probation.

## Criticism

- **Slow**: By definition, Dow Theory signals come after a significant portion of the move has already occurred. A bull signal requires both a decline and a subsequent rally above prior highs, meaning traders miss the bottom by a wide margin.
- **Subjective**: What constitutes a "definitive" reversal? How much time can pass between one average's confirmation and the other's? Different practitioners reach different conclusions from the same data.
- **Narrow scope**: Originally designed for a two-index US equity market. Modern markets are global, multi-asset, and operate 24/7 (crypto). The Industrial/Transport pair may not capture today's economy.
- **No position sizing or risk management**: Dow Theory tells you the trend direction but not how much to risk or where to place stops.

## Murphy's Integration

Murphy treats Dow Theory not as a standalone trading system but as the philosophical foundation on which all subsequent TA tools are built. Every indicator, chart pattern, and trading system Murphy covers in *[[technical-analysis-of-the-financial-markets]]* is ultimately an attempt to operationalize Dow's principles more precisely and with less lag:

- [[moving-averages]] quantify the trend that Dow identified visually
- [[rsi]] and [[macd]] measure the momentum behind Dow's three trend phases
- [[chart-patterns]] formalize the accumulation/distribution dynamics Dow described
- [[intermarket-analysis]] extends Dow's "averages must confirm" principle across asset classes

Understanding Dow Theory is prerequisite to understanding why all of these tools exist and what they are trying to accomplish. (Source: [[book-technical-analysis-of-the-financial-markets]])

## Historical Significance

Charles Dow (1851-1902) never formally published "Dow Theory" as a unified framework. His ideas were scattered across Wall Street Journal editorials between 1899 and 1902. It was Hamilton who synthesized Dow's editorials into a coherent theory, and Rhea who tested it against historical market data. Despite being over 120 years old, the core principles have survived because they describe fundamental properties of crowd psychology and price behavior that transcend specific markets or eras.
