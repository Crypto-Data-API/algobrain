Economic indicators are statistical data points released by government agencies, central banks, and private organizations that measure the health, direction, and momentum of the economy. Traders and investors use these releases to anticipate [[fed-policy|Federal Reserve policy]] changes, identify [[business-cycle]] phase transitions, and position ahead of market-moving events.

## Three Categories

### Leading Indicators

Leading indicators change direction *before* the economy does, making them the most valuable for traders:

- **[[yield-curve]]** -- An inverted curve (short-term rates exceeding long-term rates) has preceded every US recession since 1955
- **[[ism-pmi|ISM New Orders sub-index]]** -- Forward-looking component of the manufacturing PMI
- **Building permits** -- Housing construction plans signal future economic activity
- **Stock market** (S&P 500) -- Equity prices reflect collective expectations about future earnings
- **Average weekly hours (manufacturing)** -- Employers cut hours before cutting jobs
- **Conference Board Leading Economic Index (LEI)** -- Composite of 10 leading indicators

### Coincident Indicators

These move in real time with the economy and confirm the current state:

- **GDP (Gross Domestic Product)** -- The broadest measure of economic output, released quarterly with advance, preliminary, and final readings
- **Industrial production** -- Factory, mining, and utility output
- **Nonfarm employment** -- Total payrolls excluding agriculture
- **Real personal income** -- Inflation-adjusted consumer income

### Lagging Indicators

These confirm trends that have already occurred:

- **Unemployment rate** -- Peaks well after a recession has begun, troughs well after recovery starts
- **CPI (Consumer Price Index)** -- Inflation measure that reflects past pricing pressures
- **Corporate profits** -- Reported quarterly, reflecting past business conditions
- **Prime rate** -- Banks adjust lending rates after the Fed has already moved

## Most Market-Moving Releases

| Indicator | Release Schedule | Why It Matters |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Non-Farm Payrolls (NFP)** | First Friday of each month | Single most market-moving release; measures job creation, wage growth, unemployment rate |
| **CPI / PPI** | Monthly (mid-month) | Inflation gauges that directly influence [[fed-policy]] expectations; CPI for consumer prices, PPI for producer/wholesale prices |
| **[[ism-pmi|ISM PMI]]** | First business day of month (Manufacturing); third business day (Services) | Leading indicator of economic momentum; above/below 50 signals expansion/contraction |
| **GDP** | Quarterly (advance, 2nd, 3rd estimates) | Broadest economic measure; surprises move [[bond-market|bonds]] and equities |
| **Retail Sales** | Mid-month for prior month | Consumer spending is ~70% of US GDP; indicates consumer health |
| **Housing Starts / Building Permits** | Monthly | Leading indicator; housing is rate-sensitive and cyclical |
| **Consumer Confidence** (Conference Board / Michigan) | Monthly | Sentiment gauge; sharp drops can foreshadow reduced spending |
| **FOMC Rate Decision** | 8 times per year | Not technically an indicator, but the most consequential scheduled event for markets |

## How Markets React

Markets move on the **surprise component** -- the difference between actual and consensus expectations -- not the absolute number. A strong NFP number (above consensus) is typically bullish for equities and bearish for bonds in a healthy economy, but the same number can be bearish for equities in a late-cycle environment if it implies the Fed will remain hawkish.

The CME FedWatch tool translates economic data into implied probabilities of future [[fed-policy|rate changes]], and these probabilities shift within seconds of each release. Traders often position not just for the number itself, but for how it changes the Fed's reaction function.

## Economic Calendar

Professional traders maintain a weekly economic calendar marking all scheduled releases. Services like Bloomberg, Econoday, and Forex Factory provide consensus estimates, prior readings, and revision history. The most volatile trading typically occurs in the first 5-30 minutes after a major release.
