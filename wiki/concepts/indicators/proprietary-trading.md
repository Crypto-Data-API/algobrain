Proprietary trading ("prop trading") is the practice of a financial firm trading stocks, bonds, currencies, commodities, [[derivatives]], or other financial instruments using its own capital rather than client money. Profits and losses accrue directly to the firm.

## How Prop Trading Works

In a typical prop trading arrangement:
- The **firm** provides capital, technology infrastructure, market data, and risk management
- The **trader** deploys the capital using the firm's systems and within defined risk limits
- Profits are split between the firm and trader, with traders typically keeping 50-80% of their P&L
- Traders may face drawdown limits, position size caps, and mandatory stop-losses

## Historical Evolution

### Golden Era (1980s-2008)
Major investment banks -- Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Lehman Brothers, Bear Stearns -- operated large proprietary trading desks alongside their client-facing businesses. These desks were often the most profitable divisions:
- Goldman Sachs' prop desk generated billions in annual revenue
- Traders like [[john-paulson|John Paulson]] (at Bear Stearns) and many future [[hedge-funds|hedge fund]] founders cut their teeth on bank prop desks

### Volcker Rule (2010)
Section 619 of the Dodd-Frank Act, named after former Federal Reserve chairman Paul Volcker, prohibited banks from engaging in proprietary trading for their own accounts. The rationale: banks benefiting from government deposit insurance and Fed lending facilities should not be taking speculative risks with that implicit taxpayer backing.

**Impact**: Major banks shut down or spun off their prop desks. Talent migrated to [[hedge-funds|hedge funds]] and independent prop firms. The Volcker Rule reshaped the landscape of professional trading.

### Modern Prop Firms

Two distinct categories exist today:

**Institutional Prop Firms** -- Trade the firm's own capital with sophisticated technology:
- [[jane-street|Jane Street]]: Dominant in [[options-overview|options]] and ETF market-making; one of the most profitable trading firms globally
- [[jump-trading|Jump Trading]]: High-frequency trading, [[crypto-overview|crypto]], and [[quantitative-strategies|quantitative strategies]]
- [[citadel|Citadel Securities]]: Market-making powerhouse across equities, options, and fixed income
- [[two-sigma|Two Sigma]], [[de-shaw|D.E. Shaw]], Virtu Financial, Tower Research Capital

**Retail Prop Firms** -- Provide capital to individual traders who pass evaluation challenges:
- FTMO, Topstep, The Funded Trader
- Traders pay for evaluation, trade on simulated accounts, and receive funded accounts if they demonstrate profitability
- Business model has drawn scrutiny: firms profit from evaluation fees, and most traders fail the challenge

## Strategies

Prop firms employ a wide range of strategies:
- [[market-making-strategy|Market making]] and [[statistical-arbitrage|statistical arbitrage]]
- [[high-frequency-trading|High-frequency trading]] (HFT)
- [[options-overview|Options]] [[volatility-trading|volatility]] strategies
- [[quantitative-strategies|Quantitative/systematic]] approaches using [[machine-learning|machine learning]]
- [[crypto-overview|Crypto]] and [[defi|DeFi]] strategies (increasingly common)

## Advantages Over Hedge Funds

- No external investor relations or fundraising obligations
- Faster decision-making without investor approval
- No management fee/performance fee structure -- the firm retains profits directly
- Ability to take concentrated, higher-risk positions without investor redemption concerns
