Flags and pennants are short-term **[[continuation-patterns|continuation]]** [[chart-patterns]] that form during a brief consolidation within a strong [[trend]], after which price typically resumes its prior direction. Both follow a sharp, near-vertical price move (the "flagpole") and represent a pause where the market catches its breath before the next leg. They are among the most reliable continuation patterns because they pair a clear [[measured-move|measured-move]] target with a tight, well-defined invalidation level.

The three structural ingredients are the same for both patterns: (1) a **flagpole** — the explosive impulse that establishes the trend's energy; (2) a **consolidation** — a brief, orderly pause that drifts against or compresses within the move; and (3) a **breakout** — a resumption in the flagpole's direction that triggers the [[measured-move|measured-move]] projection. Get all three and you have a textbook setup; miss the flagpole and the shape is just noise.

## Anatomy at a Glance

| Element | Flag | Pennant |
|---------|------|---------|
| Consolidation shape | Small parallel channel (rectangle) | Small symmetrical triangle (converging lines) |
| Slope vs trend | Counter-trend (gentle) | Converges to a point (roughly horizontal apex) |
| Typical duration | 1–3 weeks (daily); minutes–hours (intraday) | 1–3 weeks; often shorter than flags |
| Volume on consolidation | Contracts | Contracts |
| Volume on breakout | Expands sharply | Expands sharply |
| Target | [[measured-move|Flagpole height]] from breakout | [[measured-move|Flagpole height]] from breakout |
| Invalidation | Beyond the far side of the channel | Beyond the far side of the triangle |

## How They Form

### Flags
A flag is a small parallel channel (a rectangle) that slopes *against* the prevailing trend:

- A **bull flag** forms in an uptrend: after a steep rally (the flagpole), price drifts down or sideways in a tight downward-sloping channel before breaking out to the upside.
- A **bear flag** forms in a downtrend: after a steep decline, price drifts up in an upward-sloping channel before breaking down again.

The counter-trend slope reflects mild profit-taking and shaken-out weak hands while the dominant participants hold their positions.

### Pennants
A pennant is structurally similar but consolidates inside a small **symmetrical triangle** (converging trendlines) rather than a parallel channel. It forms the same way — sharp flagpole, brief consolidation, breakout in the original direction — but the range narrows to a point as buyers and sellers reach temporary equilibrium.

### The role of volume
[[trading-volume|Volume]] is the key confirmation tool. Volume is heavy on the flagpole, contracts steadily during the flag/pennant consolidation (signaling reduced participation, not distribution), and then expands sharply on the breakout. A breakout on weak volume is the single most common failure mode — low-volume breakouts frequently fail and reverse back into the pattern.

## Trading Relevance

These patterns are favorites of [[momentum-trading]] and [[trend-following]] / [[breakout-trading]] strategies because they offer a defined, mechanical trade plan:

- **Entry** — on the [[breakout]] from the flag/pennant in the direction of the prior trend (some traders wait for a close beyond the boundary to filter false breaks).
- **Target** — the *[[measured-move|measured move]]*: take the length of the flagpole and project it from the breakout point. A $10 flagpole breaking out at $55 projects a target near $65.
- **Stop** — just beyond the opposite side of the pattern (below the flag low for a bull flag, above the flag high for a bear flag). The tight consolidation keeps the stop close, producing a favorable reward-to-risk ratio.

### Worked example: a bull flag measured move

A stock rallies from **$40 to $50** on heavy volume — a clean, near-vertical flagpole. It then drifts down to **$47** over eight sessions in a tight, slightly downward-sloping channel on contracting volume (the flag). It breaks out above the upper channel line at **$48** on a volume surge. Build the trade:

1. **Flagpole height** = $50 − $40 = **$10** (the measured-move unit).
2. **Breakout / entry** = **$48** (close above the upper flag boundary on expanding volume).
3. **[[measured-move|Target]]** = breakout + flagpole height = $48 + $10 = **$58**.
4. **Stop** = just below the flag low, ~**$46.50** (below $47 with a small buffer).
5. **Risk** = $48 − $46.50 = **$1.50 per share**. **Reward** = $58 − $48 = **$10 per share**.
6. **Reward-to-risk** = $10 / $1.50 ≈ **6.7 : 1**.

That asymmetry is the entire appeal: a tight consolidation produces a small, well-defined risk, while the measured move offers a multiple of it. Even at a modest win rate the expectancy is positive. (Round numbers are illustrative; real targets are approximate zones, not exact prices — scale out near the projection rather than expecting a precise touch.) A **bear flag** is the mirror image: a $10 drop, an upward drift, a breakdown, and a target projected $10 *below* the breakdown point, with the stop above the flag high.

### Position sizing from the stop

Because the stop is tight and known before entry, these patterns suit fixed-fractional sizing. If a trader risks 1% of a $50,000 account ($500) and the per-share risk is $1.50, the position is $500 / $1.50 ≈ **333 shares** (~$16,000 notional at $48). The pattern defines the risk; the account defines the size.

## Key Considerations

- **Duration matters.** Flags and pennants are short-term patterns — typically one to four weeks. Consolidations that drag on beyond ~3–4 weeks lose reliability; the longer the pause, the more it resembles a [[support-and-resistance|trading range]] or potential reversal rather than a continuation.
- **A clear flagpole is essential.** Without a sharp preceding impulse, a small rectangle or triangle carries no continuation bias — it is just noise.
- **Context within the larger trend.** These patterns work best as continuation signals inside an established, strong trend. The same shape appearing in a choppy, rangebound market has little predictive value.
- **Distinguish from other patterns.** A pennant differs from a symmetrical triangle mainly by its short duration and the preceding flagpole. A flag differs from a [[support-and-resistance|rectangle]] consolidation by its counter-trend slope and brevity.

## Common Pitfalls and Failure Modes

- **Low-volume breakouts.** The single most common failure: price clears the boundary but on thin [[trading-volume|volume]], then falls back into the pattern. Demand a clear volume expansion on the breakout bar.
- **No real flagpole.** A small channel that did *not* follow a sharp impulse has no continuation bias. Without stored trend energy, the "measured move" projects from nothing.
- **Over-extended consolidation.** Beyond ~3–4 weeks (on the daily chart) the pause stops being a flag and starts behaving like a [[support-and-resistance|range]] or topping/bottoming structure. The longer the drift, the weaker the signal.
- **Counter-trend trading the flag itself.** The consolidation drifts *against* the trend, which tempts traders to fade it; the edge is in the breakout *with* the trend, not in scalping the pause.
- **Chasing the move.** Entering well after the breakout (deep into the flagpole-length projection) compresses reward while widening the stop, destroying the reward-to-risk math that makes the pattern worth trading.
- **Pattern-spotting bias.** In hindsight every chart is full of "flags." Require the full checklist — flagpole, contracting-volume consolidation, expanding-volume breakout, sensible context within an established [[trend]] — before calling it a setup.
