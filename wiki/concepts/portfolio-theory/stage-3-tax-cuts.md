The Stage 3 tax cuts are changes to Australian individual income tax brackets that took effect on 1 July 2024, reducing marginal tax rates for most Australians and altering the effective rate of tax on investment income including [[capital-gains|capital gains]], dividends, and interest. Originally legislated by the Morrison Coalition government as a flat 30% rate for incomes between $45,001 and $200,000, the policy was controversially revised by the Albanese Labor government in January 2024 to spread the benefit more broadly, keeping a progressive bracket structure but lowering rates.

## Overview

The Stage 3 tax cuts were the third and final stage of a multi-year tax reform package legislated in 2018-19. Stages 1 and 2 delivered modest bracket adjustments and low/middle-income tax offsets. Stage 3 was always the largest and most contentious component.

### What Changed

| Feature | Pre-July 2024 Brackets | Original Morrison Plan | Revised Albanese Plan (What Passed) |
|---------|----------------------|----------------------|-------------------------------------|
| **$0-$18,200** | 0% | 0% | 0% |
| **$18,201-$45,000** | 19% | 19% | 16% |
| **$45,001-$120,000** | 32.5% | 30% (merged to $200K) | 30% (to $135K) |
| **$120,001-$135,000** | 32.5% | 30% | 30% |
| **$135,001-$180,000** | 37% | 30% | 37% |
| **$180,001-$190,000** | 45% | 30% | 37% |
| **$190,001-$200,000** | 45% | 30% | 45% |
| **$200,001+** | 45% | 45% | 45% |

### Key Differences from Original Plan

The Morrison plan would have created a single 30% rate for everyone earning $45,001-$200,000 — dramatically flattening the progressive tax system. The revised Albanese version:

- Reduced the bottom rate from 19% to 16% (benefiting all taxpayers, especially lower incomes)
- Set the 30% bracket to $45,001-$135,000 (instead of $200,000)
- Kept the 37% bracket at $135,001-$190,000 (slightly expanded from $120,001-$180,000)
- Moved the top rate threshold to $190,001 (from $180,001)
- **Net effect**: Every taxpayer receives a tax cut, with proportionally larger benefits for lower and middle incomes

## Impact on Investors

### Capital Gains Tax (Effective Rates)

The lower marginal rates translate directly to lower effective [[capital-gains-tax-discount|CGT rates]]:

| Income Range | Old Short-Term CGT Rate | New Short-Term CGT Rate | Old Long-Term CGT Rate (50% Discount) | New Long-Term CGT Rate (50% Discount) |
|-------------|------------------------|------------------------|---------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| $18,201-$45,000 | 19% | 16% | 9.5% | 8% |
| $45,001-$120,000 | 32.5% | 30% | 16.25% | 15% |
| $120,001-$135,000 | 32.5% | 30% | 16.25% | 15% |
| $135,001-$180,000 | 37% | 37% | 18.5% | 18.5% |
| $180,001-$190,000 | 45% | 37% | 22.5% | 18.5% |
| $190,001+ | 45% | 45% | 22.5% | 22.5% |

The most significant improvement is for earners in the $45,001-$135,000 range: their effective long-term CGT rate dropped from 16.25% to 15%. Earners between $180,001-$190,000 saw the largest improvement (from 22.5% to 18.5% effective long-term CGT).

### Dividend Income

Lower marginal rates reduce the top-up tax owed on franked dividends:

**Example: $1,000 fully franked dividend ($1,428.57 grossed up)**

| Income Bracket | Old Tax on Grossed-Up | Franking Credit | Old Net Tax | New Tax on Grossed-Up | New Net Tax | Saving |
|---------------|----------------------|-----------------|-------------|----------------------|-------------|--------|
| $45,001-$120,000 (old 32.5%, new 30%) | $464.29 | $428.57 | $35.72 | $428.57 | $0 | $35.72 |
| $120,001-$135,000 (old 32.5%, new 30%) | $464.29 | $428.57 | $35.72 | $428.57 | $0 | $35.72 |
| $135,001-$180,000 (37%) | $528.57 | $428.57 | $100.00 | $528.57 | $100.00 | $0 |
| $180,001-$190,000 (old 45%, new 37%) | $642.86 | $428.57 | $214.29 | $528.57 | $100.00 | $114.29 |
| $190,001+ (45%) | $642.86 | $428.57 | $214.29 | $642.86 | $214.29 | $0 |

At the new 30% bracket, fully franked dividends are now effectively **tax-free** (the 30% franking credit exactly matches the 30% marginal rate). This is a meaningful improvement for investors earning $45,001-$135,000.

### Franking Credit Refunds

For entities with tax rates below the corporate rate (30%), the refund amount is slightly reduced because the gap between their marginal rate and the corporate rate is smaller:

- [[superannuation]] funds (15% tax rate): No change — super rates are independent of personal tax brackets
- [[smsf]] pension phase (0%): No change — still receive full refunds
- Low-income retirees: Slightly smaller refunds if their marginal rate increased from 0% to 16% (if previously below $18,200 threshold, no change)

### Interest Income

Lower brackets reduce tax on bank interest and term deposit income:

- Investor earning $80,000 with $5,000 in interest income: tax on interest drops from 32.5% ($1,625) to 30% ($1,500) — saving $125
- This is modest, reinforcing that interest-bearing assets are better held inside [[superannuation]] or via a mortgage offset. See [[tax-efficient-investing]]

## Impact on Salary Sacrifice Calculations

The tax saving from [[super-contribution-strategies|salary sacrifice]] into [[superannuation]] is slightly reduced for some brackets because the gap between the marginal rate and the 15% super tax rate is narrower:

| Income Range | Old Marginal Rate | New Marginal Rate | Old Saving per $1 Sacrificed | New Saving per $1 Sacrificed |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------------|-----------------------------|-----------------------------|
| $45,001-$120,000 | 32.5% | 30% | 17.5c | 15c |
| $120,001-$135,000 | 32.5% | 30% | 17.5c | 15c |
| $135,001-$180,000 | 37% | 37% | 22c | 22c |
| $180,001-$190,000 | 45% | 37% | 30c | 22c |
| $190,001+ | 45% | 45% | 30c | 30c |

Salary sacrifice remains highly advantageous, especially for those in the 37% and 45% brackets.

## Political Context

The Stage 3 tax cuts were politically significant:

- **2018**: Morrison government legislated all three stages — Stage 3 to begin 1 July 2024
- **2019-2022**: Labor promised to keep Stage 3 "in full" if elected
- **January 2024**: Prime Minister Albanese announced revised Stage 3 — breaking the election promise
- **February 2024**: Revised plan passed Parliament with crossbench support
- **Criticism**: Accused of breaking a "solemn promise" — potential trust deficit for future tax policy commitments
- **Support**: Argued the revision was fairer, directing more benefit to lower incomes during a cost-of-living crisis

The political willingness to revise legislated tax cuts is relevant for investors: it demonstrates that **no tax provision is truly guaranteed**, including the [[capital-gains-tax-discount]], franking credit refunds, and [[superannuation]] concessions. Legislative risk should be factored into any long-term tax-dependent strategy.
