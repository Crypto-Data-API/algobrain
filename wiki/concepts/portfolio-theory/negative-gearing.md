Gearing magnifies losses as well as gains.

Negative gearing is an Australian tax provision that allows investors to deduct investment losses — where the expenses of holding an investment (interest, maintenance, depreciation) exceed the income it generates — against their other income, including salary, dividends, and business income. While it applies to any investment (including shares via [[margin]] loans), it is most commonly associated with property investment and is one of the most politically contentious and economically significant tax features in Australia, directly shaping how capital is allocated between property and equities.

## Overview

The principle is straightforward: if you borrow to invest and the investment costs more to hold than it earns in income, the net loss is tax-deductible against your other income. The strategy relies on the eventual capital gain on the asset exceeding the accumulated after-tax losses — effectively trading short-term tax-subsidised losses for long-term capital growth, which is then taxed at a reduced rate via the [[capital-gains-tax-discount]].

Approximately **1.3 million Australians** negatively gear investment property (as of 2023 ATO data), making it one of the most widely used tax strategies in the country. The fiscal cost to the government is estimated at $5-7 billion per year in forgone revenue.

## Gearing states: positive, neutral, negative

"Gearing" simply means borrowing to invest. The tax treatment depends on whether the investment's income covers its holding costs:

| State | Income vs holding costs | Tax position | Typical motivation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Positively geared** | Income > costs | Net income is **taxable** | Cash flow now; less tax shelter |
| **Neutrally geared** | Income ≈ costs | Roughly tax-neutral | Break-even hold |
| **Negatively geared** | Income < costs | Net loss is **deductible** vs other income | Tax-subsidised hold, betting on capital growth |

Negative gearing only delivers a benefit while the position is *running at a loss* — if rates fall or rents rise enough to flip it positive, the income becomes taxable. The strategy is therefore a bet that **capital growth** (taxed later at the discounted [[capital-gains-tax-discount|CGT]] rate) outweighs the accumulated after-tax holding losses.

## How Negative Gearing Works

### Property Example

> Marginal rate used below is **indicative**; substitute your own current bracket.

An investor earns $120,000 salary and owns a rental property:

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| **Rental income** | $20,000 |
| **Mortgage interest** | -$18,000 |
| **Council rates, insurance, management** | -$5,000 |
| **Depreciation (building and fixtures)** | -$7,000 |
| **Total expenses** | -$30,000 |
| **Net rental loss** | -$10,000 |

The $10,000 loss is deducted from the investor's $120,000 salary:

- **Taxable income**: $120,000 - $10,000 = $110,000
- **Tax saving**: $10,000 x 32.5% (marginal rate) = **$3,250 less tax paid**
- **Net out-of-pocket cost**: $10,000 loss - $3,250 tax saving = $6,750 after-tax cost of holding the property

The investor accepts a $6,750 annual after-tax cost, betting that the property's capital appreciation will exceed this accumulated cost over time.

### The Combined Strategy: Negative Gearing + CGT Discount

This is where the strategy becomes particularly powerful. Continuing the example above:

- Investor holds the property for 10 years, accumulating ~$67,500 in after-tax holding costs
- Property was purchased for $500,000 and is now worth $750,000 — a $250,000 capital gain
- After [[capital-gains-tax-discount|50% CGT discount]]: only $125,000 is assessable
- At 37% marginal rate: **$46,250 in CGT**
- Total cost: $67,500 holding costs + $46,250 CGT = $113,750
- Total return: $250,000 capital gain - $113,750 = **$136,250 net profit** (plus $200,000 in rental income received along the way)

The combination of deducting losses at the full marginal rate but paying CGT on only 50% of the eventual gain creates an asymmetric tax advantage.

## Negative Gearing with Shares

While property dominates the negative gearing conversation, the strategy applies equally to shares purchased with borrowed funds:

### Margin Loan Negative Gearing

- Borrow via a [[margin]] loan to purchase shares
- If dividend income from the shares is less than the margin loan interest, you have a net investment loss
- This loss is deductible against your other income (salary, rental income, etc.)
- The same [[capital-gains-tax-discount|CGT discount]] applies when you eventually sell the shares for a gain

### Example: Share Negative Gearing

- Borrow $100,000 at 7% interest via margin loan = $7,000 annual interest
- Invest in a portfolio yielding 4% fully franked dividends = $4,000 income (plus ~$1,700 in franking-credits)
- Net investment loss: $7,000 - $4,000 = $3,000 (before considering franking credit offset)
- Deduct $3,000 against salary
- If the portfolio grows 8% per year, the capital gain after several years will be substantial — and taxed at the discounted rate

### Advantages of Share Negative Gearing Over Property

| Factor | Shares | Property |
|--------|--------|----------|
| **Liquidity** | Sell in seconds on the ASX | Months to sell, significant transaction costs |
| **Diversification** | Spread across dozens of companies | Concentrated in a single asset |
| **Transaction costs** | Brokerage ($10-$30 per trade) | Stamp duty (2-5%), legal fees, agent fees |
| **Maintenance** | None | Ongoing repairs, management, insurance |
| **franking-credits** | Available on Australian dividends | Not applicable |
| **Leverage cost** | Margin rates (6-8%) | Mortgage rates (5-7%, typically lower) |
| **Margin call risk** | Yes — forced selling if portfolio declines | No margin calls (but mortgage default risk) |
| **Entry cost** | $1,000 minimum | Deposit of $50,000-$100,000+ |

## Why Negative Gearing Matters for Share Investors

Understanding negative gearing is critical for share investors even if they never negatively gear themselves, because it profoundly affects Australian capital allocation:

### Capital Flow Distortion

- Negative gearing (combined with the [[capital-gains-tax-discount]]) makes property investment disproportionately tax-advantaged
- This channels Australian household savings heavily toward residential property rather than equities
- Australia has one of the highest household debt-to-income ratios globally (~190%), largely due to property investment
- The relative underinvestment in equities may create opportunities for share investors — Australian stocks may be relatively undervalued compared to property due to this structural capital flow

### [[opportunity-cost]] for Investors

- Capital locked in a negatively geared property ($500K+ deposit + stamp duty + ongoing holding costs) could alternatively be invested in a diversified share portfolio
- Historical returns: Australian residential property has averaged ~7% nominal (3-4% real) vs ASX All Ordinaries ~10% nominal (6-7% real) over 30 years
- Property returns vary enormously by location; share returns are broadly accessible via [[index-funds]] and [[etf|ETFs]]
- The ITPM philosophy emphasises opportunity cost analysis — capital in property is capital not deployed in potentially higher-returning strategies

### REIT Alternative

Investors seeking property exposure without the complexity and illiquidity of direct property can invest in REITs, which:

- Provide diversified property exposure across sectors (industrial, commercial, retail)
- Are liquid — trade on the ASX during market hours
- Require no mortgage, maintenance, or management
- Can be held inside [[superannuation]] or [[smsf]]
- Some pay distributions with tax-deferred components (effectively deferring CGT)

## Political History and Risk

Negative gearing has been a political lightning rod in Australia:

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| **1985-87** | Hawke government temporarily quarantined negative gearing losses (couldn't deduct against other income). Reversed in 1987, partly due to claims of rental market pressure in Sydney and Perth |
| **2016** | Labor opposition proposed limiting negative gearing to new properties only and reducing the CGT discount to 25%. Lost the election |
| **2019** | Labor proposed the same policy again (negative gearing limited to new builds, CGT discount to 25%). Lost the "unlosable" election — franking credits and negative gearing policies widely blamed |
| **2022** | Labor won government without a negative gearing policy — the issue has become politically toxic |
| **Current** | No changes proposed by either major party. The policy is effectively locked in |

### Legislative Risk

- Future changes cannot be ruled out, especially as housing affordability worsens
- The most likely change would be grandfathering existing arrangements while restricting new negative gearing
- Any change would likely be prospective (not retrospective), protecting existing investors
- The interaction between negative gearing and the [[capital-gains-tax-discount]] means changing either one in isolation could be counterproductive

## Interest Rate Sensitivity

The value of negative gearing is directly linked to [[interest-rates]]:

- **Rising rates** (2022-2024): Higher mortgage/margin interest = larger investment loss = bigger tax deduction. But also higher out-of-pocket costs
- **Falling rates**: Smaller deductions, but investments may move toward positive gearing (income exceeds costs), which is taxable
- At extreme low rates (2020-2021, when mortgage rates were 2-3%): many investment properties were positively geared, eliminating the negative gearing tax benefit entirely
- The RBA's rate tightening cycle from 2022 restored negative gearing benefits for many property investors, while simultaneously increasing financial risk

## Risks

1. **Capital growth may not eventuate** — the entire strategy depends on the asset appreciating enough to offset accumulated losses. Property prices can stagnate or decline for extended periods
2. **Interest rate risk** — rising rates increase holding costs, potentially beyond what the investor can afford
3. **Vacancy risk** (property) — periods without a tenant mean zero income but full expenses
4. **Margin call risk** (shares) — a falling portfolio can trigger margin calls, forcing sales at the worst time. See [[leverage]] and [[margin]]
5. **Illiquidity** (property) — if you need to sell, property takes months and incurs 3-5% transaction costs
6. **Legislative risk** — future government may restrict negative gearing
7. **Concentration risk** — a single investment property is a highly concentrated, illiquid, leveraged bet on one suburb

## Common Pitfalls

- **Treating the tax refund as the return.** The deduction only ever returns a *fraction* of the loss (your marginal rate). You are still out of pocket on the rest — the strategy fails unless capital growth covers the net cost.
- **Ignoring the classification question for shares.** Margin-loan interest is deductible when there is a genuine expectation of assessable income. A pure-speculation, no-income-expectation position can have its interest deduction challenged — and whether you are an [[trader-vs-investor-classification-au|investor or trader]] changes the whole tax treatment.
- **Over-gearing into a rate cycle.** As 2022–2024 showed, rising [[interest-rates]] inflate the deduction *and* the out-of-pocket cost simultaneously; affordability, not the deduction, is the binding constraint.
- **Forgetting margin calls.** Unlike a mortgage, a [[margin]] loan can force-sell your portfolio at the worst possible time — a risk property investors do not face.
- **Banking on policy permanence.** Negative gearing is politically durable but not guaranteed; model the after-tax case *without* it as a stress test.
- **Confusing depreciation with cash cost.** Building/fixture depreciation boosts the paper loss without a cash outflow, but it reduces the cost base and increases the eventual capital gain (and CGT) — it is partly a timing benefit, not free money.
