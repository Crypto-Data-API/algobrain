# 80/20 Analysis Framework

The 80/20 analysis framework is a trading methodology taught by [[anton-kreil]] and the Institute of Trading and Portfolio Management that allocates 80% of analytical effort to fundamental-analysis and 20% to [[technical-analysis]]. The key insight is that technical analysis should be used only for timing entries and exits — never for generating trade ideas.

## Overview

Most retail traders invert this ratio, spending 80%+ of their time on charts and indicators. Professional traders at investment banks and hedge funds do the opposite: they develop trade ideas through fundamental research (macroeconomic analysis, earnings, industry dynamics) and use technical analysis as a secondary filter to time the execution.

This framework underpins the long-short-equity portfolio management methodology. (Source: [[itpm-education-methodology-overview]])

## The 80% — Fundamental Analysis

Fundamental analysis generates the "what" and "why" of a trade:

### Macroeconomic Analysis (Top-Down)
- **Economic cycle positioning**: Where are we in the business cycle? Expansion, peak, contraction, trough?
- **Central bank policy**: Interest rate direction, quantitative easing/tightening
- **Leading indicators**: Yield curve, unemployment trends, PMI data, inflation
- **Global macro themes**: Trade wars, geopolitical events, regulatory changes

### Sector Analysis (Middle Layer)
- **[[sector-rotation]]**: Which sectors benefit from current macro conditions?
- **Institutional capital flows**: Where is institutional money flowing?
- **Relative value**: Which sectors are cheap/expensive relative to their fundamentals?

### Stock Analysis (Bottom-Up)
- **Earnings catalysts**: Upcoming earnings reports, management changes, product launches
- **Competitive positioning**: Market share, moats, disruption risk
- **Valuation**: Is the stock cheap/expensive relative to peers and history?
- **News flow**: Expected events that could move the stock in the 20-60 day window

The fundamental thesis determines:
- Whether to be long or short
- Which specific stock or asset
- The expected magnitude of the move
- The catalyst and timeline

(Source: [[itpm-god-like-trader-status]])

## The 20% — Technical Analysis

Technical analysis provides the "when" of a trade:

### Entry Timing
- **Price action confirmation**: Has the stock started moving in the direction of the fundamental thesis?
- **Support/resistance levels**: Where are the key price levels for entry?
- **Volume**: Is volume confirming the move?
- **Momentum indicators**: Is momentum building in the expected direction?

### Exit Timing
- **Target prices**: Technical levels where the fundamental thesis plays out
- **Stop levels**: Where the thesis is invalidated
- **Time stops**: Maximum holding period (20-60 days for options)

### What Technical Analysis Does NOT Do
- Does not generate trade ideas
- Does not determine direction (long vs short)
- Does not override a fundamental thesis
- Is not used in isolation

(Source: [[itpm-education-methodology-overview]])

## The Professional vs Retail Inversion

| Aspect | Professional (ITPM) | Typical Retail |
|--------|--------------------|----|
| Idea generation | Fundamental research | Chart patterns |
| Analysis split | 80% fundamental / 20% technical | 80% technical / 20% fundamental |
| Technical use | Entry/exit timing only | Primary decision tool |
| Macro awareness | Core requirement | Often ignored |
| Fundamental data | Interpreted through professional lens | Taken at face value |

A critical insight from ITPM: professionals interpret the same fundamental data differently from retail traders. The data itself is freely available, but the analytical framework for interpreting it produces different (often opposite) conclusions. (Source: [[itpm-education-methodology-overview]])

## The 80/20 Volatility Rule

A related concept: only about 20% of the time do markets exhibit enough [[volatility]] for active trading to be profitable. During the other 80%, the best approach is portfolio management, research, and patience. This means:

- Professional traders spend most of their time **not trading** — they are researching, monitoring, and managing existing positions
- Active trading should be concentrated in periods of elevated volatility
- Boredom and patience are professional trading skills, not weaknesses

(Source: [[itpm-god-like-trader-status]])
