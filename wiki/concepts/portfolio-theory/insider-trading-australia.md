> **Not legal advice.** This page is an educational overview of Australian insider-trading law. Penalty amounts, multipliers and thresholds are *indicative* — maximum monetary penalties are tied to penalty units and benefit-based formulas that are periodically indexed, so the figures below may be out of date. Confirm the current law with [[asic|ASIC]], the Corporations Act, or a qualified lawyer before relying on any specific number.

Insider trading in Australia is prohibited under **Part 7.10 of the Corporations Act 2001** (sections 1042A-1043O). It is a criminal offence carrying penalties of up to **15 years imprisonment** and substantial fines for individuals (indicatively of the order of ~$1.1 million per offence, but set by a penalty-unit / benefit-multiple formula), with larger maxima for corporations (indicatively ~$11 million or a benefit/turnover-based amount, whichever is greater). [[asic|ASIC]] actively surveils the ASX and other Australian markets for unusual trading patterns preceding price-sensitive announcements, and has pursued both criminal and civil prosecutions with increasing vigour since the Hayne Royal Commission.

## Overview

Australia's insider trading laws are broadly similar to those in the US and UK but have some distinctive features — particularly the breadth of who qualifies as an "insider" and the complementary role of ASX continuous disclosure rules. Together, these provisions create a framework designed to ensure a level playing field: all investors should be trading on the same publicly available information.

For retail investors, insider trading law matters because it underpins the integrity of the market. The continuous disclosure regime means material information reaches the market quickly, and the insider trading prohibition ensures that no one should be able to trade on that information before it becomes public.

## What Is "Inside Information"

Under s1042A of the Corporations Act, inside information is information that:

1. **Is not generally available**: Not publicly disclosed, not observable in the market, and would not be known or deducible by market participants through normal research
2. **If it were generally available**: A reasonable person would expect it to have a **material effect** on the price or value of the relevant securities

"Material effect" means the information would, or would be likely to, influence persons who commonly invest in securities in deciding whether to acquire or dispose of the securities.

### Examples of Inside Information
- **Pending takeover bid or merger** — the most common trigger for insider trading prosecutions
- **Earnings results** — knowing a company's profit will be significantly above or below market expectations before the announcement
- **Major contract wins or losses** — e.g., a mining company securing or losing a major supply contract
- **Capital raisings** — planned equity issues before announcement
- **Regulatory decisions** — pending approval or rejection of key licences or products
- **Economic data** — embargoed government statistics (GDP, employment, inflation) before public release
- **Executive departures** — resignation of key personnel before announcement
- **Material litigation** — pending legal actions or settlements not yet disclosed

## Who Is an "Insider"

Australian law defines insiders broadly under s1043A. You are an insider if you possess inside information and you know, or ought reasonably to know, that the information is not generally available and would be material. This includes:

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| **Company insiders** | Directors, officers, employees, contractors |
| **Professional advisers** | Lawyers, accountants, investment bankers, auditors working on the matter |
| **Related parties** | Family members, business partners of insiders |
| **Tippees** | Anyone who receives inside information from an insider (even inadvertently — e.g., overhearing a conversation at a restaurant or reading a confidential document left on a train) |
| **Government officials** | Public servants, regulators, politicians with access to market-sensitive information |

The critical point: **you do not need to be a "traditional" insider** (director, officer, etc.) to be caught by the law. If you know the information is material and not public, trading on it (or tipping someone else) is an offence — regardless of how you came to have the information.

## Prohibited Actions

Three categories of conduct are prohibited under s1043A:

### 1. Trading on Inside Information
Subscribing for, purchasing, or selling securities (or entering into an agreement to do so) while in possession of inside information about those securities. The insider does not need to have been motivated by the information — mere possession while trading is sufficient for the civil prohibition (the criminal prohibition requires intent).

### 2. Tipping
Communicating inside information to another person if the insider knows, or ought reasonably to know, that the other person would or would be likely to:
- Trade on the information, or
- Procure someone else to trade on the information

### 3. Procuring
Procuring another person to trade on inside information — i.e., arranging for someone else to execute trades on your behalf using your inside information.

## When Information Becomes "Generally Available"

Section 1042C sets out when information is treated as generally available — and therefore *no longer* inside information:

1. **Readily observable matter** — information that is observable in the market (e.g., a publicly visible event)
2. **Made known** — information that has been made known in a manner that would, or would be likely to, bring it to the attention of persons who commonly invest in securities, **and** a reasonable period for it to be disseminated has elapsed
3. **Deduction, conclusion or inference** — information that can be deduced, concluded or inferred from the above categories

The "reasonable period for dissemination" element matters: an insider cannot trade in the seconds after an ASX announcement on the theory that it is now "public" if the market has not had a reasonable opportunity to absorb it. This dovetails with [[market-efficiency]] — the law presumes information needs time to be impounded into price.

## Defences and Exceptions

The prohibition is broad, but the Corporations Act and market practice recognise several carve-outs:

| Defence / exception | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Chinese walls (information barriers)** | A body corporate may have a defence where decisions to trade were made by a person who did not possess the information and effective arrangements (information barriers) prevented its communication |
| **Underwriting** | Limited exceptions for acquisitions/disposals in the course of underwriting |
| **Knowledge of own intentions** | A person's own intention to trade is not "inside information" about themselves (you can act on your own takeover plan) |
| **Pre-arranged trading plans** | Trading under a bona fide plan established before the person came into possession of the information may reduce risk (analogous to US Rule 10b5-1, though Australia has no identical statutory safe harbour) |
| **Equal information** | If the counterparty has the same information, the policy rationale is reduced (narrow application) |

These are technical and fact-specific. The safest course for anyone with material non-public information is to **not trade and not tip**, and to seek compliance advice — see "How It Affects Regular Investors" below.

## Criminal vs Civil Enforcement — Side by Side

| Dimension | Criminal prosecution (s 1043A) | Civil penalty proceeding |
|---|---|---|
| Standard of proof | Beyond reasonable doubt | Balance of probabilities |
| Fault element | Generally required | Possession-while-trading can suffice |
| Penalties | Imprisonment + fines | Pecuniary penalty, disgorgement, compensation |
| Run by | CDPP on ASIC referral | ASIC |
| Typical use | Egregious, well-evidenced cases | Where intent is hard to prove or civil remedy preferred |

## Penalties

### Criminal Penalties

> Monetary maxima below are **indicative**. Under the Corporations Act the actual maximum is the *greatest of* a fixed penalty-unit amount, a multiple of the benefit derived/loss avoided, or (for bodies corporate) a percentage of annual turnover. Penalty-unit values are indexed periodically, so treat dollar figures as approximate.

| Offence | Maximum Penalty (Individual) | Maximum Penalty (Corporation) |
|---------|------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Insider trading** | 15 years imprisonment and/or ~$1.1m (or benefit-multiple, indicative) | ~$11.1m or benefit/turnover-based amount, whichever is greater (indicative) |
| **Tipping** | 15 years imprisonment and/or ~$1.1m (or benefit-multiple, indicative) | ~$11.1m or benefit/turnover-based amount, whichever is greater (indicative) |

The criminal prohibition (s 1043A) requires the prosecution to prove the elements beyond reasonable doubt and generally involves a *fault* element; the **civil penalty** track has a lower (balance-of-probabilities) standard and does not require proof that the person was *motivated* by the information — mere possession while trading can suffice. ASIC frequently runs the civil track where the criminal evidentiary bar is hard to meet.

### Civil Penalties
- **Pecuniary penalties**: No statutory maximum — the court determines the appropriate amount based on the severity of the contravention, the profit made or loss avoided, and the deterrent effect
- **Disgorgement**: Courts can order profits made through insider trading to be paid to the Commonwealth or to affected investors
- **Compensation**: Affected investors can seek compensation from the insider trader

### ASIC Administrative Actions
- **Banning orders**: ASIC can ban individuals from providing financial services or managing corporations
- **Enforceable undertakings**: ASIC can accept undertakings from individuals and companies to address conduct (less severe than court proceedings)

## ASIC Market Surveillance

ASIC's **Market Analysis and Intelligence (MAI)** team conducts ongoing surveillance of all Australian financial markets:

- **Pre-announcement monitoring**: MAI tracks trading volumes, price movements, and options activity in the days and hours before price-sensitive ASX announcements. Statistically unusual activity triggers further investigation
- **Cross-market surveillance**: Monitors equities, derivatives (options, futures), and OTC markets simultaneously — catching cases where insiders trade in options rather than shares to leverage their information
- **Referral relationships**: ASIC receives referrals from the ASX's own surveillance team, from overseas regulators (through IOSCO MOUs), and from market participants who report suspicious activity
- **Data analytics**: ASIC uses sophisticated data analytics to identify networks of connected traders, unusual communication patterns (in combination with telephone records obtained under warrant), and trading patterns consistent with insider trading

## ASX Continuous Disclosure (Listing Rule 3.1)

Australia's insider trading framework works hand-in-hand with ASX continuous disclosure obligations:

- **Listing Rule 3.1**: Once an entity is or becomes aware of information concerning it that a reasonable person would expect to have a material effect on the price or value of its securities, the entity must **immediately** tell ASX that information
- **"Immediately"** means promptly and without delay — not "as soon as practicable" or "within a reasonable time"
- Limited exceptions apply (the information is incomplete, would be a breach of law, or would unreasonably prejudice the entity's interests) — but the exceptions are narrow and ASIC interprets them strictly
- **Consequence**: Material information should be publicly available within hours of the company becoming aware of it — reducing the window during which insiders can exploit non-public information

If continuous disclosure is working properly, the amount of genuinely non-public material information in existence at any given time is minimised. Insider trading and continuous disclosure are two sides of the same coin: continuous disclosure shrinks the information advantage, and insider trading law punishes those who exploit whatever advantage remains.

## Notable Australian Insider Trading Cases

### Lukas Kamay & Christopher Hill (2015) — Government Data
- **Offence**: Kamay (a NAB forex trader) and Hill (an analyst at the Australian Bureau of Statistics) colluded to trade on embargoed economic data — including employment, GDP, and retail trade figures — before public release
- **Method**: Hill provided Kamay with the data the day before publication; Kamay used it to place leveraged forex trades
- **Profits**: $7 million over approximately 12 months
- **Penalties**: Kamay sentenced to **7 years 3 months imprisonment** (non-parole period of 4 years 6 months); Hill sentenced to **3 years 3 months imprisonment**
- **Significance**: One of Australia's largest insider trading cases; demonstrated the seriousness of trading on embargoed government data

### John Hartman (2013) — Broker Front-Running
- **Offence**: Hartman, a [[commsec|CommSec]] broker, traded ahead of client orders (front-running) — placing personal trades before executing large client orders that he knew would move the price
- **Method**: Bought shares in his own account shortly before executing large buy orders for clients
- **Penalty**: **2 years imprisonment** (non-parole period of 12 months)
- **Significance**: First criminal conviction for front-running by an Australian broker

### Steve Xiao (2021) — Takeover Information
- **Offence**: Former NAB analyst who traded on inside information relating to upcoming takeover transactions that NAB was advising on
- **Method**: Used the information to trade in target company securities before the takeovers were publicly announced
- **Penalty**: Found guilty; sentenced to imprisonment
- **Significance**: Demonstrated ASIC's capacity to prosecute cases involving complex corporate advisory transactions

### Oliver Curtis (2016) — Tipping Network
- **Offence**: Received inside information from a friend (John Hartman — the CommSec broker above) and traded on it through a network of accounts
- **Profits**: $1.4 million
- **Penalty**: **2 years imprisonment** (non-parole period of 1 year)
- **Significance**: High-profile case in Sydney — Curtis was married to PR figure Roxy Jacenko, which attracted significant media attention and raised public awareness of insider trading enforcement

### Notable Cases — Summary Table

| Case | Year | Conduct | Profit / scale | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Kamay & Hill | 2015 | Trading on embargoed ABS economic data | ~$7m over ~12 months | Kamay ~7y 3m; Hill ~3y 3m imprisonment |
| Hartman | 2013 | Broker front-running of client orders | — | ~2 years imprisonment (first front-running conviction) |
| Curtis | 2016 | Tipping network (tipped by Hartman) | ~$1.4m | ~2 years imprisonment |
| Xiao | 2021 | Trading on M&A advisory information | — | Convicted; imprisonment |

These illustrate the breadth of liability: a government statistician (Hill), a broker (Hartman), a bank analyst (Xiao), and a non-professional tippee (Curtis) were all caught. The lesson for [[australian-investing|Australian investors]] is that the **tippee** path catches people who never set foot in a listed company.

## Market Integrity Rules (MIR)

ASIC's Market Integrity Rules provide the detailed regulatory framework for fair and orderly markets:

- **Pre-trade transparency**: Orders must be displayed on the order book (with limited exceptions for block trades and dark pools)
- **Best execution**: Brokers must take reasonable steps to achieve the best outcome for clients when executing orders
- **Suspicious transaction reporting**: Market participants (brokers, market makers) must report suspicious activity to ASIC — similar to AUSTRAC's SMR obligation but focused on market manipulation and insider trading
- **Short selling disclosure**: Short positions above certain thresholds must be reported to ASIC and disclosed to the market — different from many other markets where short selling is less transparent
- **Automated trading controls**: Algorithmic traders must have kill switches and risk controls

## How It Affects Regular Investors

1. **Level playing field**: Insider trading law and continuous disclosure ensure that material information is publicly available quickly, so retail investors are trading on the same information as institutional investors
2. **Confidence in market integrity**: Knowledge that ASIC surveils and prosecutes insider trading supports confidence in the fairness of ASX markets
3. **Report suspicious activity**: If you observe unusual trading activity before a major announcement, you can report it to ASIC (ASIC's report misconduct form on asic.gov.au)
4. **Be careful with information**: If you work in a listed company, a professional advisory firm, a government agency, or any role that gives you access to material non-public information — do not trade on it, do not share it, and seek compliance guidance from your employer
5. **Social media risk**: Sharing material non-public information on social media, forums, or chat groups could constitute "tipping" — even if you do not trade yourself

## How It Compares Internationally

Australia's regime sits between the US and UK approaches:

| Feature | Australia | United States | United Kingdom |
|---|---|---|---|
| Core test | "Inside information" + materiality (s 1042A) | Misappropriation / classical theory (Rule 10b-5) | MAR — inside information |
| Tippee liability | Broad — no personal-benefit requirement | Requires a personal benefit to the tipper (Dirks/Newman) | Broad |
| Continuous disclosure | ASX Listing Rule 3.1 (immediate) | Reg FD + periodic filings | MAR Article 17 |
| Civil + criminal tracks | Both (s 1043A) | Both (SEC civil, DOJ criminal) | Both (FCA) |

The notable difference: Australia (like the UK) does **not** require proof of a personal benefit flowing to the tipper, which makes tippee prosecutions structurally easier than in the US.
