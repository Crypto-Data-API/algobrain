Australia's financial regulatory system follows a **"Twin Peaks" model** — splitting regulatory responsibility between a **conduct regulator** ([[asic|ASIC]]) and a **prudential regulator** ([[apra|APRA]]), supplemented by [[austrac|AUSTRAC]] for AML/CTF, the ATO for tax compliance, the RBA for monetary policy, and the ACCC for competition. This architecture, adopted in 1998 following the Wallis Inquiry, is considered one of the clearest regulatory frameworks globally, and understanding how the pieces fit together is essential for Australian investors navigating compliance obligations, broker selection, and investor protections.

> **Not legal or financial advice.** This page is general educational information about the Australian regulatory architecture. Adviser-population figures, compensation caps, and other quantitative items below are **indicative** and change over time — confirm current figures with [[asic|ASIC]], [[apra|APRA]], or the relevant primary source before relying on them. See also [[investor-protection-australia]] for the consumer-protection detail and [[smsf-trading-tax-treatment|SMSF tax]] for the ATO/super overlay.

## The Twin Peaks Model

The 1997 **Wallis Inquiry** (Financial System Inquiry) recommended restructuring Australia's financial regulation from a sector-based model (separate regulators for banking, insurance, securities) to a function-based model. The result:

- **Peak 1 — [[asic|ASIC]] (Australian Securities and Investments Commission)**: Market conduct, disclosure, licensing, consumer protection. ASIC asks: "Are financial firms treating their customers fairly and operating with integrity?"
- **Peak 2 — [[apra|APRA]] (Australian Prudential Regulation Authority)**: Prudential regulation, capital adequacy, risk management. APRA asks: "Are financial institutions financially sound and able to meet their obligations?"

Both were established on 1 July 1998. The Twin Peaks model has since been adopted or studied by the Netherlands, Belgium, New Zealand, South Africa, and the UK (partially).

### Twin Peaks vs Other Regulatory Architectures

| Model | Structure | Examples | Trade-off |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Twin Peaks** | Separate conduct + prudential regulators | Australia, Netherlands, UK (FCA/PRA) | Clear mandates; needs strong coordination (the [[#Council of Financial Regulators (CFR)\|CFR]]) |
| **Integrated / single regulator** | One body does both | Pre-2013 UK (FSA), Japan (FSA), Singapore (MAS) | Simple; risks conduct being crowded out by prudential focus |
| **Sectoral / institutional** | Separate regulator per industry (banking, insurance, securities) | US (Fed, SEC, CFTC, OCC, state bodies), pre-1998 Australia | Specialised but fragmented; gaps at the seams |
| **Functional** | Regulator follows the activity regardless of entity | Partly the US | Consistent per activity; complex for diversified firms |

The Wallis Inquiry chose Twin Peaks specifically to fix the *seams* problem of the old sectoral model — where a conglomerate doing banking, insurance, and funds management faced three regulators with overlapping and inconsistent rules.

### Overlapping Regulation Example

A single transaction can involve multiple regulators. Consider an Australian investor buying [[bitcoin]] through a registered exchange:

1. **AUSTRAC**: The exchange must be registered as a DCE, must have verified the investor's identity (KYC), and must monitor the transaction for suspicious activity
2. **ASIC**: If the crypto product is structured as a financial product (e.g., a crypto yield product or ETF), ASIC's product regulation applies
3. **ATO**: The purchase establishes a cost base for CGT purposes; any subsequent disposal is a taxable event (see [[cryptocurrency-tax-australia]])
4. **APRA**: The bank used to transfer AUD to the exchange is APRA-regulated and must meet capital and risk requirements
5. **RBA**: The payment system used for the transfer is overseen by the RBA's Payment Systems Board

## Key Legislation Affecting Investors

### Corporations Act 2001
The cornerstone of Australian financial services regulation:
- **Chapter 7**: Financial services and markets — AFSL requirements, market integrity rules, product disclosure
- **Chapter 6D**: Fundraising (prospectus requirements)
- **Chapter 2M**: Financial reporting and audit
- **Part 7.10**: Market misconduct — insider trading, market manipulation, [[wash-trading|wash trading]]
- **Part 7.8A**: Client money — segregation requirements for brokers

### ASIC Act 2001
Establishes ASIC and its powers:
- Market surveillance and enforcement
- Product intervention power (added 2019)
- Consumer protection provisions (unconscionable conduct, misleading or deceptive conduct)
- Design and Distribution Obligations (DDO) — added 2021

### Anti-Money Laundering and Counter-Terrorism Financing Act 2006
See [[aml-ctf]] for comprehensive coverage:
- Reporting entity obligations (KYC, TTR, SMR, IFTI)
- DCE registration (see [[vasp-regulation]])
- Record-keeping requirements (7 years)

### Superannuation Industry (Supervision) Act 1993 (SIS Act)
Governs [[superannuation]] funds:
- APRA oversight of APRA-regulated funds
- ATO oversight of [[smsf|SMSFs]]
- Investment restrictions (e.g., in-house asset rules, arm's length requirement)
- Preservation rules (conditions of release)

### Income Tax Assessment Act 1997 (ITAA 1997)
Tax treatment of investments:
- Capital gains tax (CGT) — Division 104-118
- [[franking-credits|Dividend imputation]] (franking credits) — Division 207
- [[superannuation]] tax concessions — Division 290-295
- Foreign income — Division 770

### Privacy Act 1988
Governs data handling by financial institutions:
- Australian Privacy Principles (APPs) — how personal information is collected, used, disclosed, and stored
- Relevance: financial institutions hold sensitive personal and financial data; Privacy Act sets standards for handling and breach notification
- Notifiable Data Breaches scheme (since 2018) — institutions must notify affected individuals and the OAIC of eligible data breaches

## The Hayne Royal Commission (2018-2019)

The **Royal Commission into Misconduct in the Banking, Superannuation and Financial Services Industry** (the Hayne Royal Commission), chaired by Commissioner Kenneth Hayne, was a watershed moment for Australian financial regulation:

### What It Exposed
- **Fees for no service**: Major banks and wealth managers charged clients ongoing fees for financial advice that was never provided — affecting hundreds of thousands of customers and billions of dollars
- **Inappropriate advice**: Financial advisers recommending products that benefited the adviser (through commissions) rather than the client
- **Predatory lending**: Banks lending to borrowers who clearly could not afford the repayments
- **Insurance misconduct**: Life insurers unreasonably delaying or refusing legitimate claims
- **Super fund conflicts**: Trustees prioritising the interests of their parent companies over fund members

### Impact
- **76 recommendations** — the government accepted or noted all of them
- **ASIC strengthened**: Moved from a "cooperative" approach to a "why not litigate?" enforcement posture — significantly more court actions since 2019
- **APRA strengthened**: Increased focus on governance, culture, and accountability (not just financial metrics)
- **Banks exited wealth management**: CBA, NAB, Westpac, and ANZ all divested or wound down their financial advice and wealth management businesses — a fundamental reshaping of the industry
- **Stricter financial adviser standards**: Led to FASEA (Financial Adviser Standards and Ethics Authority) reforms requiring degree-level education
- **Changed the landscape for retail investors**: Fewer financial advisers available (mass exodus due to education requirements and increased compliance costs), increased cost of advice, but remaining advisers held to higher standards

## Design and Distribution Obligations (DDO) — 2021

Effective October 2021, DDO requires:
- Product issuers must create a **Target Market Determination (TMD)** for each financial product — defining who the product is designed for
- Distributors must take reasonable steps to ensure the product reaches the intended target market
- Issuers must monitor and review whether the product is reaching the right consumers
- Reporting requirements: significant dealings outside the target market must be reported to ASIC
- **Impact on investors**: Products like CFDs, options, leveraged ETFs, and structured products now have explicit target market criteria. If you do not meet the criteria, a responsible distributor should not sell you the product

## Financial Adviser Education Standards (FASEA)

The Financial Adviser Standards and Ethics Authority (FASEA, functions now absorbed by Treasury and ASIC) raised the education and ethical standards for financial advisers:

- **Degree requirement**: All financial advisers must hold an approved bachelor's degree (or equivalent) — existing advisers had until 1 January 2026 to meet this requirement
- **Exam**: All advisers must pass the FASEA financial adviser exam
- **Code of Ethics**: Mandatory compliance with a code that includes acting in the client's best interests, no conflicts of interest, and competency requirements
- **CPD**: 40 hours per year of continuing professional development

### Impact
- **Mass exodus**: The number of financial advisers in Australia dropped from approximately **28,000** in 2018 to fewer than **16,000** by 2025 (figures indicative; ASIC's Financial Advisers Register is the authoritative count) — roughly a 40%+ decline
- **Advice gap**: Fewer advisers means longer wait times and higher fees — comprehensive financial advice commonly costs in the order of $3,000-$5,000+ per year (indicative)
- **Quality improvement**: Remaining advisers are better educated and held to higher ethical standards
- **Self-directed investing growth**: The advice gap has pushed more Australians toward self-directed investing through platforms like [[commsec]], [[selfwealth]], and [[superhero]]

## Council of Financial Regulators (CFR)

The CFR is the coordinating body for Australia's financial regulators, established in 2003. Members:
- **RBA** (chairs the CFR)
- **APRA**
- **ASIC**
- **Treasury**

The CFR has no formal regulatory powers — it exists to coordinate between regulators, share information, and provide a forum for discussing systemic risks. It publishes quarterly statements on financial stability and coordinates responses to financial crises.

## The Enforcement Toolkit

ASIC and APRA can escalate along a spectrum from soft to hard measures. Knowing the toolkit helps an investor read regulatory news (a "court action" signals a far more serious posture than an "infringement notice").

| Tool | Regulator | Severity | Typical use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Guidance / Regulatory Guides** | [[asic]], [[apra]] | Soft | Setting expectations before any breach |
| **Infringement notices** | [[asic]] | Low | Minor disclosure / continuous-disclosure breaches |
| **Enforceable undertakings (EUs)** | [[asic]], [[apra]] | Medium | Negotiated remediation without admission |
| **Licence conditions / variations** | [[asic]] (AFSL), [[apra]] | Medium | Restricting an entity's permitted activities |
| **Product intervention orders** | [[asic]] | Medium-high | Banning/restricting products (binary options, CFD leverage) |
| **Banning orders** | [[asic]] | High | Removing individuals from the industry |
| **Civil penalty proceedings** | [[asic]], [[austrac]] | High | Court-ordered fines (AUSTRAC's largest penalties have been against banks/casinos for AML failures) |
| **Criminal prosecution** | [[asic]] (with CDPP) | Highest | Insider trading, market manipulation, fraud |

Post-[[#The Hayne Royal Commission (2018-2019)|Hayne]], ASIC shifted toward the harder end of this toolkit — the "why not litigate?" posture noted below.

## Digital Assets and Crypto — Current Regulatory Status

Crypto sits awkwardly across the framework because a given token may be a financial product (ASIC), a payment/exchange activity (AUSTRAC), and a taxable asset (ATO) simultaneously.

| Aspect | Status (indicative, evolving) | Regulator |
|---|---|---|
| **Exchange registration** | Digital currency exchanges (DCEs) must register and meet AML/CTF obligations | [[austrac]] |
| **Token as financial product** | If a token is an interest in a managed scheme, derivative, or security, the full AFSL/Corporations Act regime applies | [[asic]] |
| **Custody / market-licensing reforms** | Treasury has run consultations on a dedicated digital-asset-platform regime; expect AFSL-style obligations for custodians/exchanges to firm up | Treasury / [[asic]] |
| **Tax** | Crypto is a CGT asset; disposals are taxable events; data-matching is active | ATO (see [[cryptocurrency-tax-australia]]) |
| **Consumer protection** | Largely *outside* CHESS/NGF/AFCA unless a licensed product is involved | see [[investor-protection-australia]] |

The practical takeaway: AUSTRAC registration of an exchange confirms AML compliance only — it is **not** a financial-product licence and confers none of the [[investor-protection-australia|investor-protection]] layers. See [[vasp-regulation]] for the detail.

## Impact on Investors

Understanding the regulatory framework helps Australian investors:

1. **Choose safe brokers**: Verify [[afsl|AFSL]] status on the ASIC register before depositing money
2. **Understand protections**: Know what is protected ([[chess-sponsorship]], AFCA, NGF) and what is not (DeFi, unregistered platforms) — see [[investor-protection-australia]]
3. **Navigate compliance**: KYC requirements, source of funds requests, and reporting obligations are not optional
4. **Assess product suitability**: DDO means products should be designed for people like you — but you still need to understand the product
5. **Tax obligations**: The ATO, AUSTRAC, and exchange data-matching programs mean investment income is highly visible to authorities — see [[cryptocurrency-tax-australia]] and [[reporting-obligations-australia]]
6. **Recognise regulatory risk**: Changes in regulation (ASIC product interventions, APRA macroprudential controls, Treasury crypto reforms) can affect investment returns
