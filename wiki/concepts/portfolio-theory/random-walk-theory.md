Random walk theory holds that successive changes in stock prices are statistically independent and approximately unpredictable, so price movements resemble a "random walk" with no exploitable memory of past prices. If true, no amount of chart-reading or [[market-timing]] can consistently beat a buy-and-hold index, because tomorrow's price change is unrelated to today's. The idea was popularised by Burton Malkiel in *A Random Walk Down Wall Street* (1973) and is closely tied to the [[efficient-market-hypothesis|efficient market hypothesis]] (Source: [[book-a-random-walk-down-wall-street]]).

## Intellectual History

The random walk idea has a longer lineage than Malkiel's bestseller:

| Year | Figure | Contribution |
|------|--------|--------------|
| 1900 | Louis Bachelier | *Théorie de la spéculation* — modeled bond prices as Brownian motion decades before physics formalized it; the founding document of mathematical finance. |
| 1953 | Maurice Kendall | Found UK stock and commodity prices behaved as if generated by a "wandering" series with no discernible cycle. |
| 1965 | Paul Samuelson | "Proof that Properly Anticipated Prices Fluctuate Randomly" — gave the theory a rigorous economic foundation. |
| 1965 | Eugene Fama | "Random Walks in Stock Market Prices" linked the empirical random walk to market efficiency (Source). |
| 1973 | Burton Malkiel | *A Random Walk Down Wall Street* popularized it and made the case for low-cost index funds (Source: [[book-a-random-walk-down-wall-street]]). |
| 1988 | Lo & MacKinlay | Variance-ratio test rejected the *strict* random walk for weekly US index returns. |

## The Formal Model

In its simplest form prices follow a random walk with drift:

```
P(t) = P(t−1) + μ + ε(t),   where ε(t) ~ i.i.d. with mean 0
```

Equivalently, in log terms, returns `r(t) = ln(P(t)/P(t−1))` are independent and identically distributed. The drift term μ captures the long-run upward tendency of equities (the equity risk premium); the noise ε(t) is unforecastable. A key statistical consequence is that the **variance of returns scales linearly with time** — the variance-ratio test (Lo and MacKinlay, 1988) checks exactly this and is the standard empirical test for random-walk behaviour.

### Three flavours, increasing in strength

The literature (Campbell, Lo & MacKinlay) distinguishes three nested versions, which matters because real markets satisfy the weak ones but not the strong one:

| Version | Assumption | Holds in practice? |
|---------|------------|--------------------|
| **RW1** | Increments i.i.d. (identical *and* independent) | No — volatility clusters, so increments are not identically distributed. |
| **RW2** | Increments independent but not identically distributed | Largely no — volatility is forecastable. |
| **RW3** | Increments merely *uncorrelated* (martingale-like) | Approximately yes at short horizons — the version most consistent with the data. |

The practical upshot: **direction** of the next move is close to uncorrelated (RW3-like), but **magnitude** (volatility) is highly predictable, which is why the strict random walk is rejected even though prices remain hard to forecast directionally.

### Worked example: variance scaling

If daily returns are i.i.d. with standard deviation σ_d = 1%, then under a random walk the σ over `n` days is `σ_d × sqrt(n)`. Over a 252-day year: `1% × sqrt(252) ≈ 15.9%`. A *variance ratio* compares the realized n-day variance to n × the 1-day variance; a ratio of 1.0 supports the random walk, a ratio above 1.0 indicates positive autocorrelation ([[momentum]]/trending), and below 1.0 indicates [[mean-reversion]]. Lo & MacKinlay found ratios meaningfully above 1.0 for short-horizon weekly index returns — a statistical fingerprint of short-run momentum that the strict theory cannot accommodate.

## Relationship to Market Efficiency

The random walk is the *empirical signature* of a weak-form efficient market: if all information in past prices is already reflected in the current price, then only new (random) information moves prices, producing a random walk. The two ideas are distinct, however — markets can be efficient without prices being a pure random walk (returns can be predictable to the extent that risk premia vary over time), and prices can wander randomly for reasons other than efficiency.

## Evidence For and Against

- **For**: The persistent failure of the average active manager to beat passive benchmarks net of fees; the weak short-horizon autocorrelation of large-cap returns; the difficulty of detecting reliable technical patterns out-of-sample.
- **Against**: Documented anomalies inconsistent with pure randomness — [[momentum]] (returns positively autocorrelated at 3–12 month horizons), long-horizon [[mean-reversion]], the value and size premia, and volatility clustering (large moves cluster, so volatility *is* forecastable even if direction is not). Behavioural finance attributes these to systematic investor biases; practitioners such as [[itpm]] argue information asymmetries and crowd psychology create exploitable inefficiencies.

## Trading and Portfolio Relevance

If you accept a strong version of random walk theory, the rational response is low-cost, broadly diversified [[diversification|index investing]] and avoiding the costs and taxes of active trading — the core argument for [[passive-investing|passive investing]]. The practical middle ground most quants adopt: direction is hard to forecast at short horizons (consistent with random walk), but **risk is forecastable** (volatility clustering), and **specific cross-sectional premia** (momentum, value, quality) survive out-of-sample. This reframes the goal from predicting price levels to harvesting risk premia and managing [[volatility]]. Even traders who reject the theory use it as a null hypothesis: any claimed edge must beat a random walk in a properly designed backtest before it is taken seriously.

## How Traders Use Random Walk Theory

- **As the null hypothesis in [[backtesting]].** Before believing a signal, test it against the random-walk null — shuffle returns or run a Monte Carlo of random-walk paths and confirm the strategy's metrics fall outside what randomness produces. A backtest that does not beat a random walk has found nothing.
- **As the case for passive cores.** The strong-form view underpins the index-fund allocation at the center of most portfolios; active risk is then layered only where an edge demonstrably survives out-of-sample.
- **As a humility check on chart patterns.** Many "patterns" (head-and-shoulders, channels) appear by chance in random-walk data; the theory is a reminder that pattern recognition is prone to false positives unless statistically validated.
- **As the link to [[alpha-decay]].** Where exploitable departures from randomness do exist, arbitrage pushes prices back toward a random walk over time — the theory describes the equilibrium that decay drives markets toward.

## Common Pitfalls and Misreadings

- **"Random walk means prices are meaningless."** No — they still drift upward (the equity risk premium) and their *variance* is structured and forecastable. Only short-horizon *direction* is near-unpredictable.
- **Confusing unpredictable with un-investable.** Even a perfect random walk with positive drift rewards buy-and-hold; unpredictability of timing is not a reason to stay out of the market.
- **Treating the strict (RW1) form as established.** It is empirically rejected; only the weaker uncorrelated-increments (RW3) form approximately holds.
- **Survivorship and data-mining traps.** Apparent departures from randomness often vanish out-of-sample; failing to use the random walk as a null invites [[overfitting-detection|overfit]] "edges."
