An economic moat is a structural, durable competitive advantage that allows a company to earn returns on capital above its cost of capital for an extended period, protecting its market share and profitability from competitors — analogous to the moat surrounding a medieval castle. The term was popularised by Warren Buffett and operationalised by Morningstar's equity research into a five-source framework; it is central to [[value-investing-strategy|value investing]] and to [[fred-mcnaught|Fred McNaught's]] stock-selection criteria.

## Overview

A company without a moat earns excess returns only briefly: high profitability attracts competitors, supply expands, prices fall, and returns mean-revert to the cost of capital. A moat is the mechanism that *delays or blocks* that mean-reversion. The economic test of a moat is therefore quantitative — a sustained spread of [[return-on-invested-capital|return on invested capital (ROIC)]] over the [[weighted-average-cost-of-capital|weighted-average cost of capital (WACC)]] — but the *source* of the moat is qualitative. Identifying the source matters because it tells you what could destroy the advantage and how long it is likely to persist.

## The Five Sources of Moat (Morningstar Framework)

Morningstar's equity research framework identifies five primary, mutually non-exclusive sources of durable advantage:

- **Network effects** — the product becomes more valuable as more people use it, creating a chicken-and-egg barrier for entrants (e.g., Visa/Mastercard payment rails, Meta, exchanges). These are the strongest and most self-reinforcing moats.
- **Switching costs** — it is expensive, risky, or disruptive for customers to change providers (e.g., enterprise software such as SAP or Oracle, core banking systems, medical devices integrated into workflows).
- **Cost advantages** — the firm produces at structurally lower cost than rivals via scale, process, location, or unique resource access (e.g., tier-one mining assets, low-cost retailers, scaled cloud infrastructure).
- **Intangible assets** — brands, patents, and regulatory licences that competitors cannot legally or practically replicate (e.g., pharmaceutical patents, the Coca-Cola brand, banking and casino licences).
- **Efficient scale** — the market is only large enough to profitably support one or a few players, so incumbents earn rational returns and new entry would destroy economics for everyone (e.g., pipelines, railroads, regulated utilities).

## Moat Width and Durability

Morningstar assigns moat ratings of **wide**, **narrow**, or **none**:

| Classification | Implied duration of excess returns | Significance |
|---|---|---|
| **Wide moat** | 20+ years of ROIC > WACC with high confidence | Sustainable long-term compounding; "well worth owning" |
| **Narrow moat** | ~10+ years | Provides medium-term pricing power but more vulnerable |
| **No moat** | Returns revert quickly | Commodity business; competes on price only |

Critically, moats are **not static**. They widen (a network effect strengthening with scale) or erode (technology dissolving switching costs, patent cliffs, regulatory change, shifting consumer behaviour). Kodak held a wide moat in film photography that digital imaging rendered worthless; traditional media moats collapsed as the internet eliminated distribution advantages. A moat must be re-underwritten continuously, not treated as a permanent feature of the business.

## Moats and Valuation

A moat justifies a higher [[valuation]] multiple because future earnings are both larger (returns stay above cost of capital) and more predictable (lower discount for uncertainty). A wide-moat company can reinvest retained earnings at high incremental ROIC for longer, which is the engine of long-run compounding. However, **moat quality and price discipline are complementary, not substitutes** — even a wide-moat business is a poor investment if bought at a multiple that already prices in decades of perfect execution. The practitioner discipline is to buy moat businesses at reasonable prices, not quality at any price.

## Trading and Portfolio Relevance

- **Position concentration and holding period.** Moat companies justify larger, longer-held positions in a quality-tilted book because the edge is structural rather than mean-reverting. This is the opposite of a short-horizon mean-reversion trade.
- **Quality factor.** Moat is the qualitative narrative behind the systematic [[quality-factors|quality factor]] — high and stable ROIC, low earnings volatility, durable margins are the screenable proxies for an unscreenable concept.
- **Short candidates.** *Eroding* moats are high-conviction short or underweight candidates: a business whose ROIC spread is narrowing while the market still prices a wide moat (e.g., a former platform losing network effects) carries re-rating downside.
- **Cyclicality interaction.** A cost-advantage moat is most valuable in a downturn (low-cost producers survive while marginal competitors fail), whereas an intangible/brand moat is more cycle-resilient on the revenue side.

## Fred McNaught's Moat Framework

Fred places significant emphasis on moats in his stock selection:

> "Companies that can establish significant barriers to entry can be very profitable. This gives them a strong competitive advantage, often referred to as a moat. A moat that can last, say, for 20 years is referred to as a **wide moat**. These stocks are well worth owning."

(Source: [[fred-sam-session-2024-01-02]])

- **Australian banks** — the four major banks operate as an oligopoly, creating a structural (efficient-scale plus regulatory) moat. Difficult for competitors to take share, though technology may erode it over time (Source: [[fred-sam-session-2024-06-05]])
- **Temporary moats** — Fred distinguishes durable moats from temporary competitive advantages that erode over time (Source: [[fred-sam-session-2024-02-02]])
- **MOAT calculations** — Fred uses quantitative approaches to assess moat durability, incorporating market-share data and competitive positioning (Source: [[fred-sam-session-2024-02-09]])
