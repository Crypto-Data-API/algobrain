**Unemployment** measures the share of the labor force that is actively seeking work but not currently employed. As one half of the [[federal-reserve|Federal Reserve's]] dual mandate (maximum employment alongside stable prices), the unemployment rate and the monthly **non-farm payrolls (NFP)** report are among the most market-moving macroeconomic data releases, shaping expectations for [[monetary-policy]] and [[interest-rates|interest rates]].

## How It Is Measured

In the US, the Bureau of Labor Statistics publishes two key surveys on the first Friday of each month (the "Jobs Report"):

- **Household survey** → the **unemployment rate** = unemployed ÷ labor force, where the labor force = employed + unemployed (people actively looking). Those not looking are "out of the labor force" and excluded.
- **Establishment survey** → **[[non-farm-payrolls|non-farm payrolls (NFP)]]**, the net change in jobs added/lost, plus average hourly earnings and the average workweek.

The Jobs Report sits alongside other labor and activity releases in the broader suite of [[economic-indicators]] that drive the macro calendar.

Headline definitions matter. The BLS reports a family of measures from **U-3** (the official rate, narrow) to **U-6** (broadest — adds underemployed part-timers and "marginally attached" workers). The **labor force participation rate** is a critical companion: the headline rate can fall simply because discouraged workers stop looking, which is a weak signal disguised as a strong one.

| Measure | What it counts | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **U-1 / U-2** | Long-term unemployed; job losers only | Measures the "hard core" and involuntary job loss |
| **U-3** | Officially unemployed (jobless + actively looking) ÷ labor force | The **headline rate** quoted in the media and by the Fed |
| **U-4** | U-3 + discouraged workers | Adds those who gave up looking recently |
| **U-5** | U-4 + all marginally attached | Wider "want a job but not searching" pool |
| **U-6** | U-5 + part-time-for-economic-reasons | The **broadest** measure of labor-market slack; a widening U6−U3 gap signals hidden underemployment even when U-3 looks healthy |

The accompanying **establishment-survey** signals — headline NFP, **average hourly earnings (wage growth)**, and the **average workweek** — often move markets more than U-3 itself, because the Fed cares about the *wage-inflation* channel. A "Goldilocks" report is strong job growth with *cooling* wage growth; the worst combination for risk assets is weak jobs with *hot* wages (the [[stagflation|stagflationary]] tell).

## Key Concepts

- **Full employment / NAIRU** — the "natural rate of unemployment" (Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment), the level below which tight labor markets push wages and [[inflation]] higher. Estimated in the US at roughly 4–4.5%.
- **Okun's law** — an empirical rule of thumb: each ~1 percentage-point rise in unemployment above its natural rate corresponds to roughly a 2% shortfall in real GDP versus potential. This links the labor market to the output gap in the [[taylor-rule|Taylor Rule]].
- **Lagging vs. leading** — the unemployment *rate* is a lagging indicator (it peaks after a recession ends), but **initial jobless claims** (weekly) and temporary-help employment are leading indicators of turns in the [[business-cycle]].
- **Sahm Rule** — a recession trigger: when the 3-month average unemployment rate rises 0.5 pp above its 12-month low, a [[recession]] has historically already begun.

## Trading and Portfolio Relevance

The labor market is a Level-1 input in [[top-down-macro-analysis|top-down macro analysis]]:

- **Fed reaction function** — rising unemployment pushes the Fed toward cuts (risk-on for equities, bond-positive); a hot labor market with wage growth signals hikes (headwind for long-duration growth stocks). The NFP and wage data feed directly into [[interest-rates]] expectations.
- **Event volatility** — the monthly Jobs Report is a scheduled volatility event. Large NFP surprises move equity index futures, the 2-year Treasury, and the dollar within seconds; rates and FX desks position around the consensus and the "whisper" number.
- **Recession positioning** — a Sahm-rule or sharply rising claims trend prompts rotation toward defensives, duration, and reduced net equity exposure.
- **Stagflation watch** — rising unemployment *combined* with persistent inflation is the [[stagflation]] signature that breaks the normal Fed playbook and the 60/40 portfolio.
- **Caveats** — the report is heavily revised; revisions to prior months can matter more than the headline. Participation shifts and seasonal-adjustment quirks (e.g. post-pandemic distortions) regularly mislead the headline read.

## How traders read the Jobs Report

The market does not trade the *number* — it trades the **surprise** relative to consensus, conditioned on the *current Fed reaction function*. The same data point can be bullish or bearish depending on what the Fed is worried about that month.

| Regime (what the Fed fears) | Strong jobs / hot wages | Weak jobs / cooling wages |
|---|---|---|
| **Inflation-fighting** (rates restrictive) | Risk-OFF: more hikes/higher-for-longer, equities and bonds sell off | Risk-ON: cuts in view, equities and bonds rally ("bad news is good news") |
| **Growth-protecting** (cuts already underway) | Risk-ON: soft landing confirmed, equities rally | Risk-OFF: recession fear, equities fall, [[treasuries\|duration]] rallies (flight to quality) |

**Worked example (illustrative, hypothetical numbers).** Consensus expects +180k NFP and 3.8% unemployment. The print lands at **+310k with 3.6%** and average hourly earnings hotter than expected, during an inflation-fighting regime. The market reads this as the Fed staying tighter for longer: the 2-year Treasury yield jumps, rate-cut odds get priced out, the dollar strengthens, and equity index futures — especially long-duration growth/tech — sell off in the first seconds, before traders digest the wage detail and any prior-month *downward revisions* that can soften the read. The mirror case (a big *miss* with cooling wages) flips every leg of that reaction.

**Practical mechanics traders watch:**
- **The "whisper" vs. consensus** — the priced-in expectation can differ from the published consensus; the reaction is to the gap.
- **Revisions** — a strong headline with large downward revisions to the prior two months is a net-dovish print despite the green top line.
- **Wages over jobs** — in an inflation regime, average hourly earnings frequently drive the bond move more than the payroll count.
- **Cross-checks** — pair NFP with weekly **initial jobless claims** (leading), **JOLTS** openings/quits, and the **ADP** private payrolls preview to triangulate the trend rather than over-weighting one noisy print.

## Common pitfalls

- **Headline tunnel vision.** Trading only U-3 or only the NFP count misses the wage, participation, and revision detail that often dominate the market move.
- **Ignoring revisions.** Monthly data is noisy and heavily revised; a single print is a weak signal — the *trend* and the revision pattern carry the information.
- **Participation illusions.** A falling unemployment rate driven by people leaving the labor force is *weak*, not strong; rising participation can lift the rate even as the economy improves.
- **Regime blindness.** Applying "good jobs = good for stocks" without checking whether the Fed is fighting inflation or protecting growth will get the sign of the trade wrong.
- **Lagging-indicator traps.** Because the unemployment *rate* peaks after a recession ends, waiting for it to confirm a downturn means acting late; the [[#Key Concepts|Sahm Rule]] and claims turn earlier.
