**Stagflation** is the simultaneous occurrence of stagnant economic growth (or [[recession]]), high [[unemployment|unemployment]], and persistent [[inflation]]. It is the worst-case macro scenario for investors because the traditional policy toolkit breaks down: raising [[interest-rates]] to fight inflation further suppresses growth, while stimulating growth risks accelerating inflation. There is no painless exit.

## Why Stagflation Is So Dangerous

In a normal [[recession]], the Fed cuts rates and the economy eventually recovers. In a normal [[inflation]] overshoot, the Fed raises rates and prices cool. Stagflation presents a policy dilemma with no good option:

- **Tighten to fight inflation** → deepens the recession, unemployment rises further
- **Loosen to fight recession** → inflation accelerates, possibly becoming unanchored
- **Do nothing** → both problems persist and compound

This dilemma is why stagflation is sometimes called the "nightmare scenario" for [[central-bank|central banks]]. The [[federal-reserve|Fed]]'s dual mandate (maximum employment + stable prices) becomes internally contradictory.

## The 1970s: The Canonical Example

The stagflation of the 1970s remains the defining episode:

**Timeline:**
- **1971** — Nixon ends the gold standard (Bretton Woods), removing the anchor on [[money-supply|money supply]] growth
- **1973** — OPEC oil embargo quadruples oil prices, triggering cost-push [[inflation]]
- **1974-75** — US enters recession while inflation remains above 10%. The term "stagflation" enters mainstream use.
- **1978-79** — Second oil shock (Iranian Revolution). Inflation reaches 13.5%, unemployment at 6%, GDP growth stagnant
- **1979-82** — Fed Chair Paul Volcker raises the federal funds rate to 20%, deliberately inducing a severe [[recession]] to break inflationary expectations. Unemployment peaks at 10.8%, but inflation is finally tamed.

**Asset class performance during 1970s stagflation:**
- [[gold]] rose from $35 to $850/oz — the greatest gold bull market in history
- [[commodities]] broadly surged (oil, agricultural goods, metals)
- US equities lost ~50% in real terms (1968-1982) despite nominal gains
- Bonds were devastated — rising yields destroyed fixed-income portfolios
- Real estate provided mixed inflation protection depending on location and leverage

**The Volcker lesson:** The only way to break entrenched stagflation is to accept severe short-term economic pain. Volcker's willingness to induce a recession to restore price stability is considered one of the most consequential [[monetary-policy]] decisions in history.

## Causes of Stagflation

Stagflation typically requires a supply-side shock combined with accommodative policy:

1. **Supply shocks** — Oil price spikes, supply chain disruptions, tariffs, or commodity shortages raise costs for producers, who pass them through as higher prices while producing less output. This is cost-push [[inflation]] combined with reduced economic activity.

2. **Excessive [[money-supply]] growth** — If the [[central-bank]] has been too loose for too long, inflationary expectations become embedded, and the economy becomes dependent on continued stimulus. Withdrawing stimulus causes recession, but maintaining it fuels inflation.

3. **Structural rigidities** — Powerful unions, regulated industries, or price controls can prevent the economy from adjusting to shocks, prolonging both the stagnation and the inflation.

4. **[[fiscal-policy|Fiscal]] overcommitment** — Large deficits financed by money creation (fiscal dominance) can create persistent inflation even as the economy weakens.

## Stagflation Risk in 2025-2026

The current macro environment has echoes of stagflation risk:

- **Tariffs as supply shock** — The [[2025-tariff-market-volatility|2025 tariff escalation]] functions as cost-push inflation (higher import prices) while reducing economic efficiency and growth. IMF estimates tariffs raise PCE by ~0.5% while reducing GDP by ~0.5% — a textbook stagflationary impulse.
- **Persistent fiscal deficits** — The US is running deficits exceeding 6% of GDP near full [[employment]], leaving little [[fiscal-policy|fiscal]] room to stimulate if growth slows.
- **Sticky inflation** — Core inflation has proven resistant to returning to the Fed's 2% target even after aggressive tightening.

Whether this evolves into true stagflation or remains a "stagflation scare" depends on whether inflationary expectations become unanchored — the critical threshold.

## Trading Stagflation

Stagflation is the hardest macro regime to trade profitably. Most traditional portfolios (60/40 stocks/bonds) fail because both legs lose:

| Asset Class | Performance | Why |
|-------------|-------------|-----|
| **[[gold]]** | Strong winner | Inflation hedge + safe haven + monetary debasement fear |
| **[[commodities]]** | Strong (especially energy, agriculture) | Supply-constrained goods appreciate as money loses value |
| **Equities** | Weak overall | Earnings squeezed by rising costs + falling demand |
| **Value stocks** | Relative outperformer | Cash flows today worth more than growth promises tomorrow |
| **Growth stocks** | Worst hit | High duration = most sensitive to rising discount rates |
| **Bonds** | Weak | Rising inflation erodes real returns; yields must rise |
| **TIPS** | Moderate protection | Inflation adjustment helps but real yields may also rise |
| **Cash** | Losing real value | But less bad than bonds; preserves optionality |
| **Real assets** (real estate, infrastructure) | Mixed | Inflation pass-through helps revenue, but rising rates hurt valuations |

**The [[all-weather-portfolio|All Weather]] approach** — [[ray-dalio]]'s portfolio explicitly includes a stagflation quadrant: gold and inflation-linked bonds are designed to perform when growth falls and inflation rises.

Fred discusses stagflation as a risk scenario for [[interest-rates]]-sensitive stocks, where companies with high debt loads and cyclical earnings face a double headwind of rising costs and weakening demand. (Source: [[fred-sam-sessions-overview]])

## Key Stagflation Indicators to Monitor

- **Core CPI/PCE** remaining elevated while [[gdp|GDP]] growth decelerates
- **Real wages** declining (nominal wage growth below inflation)
- **[[ism-pmi|ISM PMI]]** falling toward or below 50 while price sub-indices remain elevated
- **Consumer confidence** declining sharply
- **5-year breakeven inflation** rising even as growth indicators weaken
- **Commodity prices** (especially energy) rising despite economic weakness
