A Self-Managed Super Fund (SMSF) is an Australian [[superannuation]] fund with 1–6 members where the members are also the trustees — meaning YOU control the investment decisions rather than delegating them to a professional fund manager. With approximately 600,000 SMSFs managing around AUD $900 billion (roughly 25% of the total super pool), SMSFs are a major force in Australian investing and a significant source of retail trading volume on the [[australian-investing|ASX]]. An SMSF sits at the intersection of [[portfolio-theory]], [[risk-management]], and tax structuring — it is a vehicle, not a strategy, and its value depends entirely on how the trustee uses it.

> **Figures disclaimer:** Dollar thresholds, caps, tax rates, fee ranges, and percentages on this page are indicative and change with legislation and indexation. Several figures below are illustrative examples to demonstrate mechanics, not current statutory values. Always confirm current caps, rates, and thresholds with the ATO or a licensed adviser before acting. Numbers are framed conceptually wherever possible.

## Overview

An SMSF gives members complete control over their retirement investments. Unlike industry or retail super funds where a professional investment team decides where money is allocated, SMSF trustees make every investment decision — which shares to buy, when to sell, whether to invest in property, how much to hold in cash, and how to structure the portfolio across asset classes.

This control comes with significant responsibility. SMSF trustees must comply with superannuation law, maintain an investment strategy, arrange annual audits, lodge annual returns with the ATO, and ensure the fund operates solely for the purpose of providing retirement benefits. The ATO (not [[asic|ASIC]]) is the regulator of SMSFs.

## Why People Set Up SMSFs

| Motivation | Explanation |
|------------|-------------|
| **Investment control** | Choose exactly which shares, ETFs, bonds, and other assets to hold — not limited to a fund's menu |
| **Direct share ownership** | Hold ASX shares directly via [[chess-sponsorship|CHESS]] with a personal HIN, receiving dividends and [[franking-credits]] directly |
| **Property investment** | Buy residential or commercial property within super (subject to strict rules) |
| **Cost efficiency at scale** | For balances above $500K+, SMSF costs as a percentage can be lower than industry/retail fund fees |
| **Tax planning flexibility** | Precise control over capital gains timing, pension drawdowns, and contribution strategies |
| **Estate planning** | Greater control over death benefit nominations and binding death benefit arrangements |
| **Pooling with family** | Up to 6 members (usually family) can pool super for larger investment opportunities |
| **Investment in alternatives** | Access to asset classes not available in standard super: direct property, gold, crypto, collectibles |

## Minimum Viable Balance

The ATO has consistently indicated that SMSFs generally need a balance of at least **$200,000** to be cost-effective compared to a low-fee industry fund. This is because SMSF running costs are largely fixed:

| Cost | Annual Amount |
|------|---------------|
| **Accounting & tax return** | $1,500–$3,000 |
| **Annual audit** | $500–$1,500 |
| **ASIC annual fee** | ~$60 (corporate trustee) |
| **ATO supervisory levy** | $259 |
| **Corporate trustee annual review fee** | $55 |
| **Investment platform/broker fees** | Varies |
| **Total typical range** | $2,500–$5,000+ per year |

At $200,000, these costs represent 1.25–2.5% of the balance — comparable to or higher than an industry fund. At $500,000+, costs drop to 0.5–1.0%, potentially below industry fund fees. At $1 million+, costs become a fraction of a percent.

## SMSF Structure

### Trustee Options

| Structure | Description | Pros | Cons |
|-----------|-------------|------|------|
| **Individual trustees** | Members are trustees in their own names | Simpler to set up, lower initial cost | Personal liability, harder to change members |
| **Corporate trustee** | A company acts as trustee, members are directors | Limited liability, easier member changes, cleaner structure | Additional ASIC fees, company setup cost (~$500–$1,000) |

The ATO recommends using a corporate trustee. Most advisers agree — the limited liability protection and administrative simplicity justify the small additional cost.

### Members and Roles

- **Minimum 1, maximum 6 members** (increased from 4 to 6 in July 2021)
- Each member must be a trustee (individual structure) or a director of the corporate trustee
- Members are typically family: spouse, adult children, parents
- Each member has their own "account" within the SMSF (separate accumulation/pension balances)

## Setup Process (Trustee Checklist)

Establishing and running an SMSF is a defined sequence. Treat each step as a compliance gate, not a formality:

1. **Decide if an SMSF is right for you** — balance large enough to be cost-effective (see [[#Minimum Viable Balance]]), willingness to take on trustee duties, and the investment knowledge to make your own [[portfolio-construction]] and [[risk-management]] decisions.
2. **Choose a trustee structure** — individual trustees vs corporate trustee (see [[#Trustee Options]]). Most advisers recommend a corporate trustee.
3. **Create the trust and trust deed** — the deed is the governing document; it must comply with the SIS Act and be kept current.
4. **Register with the ATO** — obtain an ABN and TFN, elect to be a regulated fund, and register for GST if turnover requires it.
5. **Set up a dedicated bank account** — fund assets must be held separately from members' personal money (the separation-of-assets rule).
6. **Prepare a written investment strategy** — document [[diversification]], liquidity, risk/return, member circumstances, and insurance (see [[#Investment Strategy (Documented)]]).
7. **Roll over existing super** — transfer balances from existing funds into the SMSF.
8. **Open broker / platform accounts** — [[chess-sponsorship|CHESS]]-sponsored broker accounts in the fund's name with the fund's HIN (see [[#SMSF Trading and Broker Integration]]).
9. **Operate compliantly, ongoing** — keep minutes of trustee decisions, value assets at market, lodge the annual return, and arrange the independent audit (see [[#ATO Reporting Requirements]]).

A missed step here is not a paperwork inconvenience — it can render the fund non-complying, which carries a punitive tax outcome (see [[#Compliance Burden]]).

## Investment Rules

SMSFs must comply with strict investment regulations under the Superannuation Industry (Supervision) Act 1993 (SIS Act):

### Sole Purpose Test

The fund must be maintained **solely** for the purpose of providing retirement benefits to members (or death benefits to dependants). You cannot use SMSF assets for personal benefit before meeting a condition of release.

**Breach examples**: Living in an SMSF-owned property, using SMSF funds to pay personal expenses, lending SMSF money to a member.

### Investment Strategy (Documented)

Every SMSF must have a **written investment strategy** that considers:

- [[diversification]] of assets
- Liquidity (ability to pay pensions and expenses)
- Risk vs return balance appropriate to members' circumstances
- Members' ages and retirement timelines
- Insurance needs of members

The investment strategy must be reviewed regularly (at least annually) and updated when circumstances change. This connects directly to [[portfolio-construction]] and [[risk-management]] principles.

### Arm's Length Rule

All SMSF investments must be made on an **arm's length basis** — transactions with related parties must be at market value with commercial terms. You cannot sell your personal property to your SMSF at below market value.

### In-House Asset Rules

No more than **5% of the fund's total assets** can be invested in assets of related parties ("in-house assets"). This includes loans to or investments in a related company or trust.

## What SMSFs Can Invest In

SMSFs have extremely broad investment powers, subject to the rules above:

### Standard Investments

| Asset Class | Details | Relevant Wiki Pages |
|-------------|---------|-------------------|
| **ASX shares** | Direct ownership via [[chess-sponsorship|CHESS]], full voting rights, direct dividends | [[australian-investing]], [[dividend]], [[franking-credits]] |
| **International shares** | Via broker or ETF; direct international shares require custodial holding | [[diversification]] |
| **ETFs** | Vanguard, Betashares, iShares extremely popular in SMSFs — instant diversification at low cost | [[etf]], [[passive-investing]] |
| **Managed funds** | Active or passive managed funds | [[portfolio-construction]] |
| **Term deposits** | Fixed interest investments via banks | |
| **Government & corporate bonds** | Fixed income allocation | [[diversification]] |
| **Cash** | Must maintain some cash for liquidity (pension payments, expenses) | |

### Alternative Investments

| Asset Class | Details | Rules |
|-------------|---------|-------|
| **Residential property** | Can buy property within SMSF — cannot be lived in by members or related parties | Must be arm's length, can use limited recourse borrowing |
| **Commercial property** | Can lease to a member's business at market rent (common for small business owners) | Must be at market rent, arm's length lease |
| **Gold / precious metals** | Physical bullion allowed (must meet purity standards, stored in approved facility) | Cannot be stored at home or personally accessed |
| **Cryptocurrency** | Bitcoin, Ethereum, etc. — allowed with proper documentation and compliant exchange | Must be in fund's name, recorded in investment strategy |
| **Collectibles & personal use assets** | Art, wine, jewellery, rare coins, memorabilia | Strict storage rules: must be insured, not used by members, stored separately, decisions documented |

### Limited Recourse Borrowing Arrangements (LRBAs)

SMSFs can borrow to purchase a **single acquirable asset** (typically property) through a limited recourse borrowing arrangement. Key features:

- The asset is held in a separate holding trust until the loan is repaid
- If the SMSF defaults, the lender's recourse is limited to the single asset — other SMSF assets are protected
- Higher interest rates than standard home loans (typically 1–2% above variable rates)
- Cannot renovate or alter the property until the loan is fully repaid (maintenance only)
- Controversial: ASIC and APRA have flagged risks of property borrowing within super

## SMSF Investment Strategies (Connected to Wiki)

### Dividend and Franking Credit Strategy

A popular SMSF approach, especially for funds approaching or in pension phase:

- Build a portfolio of high-yielding, fully franked Australian shares (Big Four banks, Telstra, Wesfarmers, BHP)
- [[franking-credits]] offset the 15% tax in accumulation phase — effective tax rate on fully franked dividends can be close to 0%
- In **pension phase**: 0% tax on earnings AND full cash refunds of franking credits from the ATO
- Example: CBA paying $4.50/share dividend fully franked → SMSF in pension phase receives $4.50 + $1.93 franking credit refund = $6.43 effective income per share
- See [[dividend]] and [[franking-credits]] for detailed mechanics

### Covered Call Income Strategy

SMSFs holding large positions in liquid ASX shares can write [[covered-call|covered calls]] to generate additional income:

- Sell call options against existing share holdings (e.g., BHP, CBA, WBC)
- Collect premium income on top of dividends
- Particularly suitable in pension phase where option premium income is tax-free
- Risk: shares may be called away if price rises above strike — capping upside
- See [[covered-call]] and [[options-overview]] for strategy mechanics

### ETF Portfolio Strategy

Many SMSFs use low-cost [[etf|ETFs]] as core holdings for instant [[diversification]]:

- **Vanguard Australian Shares ETF (VAS)**: Broad ASX 300 exposure, ~0.07% fee
- **Vanguard MSCI International Shares ETF (VGS)**: Global developed market exposure, ~0.18% fee
- **Betashares Australia 200 ETF (A200)**: ASX 200 exposure, ~0.04% fee
- **iShares Core S&P 500 ETF (IVV)**: US S&P 500 exposure on ASX, ~0.04% fee
- A simple 60% VAS / 40% VGS portfolio provides diversified Australian and international equity exposure at minimal cost

### Value and Growth Approaches

- [[value-investing]]: Screen for undervalued ASX companies based on P/E, P/B, dividend yield — suits patient SMSF investors in accumulation
- [[growth-investing]]: Allocate a portion to higher-growth companies (tech, healthcare) for capital appreciation
- Many SMSFs use a barbell approach: core of defensive, high-yield blue chips + satellite of growth opportunities

### Property Exposure via REITs

Rather than buying direct property (which creates liquidity and borrowing complications), many SMSFs gain property exposure through [[reits|REITs]]:

- **Goodman Group (GMG)**: Industrial/logistics property
- **Scentre Group (SCG)**: Westfield shopping centres
- **Dexus (DXS)**: Office and industrial property
- **Stockland (SGP)**: Diversified property
- REITs provide liquidity, diversification, and income without the complexity of direct property ownership

### Portfolio Rebalancing

SMSFs should implement a [[portfolio-rebalancing]] discipline:

- Review asset allocation at least annually (or when drift exceeds 5–10% thresholds)
- Rebalance by directing new contributions to underweight asset classes (tax-efficient) or selling overweight positions
- Document rebalancing decisions as part of the investment strategy review

## SMSF in Pension Phase

When members reach preservation age (60) and retire, the SMSF can commence a pension (account-based pension). This is where SMSFs become extraordinarily tax-advantaged:

| Feature | Accumulation Phase | Pension Phase |
|---------|-------------------|---------------|
| **Tax on earnings** | 15% (10% on long-term CGT) | **0%** |
| **Tax on franking credits** | Offset against 15% tax | **Full cash refund from ATO** |
| **Tax on pension payments** | N/A | **0% (age 60+)** |
| **Minimum drawdown** | N/A | 4% of balance (age <65), 5% (65–74), etc. |
| **Maximum drawdown** | N/A | No maximum |

The combination of 0% tax on earnings and full franking credit refunds makes an SMSF in pension phase arguably the most tax-advantaged investment structure available in Australia. A $1 million SMSF portfolio yielding 5% fully franked generates approximately $50,000 in dividends plus ~$21,400 in franking credit refunds — totalling $71,400 in income, all completely tax-free.

### Transfer Balance Cap

The amount that can be transferred from accumulation phase to pension phase is subject to the **transfer balance cap** — currently $1.9 million per person ($3.8 million for a couple with two SMSF members). Amounts above this cap must remain in accumulation phase (taxed at 15%).

## SMSF vs Industry Fund Comparison

| Feature | SMSF | Industry Fund (e.g., AustralianSuper) |
|---------|------|---------------------------------------|
| **Investment control** | Complete — choose every investment | Limited to fund's menu of options |
| **Direct share ownership** | Yes — via CHESS | No — pooled investments |
| **Franking credit refunds** | Direct to fund, cash refunds in pension | Passed through proportionally |
| **Property investment** | Direct property allowed (including LRBA) | Via property allocation in balanced options |
| **Costs (at $200K balance)** | $2,500–$5,000 (1.25–2.5%) | ~$1,200–$2,000 (0.6–1.0%) |
| **Costs (at $1M balance)** | $3,000–$6,000 (0.3–0.6%) | ~$5,000–$10,000 (0.5–1.0%) |
| **Administration burden** | High — annual audit, tax return, trustee duties | Zero — fund handles everything |
| **Insurance** | Must arrange separately (often more expensive) | Group insurance (cheaper due to scale) |
| **Unlisted assets** | Limited access | Access to infrastructure, private equity, unlisted property |
| **Estate planning control** | Maximum control over death benefits | Limited to fund's nomination options |
| **Regulatory risk** | Individual trustee penalties for breaches | Fund handles compliance |
| **Default investment quality** | Depends entirely on trustee knowledge | Professional fund managers, strong track records |

## Risks and Considerations

### Compliance Burden

- Annual audit by an approved SMSF auditor (mandatory)
- Annual return lodged with ATO
- Investment strategy must be documented and reviewed
- Records of all trustee decisions and meetings
- Failure to comply can result in penalties, fund being made non-complying (46.5% tax on entire balance), or personal trustee penalties

### Single-Trustee Risk

If a sole member/trustee becomes incapacitated, who manages the fund? SMSFs should have:

- An enduring power of attorney that covers SMSF trustee duties
- A corporate trustee (so a new director can be appointed)
- Clear binding death benefit nominations

### Liquidity Requirements

SMSFs in pension phase must be able to pay minimum pension amounts. This means maintaining sufficient liquid assets — a portfolio entirely in illiquid assets (e.g., direct property) can create problems if pension payments are required and nothing can be sold quickly.

### Investment Knowledge

SMSF trustees ARE the investment managers. Poor investment decisions — concentrated portfolios, speculative bets, timing mistakes — are borne entirely by the members. Unlike an industry fund where professional managers make decisions, SMSF outcomes depend entirely on the trustees' knowledge of [[portfolio-construction]], [[risk-management]], [[diversification]], and market dynamics.

### SMSF Property Borrowing Risks

Limited recourse borrowing for property carries specific risks:

- Higher interest rates than standard home loans
- Cannot renovate until loan repaid
- Property illiquidity may prevent pension payments
- Negative gearing benefits are less effective at 15% tax rate
- ASIC has flagged risks of SMSF property borrowing in multiple warnings

## Worked Example (Illustrative)

> All figures below are hypothetical and chosen only to illustrate mechanics. They are NOT current statutory rates or guaranteed outcomes.

Consider a two-member SMSF (a couple) with a combined balance, both members in accumulation phase, who decide to move toward pension phase over time. Their decision chain illustrates how the structure's levers interact:

1. **Structure choice** — they adopt a corporate trustee so that if one member becomes incapacitated, a replacement director can be appointed without re-titling every asset (see [[#Single-Trustee Risk]]).
2. **Investment strategy** — they document a target allocation: a core of [[etf|ETFs]] for [[diversification]], a sleeve of fully franked blue-chip ASX shares for [[franking-credits]] income, a [[reits|REIT]] sleeve for property exposure without the liquidity problems of direct property, and a cash buffer sized to cover anticipated pension payments and fund expenses (the liquidity requirement).
3. **Income engine** — in accumulation, the 15% concessional rate on earnings combined with franking credits drives the effective tax on fully franked dividends well below 15%. The mechanics (not the exact numbers) are shown in [[#Dividend and Franking Credit Strategy]].
4. **Transition to pension** — once a member meets a condition of release, the fund commences an account-based pension on that member's balance (up to the transfer balance cap). Earnings supporting the pension are taxed at 0% and franking credits become refundable in cash — the single most powerful feature of the structure (see [[#SMSF in Pension Phase]]).
5. **Risk discipline** — they review the [[portfolio-rebalancing|rebalance]] annually, keep the cash buffer topped up so pension payments never force a fire sale, and avoid concentrating in a single illiquid asset.

The lesson the example carries: the SMSF's tax advantages are real, but they are *unlocked by process discipline* — documented strategy, liquidity management, and compliance — not by the structure alone. A poorly run SMSF underperforms a well-run industry fund despite identical tax law.

## Common Pitfalls and Failure Modes

| Pitfall | What goes wrong | Mitigation |
|---------|-----------------|------------|
| **Sub-scale balance** | Fixed running costs eat a large % of a small balance; SMSF underperforms a low-fee industry fund | Only establish above a cost-effective balance; review cost ratio annually |
| **Sole purpose breach** | Using fund assets for present-day personal benefit (living in fund property, personal use of collectibles) | Keep all fund assets at arm's length; document that every asset serves retirement purpose |
| **Liquidity trap** | Fund holds illiquid assets (direct property) and cannot meet minimum pension payments | Maintain a cash buffer; favour liquid [[reits]]/[[etf|ETFs]] over direct property where pension cash flow matters |
| **Asset concentration** | Whole balance in one stock, one property, or one theme; no [[diversification]] | Apply [[portfolio-construction]] discipline; document diversification in the investment strategy |
| **Compliance lapse** | Late lodgement, no audit, no minutes; fund risks being made non-complying (severe tax penalty) | Calendar all deadlines; engage an SMSF accountant and approved auditor |
| **Trustee incapacity / death** | Sole trustee becomes incapacitated and no one can act for the fund | Corporate trustee + enduring power of attorney + binding death benefit nomination |
| **LRBA over-reach** | Geared property purchase becomes illiquid and cash-flow negative inside super | Stress-test the borrowing against vacancy and rate rises; heed ASIC's warnings (see [[#SMSF Property Borrowing Risks]]) |
| **Investment overconfidence** | Trustee chases speculative trades the fund cannot afford to lose | Treat the fund as a retirement nest egg; apply [[risk-management]] and [[position-sizing]] discipline |
