A portfolio construction approach where an investor maintains two distinct portfolios with different objectives, time horizons, and sell disciplines. This is [[fred-mcnaught|Fred McNaught's]] core portfolio architecture and a foundational concept in [[alfred|Alfred's]] investment philosophy. It is a practical retail form of the institutional **core-satellite** model: a stable, low-turnover "core" that compounds, plus a smaller, active "satellite" bucket that takes opportunistic risk.

## The Two Portfolios

### Portfolio 1: Core (Long-Term Growth & Dividends)

The core portfolio holds high-quality stocks for the long term. Positions are sold only when structural changes occur that make the investment thesis no longer valid.

**Characteristics:**
- Held for years to decades
- Focus on [[earnings-per-share|EPS]] growth and [[franking-credits|franked]] [[dividend-yield|dividends]]
- [[compounding|Compound returns]] through [[dividend-yield|DRP]] (dividend reinvestment plans)
- Diversified across sectors and geographies
- Includes defensive assets (infrastructure, utilities funds)

**Fred's broker:** Macquarie Private Wealth (for core holdings)

### Portfolio 2: Trading (Short-Term Capital Gain)

The trading portfolio holds stocks bought for cyclical or opportunistic reasons with the intent to sell for capital gain. Fred refers to this as "speculating" — distinct from investing.

**Characteristics:**
- Held for weeks to months
- Bought for specific reasons — generally cyclical
- Sold when the trade thesis plays out
- No emotional attachment to positions

**Fred's broker:** ComSec (for trading positions)

## Allocation by Age

Fred's key principle: the split between portfolios depends on age.

- **While young:** Trading portfolio can be "somewhat high" — more risk tolerance, longer time to recover
- **As you get older:** Must reduce trading allocation and revert to Portfolio 1 — prioritise income and capital preservation

(Source: [[fred-sam-session-2024-01-02]], [[fred-sam-session-2024-01-18]])

## Fred's Allocation

Fred allocates approximately **15-20% to the trading portfolio** and the remainder to core holdings. (Source: [[fred-sam-session-2024-01-02]])

## Why Separate the Books

Maintaining two physically separate accounts (or at least two separate mental and accounting ledgers) is a [[risk-management|risk-management]] and behavioural device, not just bookkeeping:

- **Prevents thesis drift** — the single most common retail failure is the "accidental investor": buying a stock as a short-term trade, watching it fall, then "becoming a long-term holder" to avoid booking the loss. Hard-walling the two books forces a position to be classified at entry and held to that book's sell discipline.
- **Different sell rules** — the core book sells only on a broken thesis (structural change); the trading book sells when the catalyst plays out or a stop is hit. Mixing them corrupts both disciplines.
- **Caps active risk** — sizing the trading book at a fixed fraction (Fred's 15-20%) is a [[position-sizing]] rule that bounds how much of total wealth is exposed to speculative drawdowns, regardless of conviction in any single idea.
- **Cleaner performance attribution** — separating the compounding engine from the active book makes it possible to honestly measure whether the trading activity actually adds value over simply leaving the capital in the core.

This mirrors the institutional **core-satellite** construction, where a passive/strategic core (often index or high-quality dividend compounders) is paired with satellite sleeves that pursue tactical alpha — the satellite's job is to add return without letting its volatility dominate the total portfolio.
