CAN SLIM is a growth stock selection and timing system developed by [[william-o-neil|William O'Neil]], founder of Investor's Business Daily (IBD). The acronym represents seven fundamental and technical criteria that O'Neil identified by studying every major stock market winner from 1880 to the present. The system combines [[fundamental-analysis|fundamental analysis]] (earnings growth, institutional sponsorship) with [[technical-analysis-overview|technical analysis]] ([[breakout-strategies|breakout entries]], relative strength) to identify growth stocks at the point of maximum acceleration.

## Overview

O'Neil's research, detailed in *[[how-to-make-money-in-stocks|How to Make Money in Stocks]]*, analyzed the common characteristics of the best-performing stocks before they made their biggest price advances. He found that virtually all major winners shared the same seven traits, which he codified as CAN SLIM. The system is not a screen that produces a buy list -- it is a framework for evaluating whether a stock has the characteristics of a potential leader and whether the timing is right to enter. (Source: [[book-how-to-make-money-in-stocks]])

## The Seven Criteria

### C -- Current Quarterly Earnings

**Look for**: Quarterly earnings per share (EPS) growth of at least 25% year-over-year, with acceleration preferred.

- The stronger the earnings growth, the better. Stocks showing 50-100%+ quarterly EPS growth are ideal
- Earnings acceleration (e.g., 15% -> 25% -> 40% growth across consecutive quarters) is a powerful bullish signal
- Revenue growth should confirm earnings growth -- earnings rising on cost-cutting alone are less sustainable
- Watch for "whisper numbers" and earnings surprises that beat consensus

### A -- Annual Earnings Growth

**Look for**: Annual EPS growth of 25%+ over the past 3-5 years.

- Consistency matters: look for a steady uptrend in annual earnings, not erratic spikes
- Return on equity (ROE) should be at least 17%, preferably 25%+
- Annual earnings growth confirms that the quarterly strength is not a one-time event

### N -- New Products, New Management, New Highs

**Look for**: A "new" catalyst driving the stock.

- **New products or services**: A company launching an innovative product that opens a large addressable market (Apple's iPhone, Netflix's streaming pivot)
- **New management**: A leadership change that transforms company direction
- **New price highs**: Counterintuitively, O'Neil found that stocks making new 52-week or all-time highs tend to go higher, while stocks near 52-week lows tend to go lower
- The "N" factor is the narrative catalyst that explains WHY earnings are accelerating

### S -- Supply and Demand

**Look for**: Smaller float + high demand = price appreciation.

- Companies with a smaller share float (under 25 million shares) tend to make bigger moves
- Share buybacks reduce supply and signal management confidence
- High [[volume-analysis|volume]] on up days and low volume on down days indicates institutional accumulation
- Volume should surge 50-100%+ above average on the [[breakout-strategies|breakout]] day

### L -- Leader or Laggard

**Look for**: Industry group leaders with high relative strength.

- **Relative Strength (RS) Rating**: O'Neil's IBD ranks stocks on a 1-99 scale based on price performance versus all other stocks over the trailing 12 months
- Only buy stocks with an RS rating of 80 or higher -- meaning the stock is outperforming at least 80% of all stocks
- Buy the #1 or #2 stock in a leading industry group, never the laggard hoping for a catch-up trade
- "Sympathy plays" (buying cheaper stocks in the same industry as the leader) rarely work as well

### I -- Institutional Sponsorship

**Look for**: Increasing institutional ownership by quality funds.

- Look for stocks owned by a growing number of mutual funds, especially top-performing funds
- Quality of sponsors matters more than quantity -- being held by Fidelity Contrafund or T. Rowe Price Growth Stock is more meaningful than being in 200 mediocre funds
- A stock with NO institutional ownership lacks the buying power to sustain a major advance
- But a stock owned by TOO MANY institutions may be late-stage -- everyone who wants to buy already has

### M -- Market Direction

**Look for**: A confirmed market uptrend (don't buy in a downtrend).

- Three out of four stocks follow the market's general direction -- buying growth stocks in a bear market is a losing strategy
- O'Neil uses "follow-through days" (a strong gain on increasing volume on day 4-7 of an attempted rally) to confirm new uptrends
- "Distribution days" (declines on higher volume than the prior session) signal institutional selling; 4-5 distribution days within a few weeks often precede corrections
- The M factor is the most important risk management rule: stay mostly in cash during confirmed downtrends

## The Buy Point

CAN SLIM is not just a stock selection screen -- the entry point matters enormously:

1. The stock forms a **base pattern** (flat base, [[cup-and-handle|cup-and-handle]], double bottom, ascending base) during a consolidation period of at least 5-7 weeks
2. The **buy point** is the highest price in the handle or pattern plus $0.10 (the "pivot point")
3. Volume on the breakout day should be at least 40-50% above average
4. Buy within 5% of the pivot point -- chasing extended stocks increases risk dramatically
5. If the stock drops 7-8% below the purchase price, sell with no exceptions (O'Neil's "7-8% loss-cutting rule")

## Risk Management Rules

O'Neil's loss management is one of CAN SLIM's most important features:

- **Cut all losses at 7-8%** from the purchase price -- no exceptions, no hoping, no averaging down
- **Never average down** -- adding to a losing position violates every CAN SLIM principle
- **Take profits at 20-25%** gains for most positions (the "3:1 ratio" -- risk 7% to gain 20%)
- **Hold exceptional winners** -- if a stock rises 20%+ in the first 2-3 weeks, hold for at least 8 weeks to see if it becomes a bigger winner
- **Concentrate positions** -- O'Neil favors 4-6 positions in a focused portfolio rather than wide diversification

## Performance and Criticism

**Strengths**:
- Grounded in extensive historical research (O'Neil studied 125+ years of market winners)
- Combines fundamental quality with technical timing
- Built-in [[risk-management|risk management]] with defined loss limits
- IBD's stock lists and ratings provide systematic implementation tools

**Criticisms**:
- Growth stocks with high RS ratings have high valuations -- buying high PE stocks feels uncomfortable and contradicts value investing principles
- The system requires patience -- many months may pass without valid setups
- Works best in bull markets; much harder in sideways or bear markets (by design -- M factor keeps you in cash)
- Survivorship bias concerns in historical studies of "greatest winners"
