Super contribution strategies are methods of legally directing more money into [[superannuation]] to take advantage of its concessional 15% tax rate on contributions and earnings (compared to marginal rates of up to 45% + 2% Medicare outside super) and the extraordinary 0% tax rate in pension phase. When combined with [[compounding]] over decades, the tax arbitrage between personal tax rates and super tax rates can result in hundreds of thousands of dollars in additional retirement wealth. These strategies range from simple salary sacrifice (accessible to anyone) to sophisticated techniques like catch-up contributions, re-contribution strategies, and downsizer contributions. They are most powerful inside a self-directed structure such as an [[smsf]], but apply equally to industry and retail funds.

> **Figures disclaimer:** Contribution caps, thresholds, tax rates, and offsets shown on this page are indicative and change frequently through legislation and indexation. Many of the dollar figures below are illustrative examples used to demonstrate the *mechanics* of each strategy, not current statutory values. Always confirm the current year's caps and rates with the ATO or a licensed adviser before contributing — exceeding a cap can negate the entire tax benefit. Worked-example numbers are clearly marked as illustrative.

## Overview

The fundamental principle: every dollar taxed at 15% inside super instead of 32.5-47% outside super retains 17.5-32 cents more per dollar. Over 20-30 years of [[compounding]], these retained cents compound into substantial sums.

The key constraint: super is locked until preservation age (60 for most people), so every contribution strategy involves a trade-off between tax efficiency and liquidity. The younger you are, the longer the lock-up — but also the longer the compounding runway.

### Two Families of Contributions

Every strategy on this page is a variation on two categories. Understanding the distinction is the foundation for everything else:

| | **Concessional (pre-tax)** | **Non-concessional (after-tax)** |
|---|---|---|
| **Source** | Pre-tax salary, employer SG, personal deductible | After-tax savings, inheritances, asset sales |
| **Tax going in** | 15% contributions tax (or 30% with [[#Division 293 Tax]]) | Nil (already taxed at marginal rate) |
| **Deductible?** | Yes (reduces taxable income) | No |
| **Cap type** | Annual concessional cap (+ carry-forward) | Annual non-concessional cap (+ bring-forward) |
| **Best for** | Reducing this year's income tax | Sheltering an existing lump sum's *future earnings* |
| **Examples on this page** | Salary sacrifice, catch-up, personal deductible | Non-concessional, government co-contribution, spouse, downsizer, re-contribution |

The earnings on *both* types are taxed at the same concessional 15% (accumulation) / 0% (pension) rate once inside the fund — that shared earnings advantage is the real long-term engine; the contribution-tax treatment just changes the entry cost.

## Strategy 1: Salary Sacrifice

**The most common and accessible strategy.**

Redirect pre-tax salary into super before income tax is applied. Instead of receiving the salary (taxed at marginal rate), the employer contributes it directly to your super fund (taxed at 15%).

### How It Works

1. Arrange with your employer to reduce your pre-tax salary by a chosen amount
2. The employer contributes the sacrificed amount to your super fund
3. The contribution is taxed at 15% within the fund (contributions tax)
4. You pay income tax on your reduced salary

### Example (Illustrative)

> Figures below are hypothetical and use round numbers to show the mechanism. Confirm current tax brackets and the concessional cap with the ATO.

**Without salary sacrifice** ($120,000 salary):
- Tax on $120,000 (2024-25): approximately $29,467 + Medicare
- Take-home: approximately $88,133

**With $15,000 salary sacrifice** (effectively $105,000 salary + $15,000 to super):
- Tax on $105,000: approximately $24,967 + Medicare
- Super tax on $15,000: $15,000 x 15% = $2,250
- Total tax: approximately $27,217
- Take-home + super: $78,133 cash + $12,750 in super = $90,883 total
- **Net benefit: approximately $2,750 better off** (immediately)
- Plus: $12,750 now compounding at 15% tax vs 30% tax on earnings

### Key Rules

- **Total concessional cap: $30,000/year** — this includes employer Superannuation Guarantee (SG) contributions + salary sacrifice + personal deductible contributions
- At 11.5% SG on $120,000 salary, employer already contributes $13,800 — leaving $16,200 of cap available for salary sacrifice
- Exceeding the cap results in the excess being taxed at your marginal rate (plus an interest charge) — effectively negating the benefit
- You can salary sacrifice at any age while still working

### Who Benefits Most

| Marginal Rate | Tax Saving per $1 Sacrificed | Annual Saving on $15,000 Sacrifice |
|---------------|-----------------------------|------------------------------------|
| 16% ($18,201-$45,000) | 1c | $150 |
| 30% ($45,001-$135,000) | 15c | $2,250 |
| 37% ($135,001-$190,000) | 22c | $3,300 |
| 45% ($190,001+) | 30c | $4,500 |
| 47% (45% + 2% Medicare) | 32c | $4,800 |

At the 16% bracket, salary sacrifice provides minimal benefit (and locks money away). At 37%+, it is one of the most effective tax strategies available.

## Strategy 2: Catch-Up (Carry-Forward) Contributions

**For those with variable income or who haven't maximised contributions in previous years.**

If your total super balance is under $500,000, you can carry forward unused concessional contribution cap amounts from the previous **5 financial years** (starting from 2018-19 onward).

### How It Works

- Each year you don't fully use your $30,000 concessional cap, the unused amount rolls forward
- After 5 years: up to $150,000 in accumulated unused cap (in theory)
- You must use the oldest year's unused cap first

### Example

An investor contributed only $15,000/year in each of the last 3 years (employer SG only):

| Year | Cap | Used | Unused | Carried Forward |
|------|-----|------|--------|-----------------|
| 2022-23 | $27,500* | $15,000 | $12,500 | $12,500 |
| 2023-24 | $30,000 | $15,000 | $15,000 | $27,500 |
| 2024-25 | $30,000 | $15,000 | $15,000 | $42,500 |

*Cap was $27,500 until 2023-24.

In 2025-26, this investor could contribute up to $30,000 (current cap) + $42,500 (carried forward) = **$72,500** in concessional contributions — all taxed at 15%.

### Ideal Use Cases

- Received a bonus or windfall and want to shelter it from tax
- Returned to work after parental leave or career break
- Sold a business or asset and have a large taxable income for one year
- Approaching retirement and want to turbo-charge super balance

## Strategy 3: Non-Concessional (After-Tax) Contributions

**For those who have maximised concessional contributions and want to put more into super.**

Non-concessional contributions are made from after-tax income. They are NOT tax-deductible going in, but the earnings on these contributions are taxed at only 15% (accumulation) or 0% (pension) — vs marginal rates outside super.

### Caps and Rules

| Rule | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Annual cap** | $120,000 per year |
| **Bring-forward rule** | If under 75, can contribute up to $360,000 in a single year (uses 3 years of cap) |
| **Total super balance test** | Cannot make non-concessional contributions if total super balance exceeds $1.9M |
| **Bring-forward balance test** | Reduced bring-forward amount if balance is between $1.68M and $1.9M |

### When This Makes Sense

- You've maxed out concessional contributions ($30,000) but have more to invest
- You have a lump sum (inheritance, property sale, savings) that you want to grow tax-efficiently
- Your super balance is well below $1.9M and you want to build it faster
- Combined with the re-contribution strategy (see below) for estate planning

## Strategy 4: Government Co-Contribution

**Free money for low-to-middle income earners.**

If your total income is below $58,445 (2024-25), the government will match your after-tax super contributions at 50 cents per dollar, up to a maximum of $500.

| Income | Government Match Rate | Max Co-Contribution |
|--------|----------------------|---------------------|
| Up to $43,445 | 50c per $1 (on first $1,000) | $500 |
| $43,446-$58,445 | Reduced proportionally | Reducing to $0 |
| Over $58,445 | 0 | $0 |

**Action**: If your income is below $58,445, contribute at least $1,000 in after-tax contributions to receive the full $500 government match. This is a **50% guaranteed return** — no investment can match this.

## Strategy 5: Spouse Contribution Tax Offset

**For couples where one partner earns significantly less.**

Contribute $3,000 to a spouse's super (where the spouse earns less than $40,000) and receive a tax offset of up to $540.

| Spouse's Income | Tax Offset |
|----------------|------------|
| Up to $37,000 | $540 (maximum) |
| $37,001-$40,000 | Reduces proportionally |
| Over $40,000 | $0 |

This is a modest benefit but combines well with other strategies (contribution splitting, government co-contribution for the low-earning spouse).

## Strategy 6: Contribution Splitting

**For equalising super balances between spouses.**

Split up to 85% of your concessional contributions (employer SG + salary sacrifice) to your spouse's super account.

### Benefits

- **Equalise balances**: Both spouses can access up to $1.9M each in pension phase = $3.8M total at 0% tax (vs $1.9M if one spouse has all the super)
- **Earlier access**: If one spouse is closer to preservation age, splitting can provide earlier access to super income
- **Protect from creditors**: In some circumstances, splitting super to a non-business-owner spouse provides asset protection

### How It Works

- Apply to your super fund after the financial year (to split contributions made in the previous year)
- Can split up to 85% of concessional contributions or $30,000 x 85% = $25,500 (whichever is lower)
- The split amount is transferred to the spouse's super account
- No additional tax is payable on the split

## Strategy 7: Personal Deductible Contributions

**For self-employed or those wanting flexibility.**

Since 1 July 2017, anyone (not just the self-employed) can make personal contributions to super and claim a tax deduction, provided they notify their fund with a "Notice of Intent to Claim."

- Same $30,000 concessional cap applies (combined with employer SG and salary sacrifice)
- Useful if your employer doesn't offer salary sacrifice, or you receive irregular income (freelance, contract work, investment income)
- The contribution is made from your bank account to your super fund, then you claim the deduction on your tax return
- **Must lodge the Notice of Intent before lodging your tax return or rolling over the super**

## Strategy 8: Downsizer Contributions

**For those aged 55+ selling the family home.**

If you sell a home you've owned for 10+ years, you can contribute up to $300,000 per person ($600,000 for a couple) into super — regardless of your age, total super balance, or contribution caps.

### Key Features

- **No age limit**: Can contribute even after age 75
- **No total super balance test**: Can contribute even if balance exceeds $1.9M
- **No work test**: Can contribute even if not working
- **Not counted against concessional or non-concessional caps**
- **Must be from the sale proceeds of a qualifying dwelling** (your home or former home, owned 10+ years)

### Example

A couple, both aged 65, sell their family home for $1.5M (purchased 25 years ago for $300,000). They can each contribute $300,000 ($600,000 total) into super. If they move this to pension phase: $600,000 generating 5% = $30,000/year in tax-free income, indefinitely.

### Caveats

- The contribution **counts toward the transfer balance cap** ($1.9M) if moved to pension phase
- The contribution may affect Age Pension eligibility (super assets are means-tested)
- Only one downsizer contribution can be made per person (ever)

## Strategy 9: First Home Super Saver Scheme (FHSS)

**Using super to save for a first home with a tax advantage.**

Voluntarily contribute up to $15,000 per year ($50,000 total) into super, then withdraw the contributions (plus deemed earnings) for a first home purchase.

### How It Works

1. Make voluntary contributions (salary sacrifice or personal deductible) — taxed at 15% instead of marginal rate
2. When ready to buy: apply to the ATO for a FHSS determination
3. ATO releases contributions + deemed earnings (based on SIC rate, ~4-5%)
4. Withdrawn amount is taxed at marginal rate **minus a 30% offset** — effective rate well below normal marginal rate
5. Must sign a contract to purchase or build within 12 months (extensions available)

### Tax Advantage Example

Investor in the 30% bracket contributes $50,000 over 3+ years:
- **Without FHSS**: $50,000 earned, taxed at 30% = $35,000 saved
- **With FHSS**: $50,000 contributed to super at 15% = $42,500 in super. On withdrawal: taxed at marginal rate minus 30% offset (effective ~4.5% at 30% bracket) = approximately $40,500 received
- **Benefit**: approximately $5,500 more for the deposit

## Strategy 10: Re-Contribution Strategy

**Advanced estate planning technique to minimise tax for beneficiaries.**

When a member dies, the taxable component of their super (most concessional contributions and earnings) may be taxed at up to 15% + 2% Medicare when paid to non-tax-dependant beneficiaries (typically adult children). The re-contribution strategy converts taxable components to tax-free components.

### How It Works

1. Member (aged 60+, met a condition of release) withdraws a lump sum from super — this is **tax-free** to the member
2. The member immediately re-contributes the amount as a **non-concessional** contribution — which becomes **100% tax-free component**
3. The fund's composition shifts from taxable to tax-free
4. On the member's death, the beneficiary receives more of the balance tax-free

### Example

- Member has $800,000 in super: $600,000 taxable, $200,000 tax-free
- Without strategy: adult child inherits and pays ~$600,000 x 17% = $102,000 in tax
- Member withdraws $360,000 (bring-forward non-concessional cap) and re-contributes: now $240,000 taxable, $560,000 tax-free
- Adult child inherits: $240,000 x 17% = $40,800 in tax — **saving $61,200**

### Constraints

- Must be aged 60+ and have met a condition of release
- Subject to non-concessional contribution caps ($120,000/year or $360,000 bring-forward)
- Total super balance must be under $1.9M to make non-concessional contributions
- Must consider the impact on transfer balance cap and pension income

## Division 293 Tax

An additional 15% tax on concessional contributions for high-income earners:

- Applies when **income + concessional super contributions exceed $250,000**
- Effective tax rate on concessional contributions becomes **30%** (15% + 15%)
- Still lower than the top marginal rate of 45% + 2% Medicare = 47%
- **Net saving**: even with Division 293, salary sacrifice saves 17 cents per dollar for top-bracket earners
- The ATO issues a Division 293 assessment automatically — you can pay from your personal funds or have the tax deducted from your super balance

## Transfer Balance Cap

The maximum amount that can be transferred from accumulation phase to pension phase:

- **Current cap**: $1.9 million per person (indexed, was $1.6M when introduced in 2017)
- **Per couple**: $3.8 million (each person has their own cap)
- Amounts above the cap must remain in accumulation phase (taxed at 15%)
- Exceeding the cap results in excess transfer balance tax (15% on first breach, 30% on subsequent)
- Contribution splitting between spouses helps both reach the cap, maximising the total amount in the 0% pension phase

## Contribution Strategy by Life Stage

| Life Stage | Priority Strategies | Rationale |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------|
| **20s-30s** | Salary sacrifice (modest), government co-contribution, FHSS | Long compounding runway; FHSS for first home |
| **30s-40s** | Salary sacrifice (aggressive), spousal contributions | Peak earning years; maximise tax arbitrage |
| **40s-50s** | Salary sacrifice (max), catch-up contributions, contribution splitting | Approaching retirement; accelerate accumulation |
| **50s-60s** | Catch-up contributions, downsizer, non-concessional bring-forward | Final push; downsizer if selling home |
| **60s+** | Re-contribution strategy, pension commencement | Estate planning; commence tax-free pension income |

## Strategy Selection Framework

A rough priority order when deciding where the next available dollar should go. (Order is a heuristic, not advice — individual circumstances dominate.)

1. **Is income below the co-contribution threshold?** If yes, contribute enough after-tax to capture the full [[#Strategy 4: Government Co-Contribution|government co-contribution]] first — it is a guaranteed return no investment matches.
2. **Is marginal rate ≥ 30%?** If yes, [[#Strategy 1: Salary Sacrifice|salary sacrifice]] / personal deductible contributions up to the concessional cap deliver the largest tax arbitrage per dollar.
3. **Unused cap from prior years and balance under the threshold?** Use [[#Strategy 2: Catch-Up (Carry-Forward) Contributions|carry-forward]] to absorb a windfall, bonus, or capital gain in a high-income year.
4. **Concessional cap exhausted but more to invest?** Move to [[#Strategy 3: Non-Concessional (After-Tax) Contributions|non-concessional]] contributions (or bring-forward) to shelter future earnings at 15%/0%.
5. **Couple with unequal balances?** Layer [[#Strategy 6: Contribution Splitting|contribution splitting]] and [[#Strategy 5: Spouse Contribution Tax Offset|spouse contributions]] to equalise and double the pension-phase capacity.
6. **Selling the family home at 55+?** Consider a [[#Strategy 8: Downsizer Contributions|downsizer contribution]] — it sits outside the standard caps.
7. **Aged 60+ with estate-planning goals?** Apply the [[#Strategy 10: Re-Contribution Strategy|re-contribution strategy]] to convert taxable to tax-free components for beneficiaries.

The unifying logic: capture free money first, then the highest-rate tax arbitrage, then shelter future earnings, then optimise for estate and couple-level outcomes.

## Common Pitfalls and Failure Modes

| Pitfall | Consequence | Mitigation |
|---------|-------------|------------|
| **Exceeding the concessional cap** | Excess taxed at marginal rate plus an interest charge — the benefit is reversed | Track employer SG + salary sacrifice + personal deductible together; leave headroom |
| **Exceeding the non-concessional cap** | Excess and associated earnings face penalty tax | Confirm total super balance test and bring-forward status before large contributions |
| **Missing the Notice of Intent deadline** | Personal contribution loses its deduction entirely | Lodge the Notice of Intent before lodging the tax return or rolling over |
| **Locking away money you need** | Funds inaccessible until preservation age and a condition of release | Match contribution aggression to your liquidity needs (see [[risk-management]]) |
| **Salary sacrificing at a low marginal rate** | Minimal tax saving while sacrificing liquidity | Reserve aggressive sacrifice for the 30%+ brackets |
| **Ignoring [[#Division 293 Tax]]** | High earners pay 30% on concessional contributions, not 15% | Still usually worthwhile, but recompute the net benefit |
| **Breaching the transfer balance cap** | Excess transfer balance tax; amount stuck in 15%-taxed accumulation | Use [[#Strategy 6: Contribution Splitting|splitting]] to spread balances across both partners |
| **Assuming current caps are static** | Acting on outdated thresholds | Verify each year's figures with the ATO |

## Interaction with the SMSF Structure

These strategies are structure-agnostic — they work in industry, retail, or self-managed funds — but several reach full potency only inside an [[smsf]]:

- **Franking credit refunds** (franking-credits) flow directly to an SMSF in pension phase, amplifying the value of contributions invested in fully franked Australian shares.
- **Contribution timing and asset selection** are under the trustee's direct control, allowing precise [[tax-efficient-investing|tax-efficient]] sequencing of contributions, dividends, and [[capital-gains-tax-discount|capital gains]].
- **Re-contribution and estate planning** integrate with the SMSF's binding death benefit nominations.

See [[smsf]] for the structural mechanics, trustee duties, and compliance obligations that govern how these contribution strategies are executed in a self-managed fund.
