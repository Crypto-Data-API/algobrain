Tax-efficient investing is the practice of structuring your investments to legally minimise the tax paid on returns, thereby maximising after-tax [[compounding]]. In Australia, the combination of the [[capital-gains-tax-discount]], [[franking-credits|dividend imputation]], concessional [[superannuation]] tax rates, and the absence of a wash sale rule creates one of the richest sets of legal tax optimisation strategies available to individual investors anywhere in the developed world. The key insight is that **it is not what you earn that matters, but what you keep** — a 10% pre-tax return at a 45% marginal rate produces a 5.5% after-tax return, while the same return in an [[smsf]] pension phase produces the full 10%.

> **Not tax or financial advice.** This page is general educational information about how Australian tax structures interact with investing. Tax rates, contribution caps, transfer balance caps, and bracket thresholds change frequently and depend on your personal circumstances. Dollar figures below are indicative and may be out of date — confirm current rates, caps, and thresholds with the [ATO](https://www.ato.gov.au) and a registered tax agent or financial adviser before acting. See [[australian-investor-tax]] for the underlying rules.

## The After-Tax Lens

The single mental model that unifies every strategy below is the **after-tax return**:

`after-tax return = pre-tax return × (1 − effective tax rate on that return)`

Two returns with identical headline numbers can leave wildly different amounts in your pocket once the *character* of the income (capital gain vs franked dividend vs interest), the *structure* holding it (personal, super, trust, company), and the *holding period* are accounted for. The four levers of tax-efficient investing map directly onto this equation:

| Lever | What it changes | Primary mechanism |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Character** | The type of income | Capital gain (discountable) beats interest (fully taxed); franked dividend carries a credit |
| **Structure** | The effective rate | Super (concessional) and pension phase (often nil) beat top marginal rate |
| **Timing** | Which year/rate the income lands in | Holding period unlocks the [[capital-gains-tax-discount]]; spreading sales smooths brackets |
| **Offset** | The taxable base | [[tax-loss-harvesting-australia|Harvested losses]] and deductions reduce assessable income |

Every numbered strategy that follows pulls one or more of these levers.

## Overview

Tax-efficient investing is not about aggressive tax avoidance or complex structures. It is about making informed decisions about:

1. **Where** to hold assets (personal name, super, trust, company)
2. **What** to hold in each structure (growth vs income, franked vs unfranked)
3. **When** to buy and sell (holding periods, financial year timing)
4. **How** to manage losses (tax-loss harvesting, loss carry-forward)

Each strategy below is legally sanctioned by the ATO and widely used by Australian investors and their advisers.

## The 12 Core Strategies

### 1. Hold Assets for More Than 12 Months

**Impact: Very High**

The [[capital-gains-tax-discount]] is the simplest and most powerful tax strategy available. Holding a CGT asset for more than 12 months before selling halves the assessable gain.

| Tax Bracket | Effective Rate (Short-Term) | Effective Rate (Long-Term, 50% Discount) | Tax Saved on $10,000 Gain |
|-------------|----------------------------|------------------------------------------|--------------------------|
| 30% | 30% | 15% | $1,500 |
| 37% | 37% | 18.5% | $1,850 |
| 45% | 45% | 22.5% | $2,250 |
| 47% (incl. Medicare) | 47% | 23.5% | $2,350 |

**Action**: Before selling any profitable position, check the acquisition date. If you are within weeks of the 12-month mark, the tax saving from waiting almost certainly exceeds any expected short-term decline.

### 2. Maximise Franking Credits

**Impact: High (especially for low-tax entities)**

Invest in companies paying fully franked [[dividend|dividends]] — the [[franking-credits]] provide a tax credit representing corporate tax already paid.

- At the 30% tax bracket: fully franked dividends are effectively **tax-free** (credit exactly offsets tax owed)
- At 15% ([[superannuation]]): you receive a **cash refund** for the 15% difference
- At 0% ([[smsf]] pension phase, low-income retirees): you receive the **full 30% credit as a cash refund**

**Key stocks for franking credit investors**: CBA, WBC, NAB, ANZ, Telstra, Wesfarmers, Woolworths, BHP — the blue-chip, fully franked dividend payers that form the backbone of Australian income portfolios.

**Note**: The 45-day holding rule requires you to hold shares "at risk" for at least 45 days to claim the franking credit (if total credits exceed $5,000/year). See [[franking-credits#The 45-Day Holding Rule]].

### 3. Salary Sacrifice into Super

**Impact: Very High for high-income earners**

Redirect pre-tax salary into [[superannuation]], where it is taxed at 15% (vs up to 45% + 2% Medicare outside super).

- Maximum concessional contributions: **$30,000/year** (includes employer SG)
- At the 45% bracket: sacrificing $20,000 saves $20,000 x (47% - 15%) = **$6,400 per year**
- Money compounds at 15% tax on earnings (vs up to 47% outside super)
- See [[super-contribution-strategies]] for catch-up contributions, non-concessional strategies, and other advanced techniques

**Trade-off**: Money is locked until preservation age (60). Not suitable for short-term goals.

### 4. Tax-Loss Harvest Before 30 June

**Impact: Medium to High (depends on portfolio)**

Sell losing positions before the end of the financial year to crystallise capital losses. See [[tax-loss-harvesting-australia]] for detailed methodology.

- Losses offset gains in the current financial year
- **No wash sale rule in Australia** — can repurchase the same asset within days
- Excess losses carry forward indefinitely
- Particularly effective for volatile assets like [[cryptocurrency-tax-australia|cryptocurrency]]

### 5. Use an SMSF for Long-Term Wealth

**Impact: Very High (for eligible investors)**

An [[smsf]] provides the ultimate tax-efficient structure for investors with sufficient balance ($200K+):

| Phase | Tax on Earnings | Tax on Capital Gains (>12 months) | Tax on Franking Credits |
|-------|-----------------|-----------------------------------|------------------------|
| Accumulation | 15% | 10% | Refund of excess credits |
| Pension | **0%** | **0%** | **Full 30% cash refund** |

A $1 million SMSF portfolio in pension phase yielding 5% fully franked generates ~$71,400 in total income (dividends + franking refunds), all tax-free.

### 6. Margin Loan Interest Deductions (Negative Gearing for Shares)

**Impact: Medium**

Interest on money borrowed to invest is tax-deductible. If total investment expenses (margin interest) exceed investment income, you have a [[negative-gearing|negatively geared]] share portfolio:

- The net loss reduces your taxable income from other sources
- Combined with eventual [[capital-gains-tax-discount|CGT discount]] on gains, this creates an asymmetric tax benefit
- **Risk**: [[margin]] loans carry margin call risk — forced selling at the worst time. See [[leverage]] and [[risk-management]]

### 7. ETF vs LIC Structure Selection

**Impact: Medium (for large portfolios)**

The tax efficiency of your investment wrapper matters:

**ETFs** (Exchange Traded Funds):
- Pass through CGT events to holders via AMIT (Attribution Managed Investment Trust) tax statements
- If the ETF rebalances or has net inflows/outflows, unitholders may receive capital gains distributions — even if they haven't sold
- This can create "phantom" tax events — receiving a tax bill without having sold anything

**LICs** (Listed Investment Companies):
- Retain and manage CGT internally at the company level (30% tax rate)
- Distribute dividends (potentially franked) rather than passing through CGT events
- No phantom tax events for shareholders
- But: no [[capital-gains-tax-discount]] at the company level (companies don't get the 50% discount)

**Strategy**: For buy-and-hold investors in high tax brackets, LICs may be more tax-efficient than ETFs due to avoiding phantom CGT distributions. For accumulation-phase [[superannuation]], ETFs are generally fine (15% tax rate is low regardless).

### 8. Mortgage Offset Account Strategy

**Impact: Medium**

If you have a mortgage (owner-occupied), use an offset account instead of a savings account or term deposit:

- **Savings account interest**: Taxable at marginal rate. Earn 5% on $50,000 = $2,500 income = ~$925 tax (at 37%)
- **Mortgage offset**: Reduce mortgage interest by the same $2,500 = $2,500 saving, **completely tax-free**

The after-tax benefit of the offset is always higher than the after-tax interest on a savings account (assuming the same rate), because saving interest is tax-free while earning interest is taxable.

**Exception**: If you have an investment property mortgage, the interest is already tax-deductible — so offsetting an investment loan is less advantageous than offsetting an owner-occupied loan.

### 9. Family Trust Distributions

**Impact: High (for families with income disparity)**

A family (discretionary) trust can receive investment income and distribute it to beneficiaries in lower tax brackets:

- Partner on parental leave or part-time work: distribute investment income to them (taxed at lower marginal rate)
- Adult children: distribute up to $18,200 tax-free (if they have no other income)
- **Complexity**: Trusts require setup ($1,500-$3,000), annual accounting ($1,000-$2,000), and compliance with trust law
- **ATO scrutiny**: Section 100A anti-avoidance provisions prevent distributing income to beneficiaries who do not actually receive the economic benefit
- **Professional advice essential** — trust structures are complex and the tax law is evolving

### 10. Timing of Asset Sales

**Impact: Medium**

- **Sell in years with lower income**: If you know your income will be lower next financial year (e.g., taking leave, reducing hours, retiring), defer asset sales to the lower-income year
- **Spread sales across financial years**: If you have a large gain to realise, sell half before 30 June and half after 1 July to split the gain across two tax years, potentially keeping more of the gain in lower brackets
- **Avoid pushing into a higher bracket**: Be aware of the [[stage-3-tax-cuts]] bracket thresholds when timing sales

### 11. Charitable Donations

**Impact: Low to Medium**

Donations of $2 or more to Deductible Gift Recipient (DGR) registered charities are tax-deductible.

- At the 45% bracket: a $1,000 donation costs you $550 after the tax deduction
- You can also donate listed shares directly to a DGR — this avoids triggering a CGT event on the shares (no gain is taxable, and the charity receives the full market value)

### 12. Super Contribution Splitting

**Impact: Medium (for couples)**

Split up to 85% of concessional contributions with a lower-earning spouse. See [[super-contribution-strategies]] for details.

- Evens out super balances between spouses
- Allows the lower-balance spouse to reach preservation age and access tax-free income sooner
- Maximises the household's use of the $1.9M transfer balance cap per person ($3.8M for a couple)

## Tax-Efficient Portfolio Ordering

The concept of "asset location" — holding the right assets in the right structure — is as important as asset allocation:

| Asset Type | Most Tax-Efficient Location | Reason |
|------------|---------------------------|--------|
| **Growth assets (capital gains)** | Personal name or family trust | [[capital-gains-tax-discount|50% CGT discount]] available |
| **Fully franked dividends** | [[smsf]] pension phase, personal (low bracket) | Cash refund of [[franking-credits]] in pension; effective rate ≤30% for all |
| **Unfranked dividends** | [[superannuation]] (accumulation) | 15% tax vs up to 47% personally |
| **Interest-bearing assets** | [[superannuation]] or mortgage offset | 15% tax on interest in super vs 47% personally; offset is tax-free |
| **International shares (growth)** | Personal name or super | 50% discount personally; 33.33% in super; no franking benefit anyway |
| **Crypto (long-term hold)** | Personal name | 50% discount applies; can be held in SMSF but more complex |
| **Cash** | Mortgage offset account | Tax-free savings via reduced interest vs taxable interest income |

## Combining Strategies: A Practical Example

**Investor profile**: $150,000 salary, 37% bracket, $80K in a share portfolio (personal), $400K in super, home mortgage of $500K.

**Before optimisation**:
- Earns $6,000 in unfranked dividends (taxed at 37% = $2,220 tax)
- Earns $2,000 in bank interest (taxed at 37% = $740 tax)
- Realises $15,000 in short-term capital gains (taxed at 37% = $5,550 tax)
- Total investment tax: **$8,510**

**After optimisation**:
1. **Salary sacrifice $10,000 into super** — saves $10,000 x (37% + 2% Medicare - 15%) = $2,400
2. **Move cash from savings to mortgage offset** — saves $740 (the interest tax is eliminated)
3. **Hold shares >12 months** — $15,000 gain discounted to $7,500, taxed at 37% = $2,775 (saving $2,775)
4. **Sell losing positions worth $5,000 before 30 June** — offsets $5,000 of remaining gains, saving $5,000 x 18.5% (discount rate) = $925
5. **Switch from unfranked to fully franked dividend stocks** — $6,000 fully franked with $2,571 franking credit: tax on $8,571 at 37% = $3,171, minus $2,571 credit = $600 (vs $2,220 previously, saving $1,620)

**Total tax after optimisation**: ~$3,100 — saving approximately **$5,400 per year**. Over 20 years compounded at 8%, this tax saving alone grows to approximately $265,000.

*(All figures above are indicative and use illustrative bracket rates. Confirm current rates and caps with the ATO before applying.)*

## End-of-Financial-Year (EOFY) Checklist

The Australian financial year ends **30 June**. Most timing-dependent strategies must be executed before that date. A disciplined EOFY review (ideally in May, with a buffer before the 30 June settlement cut-off) typically runs through:

- [ ] **Review unrealised losses** — identify positions sitting below cost base that could be harvested (see [[tax-loss-harvesting-australia]]).
- [ ] **Review realised gains year-to-date** — quantify the gains your losses can offset; apply losses *before* the [[capital-gains-tax-discount]].
- [ ] **Check holding periods** — for any planned sale, confirm whether waiting past the 12-month mark unlocks the 50% discount.
- [ ] **Top up concessional contributions** — ensure salary sacrifice / personal deductible contributions are *received by the fund* before 30 June (allow processing time; do not rely on a 29 June transfer).
- [ ] **Consider carry-forward (catch-up) concessional caps** — if your super balance is under the threshold and you have unused cap from prior years (see [[super-contribution-strategies]]).
- [ ] **Verify franking eligibility** — confirm the 45-day at-risk holding rule is met for dividends where total credits exceed the small-shareholder threshold (see [[franking-credits]]).
- [ ] **Reconcile DRP and corporate-action records** — reinvested dividends are still taxable; corporate actions adjust cost base (see [[australian-investor-tax]]).
- [ ] **Plan large sales across the 30 June / 1 July boundary** — splitting a big gain across two years can keep more of it in lower brackets.
- [ ] **Make any planned DGR donations** — deductible in the year paid.

Settlement, not trade date, governs when a CGT event occurs for some assets — leave a buffer so trades settle within the financial year you intend.

## Sequencing the Strategies

Tax efficiency compounds when strategies are applied in the right order. A pragmatic priority stack for most investors:

1. **Eliminate the worst-taxed income first** — move cash to a mortgage offset (Strategy 8) or interest-bearing assets into super (asset location).
2. **Capture "free" rate reductions** — salary sacrifice up to the concessional cap (Strategy 3), which is almost always positive-EV for anyone above the lowest bracket.
3. **Default to the discount** — hold growth assets >12 months (Strategy 1) and choose franked income where equivalent (Strategy 2).
4. **Run the annual offset routine** — tax-loss harvest each EOFY (Strategy 4).
5. **Consider structural upgrades** — once the balance and complexity justify it, evaluate an [[smsf]] (Strategy 5) or family trust (Strategy 9) with professional advice.

## Asset Location vs Asset Allocation

A common conflation: *allocation* decides **how much** of each asset class you hold (the risk/return decision from [[portfolio-construction]] and [[portfolio-theory]]); *location* decides **which structure** holds each one (the tax decision). The two are independent — you can run an identical 60/40 allocation across two investors, and the one with better asset location keeps materially more after tax. The [[#Tax-Efficient Portfolio Ordering|location table]] above is the actionable summary.

## The Tax Tail Should Not Wag the Investment Dog

The most important caveat in tax-efficient investing: **never let a tax saving drive a bad investment decision.**

- Holding a deteriorating position purely to reach the 12-month mark can cost more in price decline than the discount saves.
- Concentrating a portfolio in a handful of fully franked banks for the credits sacrifices diversification (see [[risk-management]] and [[portfolio-construction]]).
- [[negative-gearing|Negative gearing]] only "works" if the eventual capital gain exceeds the cumulative after-tax holding cost — the deduction is a subsidy on a loss, not a profit.
- Super and trust structures lock up or complicate access to capital; liquidity and life goals come before the marginal tax point.

A tax saving is a *second-order* benefit applied to a *first-order* sound portfolio. Get the investment right first.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Ignoring holding periods**: Selling at 11 months instead of 13 months doubles the effective tax rate on gains
2. **Holding interest-bearing assets personally**: Bank interest taxed at up to 47% — far better in super (15%) or as mortgage offset (0%)
3. **Not salary sacrificing**: Even modest salary sacrifice provides significant tax savings for those above the 19% bracket
4. **Forgetting to tax-loss harvest**: Unrealised losses have no tax value — they must be crystallised before 30 June
5. **Ignoring franking**: Choosing unfranked dividend stocks when equivalent fully franked alternatives exist
6. **Over-trading**: Every short-term trade is taxed at the full marginal rate — the CGT discount rewards patience
7. **Not considering SMSF**: For balances above $200K-$500K, an [[smsf]] can provide dramatically lower tax on investment earnings
8. **Confusing pre-tax and after-tax returns**: A 10% return in a 47% bracket is only 5.3% after tax; a 7% return in super (15% tax) is 5.95% after tax — the lower pre-tax return wins after tax
