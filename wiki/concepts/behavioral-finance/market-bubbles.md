A market bubble occurs when asset prices rise far above any reasonable estimate of intrinsic value, driven by speculation, [[herding|herd behaviour]], easy credit, and a self-reinforcing narrative — followed by a sharp collapse when the marginal buyer disappears. Bubbles are among the most studied phenomena in [[behavioral-finance]] precisely because they recur with the same structure across centuries, instruments, and cultures despite the participants believing each time that "this time is different."

[[fred-mcnaught|Fred McNaught]]: "In every boom, a dominant theme emerges that provides scope for valuations to disengage with reality — and they are always false." (Source: [[fred-sam-session-2024-01-02]])

## Anatomy of a Bubble

The most influential model is economist Hyman Minsky's five-stage framework, popularised by Charles Kindleberger in *Manias, Panics, and Crashes*:

1. **Displacement** — an exogenous shock (a new technology, a policy change, a fall in interest rates) creates genuine new profit opportunities and shifts expectations. Examples: the internet (1995), securitised mortgages (2003), zero rates and stimulus (2020-2021).
2. **Boom** — prices rise on the back of real fundamentals. Credit expands. Early adopters earn outsized returns, validating the thesis.
3. **Euphoria** — the displacement narrative detaches from fundamentals. Valuation metrics are abandoned ("eyeballs," "TAM," "number go up"). New entrants buy purely because prices are rising. [[herding|Informational cascades]] and FOMO dominate.
4. **Profit-taking / distortion** — sophisticated insiders begin to sell quietly while the public is still buying. Leverage peaks. Warning signs (deteriorating breadth, failed IPOs, fraud revelations) are dismissed.
5. **Panic / revulsion** — a trigger removes the marginal buyer. Prices fall, forced selling from leveraged positions accelerates the decline, and the same herding dynamic that drove the boom now operates in reverse. See [[market-crashes]].

## Why Bubbles Form

- **Behavioural drivers** — [[herding]], the [[overconfidence-bias|overconfidence]] of new participants, extrapolation of recent returns into the future, and the "greater fool" belief that someone will buy at a higher price.
- **Structural / credit drivers** — cheap and abundant credit is present in nearly every major bubble. Leverage amplifies gains on the way up and forces liquidation on the way down. Minsky's "financial instability hypothesis" holds that stability itself breeds the risk-taking that ends stability.
- **Narrative** — bubbles require a compelling story that explains why old valuation rules no longer apply. The story is usually anchored in a real innovation, which is what makes it persuasive.
- **Limits to arbitrage** — rational traders who recognise the bubble often cannot short it profitably: prices can stay irrational longer than a short-seller can stay solvent (the "Keynes problem"), and shorting carries unlimited risk and borrow costs. This is why bubbles persist even when many participants privately know they are bubbles.

## Identifying a Bubble (Imperfectly)

There is no reliable real-time bubble detector — Eugene Fama disputes the term entirely, while Robert Shiller built a career arguing bubbles are identifiable via valuation extremes. Practical warning signs traders watch:

- Valuation multiples (CAPE, price-to-sales, price-to-book) at multi-standard-deviation extremes versus history
- Parabolic price action — accelerating gains on a log chart
- Surging retail participation, margin debt, IPO/SPAC volume, and credit issuance
- Narrative saturation in mainstream media and the abandonment of traditional metrics
- Deteriorating market breadth (fewer stocks driving index gains)
- Extreme bullish [[sentiment]] readings and collapsing [[volatility]] (complacency)

## Trading Relevance

- **Contrarian framing** — extreme bubble conditions are the setup [[contrarian-investing|contrarian]] strategies seek, but timing is the killer: identifying a bubble and timing its collapse are entirely different problems.
- **Riding vs fading** — trend-following and momentum profit from the boom and euphoria phases; the danger is overstaying. The asymmetry (slow grind up, violent crash down) means crash risk is poorly compensated near the top.
- **Position sizing and leverage discipline** — bubbles destroy capital primarily through leverage and concentration. Surviving a bubble's collapse is mostly a [[risk-management|risk-management]] problem, not a forecasting one.
- **Shorting hazards** — never short a parabola naively; borrow costs, squeezes (see [[short-squeeze]]), and "stay irrational longer than you stay solvent" dynamics mean defined-risk structures (puts) are usually safer than outright shorts.
