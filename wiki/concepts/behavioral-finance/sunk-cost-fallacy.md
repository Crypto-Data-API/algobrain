The sunk-cost fallacy is the tendency to keep investing time, money, or attention into a losing course of action *because* of what has already been spent, even when a clear-eyed look forward says to stop. In trading it is the engine behind the most common version of the question *"why do I keep holding a loser hoping to break even?"* — the past investment cannot be recovered, yet it irrationally dominates the decision about what to do next.

## The Core Idea

A **sunk cost** is any cost that has already been incurred and cannot be recovered regardless of future action. Rational decision-making says sunk costs should be *ignored*: the only thing that matters is the forward-looking expected value of your remaining choices. The fallacy is the failure to ignore them — letting "I have already put so much into this" override "is this the best use of my capital from here?"

The classic non-market illustration: having paid for a concert ticket, you drive through a blizzard to attend an event you no longer want to see, *because* you paid — even though the money is gone either way and your only real choice is "miserable drive" versus "comfortable evening at home."

## How It Shows Up in Trading

- **Holding losers to "get back to even."** The original entry price becomes a target you owe yourself, so you refuse to sell until the position recovers — see [[disposition-effect]] and [[anchoring-bias]].
- **Averaging down to justify the original thesis.** Adding to a losing position to lower the average price is often framed as conviction, but is frequently an attempt to validate the money already committed rather than a fresh assessment.
- **Refusing to honour a stop.** A pre-set [[stop-loss]] gets cancelled in the moment because exiting would "waste" the loss already taken — converting a planned small loss into an unplanned large one.
- **Sticking with a broken strategy or system** because of the months spent building and trading it, rather than because it still has an edge.
- **Riding a position down through your kill criteria** because selling now would mean admitting the time and analysis spent on it produced nothing.

The fallacy is amplified by [[confirmation-bias]] (seeking news that supports the original thesis) and [[overconfidence-bias]] (believing the recovery is more certain than it is).

## Why It Happens

The mechanism is closely tied to [[loss-aversion]] and [[prospect-theory]]. Selling a loser *crystallises* the loss — it turns an open, still-deniable paper loss into a realised, undeniable one. Because losses are felt roughly twice as intensely as equivalent gains, the mind prefers to gamble on recovery (risk-seeking in the loss domain) rather than accept the certain pain of booking the loss now. Closing the position also feels like admitting the original decision — and the resources spent on it — were a mistake, which is psychologically costly. Economists call the broader pattern **escalation of commitment**: the more we have already invested, the harder it becomes to walk away.

## A Hypothetical Example

*This example is illustrative, not a real event.* A trader, "Riley," buys a stock at $100 with a plan to exit at $90 if the thesis breaks. The stock falls to $88 on news that genuinely weakens the thesis. Instead of taking the planned $10 loss, Riley reasons "I have already lost $12 on paper and done all this research — I can't sell now." Riley holds, then adds more at $80 to "average down," doubling the exposure. The stock drifts to $65. The original $12 paper loss — money that was already gone the moment the thesis broke — has anchored Riley into a far larger, doubled-up loss. The correct question at $88 was never "how do I recover what I have lost?" but "would I open this position today, at this size, knowing what I now know?"

## How to Counter It

- **Decide on forward expected value only.** Ask: *"If I had no position and none of this history, would I buy this here?"* If the answer is no, the only reason you are holding is the sunk cost.
- **Pre-commit to a [[stop-loss]] and honour it mechanically.** The exit is decided before the emotion and the sunk cost exist; automation removes the in-the-moment override.
- **Separate the decision from the ego.** A closed losing trade is a business expense and a data point, not a verdict on you. Booking it frees capital and attention for better opportunities.
- **Define kill criteria in advance** (see [[risk-management]]) and treat hitting them as a non-negotiable exit, not a debate.
- **Evaluate at the portfolio level.** Capital tied up in a loser has an opportunity cost; the relevant comparison is "this position" versus "the best alternative use of the same capital."
- **Keep a [[trading-journal]].** Reviewing how break-even-hope holds actually resolved builds evidence that the sunk-cost instinct is expensive.
