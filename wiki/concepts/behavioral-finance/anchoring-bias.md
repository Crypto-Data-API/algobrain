Anchoring bias is the cognitive tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information encountered (the "anchor") when making subsequent judgments and decisions. Identified by [[daniel-kahneman|Kahneman]] and Tversky (1974), anchoring is one of the most robust and experimentally replicated findings in cognitive psychology, and it systematically distorts trading decisions around price levels, valuations, and expectations.

## How Anchoring Works

When people estimate an unknown quantity, they start from an available reference point (the anchor) and adjust from it — but the adjustment is typically insufficient. This occurs even when the anchor is obviously arbitrary. In Kahneman and Tversky's classic experiment, subjects who spun a rigged wheel landing on 65 estimated that 45% of African nations belonged to the UN, while those who saw 10 estimated only 25%. The random number influenced the "rational" estimate.

In markets, anchors are rarely random — they are emotionally or cognitively salient price levels that traders fixate on.

## Common Trading Anchors

### Purchase Price

The most damaging anchor for individual traders. Once a position is opened at a specific price, that price becomes the reference point for all subsequent decisions:
- Refusing to sell below the purchase price ("I'll wait until I'm at least break-even")
- Setting profit targets relative to entry rather than relative to current market conditions
- This directly drives the [[disposition-effect]] — the entry price anchors the definition of "winning" and "losing"

### 52-Week High/Low

Traders and investors anchor to round-number reference points:
- Stocks near their 52-week high are perceived as "expensive" even if fundamentals justify higher prices
- Stocks near their 52-week low are perceived as "cheap" even if they're declining for fundamental reasons (see [[value-trap]])
- This creates measurable market effects: the 52-week high momentum strategy exploits the tendency of stocks to under-react when approaching the 52-week high (George & Hwang, 2004)

### Analyst Price Targets

Published price targets serve as anchors for both retail and institutional traders:
- When an analyst sets a target of $100, traders anchor to that number even if the analysis is outdated or the analyst's track record is poor
- Consensus price targets can create self-fulfilling support/resistance levels because many participants anchor to them simultaneously

### Previous Support/Resistance Levels

Technical traders anchor to prior price levels:
- A stock that previously bounced at $50 creates an anchor, leading traders to expect it will bounce there again
- While technical support/resistance has empirical validity in many cases, anchoring causes traders to overweight these levels and ignore when the context has changed

### Round Numbers

Psychological anchoring to round numbers ($10, $50, $100, $1000) creates measurable effects:
- Options open interest clusters at round-number strikes
- Stop-loss and take-profit orders cluster at round numbers
- [[order-flow]] is visibly different at round-number price levels

## Counter-Strategies

1. **Focus on current fundamentals** — when evaluating a position, ask "what is this worth today based on current information?" rather than "how far is it from where I bought it?"
2. **Forward-looking analysis** — evaluate expected return from the current price, not from the entry price. Every day is a new decision: "would I buy this position today?"
3. **Multiple reference points** — deliberately consider multiple valuation frameworks and scenarios rather than fixating on a single number
4. **Process-based exits** — define exit criteria based on conditions (e.g., "sell if revenue growth drops below 10%") rather than price levels anchored to entry
5. **Base rate thinking** — what does the distribution of outcomes look like for similar situations? This provides a statistical anchor that is more informative than a single price point

## Interaction with Other Biases

- **[[confirmation-bias]]** — once anchored to a price or view, traders seek confirming information for why the anchor is "right"
- **[[disposition-effect]]** — directly caused by anchoring to purchase price
- **[[loss-aversion]]** — losses are calculated relative to the anchor (entry price), amplifying the reluctance to realize losses
- **[[overconfidence-bias]]** — overconfident traders anchor more strongly to their own estimates and price targets
