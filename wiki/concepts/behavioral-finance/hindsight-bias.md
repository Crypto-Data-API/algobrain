Hindsight bias is the tendency to perceive past events as having been more predictable than they actually were. After the 2008 crisis, everyone "knew" the housing bubble would burst. After Bitcoin hit $60K, everyone "knew" it was going to moon. Before these events, the same people had no such confidence. The bias is one of the primary mechanisms by which traders overestimate their skill, and it is a recurring theme in [[nassim-taleb]]'s *Fooled by Randomness* (Source: [[book-fooled-by-randomness]]).

## The Core Mechanism

Hindsight bias operates through memory distortion. Once an outcome is known, the brain retroactively adjusts its estimate of the prior probability — the outcome feels more inevitable than it was. Psychologists Baruch Fischhoff and Ruth Beyth first documented this experimentally in 1975: subjects who learned the outcome of an event retrospectively claimed they had assigned it higher probability before the event than they actually had.

In markets, this is pervasive. After a profitable trade, the entry signal feels "obvious." After a crash, the warning signs seem "clear." But at the time of the decision, the same signals were ambiguous and contested — one of many possible interpretations of incomplete information.

## Connection to Fooled by Randomness

Taleb argues that hindsight bias is one of the primary mechanisms by which traders overestimate their skill (Source: [[book-fooled-by-randomness]]). After a profitable trade, the logic feels obvious — "of course I should have bought there." But before the trade, the same setup was one of many possible interpretations. Hindsight bias converts ambiguous real-time decisions into "obvious" retrospective calls.

The effect compounds over a career. Each successful trade is remembered as a demonstration of skill (the reasoning was "clear"), and each failed trade is remembered as bad luck (the outcome was "unforeseeable"). Over years, this selective memory reconstruction creates an inflated self-assessment that has no basis in the actual quality of real-time decisions.

## Why It's Dangerous in Trading

### 1. Inflates Perceived Skill

"I knew that would happen" — no, you didn't, but your brain rewrites memory to make it seem that way. This inflated sense of skill leads to overconfidence, which leads to oversized positions, which leads to outsized losses when the next "obvious" trade fails.

### 2. Undermines Risk Management

If you "knew" the trade would work, why hedge? Why size conservatively? Hindsight bias encourages larger, less-hedged positions in future similar setups. The trader who "knew" the last three breakouts would work reduces position sizing discipline on the fourth — which is the one that reverses.

### 3. Corrupts Post-Trade Review

If the outcome was "obvious," there is nothing to learn. Honest post-trade review requires acknowledging the genuine uncertainty that existed at the time of the decision. Hindsight bias destroys this honesty by making every outcome feel predetermined, which eliminates the motivation to study what was genuinely uncertain and what signals actually had predictive value.

### 4. Creates False Pattern Confidence

Every chart pattern, indicator signal, and fundamental thesis looks brilliantly clear in hindsight. The [[head-and-shoulders|head and shoulders]] that preceded the crash was "textbook." The divergence between price and RSI was "screaming sell." In real-time, the same signals are ambiguous and contested — many identical patterns fail to produce the expected outcome. Hindsight bias selectively highlights the patterns that worked and renders the failures invisible.

## Taleb's Trifecta of Dangerous Biases

Hindsight bias forms a destructive triangle with two related biases that Taleb identifies as particularly dangerous for traders (Source: [[book-fooled-by-randomness]]):

1. **Hindsight bias** makes past events feel predictable ("I knew it would happen")
2. **[[narrative-fallacy]]** constructs post-hoc stories that make the outcome seem causally determined ("it happened because...")
3. **[[outcome-bias]]** confirms that the decision was correct because the result was good ("it worked, so I was right")

Together, these three biases create a self-reinforcing delusion: I knew it would happen (hindsight), here's why it happened (narrative), and I was right because it worked (outcome). Add [[survivorship-bias]] (we only see the traders for whom this worked), and the entire picture is false.

## Defenses

- **Keep a trading journal with pre-trade reasoning** written BEFORE the outcome is known. This creates an objective record that cannot be retroactively revised by memory.
- **Review decisions based on the information available at decision time**, not the outcome. Ask: "Given what I knew then, was this the best decision I could make?"
- **Ask the counterfactual test**: "Could I have reasonably predicted the opposite outcome?" If yes, the outcome was genuinely uncertain regardless of how obvious it feels now.
- **Study near-misses**: Deliberately review trades that almost worked but didn't, and trades that almost failed but succeeded. These reveal the genuine uncertainty that hindsight bias obscures.
- **Use base rates**: Before evaluating any individual trade, anchor on the base rate of success for that type of setup. If the base rate is 55%, your successful trade was likely within the normal distribution of outcomes, not proof of special insight.
