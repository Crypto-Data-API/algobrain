# Trading Psychology

Trading psychology encompasses the emotional and mental factors that influence a trader's decision-making, including fear, greed, discipline, confidence, and cognitive biases.

## Core Emotional Challenges

- **Fear** - Prevents entering valid setups, causes premature exits, or triggers panic selling
- **Greed** - Leads to oversized positions, holding too long, or chasing extended moves
- **Revenge Trading** - Trying to immediately recover losses, often leading to [[overtrading]]
- **FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)** - Entering trades impulsively because others are profiting
- **Overconfidence** - After a winning streak, taking excessive risk or abandoning the plan

## The Cognitive-Bias Substrate

The emotional challenges above are surface expressions of documented [[cognitive-biases]] that distort decision-making under uncertainty. Trading psychology is, in practice, the applied management of these biases:

- **[[loss-aversion]]** — losses register roughly twice as intensely as equivalent gains (Kahneman & Tversky), producing premature profit-taking and reluctance to cut losers.
- **[[disposition-effect]]** — the direct market consequence of loss aversion plus [[prospect-theory|reference dependence]]: holding losers, selling winners.
- **[[overconfidence]]** — overestimating one's edge and information, driving oversized positions and [[overtrading]].
- **Recency / availability bias** — over-weighting the last few trades or vivid recent events.
- **Confirmation bias** — seeking information that supports an open position and discounting contradicting evidence.

Because these are hardwired System-1 responses (Source: [[book-thinking-fast-and-slow]]), they cannot be willed away. The professional answer is *structural*: rules, pre-commitment, and process that constrain behaviour before the emotional moment arrives — the distinction at the heart of the [[professional-vs-retail-mindset]].

## Why It Matters

Most traders have access to similar strategies and information. The differentiator is often psychological -- the ability to execute a plan consistently under pressure (Source: [[book-trading-in-the-zone]]). Studies consistently show that emotional decision-making is the primary reason retail traders underperform. As Mark Douglas argues, trading success comes from thinking in probabilities and eliminating the emotional interference that causes traders to deviate from their systems (Source: [[book-trading-in-the-zone]]).

## Building Psychological Resilience

1. **Develop a trading plan** - Predefined rules remove emotion from decision points
2. **Keep a [[trading-journal]]** - Recording emotions alongside trades reveals behavioral patterns
3. **Accept losses** - Understanding [[loss-aversion]] helps normalize losing trades as a cost of doing business (Source: [[book-thinking-fast-and-slow]])
4. **Position sizing** - Trading small enough to think clearly and follow the plan
5. **Take breaks** - Stepping away after losses prevents compounding emotional mistakes

## Recommended Reading

Mark Douglas's *Trading in the Zone* and Brett Steenbarger's *The Psychology of Trading* are foundational texts on the subject. Jack Schwager's *Market Wizards* interviews reveal that the top traders universally cite discipline, emotional control, and the ability to cut losses as the key differentiators (Source: [[book-market-wizards]]). Edwin Lefevre's *Reminiscences of a Stock Operator*, based on [[jesse-livermore]]'s career, remains one of the most vivid depictions of the psychological battles traders face (Source: [[book-reminiscences-of-a-stock-operator]]).
