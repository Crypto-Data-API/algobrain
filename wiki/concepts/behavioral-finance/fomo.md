FOMO — the fear of missing out — is the anxiety that other people are profiting from a move you are not part of, which pushes you to buy (or add) after a price has already run, usually near a local top and usually without a plan. It is one of the most common questions traders ask in a different form: *"why did I buy right at the top?"* The short answer is that the decision to enter was driven by the emotional discomfort of watching others win, not by a forward-looking assessment of value or risk.

## How It Shows Up in Trading

FOMO is a *trigger*, not a strategy. It typically appears as:

- **Chasing green candles** — buying an asset specifically *because* it is already up sharply on the day, rather than despite it.
- **Abandoning your watchlist plan** — ignoring a pre-defined entry level and buying at a worse price because waiting feels intolerable while the price climbs.
- **Sizing up impulsively** — putting on a larger position than your rules allow because "this is the one" and you do not want to under-participate.
- **Social-feed driven entries** — buying after seeing screenshots of other people's gains, which is where FOMO overlaps with [[herding]] and fuels [[meme-stocks]].
- **Re-entering immediately after taking profit** — paying a higher price to get back into a name you just sold, because watching it keep rising is more painful than the original gain felt good (a [[loss-aversion]] flavour applied to *unrealised* opportunity).

FOMO is most dangerous in fast, parabolic, attention-driven moves — exactly the conditions where [[market-bubbles]] form and where the risk/reward of a late entry is at its worst.

## Why It Happens

FOMO sits on top of several deeper biases rather than being a single discrete one:

- **Loss framing of missed gains** — humans treat a missed gain as if it were a real loss. The unrealised opportunity is mentally booked as something you *had* and lost, which engages [[loss-aversion]].
- **Social proof and herding** — if many people are buying and talking about a move, the crowd's behaviour is read as information ("they must know something"). See [[herding]].
- **Recency and extrapolation** — a few days of sharp gains get extrapolated forward as if the trend must continue, which is [[recency-bias]] doing the driving.
- **Salience** — vivid, emotional stories of other people's wins are far more available to the mind than the quiet, invisible majority who lost, a cousin of [[availability-bias]].

## A Hypothetical Example

*This example is illustrative, not a real event.* Imagine a trader, "Jordan," who has a written plan to buy a stock only on a pullback to $40. The stock instead gaps up and runs from $42 to $55 over three sessions while their social feed fills with celebration. The unrealised "miss" feels like money taken from their pocket. Jordan abandons the $40 plan and buys a double-sized position at $54, near the local top. The stock mean-reverts to $46 over the next week. Jordan now holds a position that is both larger than the rules allowed and bought at a worse price than the original plan — two separate FOMO-driven errors compounding.

## How to Counter It

- **Trade a written plan with pre-set entries.** If price is not at your level, there is no trade. A missed move is a non-event, not a loss.
- **Use a "would I buy this now with no history?" reframe.** If you had never been watching this asset, would today's price and risk/reward justify a new position at your normal size? If not, the only thing pulling you in is FOMO.
- **Pre-commit position size before the emotion arrives.** Size is decided by [[risk-management]] rules and [[position-sizing]], not by how exciting the move feels.
- **Accept that there will always be another trade.** Markets produce a steady supply of setups; missing one has no bearing on your long-run results.
- **Reduce the trigger.** Lower the frequency of checking price and social feeds during a parabolic move; salience and [[herding]] feed on constant exposure.
- **Keep a [[trading-journal]].** Logging FOMO entries and their outcomes builds a personal evidence base that chasing late tends to pay poorly.
