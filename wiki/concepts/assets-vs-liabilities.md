An **asset** is a resource controlled by an entity that is expected to produce future economic benefit; a **liability** is a present obligation that will require an outflow of resources to settle. The relationship between the two is governed by the fundamental accounting identity — **Assets = Liabilities + Equity** — which is the basis of the [[balance-sheet]] and one of the most-used starting points in [[fundamental-analysis]].

## The Accounting Identity

Every [[balance-sheet]] balances because equity is defined as the residual:

```
Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity
Shareholders' Equity (Book Value) = Assets − Liabilities
```

Equity (also called net worth or [[book-value]]) is therefore what owners would be left with if all assets were liquidated at their carrying value and all liabilities repaid. This residual framing is why analysts treat liabilities as senior claims that must be satisfied before equity holders see anything — a fact that drives the entire [[capital-structure|capital-structure]] hierarchy.

## Classifying Assets

Assets are ordered by liquidity:

- **Current assets** — convertible to cash within one year: cash and equivalents, marketable securities, accounts receivable, inventory.
- **Non-current (fixed) assets** — property, plant and equipment (PP&E), long-term investments.
- **Intangible assets** — goodwill, patents, brands, capitalized software. These carry the greatest valuation uncertainty and are the first thing skeptical analysts discount.

## Classifying Liabilities

- **Current liabilities** — due within one year: accounts payable, short-term debt, accrued expenses, the current portion of long-term debt.
- **Non-current liabilities** — long-term debt, deferred tax, pension and lease obligations.
- **Off-balance-sheet items** — operating leases (now largely capitalized under IFRS 16 / ASC 842), guarantees, and contingent liabilities that may not appear at face value.

## The Robert Kiyosaki Reframe

In personal-finance literature — popularized by Robert Kiyosaki's *Rich Dad Poor Dad* — "asset" and "liability" are redefined behaviourally: an asset puts money *into* your pocket (rental property, dividend stocks, a business) and a liability takes money *out* (a mortgaged home, a car loan, consumer debt). This is not the accounting definition, but it is a useful heuristic for thinking about cash-flow direction and is frequently invoked in trading-psychology and capital-allocation discussions. The accounting and cash-flow framings are complementary, not contradictory.

## Trading Relevance

For investors and traders, the asset/liability split feeds directly into the ratios that screen for financial strength and distress:

- **[[leverage]] / [[debt-to-equity]]** — total liabilities relative to equity flags balance-sheet risk; high leverage amplifies both returns and ruin probability.
- **Current ratio / quick ratio** — current assets over current liabilities measures short-term [[liquidity]] and solvency.
- **[[working-capital]]** — current assets minus current liabilities indicates operating runway.
- **[[book-value]] and net-asset value** — assets minus liabilities underpins value-investing screens (price-to-book) and is the backstop in deep-value and net-net strategies.

A company whose liabilities are growing faster than its assets, or whose asset base is dominated by intangibles and goodwill, is a classic short-side candidate. Conversely, asset-rich, low-liability balance sheets provide a margin of safety that supports long-term holding through drawdowns.
