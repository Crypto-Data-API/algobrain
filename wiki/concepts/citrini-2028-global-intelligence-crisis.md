The **2028 Global Intelligence Crisis** is a named tail-risk scenario published by citrini-research in February 2026 that posits a US unemployment rate above 10% and a roughly 40% equity-market decline if superintelligent AI deployment outpaces the labor market's ability to reabsorb displaced workers. It triggered the 2026-02-citrini-tech-selloff and has since become an institutional reference point for pricing AI-related tail risk (Source: [[2026-04-22-gap-finder-possible-ai-fueled-global-job-loss-reces]]).

## Overview

The scenario maps a specific deployment timeline (frontier AI models reaching broad enterprise penetration by 2027-2028) to two terminal market outcomes:

- **Unemployment**: 10%+ headline rate (more than double the 4.5-4.8% observed through 2026)
- **Equities**: ~40% peak-to-trough decline in major US indices
- **Sector pattern**: service sectors (finance, real estate, media, medical services, legal) decline alongside software, breaking the assumption that "traditional defensives" hedge tech-led drawdowns

It is a "named" scenario in the same sense that "the 2008 GFC" or "Long-Term Capital Management 1998" are named — institutional traders now reference it by shorthand when sizing tail hedges and discussing portfolio scenarios.

## Why it matters

Before the Citrini publication, AI-driven recession was discussed mostly as a vibes-based concern in financial media. The Citrini report did three things that gave it institutional traction:

1. Published a specific, falsifiable scenario with timing, magnitude, and sector mapping
2. Distributed it through channels read by allocators and prop desks
3. Coincided with — and arguably accelerated — a sharp tech selloff in early February 2026

The combination meant that "discount the Citrini scenario at probability X" became a real input to portfolio decisions, similar to how the [[ai-layoff-trap]] mathematical paper provides academic grounding.

## Connection to the AI Layoff Trap

The Citrini scenario is essentially the empirical/numerical realization of the [[ai-layoff-trap]] model. Falk and Tsoukalas provide the proof that an automation death spiral is a stable equilibrium under standard firm behavior; Citrini provides numbers (unemployment %, equity decline %, sector mapping, timing) that make the scenario tradeable.

Both rest on the same core mechanism:

- Capital captures productivity gains first ([[capital-vs-labor-asymmetry]])
- Labor losses cascade through the [[service-sector-multiplier]] over 2-4 quarters
- [[wage-compression-vs-job-loss]] hides part of the stress in headline rates
- Aggregate consumer demand collapses, triggering a second wave of automation

## February 2026 sell-off

The publication coincided with notable market action (Source: [[2026-04-22-gap-finder-possible-ai-fueled-global-job-loss-reces]]):

- Tech sector sold off sharply in early February 2026
- Service sectors (finance, real estate, media, medical services, legal) plummeted alongside software — pattern previously associated only with broad systemic shocks
- Energy +12% YTD as tech retreated; 25 percentage-point performance gap at peak (sector rotation hedge proven viable)
- Hedge funds reportedly hit by the AI sell-off (per Goldman Sachs analysis)
- Bitcoin: peaked at $126K in October 2025, fell below $65K, recovered to $70K+

## How traders use the scenario

- **Probability-weighted tail sizing**: assign a probability (often 5-15%) to the scenario and size hedges accordingly
- **Scenario stress test**: run portfolios through the Citrini sector-mapping (-40% equity, services hit alongside tech) to find concentration risk
- **Reference for option skew**: when SPX put skew steepens, traders attribute part of the move to "Citrini repricing"
- **Trigger watching**: emergence of further AI-attributed layoffs, [[anthropic]]-style productivity tools, or BLS revisions can move the implied probability

## Limits and critiques

- The scenario is not a forecast — Citrini explicitly framed it as one of several possible outcomes
- 2026 GDP growth (2.25-2.6%) and unemployment (4.5-4.8%) are inconsistent with the scenario being already underway, though the [[service-sector-multiplier]] lag accommodates this
- Critics note prior tech transitions did not produce 10%+ unemployment; bulls argue labor reabsorption will work again
- The "2028" timing is arbitrary — mechanism could trigger earlier or later, or partially
