"Stocks are a good inflation hedge" is one of the most repeated — and most misunderstood — claims in investing. This page answers the practical FAQ for an equity holder: **"is inflation good or bad for my stocks, and which kinds of companies hold up?"** The short answer: it depends on the *type*, *level*, and *speed* of inflation, and on the kind of stocks you own. (For the macro mechanics of inflation itself, see [[inflation]].)

## The Two Conflicting Forces

Inflation pushes stocks in opposite directions at once:

1. **Nominal earnings can rise with inflation (a tailwind).** Companies that can raise prices grow their revenue and earnings in nominal terms, so over the *long run* equities have tended to roughly keep pace with inflation — that is the kernel of truth in "stocks are an inflation hedge."
2. **The discount rate rises (a headwind).** High inflation forces the [[fed-policy|central bank]] to raise [[interest-rates|rates]], which lifts the discount rate (risk-free yield + [[equity-risk-premium]]) and compresses valuation multiples — see [[bond-yields-and-stock-prices]]. Inflation also tends to *widen* the equity risk premium because uncertainty rises.

Which force wins depends on the regime.

## The Goldilocks Zone vs the Danger Zone

| Inflation regime | Typical effect on stocks |
|------------------|--------------------------|
| **Low and stable (~2-3%)** | Best case — earnings grow, rates are contained, multiples healthy |
| **Rising / surprising upside** | Headwind — rate hikes lift the discount rate, multiples compress; long-duration growth hit hardest |
| **High (well above target)** | Generally bad — aggressive tightening, multiple compression, margin pressure |
| **[[stagflation|Stagflation]] (high inflation + weak growth)** | Worst case — earnings *and* multiples fall together (the 1970s playbook) |
| **Deflation (falling prices)** | Also bad — squeezes nominal revenue, raises real debt burdens |

The relationship is **non-linear**: moderate inflation is fine, but both high inflation and deflation are damaging. It is also driven by *surprises* — markets pre-price expected inflation, so an unexpected jump moves stocks more than a high-but-anticipated level.

## The Real-Return Distinction (What "Hedge" Actually Means)

A crucial nuance for investors: stocks can rise in **nominal** terms while losing ground in **real** (inflation-adjusted) terms. In the high-inflation 1970s, US equities were roughly flat to up in price but lost significant *purchasing power* over the decade. So "stocks hedge inflation" is a **long-horizon, real-return** statement, not a promise that prices rise *during* an inflation spike. Over multi-decade horizons equities have outpaced inflation; over a 1-3 year inflation shock they often do not. Watch [[real-interest-rates|real yields]], not just nominal ones.

## Pricing Power Is the Deciding Factor

The single best lens for "will *this* company survive inflation" is **pricing power** — the ability to pass higher costs on to customers without losing volume:

- **Winners:** firms with strong brands, dominant market positions, inelastic demand, low capital intensity, and short-cycle pricing. They protect margins by raising prices.
- **Losers:** firms in competitive, commoditised markets that *absorb* rising input costs, plus capital-intensive businesses facing soaring costs and higher financing rates.

Two companies with identical revenue can have opposite inflation outcomes purely on pricing power and margin structure.

## Sector and Style Winners / Losers

| Group | Tendency under rising inflation | Why |
|-------|-------------------------------|-----|
| **Energy, Materials, [[gold|Gold]] miners** | Often **benefit** | Sell the very things whose prices are rising (real assets) |
| **Value / cash-flow-now stocks** | Relatively resilient | Short [[duration]]; less hurt by rising discount rate |
| **High-growth / profitless tech** | Hurt most | Long-duration cash flows discounted harder (see interest-rate-sensitive-sectors) |
| **Consumer Staples with brands** | Mixed-defensive | Some pricing power, but margins can lag input costs |
| **Capital-intensive, debt-heavy firms** | Hurt | Rising input *and* financing costs |
| **Fixed-margin / regulated** | Hurt | Can't raise prices fast enough |

This largely mirrors the rate-sensitivity map, because the main channel through which inflation hits stocks is *higher rates*.

## Worked Illustration (Hypothetical)

Imagine an inflation surprise pushes the [[bond-yields-and-stock-prices|10-year yield]] up sharply. Two firms each grow nominal earnings 8% on higher prices:

- **Firm A** (strong brand, low debt) keeps its margins and its earnings genuinely rise; its main hit is multiple compression from the higher discount rate.
- **Firm B** (commodity producer of finished goods, heavily indebted) sees input costs rise faster than it can raise prices, so margins shrink *and* its multiple compresses *and* interest expense climbs — a triple hit.

Same headline inflation, very different outcomes. (Illustrative, not a forecast.)

## Direct Inflation Hedges (Beyond Stocks)

For investors specifically worried about purchasing power, common complements to equities include **TIPS** (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities, principal adjusts with CPI), **I-Bonds**, **commodities**, and **[[gold]]**. These are covered on the [[inflation]] page; the point here is that *equities alone* are an imperfect short-run hedge, so diversification matters.

## What to Watch as a Stock Investor

- **Inflation surprises** (CPI/PCE vs expectations), not just the level — markets price the expected path.
- **[[real-interest-rates|Real yields]]** — rising real rates are the cleanest valuation headwind.
- **The [[fed-policy|Fed's reaction]]** — tightening is the transmission belt from inflation to stock multiples.
- **Margin trends** — whether companies are holding or losing pricing power.
- **Your portfolio's duration mix** — a growth-heavy book is implicitly a bet on *falling* inflation and rates.

## Limitations and Cautions

- The "stocks hedge inflation" claim holds **long-run and in real terms**, not over short inflation shocks.
- Relationships are **regime-dependent**; moderate inflation and runaway inflation produce opposite reactions.
- Bond-stock correlation can turn **positive** in high-inflation regimes, eroding the usual diversification benefit of holding both.
- Pricing power can erode quietly — a brand that protected margins in one cycle may not in the next.
