# EC Merger Regulation

The **European Commission Merger Regulation (EUMR)** — formally Regulation (EC) No 139/2004 — vests the European Commission's **Directorate-General for Competition (DG-COMP)** with exclusive jurisdiction to review mergers with "Union dimension" — those exceeding specified turnover thresholds in the European Union. EUMR is the EU equivalent of [[hsr-review|HSR]] and one of the three primary regulatory hurdles for global M&A, alongside US ([[hsr-review|HSR]] / [[cfius|CFIUS]]) and Chinese (SAMR / MOFCOM) review.

## Jurisdictional Thresholds

A merger has "Union dimension" if **either**:

**Threshold 1:**
- Combined worldwide turnover of all parties >€5 billion, AND
- Aggregate EU-wide turnover of at least two parties >€250 million each.

**Threshold 2 (alternative):**
- Combined worldwide turnover >€2.5 billion, AND
- In each of at least three Member States: combined turnover >€100M and individual turnover of at least two parties >€25M each, AND
- EU-wide turnover of at least two parties >€100M each.

Below these thresholds, mergers fall to national competition authorities (Bundeskartellamt in Germany, AGCM in Italy, Autorité de la Concurrence in France, etc.). Above, exclusive Commission jurisdiction.

## Process

### Phase I (25 working days, extendable to 35)

DG-COMP reviews the notification. Outcomes:

1. **Cleared unconditionally** (~85-90% of cases).
2. **Cleared with Phase I commitments** (modest divestitures).
3. **Phase II opened** for in-depth investigation.

### Phase II (90 working days, extendable to 125)

For deals raising serious doubts:

1. **Cleared with Phase II commitments** — substantial divestitures or behavioral remedies.
2. **Cleared unconditionally** (rare in Phase II).
3. **Prohibited** (very rare — typically <0.5% of all filings).

### Implementation

Once cleared, parties may close. Commitments are typically embedded in the clearance decision and monitored by an independent trustee for 5-10 years.

## Outcomes by Frequency

Of ~300-400 EUMR notifications per year:

| Outcome | Approximate Frequency |
|---------|----------------------|
| Phase I clearance, no commitments | 88-92% |
| Phase I clearance, with commitments | 4-7% |
| Phase II opened | 1-3% |
| Phase II clearance | Most of those that proceed |
| Prohibited | 0-3 deals per year (very rare) |

## High-Profile EUMR Decisions

**Prohibitions (signal cases):**

| Year | Deal | Reasoning |
|------|------|-----------|
| 2001 | GE / Honeywell | Bundling concerns; cleared by US, blocked by EU — major transatlantic friction |
| 2007 | Ryanair / Aer Lingus | Air travel concentration |
| 2016 | Hutchison / O2 UK | Mobile market concentration |
| 2019 | Siemens / Alstom | Rail signaling and high-speed train concentration |
| 2021 | Illumina / GRAIL | Vertical foreclosure of cancer-screening tests |
| 2024 | Booking / eTraveli | Hotel booking platform concentration |

**Forced divestitures:**

| Year | Deal | Divestiture |
|------|------|-------------|
| 2014 | Liberty Global / Ziggo | Dutch broadcasting interests |
| 2018 | Bayer / Monsanto | $2.2B BASF crop-science assets |
| 2022 | Microsoft / Activision Blizzard | UK CMA blocked initially; ultimately approved with cloud-gaming concessions |

## Margrethe Vestager Era (2014-2024)

Danish Commissioner Margrethe Vestager's two-term tenure was the most active EU antitrust era in 25 years. Big-tech enforcement (Google triple-fines, Apple-Ireland tax, Amazon Marketplace data) drew most attention; merger control under her was selective but strict on key cases (Siemens-Alstom, Illumina-GRAIL).

Successor Teresa Ribera (since Dec 2024) signals continued strict posture.

## What Risk Arbs Watch

- **Notification filed** — start of clock.
- **Phase I commitment offers** — signal parties believe Phase II is risk.
- **Phase II opening** — major spread blowout.
- **Trustee structure** — implies divestiture is locked in.
- **State of Play meeting outcomes** — leak signal of remedies acceptability.

## Distinct from US Process

| Feature | EU EUMR | US HSR |
|---------|---------|--------|
| Jurisdiction trigger | Turnover-based | Transaction-size + size-of-person |
| Process | Centralized at Commission | Distributed (FTC + DOJ + state AGs) |
| Standard waiting period | 25 working days | 30 calendar days |
| In-depth investigation | Phase II (90+ days) | Second Request (no statutory cap) |
| Outcome venue | Commission decision; appeals to General Court | Federal court litigation |
| Timeline to outcome | 4-9 months typical | 6-24+ months if challenged |

## Strategic Implications for Arbs

EUMR Phase II openings move spreads as dramatically as US Second Requests. Phase II proceedings are generally more predictable than US litigation — DG-COMP usually telegraphs outcomes via the "State of Play" meetings 8-12 weeks before final decision.

Cross-jurisdiction discrepancies (deal cleared in US, blocked in EU) create "regulatory arbitrage of regulators" — see GE/Honeywell 2001 as the canonical case study.
