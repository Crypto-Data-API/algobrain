# CFIUS (Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States)

The **Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS)** is an interagency committee chaired by the U.S. Treasury that reviews **foreign investment in U.S. businesses for national security risks**. Modernized and expanded in 2018 by the **Foreign Investment Risk Review Modernization Act (FIRRMA)**, CFIUS has become a leading regulatory risk in cross-border M&A — particularly U.S. deals involving Chinese, Russian, or Saudi acquirers in tech, semiconductors, biotech, real estate near military bases, and critical infrastructure.

## Member Agencies

CFIUS draws from nine voting members chaired by Treasury:
- Treasury (chair)
- Justice
- Defense
- Commerce
- Energy
- State
- Homeland Security
- USTR
- Office of Science and Technology Policy

Plus White House observers (NSC, NEC, OMB).

## Process

### Voluntary Filing
Historically all CFIUS filings were *voluntary* — parties chose to file to obtain "safe harbor" against post-closing unwinding.

### Mandatory Filing (post-FIRRMA)
FIRRMA created **mandatory filing** for transactions involving:
- Foreign investment in **TID U.S. Businesses** (Critical Technology, Critical Infrastructure, Sensitive Personal Data).
- Foreign-government-controlled investors with substantive interest.
- Investments by countries on the "covered investment" list.

### Two Tracks

**Declaration:** 5-page short-form filing; CFIUS has 30 days to clear or escalate.

**Notice:** comprehensive long-form filing; 45-day initial review, plus 45-day investigation if escalated, plus 15-day Presidential decision period if it gets there.

## Outcomes

| Outcome | Frequency (modern, 2019-2024) |
|---------|-------------------------------|
| Cleared (no mitigation) | 75-85% |
| Cleared with mitigation agreement | 10-15% |
| Withdrawn before completion | 5-10% |
| Blocked by Presidential order | <1% (rare but newsworthy) |

Presidential blocks are rare but politically dramatic. Notable blocks:

| Year | Deal | Acquirer | Reasoning |
|------|------|----------|-----------|
| 2018 | Broadcom-Qualcomm | Singapore (perceived China-proxy) | Trump EO; 5G national security |
| 2020 | TikTok / ByteDance | China | Trump EO; data security; eventually litigation deferred |
| 2022 | Magnachip-Wise Road Capital | China | Withdrew under CFIUS pressure |
| 2024 | Nippon Steel / U.S. Steel | Japan | Biden blocked despite ally status; political optics |

## Mitigation Agreements

When CFIUS doesn't block but has concerns, it negotiates a **mitigation agreement**:
- Limit foreign access to specific data, IP, or facilities.
- Install US-citizen-staffed boards or subsidiaries.
- Require reporting / audit rights for CFIUS.
- Periodic compliance certifications.

Mitigation agreements add 6-18 months and 5-15% transaction cost.

## What Risk Arbs Watch

- **Filing announcement** — implies parties think CFIUS exposure is real.
- **Acquirer nationality + industry** — first-pass risk score.
- **Withdrawal-and-refile** — almost always indicates negotiation underway.
- **Public Treasury statements** — rare but signal.
- **Congressional letters** — Senators on Banking, Foreign Relations, Armed Services often pressure CFIUS publicly.

## Spread Behavior in CFIUS Deals

| Phase | Typical Spread Move |
|-------|---------------------|
| Pre-filing | Wide if Chinese acquirer (15-25%); narrow if EU/Japan/Korea (3-8%) |
| Filing accepted | Slight compression |
| 45-day review extended | Spread blowout 200-400 bp |
| Mitigation negotiation leaks | Mixed; depends on terms |
| Withdrawal | Catastrophic gap (-15-30%) |
| Block | Catastrophic gap (-20-50%) |
| Clearance | Snap to deal close |

## High-Profile CFIUS Cases for Arbs

**Qualcomm-NXP (2017-2018)** — Not formally CFIUS, but China MOFCOM block ($26B deal abandoned). The mirror-image foreign-regulator risk.

**TikTok divestiture saga (2020-2025)** — Multi-year multi-administration. ByteDance's US operations subject to repeat CFIUS / EO action.

**Smithfield Foods / Shuanghui (2013)** — Cleared with mitigation; precedent-setting for Chinese acquirers.

**MoneyGram / Ant Financial (2018)** — Blocked by CFIUS; Ant withdrew. $1.2B deal terminated.

**Magnachip / Wise Road Capital (2021-2022)** — Korean target, Chinese acquirer; withdrew under CFIUS pressure.

## FIRRMA Expansion

The 2018 reform broadened CFIUS jurisdiction to:
- **Non-controlling investments** in TID businesses (Critical Technology, Critical Infrastructure, Sensitive Personal Data).
- **Real estate transactions** in proximity to military bases or sensitive facilities.
- **Outbound investments** (added 2024 by EO — outbound semis/AI/quantum to China).
