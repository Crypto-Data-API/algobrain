When a company you own is **acquired**, the takeover changes your position in a way you usually can't opt out of. This page covers M&A strictly from a **shareholder's point of view** — what happens to your shares, your cost basis, and your tax bill — rather than the deal-making or the [[merger-arbitrage|arbitrage]] angle. The single most important question for a holder is the **form of consideration**: are you being paid in **cash**, in the **acquirer's stock**, or in a **mix** of both? Each has very different consequences for your taxes and your ongoing exposure.

## Why companies do M&A (briefly)

Acquirers buy other companies to gain scale and synergies, enter new markets, acquire technology or talent, eliminate a competitor, or deploy excess cash. Because the buyer must persuade shareholders to part with their stock, deals are almost always struck at a **premium** to the pre-announcement price — which is why the target's shares typically jump on announcement. (The mirror-image event, where a company *separates* a division and hands you new shares, is a [[demergers|demerger / spin-off]].)

## Cash deal vs. stock deal vs. mixed

**Cash deal.** You receive a fixed cash amount per share when the deal closes; your shares are cancelled and the position disappears from your account.
- *Tax:* generally an immediate **taxable disposal** — you realise a [[capital-gains|capital gain or loss]] versus your cost basis right away.
- *Exposure:* clean exit. You are no longer invested once it closes; you hold cash.
- *Certainty:* the price you'll get is fixed in dollars, so the only real variable is whether the deal closes.

**Stock deal.** You receive shares of the **acquirer** at a stated **exchange ratio** (e.g., 1.5 acquirer shares for each of your shares).
- *Tax:* an all-stock merger is often structured as a **tax-deferred** "reorganisation" — no gain is crystallised until you later sell the acquirer stock (rules vary by jurisdiction; confirm yours).
- *Exposure:* you are now a shareholder of the **combined company** and ride its future ups and downs.
- *Certainty:* depends on the ratio type. A **fixed exchange ratio** locks the *number* of shares (so the *value* floats with the acquirer's price); a **floating ratio** locks the *value* (so the *number* of shares floats). Many deals add a **collar** that caps how far the terms can move.

**Mixed (cash-and-stock).** A blend — e.g., "$20 cash plus 0.4 acquirer shares per share." You get partial liquidity, partial rollover, and a partial taxable event on the cash portion.

## How the process unfolds

1. **Announcement** — terms and premium are disclosed; the target's price jumps toward (but stays below) the offer, leaving a **deal spread** that reflects deal-break risk.
2. **Shareholder vote and/or [[tender-offer|tender offer]]** — a merger usually needs target-shareholder approval; a tender offer goes directly to holders.
3. **Regulatory approval** — antitrust and sector regulators (e.g., under [[ec-merger-regulation|EU merger control]] or US Hart-Scott-Rodino review) can delay, condition, or block a deal.
4. **Close** — consideration is paid, the target [[delisting|delists]], and your position converts to cash and/or acquirer stock.

## Hypothetical example

*Illustrative only.* You own 1,000 shares of TargetCo, bought at $25 (cost basis $25,000). TargetCo trades at $40 when BuyCo announces a deal worth **$30 cash plus 0.5 BuyCo shares** per TargetCo share, with BuyCo at $40 (so the stock portion is worth ~$20, total ~$50 — a premium to $40). TargetCo rises to ~$48, the spread reflecting the months until close and the antitrust risk. At close you receive **$30,000 cash** and **500 BuyCo shares**. The cash portion realises a taxable gain now; the stock portion may roll over tax-deferred and leaves you holding BuyCo, whose price will then drive your remaining return. If antitrust regulators had instead blocked the deal, TargetCo would likely have fallen back toward $40 and you'd keep your original shares.

## What to watch for

- **Cash vs. stock tax** — cash is taxable now; all-stock is often deferred. This alone can change which is "better" for you.
- **Fixed vs. floating ratio and collars** — in a stock deal, know whether your *value* or your *share count* is fixed.
- **Deal conditions** — financing, shareholder vote, regulatory approvals, "material adverse change" clauses, and break/walk fees all affect closing odds.
- **The spread** — a wide gap below the offer price signals the market doubts completion; don't assume the headline price is locked.
- **Your choice at the vote / tender** — you may be able to elect cash vs. stock (subject to pro-ration), or dissent and seek [[appraisal-rights-arbitrage|appraisal]] if you think the price is unfair.
- **Hold-to-close vs. sell-into-spread** — selling in the market now locks a price minus the spread; holding to close captures the last few percent but bears deal-break risk.
