A support level is a price zone where buying pressure historically exceeds selling pressure, causing a downtrend to pause or reverse. It is the downside mirror of [[resistance]]: where resistance is a ceiling of latent supply, support is a floor of latent [[supply-and-demand|demand]]. Traders identify support by looking at prior price action -- areas where the asset previously bounced upward indicate willing buyers. The more times a level has been tested and held, the stronger it is considered, though each successive test without a catalyst to drive buyers can *weaken* the level as willing buyers are gradually exhausted.

## Why Support Forms

Support is a [[supply-and-demand]] phenomenon read off a chart. Buying pressure clusters at a level because of several overlapping groups:

- **Value buyers:** participants who consider the price cheap and step in with bids.
- **Bounce buyers:** traders who profited from a previous bounce off the level and try to repeat it.
- **Short-coverers:** short sellers booking profits near the level, whose buying-to-close adds demand.
- **Stop magnets:** ironically, the resting stop-loss orders *below* support mean that a clean break can cascade -- one reason support, once broken, often breaks decisively.

## Types of Support

| Type | How it is drawn | Strength signal |
|------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Horizontal | Fixed price of prior swing lows / consolidation floor | More touches, higher volume |
| Trendline | Connects a series of ascending lows in an uptrend | More touches along the line |
| [[moving-average]] | Rising 50-day or 200-day SMA acting as a dynamic floor | Confluence with horizontal level |
| Psychological | Round numbers where bids cluster | Visible in [[order-book]] |
| [[fibonacci-retracement]] | 38.2% / 50% / 61.8% retracement of a prior swing | Confluence with other types |
| Volume profile | High-volume nodes / point of control below price | Large traded volume at the price |

Institutional traders frequently reference the 50-day and 200-day moving averages, which can create self-fulfilling buying pressure as many participants act on the same level.

## Psychology of Support

Support levels work in part because of anchoring and loss aversion. Traders who bought near a prior low and saw the price rise feel validated if the price returns to that level, and often buy again to defend their thesis. Traders who missed the prior move see the return to support as a second chance. This clustering of buy orders creates genuine demand. However, when a support level breaks, the psychology inverts: former buyers who are now underwater become motivated sellers, and the broken support level frequently acts as [[resistance]] -- the **polarity** (role-reversal) principle. [[volume|Volume]] confirmation matters: a bounce on high volume is more meaningful than one on thin volume, and a break below support on heavy volume signals genuine distribution.

## Worked Example

A stock has bounced twice near **$40** -- horizontal support, roughly $39.50-$40.50.

- **Bounce / range play:** a trader buys near $40 with a stop at $39 (just below the zone), targeting the range ceiling at $48. Risk ≈ $1; reward ≈ $8 -- a ~8:1 [[risk-reward-ratio|risk/reward]] if support holds.
- **Breakdown play:** price closes at $38 on heavy [[volume]]. A short seller enters, placing a stop back above $40.50 (former support, now expected [[resistance]]), targeting a measured move equal to the prior range.
- **Failed breakdown (bear trap):** price dips to $39.20 intraday on light volume then closes back at $41. Breakdown shorts are trapped; their stops above the range can fuel a sharp rally.

The same $40 level produces opposite trades depending on whether it holds or breaks -- which is why predefined rules around the level matter more than the level itself.

## How Traders Use Support

- **Entries:** buy near support with a stop just below it for a defined, favourable [[risk-reward-ratio|risk/reward]].
- **Stop placement:** for longs, park stops below support; a clean break invalidates the thesis.
- **Targets for shorts:** cover shorts into support, where demand is expected.
- **Breakdown triggers:** a confirmed close below support with volume is a [[breakout-trading|breakdown]] short signal.
- **Trend confirmation:** in an uptrend, higher swing lows that hold as support confirm the trend ([[trend-following]]).

## Common Pitfalls

- **Catching a falling knife:** buying support in a strong downtrend before any sign of demand -- support in a true bear regime is often just a brief pause.
- **Trading wicks, not closes:** an intraday spike below support that closes back inside is not a breakdown.
- **Ignoring volume:** a bounce on thin volume is weak; a break on heavy volume is real.
- **Stops sitting at the obvious level:** placing stops exactly at support invites a "stop run" where price briefly dips, triggers stops, then reverses. Allow buffer for the zone.
- **Treating zones as exact lines:** support "at $40" is an area; over-precision causes whipsaw.

## Trading Applications

Support levels are used across strategies from [[day-trading|intraday scalping]] to [[position-trading|position trading]]. Common approaches include buying near support with a stop-loss just below it (offering a defined [[risk-reward-ratio|risk/reward]]), and recognizing that a decisive break below support often triggers accelerated selling as stop-loss orders fire. Support and [[resistance]] together form the foundation of range-bound trading and [[breakout-trading|breakout]] strategies, and are studied jointly as [[support-and-resistance]].
