The **Form 8-K** is the "current report" U.S. public companies file with the [[sec|SEC]] to disclose **material events** as they happen — between the scheduled annual [[10-k|10-K]] and quarterly [[10-q|10-Q]] reports. Where the 10-K and 10-Q are periodic, the 8-K is *event-driven*: it is the official channel through which a company tells the market about a development important enough that investors should know promptly. Because 8-Ks land in near-real time on [[edgar|EDGAR]], they are a foundational feed for [[event-driven-trading|event-driven]] and [[information-arbitrage|information-arbitrage]] strategies.

## Overview

An 8-K is triggered by a specific event rather than the calendar. A company may file dozens in a busy year or only a handful in a quiet one. Each 8-K cites the numbered **Item** that triggered it, which immediately tells a reader what kind of news it is without opening the document. The earnings press release that drives the headline reaction every quarter is itself usually filed as an 8-K (under Item 2.02), so the 8-K sits at the center of the [[earnings|earnings]] event as well.

## What Triggers an 8-K

The triggering events are grouped into sections; the most market-relevant Items include:

- **Item 1.01 — Entry into a Material Definitive Agreement** (a major contract, financing, or M&A agreement).
- **Item 1.03 — Bankruptcy or Receivership**.
- **Item 2.01 — Completion of an Acquisition or Disposition of Assets**.
- **Item 2.02 — Results of Operations and Financial Condition** (the quarterly **earnings release**).
- **Item 2.03 / 2.04 — Creation of, or default on, a material financial obligation**.
- **Item 3.01 — Notice of Delisting or Failure to Satisfy a Listing Rule**.
- **Item 4.01 — Change in the Registrant's Certifying Accountant** (an **auditor change** — often a red flag).
- **Item 4.02 — Non-Reliance on Previously Issued Financial Statements** (a **restatement** — a serious red flag).
- **Item 5.02 — Departure or Election of Directors or Officers** (CEO/CFO changes).
- **Item 5.07 — Submission of Matters to a Vote of Security Holders** (annual-meeting voting results).
- **Item 7.01 — Regulation FD Disclosure** (information disclosed to satisfy fair-disclosure rules).
- **Item 8.01 — Other Events** (a catch-all for anything management deems material).
- **Item 9.01 — Financial Statements and Exhibits** (where the press release or contract is attached).

## What an Investor Looks For

- **The Item number first.** It instantly classifies the news: a 4.02 (restatement) or 4.01 (auditor change) is far more alarming than an 8.01 routine update.
- **Management and auditor changes (Items 5.02 and 4.01).** An abrupt CFO departure or a switched auditor frequently precedes accounting trouble.
- **Restatements (Item 4.02).** A statement that prior financials "should no longer be relied upon" is one of the strongest negative signals in disclosure.
- **The actual exhibit (Item 9.01).** The 8-K body is often a brief cover; the real content — the contract, the press release, the financing terms — is the attached exhibit.
- **Speed.** Because 8-Ks publish within minutes of acceptance, reading them faster and more completely than the crowd is a classic [[information-arbitrage|information edge]]; the headline a newswire writes may miss a buried detail.

## Timing and Cadence

The general rule is that an 8-K must be filed **within four business days** of the triggering event. Some Items have different timing — for example, Regulation FD disclosures (Item 7.01) can be required the same day. Unlike the 10-K and 10-Q, there is no fixed calendar: 8-Ks appear whenever material events occur, which makes a real-time EDGAR feed essential for anyone trading around corporate news.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Reacting to the headline, not the filing.** A wire service may flag "8-K filed" without saying it is a routine Item 7.01; conversely it may miss a buried restatement. Read the Item and the exhibit.
- **Confusing a voluntary disclosure with a required one.** Item 8.01 ("Other Events") is voluntary and can be used to control narrative; weigh it accordingly.
- **Ignoring after-hours timing.** Many 8-Ks (earnings, executive departures) hit after the close, so the reaction shows up as an overnight gap — [[stop-loss|stop orders]] do not protect across it.
- **Overweighting a single 8-K.** Context matters: an Item 5.02 director departure can be routine retirement or a sign of boardroom conflict; cross-check against [[form-4|Form 4]] insider activity and the next [[10-q|10-Q]].
- **Assuming silence means nothing happened.** Some material developments are disclosed only at the next periodic filing; the absence of an 8-K is not proof of calm.

## A Hypothetical Example

Suppose **XYZ Corp** (fictional) files an 8-K at 4:15 p.m. citing **Item 4.02**. The body states that, due to an error in revenue recognition, its financial statements for the prior two years "should no longer be relied upon" and will be restated. Even with no other news, this single filing tells the market that the audited [[10-k|10-K]] numbers were wrong — a far more serious signal than a normal earnings miss. Traders monitoring the EDGAR 8-K stream in real time see the Item 4.02 tag and react before a full news article is even written. This is precisely why event-driven desks watch the 8-K feed continuously.
