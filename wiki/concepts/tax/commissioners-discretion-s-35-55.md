> **Not tax advice.** Applications for the Commissioner's discretion are fact-specific, subject to formal application procedures, and almost always require professional preparation. Confirm with a registered tax agent.

Section 35-55 of the ITAA 1997 provides the **Commissioner's discretion** to disregard the loss-deferral rule in [[non-commercial-losses-div-35|Division 35]] in cases where the objective tests in s 35-30 to s 35-45 are not met. It is the only path to immediate loss deduction for taxpayers who:

- Earn more than the **$250,000 adjusted taxable income** gate (and so cannot access any of the four exception tests), OR
- Fall short of all four exception thresholds despite carrying on a genuine business

The discretion is granted on **two grounds only** — *special circumstances* (s 35-55(1)(a)) or *lead time* (s 35-55(1)(b) and (c)) — and the [[pcg-2021-2-div-35-discretion|Commissioner's published approach]] in PCG 2021/2 frames how applications are assessed in practice.

## Statutory grounds

### Ground 1: Special circumstances — s 35-55(1)(a)

The Commissioner may decide that Division 35 does not apply if:

> *because of [special circumstances outside the taxpayer's control], the business activity will not satisfy any of the tests in section 35-30, 35-35, 35-40 or 35-45 for the income year.*

"Special circumstances" must be **outside the taxpayer's control** and must have caused the test failure. Typical accepted examples in published rulings:

- **Natural disasters** — drought, flood, fire, cyclone destroying business assets
- **Pandemic-driven shutdowns** — COVID-19 disruptions to specific activities (numerous private rulings 2020–2022)
- **Illness or injury of the operator** — significant prolonged illness preventing operation of the business
- **Government action** — regulatory shutdown of an activity
- **Major market disruption affecting a specific activity** (rare for general trading losses)

Typical rejected examples:

- **Ordinary market downturn** — broad equity declines, recession, rate moves
- **Business judgment errors** — bad trades, mis-sizing, strategy failure
- **Personal financial pressure** — needing cash forcing forced sales
- **Foreseeable risks of the activity** — volatility, drawdowns, single-name blow-ups

**For traders specifically**: ordinary losses from market movement do not qualify. The discretion is not a safety net for bad trading years. A trader experiencing a once-in-a-generation event that specifically destroyed their ability to execute (e.g., prolonged hospitalisation; exchange-wide infrastructure failure preventing closeout) might qualify; a trader who simply lost money in the market will not.

### Ground 2: Lead time — s 35-55(1)(b) and (c)

The Commissioner may decide that Division 35 does not apply where:

> *the business activity has started to be carried on and, for that or those income years, because of its nature, has not produced, or will not produce, assessable income greater than the deductions attributable to it; AND there is an objective expectation, based on evidence from independent sources where available, that, within a period that is commercially viable for the industry concerned, the activity will produce assessable income for an income year greater than the deductions attributable to it, or will satisfy one of the tests in section 35-30, 35-35, 35-40 or 35-45.*

The lead time discretion exists for activities that **inherently require a multi-year start-up period** before they can generate assessable income — primary production (e.g., vineyards, plantations), property development, certain professional practices.

**For traders specifically**: lead time is rarely available. A trading business does not have a natural "inception period" — trading either generates assessable income from year one (gross trading receipts) or it does not. The Commissioner has generally taken the position that share/derivative trading does not have inherent lead-time characteristics that justify the discretion.

The only plausible scenarios for a trader:

- Building proprietary trading infrastructure where the activity is genuinely paused during the build (e.g., developing HFT systems before deployment)
- Multi-year capital build-up phase where the activity is below scale by design

Even these are weak — the ATO will typically conclude that smaller trading activity is still trading activity, not a pre-revenue start-up.

## Application process

A taxpayer cannot simply claim the discretion on their tax return. The Commissioner's discretion is exercised by **private ruling application** under s 359-5 of Schedule 1 to the Tax Administration Act 1953, normally lodged before the relevant return.

### Steps

1. **Determine eligibility.** Have you genuinely failed all four objective tests in s 35-30 to s 35-45? Is your adjusted taxable income above $250k? Do you have a plausible "special circumstances" or "lead time" argument?
2. **Prepare the application.** Standard form is a private ruling request. Must include:
   - Full description of the business activity (TR 97/11 facts)
   - Financial information (income, losses, assessable income from the activity)
   - Specific identification of the test(s) failed and why
   - The ground claimed (s 35-55(1)(a) or (b)/(c))
   - Supporting evidence — the more independent and contemporaneous, the better
3. **Lodge with the ATO.** The application is reviewed by a specialist team; turnaround is typically 28–90 days.
4. **Outcome.** A favourable ruling allows the loss to be claimed in the relevant year; an unfavourable ruling defers the loss under s 35-10.
5. **Appeal rights.** If refused, the taxpayer has objection rights under Part IVC of the TAA 1953.

### Evidence the Commissioner expects

- **Contemporaneous records** of the special circumstance or lead-time plan
- **Independent corroboration** — medical certificates, government notifications, insurance claims, industry expert reports
- **Causation chain** — clear evidence the test failure was caused by the special circumstance, not by ordinary business risk
- **Forward-looking projections** for lead-time applications — when will the activity meet the tests, and on what basis

## Realistic expectations for traders

Based on the public ruling record:

| Trader scenario | Likely outcome |
|---|---|
| Bad trading year from ordinary market movement | **Refused** |
| Loss-making year because of margin call during 2020 COVID crash | Possible accept on COVID-specific circumstances |
| Trader hospitalised for 6+ months, unable to manage positions, forced losses | Possible accept on illness ground |
| New trader in first year, no profit yet | **Refused** — no lead-time character to ordinary trading |
| Algorithmic system being rebuilt, trading paused | **Likely refused** unless rebuild is a clear pre-revenue project |
| Exchange or broker insolvency causing forced losses | Possible accept on special circumstances |

The bar is high and the typical refusal turnaround is fast.

## Strategic implications

For a trader who anticipates loss years above the $250k income gate, the discretion is **not** a reliable backstop. Better approaches:

1. **Structure the activity** in a non-Div 35 entity from the outset — company, [[smsf|SMSF]], trust (with Div 35 attribution understood)
2. **Manage income timing** to keep below $250k in expected loss years (where possible)
3. **Time loss realisation** to align with years where exception tests can be satisfied
4. **Maintain documentation** sufficient to apply for the discretion if a genuinely unusual event occurs
5. **Get private rulings** in advance for any significant activity changes — the Commissioner will rule on prospective facts

## Relationship to PCG 2021/2

The Commissioner publishes [[pcg-2021-2-div-35-discretion|Practical Compliance Guideline 2021/2]] setting out the practical approach to applying s 35-55. The PCG:

- Articulates the kinds of facts the Commissioner considers "special circumstances"
- Provides examples of accepted and refused applications
- Sets out documentation expectations
- Identifies low-risk situations where the Commissioner will generally apply the discretion without detailed review

Reading the PCG before preparing an application is essential.
