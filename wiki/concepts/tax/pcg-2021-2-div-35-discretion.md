> **Not tax advice.** PCG 2021/2 is a public administrative guideline, not law. The Commissioner can depart from it on individual facts. Confirm any application of the PCG with a registered tax agent.

**Practical Compliance Guideline PCG 2021/2** is the ATO's public statement of how the Commissioner exercises the discretion in [[commissioners-discretion-s-35-55|s 35-55]] of Division 35 (non-commercial losses). PCGs are administrative guidance, not legally binding, but they describe the Commissioner's settled approach and tell taxpayers what to expect in private-ruling applications and audits. For traders preparing an application or assessing the likely outcome, the PCG is the second document to read after the statute itself.

## What a PCG is

Practical Compliance Guidelines are published by the ATO under their public guidance program. They:

- Are **not binding law** — they do not modify the statute or override case law
- **Are binding on the Commissioner** in the sense that the ATO will generally follow them in administering the relevant provision
- Provide **safe harbours, low-risk indicators, and high-risk indicators** for taxpayers to self-assess their position
- Are amended periodically as the Commissioner's view evolves

Taxpayers who structure their affairs within the PCG can generally expect the indicated treatment without audit risk. Taxpayers outside the PCG are not necessarily wrong but face heightened scrutiny.

## Scope of PCG 2021/2

PCG 2021/2 deals specifically with the Commissioner's discretion under s 35-55 — i.e., the path to immediate loss deduction when the four exception tests in s 35-30 to s 35-45 are not met. It covers:

- The two grounds: **special circumstances** (s 35-55(1)(a)) and **lead time** (s 35-55(1)(b)/(c))
- The Commissioner's interpretation of "special circumstances"
- How long is "commercially viable" for lead-time activities
- Documentation expected with applications
- Examples of accepted and refused circumstances

## Where the discretion sits in Division 35

The Commissioner's discretion is the *last resort* in [[non-commercial-losses-div-35|Division 35]], reached only after the other gates have failed. Understanding the order matters: most traders never need the discretion because they either fail to "carry on a business" at all or pass one of the four objective tests.

| Step | Question | Provision | Outcome if satisfied |
|------|----------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | Is the activity a *business* (not a hobby/investment)? | [[tr-97-11-carrying-on-a-business\|TR 97/11]], [[trader-vs-investor-classification-au]] | If not, Div 35 does not apply at all |
| 2 | Is adjusted taxable income < $250k? | s 35-10(2E) income requirement | If not, all four tests are unavailable; only discretion remains |
| 3 | Does the activity pass one of the four tests? | s 35-30 to s 35-45 | If yes, loss is deductible this year |
| 4 | If no test is passed, will the Commissioner exercise discretion? | [[commissioners-discretion-s-35-55\|s 35-55]] (this PCG) | If yes, loss is deductible; if no, loss is deferred |

The four objective tests in step 3 are, in brief:

| Test | Section | Threshold (indicative) |
|------|---------|------------------------|
| Assessable income test | s 35-30 | Activity produces at least ~$20k assessable income |
| Profits test | s 35-35 | Profit in 3 of the past 5 years (incl. current) |
| Real property test | s 35-40 | Uses real property of at least ~$500k value |
| Other assets test | s 35-45 | Uses other assets of at least ~$100k value |

Threshold figures are the long-standing statutory amounts and are indicative — confirm current values with a registered tax agent. If a loss is *deferred* under Div 35 it is not lost: it is quarantined and can offset future income from the same activity.

## Special circumstances — Commissioner's approach

The PCG develops the statutory phrase "special circumstances outside the taxpayer's control" in considerable detail. The Commissioner's settled view:

### What qualifies

- **Natural disasters** — drought, flood, bushfire, cyclone, hailstorm — where the activity is directly affected
- **Disease outbreaks** affecting the specific activity (livestock, crops)
- **Government action** — regulatory shutdown, compulsory acquisition, biosecurity controls
- **Pandemics with direct activity impact** — COVID-19 specifically was addressed by interim guidance and ATO statements 2020–2022
- **Catastrophic loss or destruction of business assets** outside ordinary risk
- **Significant illness or injury** of the principal operator preventing carrying on the activity

The connecting feature is that the circumstance must be:

1. **Outside the taxpayer's control** — not the result of business judgment or ordinary risk-taking
2. **Causative** — it actually prevented satisfaction of the relevant test, not just made it harder
3. **Documented** — supported by independent contemporaneous evidence

### What does not qualify

- **Ordinary market or economic fluctuations** — recessions, interest-rate moves, equity drawdowns
- **Foreseeable business risks** — volatility, single-name losses, strategy underperformance
- **Personal financial difficulty** unrelated to the activity
- **Business decisions made by the taxpayer** — even bad ones
- **Lack of capital** at the activity's inception (unless lead-time ground applies)
- **Difficulty obtaining customers or counterparties** in normal market conditions

For traders, the second list rules out the most common reasons for a loss year. The Commissioner has been consistent that **ordinary market-driven losses are not special circumstances**, regardless of magnitude.

## Lead time — Commissioner's approach

The PCG addresses lead time discretion for activities that, by nature, take multiple years to become profitable. The Commissioner identifies activities where lead time is recognised:

- **Primary production** — most plantations, viticulture, orchards, certain livestock
- **Property development** of long-duration projects
- **Professional practices** in initial build-up
- **Research and development** activities with long payback

The PCG does **not** identify share trading, derivatives trading, or financial-market activities as having inherent lead-time character. The Commissioner's position is that financial trading either generates assessable income from inception or the activity does not yet exist; there is no industry-standard incubation period.

This leaves traders with effectively no lead-time argument for ordinary trading. The exception would be a substantial pre-revenue project (e.g., building proprietary HFT systems with no live trading during the build) — but even there, the Commissioner is likely to argue that the activity hasn't yet commenced.

## "Commercially viable period" — lead-time benchmarks

For activities where lead time is available, the PCG provides industry benchmarks for what the Commissioner considers "commercially viable":

- **Plantations** — 5–25 years depending on the species
- **Vineyards** — 5–10 years to commercial production
- **Cattle breeding** — 3–7 years to herd build-up
- **Property development** — 2–5 years per project depending on scale

These benchmarks are illustrative; the taxpayer's actual industry expectation must be evidenced. For a trader claiming a build-out lead time, the industry benchmark is essentially nil — there is no recognised multi-year industry standard.

## Documentation expectations

PCG 2021/2 sets out the evidence the Commissioner expects with an application for the discretion:

| Ground | Expected evidence |
|---|---|
| **Natural disaster** | Government declaration; insurance claims; photographs; independent valuations of damage |
| **Disease outbreak** | Veterinary or biosecurity reports; quarantine notices |
| **Government action** | Official notice; legal correspondence |
| **Illness / injury** | Medical certificates; specialist reports; period of incapacity |
| **Pandemic disruption** | Documented activity-specific impact; revenue records before/during/after |
| **Lead time** | Business plan; industry expert evidence on viability period; financial projections; capital deployment records |

The Commissioner is unlikely to grant the discretion without **independent corroborating evidence**. Self-prepared statements alone are not enough.

## Safe harbours and low-risk indicators

The PCG identifies situations where the Commissioner will generally apply the discretion without detailed review:

- Activities directly affected by a Commonwealth-declared disaster
- Activities where the operator has been incapacitated for a documented period
- Activities in their first or second year where the industry lead-time pattern is well-established (primary production)

There is no safe harbour for trading losses caused by market movement.

## High-risk indicators (likely refusal)

The Commissioner identifies high-risk indicators where applications will be scrutinised heavily or refused:

- Applications based on ordinary market or economic conditions
- Activities with no clear path to commercial viability
- Repeated applications across multiple years from the same taxpayer
- Activities where the taxpayer's other income exceeds $250k for several consecutive loss years
- Documentation that does not include independent third-party evidence

## Practical use of the PCG

For traders considering an application:

1. **Read the PCG first.** Most ordinary trading-loss applications are doomed and the PCG will tell you so.
2. **Identify the specific ground.** Either you have a genuine special circumstance with contemporaneous evidence, or you have a genuine lead-time argument grounded in industry practice. Both bars are high for trading.
3. **Assemble evidence before applying.** Independent corroboration is decisive.
4. **Consider alternatives.** If the discretion is unlikely, structural changes (entity choice, income timing, loss timing) may produce better long-term outcomes than fighting for the discretion.

## Worked scenarios for traders (illustrative only)

These are stylised illustrations of how the Commissioner's published approach tends to apply to trading fact patterns. They are **not advice** and not predictions for any individual — outcomes turn on specific facts and current ATO guidance.

| Scenario | Ground argued | Likely posture under the PCG |
|----------|---------------|------------------------------|
| Discretionary equity trader has a large loss year because the market fell | Special circumstances | **Refused** — ordinary market movement is a foreseeable business risk, not a special circumstance |
| Trader hospitalised for several months, unable to operate the activity | Special circumstances (illness) | **Plausible** if incapacity is documented and causative, supported by medical evidence |
| Trader's broker/exchange collapses (e.g. counterparty insolvency) freezing the account | Special circumstances | **Fact-dependent** — may qualify if genuinely outside control and causative; needs independent evidence |
| New trader spends 2 years building an HFT system before any live trading | Lead time | **Weak** — the Commissioner does not recognise financial trading as a lead-time industry, and may argue the activity has not yet commenced |
| Trading losses during a Commonwealth-declared disaster that physically disrupted operations | Special circumstances | **Possible** but the disaster must actually have caused the failure of the test, not merely coincided with a bad year |

The recurring theme for traders: **market-driven losses are the one thing the discretion will not rescue.** The viable arguments are narrow — genuine, documented, external events that prevented the activity from meeting a test.

## How the PCG evolves

The Commissioner has updated the PCG (or issued interim guidance) at moments of unusual disruption — most notably the COVID-19 pandemic and major natural disaster events. Traders should check for current ATO statements before relying on an older version of the PCG.
