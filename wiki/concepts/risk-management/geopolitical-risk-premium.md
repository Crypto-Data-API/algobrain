# Geopolitical Risk Premium

The geopolitical risk premium is the additional price embedded in commodity and asset prices to compensate for the risk of supply disruptions, trade restrictions, or demand shocks caused by geopolitical events. It represents the market's collective assessment of political instability, armed conflict, sanctions, and other non-economic factors that can affect supply and demand. Unlike temporary [[volatility]] spikes, geopolitical risk premiums can persist for months or years as structural pricing components. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Measuring Geopolitical Risk

### Geopolitical Risk Index (GPR)

The GPR index, developed by Caldara and Iacoviello (Federal Reserve Board), quantifies geopolitical risk by counting the frequency of newspaper articles related to geopolitical tensions. Research findings on commodity impacts:

- **[[crude-oil]]**: GPR shocks negatively affect crude oil returns — supply disruption fears push prices higher, but demand destruction from conflict can offset this
- **[[gold]]**: Positive impact — gold functions as the primary safe-haven asset during geopolitical stress
- **Platinum, silver**: Negative returns during heightened geopolitical risk (industrial demand fears dominate safe-haven flows)
- **Agricultural commodities**: Mixed — depends on whether conflict zones overlap with production regions (e.g., Ukraine/Russia grain corridor)

### Key Indicators Traders Watch

- Satellite imagery of military deployments and shipping traffic
- Sanctions announcements and enforcement actions
- OPEC+ meeting outcomes and compliance data
- Shipping insurance rates through conflict zones (war risk premiums)
- Credit default swap spreads on sovereign debt of affected nations

## Major Geopolitical Risk Factors for Commodities

### Supply Route Chokepoints

| Chokepoint | Volume | Key Commodities | Risk Scenario |
|-----------|--------|-----------------|---------------|
| Strait of Hormuz | ~20% of global oil supply | [[crude-oil]], [[natural-gas]] (LNG) | Iran conflict, mining |
| Red Sea / Bab el-Mandeb | ~10% of global trade | Oil, LNG, container freight | Houthi attacks (2023-24) |
| Suez Canal | ~12% of global trade | All seaborne commodities | Blockage (Ever Given 2021) |
| Strait of Malacca | ~25% of global trade | Crude oil, LNG, metals | South China Sea tensions |
| Turkish Straits (Bosporus) | ~3% of global oil | Crude oil, grain | Russia-Ukraine conflict |
| Black Sea grain corridor | ~30% of global wheat exports | Wheat, corn, sunflower oil | Russia-Ukraine conflict |

### OPEC+ Production Decisions

[[opec]] and its expanded OPEC+ coalition (including Russia) control approximately 40% of global crude oil production. OPEC decisions are inherently geopolitical:

- Production cuts to support prices transfer wealth from consumers to producers
- Quota compliance is uneven — member cheating erodes cartel discipline
- Saudi Arabia acts as swing producer, absorbing cuts to enforce discipline
- Russian participation post-2022 added a sanctions dimension to OPEC+ dynamics

### Sanctions Regimes

Sanctions on energy-producing nations create persistent supply disruptions and trade rerouting:

- **Iran**: U.S. sanctions have removed 1-2 million bpd from global markets (depending on enforcement intensity)
- **Russia (post-2022)**: EU oil embargo, G7 price cap, insurance restrictions rerouted Russian crude to China/India at discounted prices
- **Venezuela**: U.S. sanctions reduced output from ~2.5M bpd (2015) to ~0.7M bpd, with periodic license adjustments
- **Effects**: Sanctions don't eliminate supply — they reroute it, create discounts for sanctioned barrels, and fragment global commodity markets

### Government Policy and Strategic Reserves

- **Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR) releases**: U.S. has used SPR releases to combat high prices (most recently 180M barrels in 2022)
- **Carbon taxes and climate policy**: EU carbon border adjustment mechanism (CBAM) affects the competitive positioning of carbon-intensive commodities
- **Export bans**: Indonesia (palm oil, nickel ore), India (wheat, rice), Argentina (beef) — policy-driven supply shocks

## Impact on Trading

### Pricing Mechanisms

- **Fear premium**: Markets price in worst-case supply disruption scenarios, often overshooting fundamental impact
- **Asymmetric response**: Geopolitical risks create positive skew in energy prices — downside is bounded (oil can't go below zero... mostly), but upside is theoretically unlimited
- **Persistence**: Unlike technical or weather-driven moves, geopolitical premiums can persist for extended periods as conflicts are slow to resolve

### Trading Implications

- [[gold]] as the primary geopolitical hedge — "safe haven" flows during acute crises
- [[crude-oil]] options skew steepens (upside calls become expensive) when geopolitical risk elevates
- [[trend-following-cta]] strategies capture sustained geopolitical-driven trends
- Energy spreads (time spreads, geographic spreads) can embed geopolitical risk differently in different legs
- Defense sector equities and shipping stocks correlate positively with geopolitical risk

### Historical Episodes

| Event | Year | Impact |
|-------|------|--------|
| [[1973-oil-crisis]] | 1973 | Oil quadrupled ($3 → $12); created IEA, SPR |
| Iranian Revolution | 1979 | Oil doubled; second oil shock |
| Gulf War (Iraq-Kuwait) | 1990 | Oil spiked from $17 to $41; rapid reversal |
| 9/11 attacks | 2001 | Brief oil spike, then collapse on demand fears |
| Iraq War | 2003 | Oil rose from $25 to $35; beginning of supercycle |
| Arab Spring | 2011 | Libyan supply disruption; Brent spiked above $120 |
| Russia-Ukraine | 2022 | Brent above $130; European energy crisis; gas 10x |
| Red Sea / Houthi | 2023-24 | Shipping rerouting; freight rates spiked 5-10x |

## Limitations

- Geopolitical risk premiums are notoriously difficult to quantify precisely — they are embedded in prices alongside supply/demand fundamentals
- Markets habituate to persistent threats (the "crying wolf" effect reduces premium over time)
- Geopolitical events can be bearish for commodities if they destroy demand (e.g., war-induced recession fears)
- Gold's safe-haven behavior is not guaranteed — in 2008, gold initially fell during the acute crisis phase as margin calls forced liquidation across all assets
