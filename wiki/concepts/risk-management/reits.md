Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs) are publicly traded companies that own, operate, or finance income-producing [[real-estate]]. By law, REITs must distribute at least 90% of their taxable income to shareholders as [[dividend|dividends]], making them one of the highest-yielding equity asset classes. REITs provide investors with liquid, diversified exposure to real estate without the complexity, illiquidity, and capital requirements of direct property ownership.

> **Data disclaimer:** Yield figures and historical drawdowns below are qualitative ranges drawn from general market history, not live quotes. REIT yields, prices, and sector composition change continuously and vary by individual security. Do not treat any number here as current — confirm against a live data source before acting.

## Overview

REITs were created by the U.S. Congress in 1960 to give ordinary investors access to large-scale, income-producing real estate — an asset class previously available only to wealthy individuals and institutional investors. The structure has since been adopted in over 40 countries worldwide.

The key regulatory requirement is the 90% distribution rule: in exchange for distributing nearly all taxable income, REITs receive pass-through tax treatment and avoid corporate income tax at the entity level. This eliminates the double taxation that applies to regular corporations (taxed once at the corporate level and again when dividends reach shareholders). The tradeoff is that REITs retain very little earnings for reinvestment, so growth typically requires issuing new equity or debt.

ITPM methodology recommends REITs over direct buy-to-let property investment for most individual investors, citing several advantages: liquidity (can sell REIT shares instantly vs. months to sell property), diversification (a single REIT may own hundreds of properties), professional management, no maintenance responsibilities, and lower capital requirements. The main counterargument is that direct property ownership provides leverage benefits (mortgage) and tax advantages (depreciation) that REIT ownership does not replicate.

## How It Works

**REIT categories by property type**:

- **Residential**: Apartment buildings, single-family rental homes, manufactured housing. Demand driven by housing affordability and demographic trends.
- **Commercial/Office**: Office buildings and business parks. Sensitive to employment trends and remote work adoption. Post-2020 secular headwinds from work-from-home shifts.
- **Retail**: Shopping malls, strip centers, and outlet centers. Under pressure from e-commerce but well-located retail remains resilient.
- **Industrial**: Warehouses, distribution centers, and logistics facilities. Strong tailwinds from e-commerce growth driving demand for fulfillment infrastructure.
- **Data center**: Facilities housing server infrastructure. High growth driven by cloud computing, AI, and digital transformation. Examples include Equinix and Digital Realty.
- **Healthcare**: Hospitals, senior living facilities, medical offices. Demographic tailwind from aging populations.
- **Specialty**: Cell towers (American Tower, Crown Castle), self-storage (Public Storage), timber, farmland, and infrastructure.

**Mortgage REITs (mREITs)**: Unlike equity REITs that own physical property, mortgage REITs invest in mortgage-backed securities and earn the spread between short-term borrowing costs and long-term mortgage yields. Higher yields but significantly higher risk — highly sensitive to interest rate changes and credit risk.

**Valuation metrics**: REITs are valued using Funds From Operations (FFO) rather than traditional [[earnings]] because depreciation charges on real estate significantly understate cash flow. FFO = Net Income + Depreciation - Gains on Property Sales. Adjusted FFO (AFFO) further subtracts recurring maintenance capex to better approximate distributable cash. Price-to-FFO is the REIT equivalent of [[price-to-earnings-ratio|P/E ratio]]. Net Asset Value (NAV) — the estimated market value of all properties minus liabilities — serves a similar function to [[price-to-book-ratio|P/B ratio]]; REITs trading at a discount to NAV may be cheap, while persistent premiums signal market confidence in management's ability to add value.

## Equity vs Mortgage REITs — Where Each Fits

The two structural categories behave very differently and serve different roles in a portfolio:

| Dimension | Equity REIT | Mortgage REIT (mREIT) |
|-----------|-------------|------------------------|
| What it owns | Physical, income-producing property | Mortgages and mortgage-backed securities |
| Income source | Rents (and property appreciation) | Net interest spread (long mortgage yield − short funding cost) |
| Primary risk | Occupancy, rents, property values, the economic cycle | [[interest-rate-risk]], yield-curve shape, credit risk, leverage |
| Leverage | Moderate, secured by property | High; balance sheet often levered several times over |
| Yield | Lower, more stable | Higher, more volatile and prone to dividend cuts |
| [[duration]] sensitivity | Indirect (rates affect cap rates and financing) | Direct and acute (book value moves with rates) |
| Typical use | Core income + inflation-aware exposure | Higher-octane income for risk-tolerant investors |

mREITs earn the spread between short-term borrowing and long-term mortgage yields, so a flattening or inverting [[yield-curve]] compresses their margin, and rising rates can mark down their securities portfolio faster than the hedge offsets — which is why mREIT dividends are far less dependable than equity-REIT dividends.

## Interest-Rate Sensitivity

REITs are among the most rate-sensitive equity sectors, for two reinforcing reasons:

1. **Bond-substitute repricing.** Income investors compare a REIT's [[dividend-yield]] against the risk-free rate. When government-bond yields rise, the relative appeal of REIT income falls, and prices often de-rate until the yield spread is restored — a mechanism similar to [[duration]] in fixed income.
2. **Financing cost.** REITs are capital-intensive and carry property debt. Higher rates raise refinancing costs and lower the present value of future rents, compressing property valuations (cap-rate expansion).

This is why REITs frequently sell off when long rates spike and rally when the market anticipates rate cuts. It also complicates the "inflation hedge" narrative: rents do tend to rise with inflation, but the rate increases that usually accompany inflation work in the opposite direction, so the net effect depends on which force dominates. See [[interest-rate-risk]] and [[yield-curve]].

## Worked Example: Reading a REIT (Illustrative)

A simplified, illustrative walkthrough (figures are made up to show the method, not a real security):

- A REIT reports **net income of $80M**, **depreciation of $120M**, and **$10M of gains on property sales**.
- **FFO = 80 + 120 − 10 = $190M.** Depreciation is added back because buildings often appreciate rather than wear out economically.
- With **100M shares**, FFO per share ≈ **$1.90**.
- If the price is **$30**, the **Price-to-FFO ≈ 15.8×** — compare this to sector peers rather than to a stock [[price-to-earnings-ratio|P/E]].
- If management's estimated **NAV is $34/share**, the REIT trades at roughly a **12% discount to NAV** — potentially attractive if the NAV estimate is credible.
- A **$1.50 annual distribution** implies a payout of about **79% of FFO** — comfortably inside the 90%-of-taxable-income rule (taxable income differs from FFO) and leaving a thin cushion for reinvestment.

The point of the exercise is that you cannot analyse a REIT with ordinary [[earnings]]-based ratios; FFO/AFFO and NAV are the correct lenses.

## Trading Applications

- **Income generation**: REIT dividend yields typically range from 3-7%, significantly above the S&P 500 average of roughly 1.5%. This makes them a core holding for income-oriented portfolios, particularly for retirees seeking cash flow.
- **[[diversification]]**: Real estate has historically shown moderate [[correlation]] with stocks and low correlation with bonds, making REITs a useful diversifier in [[portfolio-construction]]. However, correlations increase during market crises — REITs dropped 37% in 2008.
- **Inflation hedge**: Real estate rents tend to rise with inflation, providing a partial hedge. However, rising interest rates (which often accompany inflation) increase REIT borrowing costs and make their yields less attractive relative to bonds, creating a complicated relationship with inflation.
- **Sector rotation**: Different REIT sectors perform differently depending on the economic cycle. Industrial and data center REITs benefit from secular growth trends; retail and office REITs are more cyclical. Sector selection within REITs is itself an active investment decision.
- **Tax considerations**: REIT dividends are generally taxed as ordinary income (not qualified dividends), making them less tax-efficient in taxable accounts. They are most advantageous in tax-deferred accounts (IRAs, 401(k)s) where the tax disadvantage is neutralized.

## Pitfalls and Risks

- **Interest-rate drawdowns.** As above, REITs can fall sharply when long rates rise; treating them as a "safe" bond proxy is a mistake. Their equity beta also rises in crises — historically REITs fell far harder than bonds in 2008.
- **Yield is not safety.** A high [[dividend-yield]] can signal distress rather than value — if the market expects a distribution cut, it prices the shares down so the trailing yield looks high. Always check FFO/AFFO payout coverage, not just the headline yield.
- **Chronic dilution.** Because the 90% payout rule leaves little retained capital, REITs fund growth by issuing equity. Frequent share issuance can dilute existing holders if it is done below NAV or fails to earn an adequate return.
- **Leverage and refinancing risk.** Property debt magnifies both returns and losses. A wall of maturing debt in a high-rate or tight-credit environment can force dilutive raises or distressed asset sales.
- **Sector-specific secular decline.** Some categories face structural, not cyclical, headwinds — office (remote work), some retail (e-commerce). A diversified REIT index does not protect against picking a structurally challenged single name.
- **mREIT fragility.** Mortgage REITs combine high leverage, [[interest-rate-risk]], prepayment risk, and credit risk; dividend cuts and book-value erosion are common in stressed conditions.
- **Tax drag in taxable accounts.** Ordinary-income treatment of most REIT distributions can meaningfully reduce after-tax returns outside tax-advantaged accounts.
- **Correlation rises when you need diversification most.** The diversification benefit versus equities partly evaporates in systemic sell-offs.
