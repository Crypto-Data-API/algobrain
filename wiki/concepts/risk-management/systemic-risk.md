# Systemic Risk

Systemic risk is the risk that the failure of one institution or component triggers a cascading collapse across the entire financial system, as opposed to risk contained within a single entity.

## How Systemic Risk Builds

- **Interconnectedness** - Financial institutions are linked through lending, derivatives, and counterparty relationships. One failure propagates through the chain.
- **Leverage** - High system-wide leverage amplifies losses and reduces the capacity to absorb shocks.
- **Concentration** - When many participants hold similar positions, forced liquidations create feedback loops.
- **Contagion** - Loss of confidence spreads from one market or institution to others, even if fundamentally unrelated.

## Historical Examples

- **2008 Financial Crisis** - The collapse of Lehman Brothers triggered a global credit freeze, bank failures, and the deepest recession since the 1930s.
- **1998 LTCM Crisis** - A single hedge fund's failure threatened the stability of the global financial system.
- **2022 Crypto Contagion** - The collapse of LUNA/UST, Three Arrows Capital, and FTX created cascading failures across the crypto ecosystem.

## Trading Relevance

Individual traders cannot control systemic risk, but they must account for it. During systemic events, correlations spike toward 1.0, diversification fails, [[liquidity-risk]] surges, and [[counterparty-risk]] becomes critical. Maintaining adequate cash reserves, using regulated custodians, and limiting leverage are the primary defenses.

## Regulatory Response

Post-2008 regulations (Dodd-Frank, Basel III) aim to reduce systemic risk through higher capital requirements, stress testing, and central clearing of derivatives. However, new forms of systemic risk continually emerge.

## AI as a Systemic Risk Channel (2025-2026)

A new class of systemic risk is being recognized at the institutional level: AI models, AI-driven labor displacement, and AI-mediated market activity opening transmission paths that the post-2008 regulatory architecture was not designed to absorb.

**ESRB December 2025 advisory** — The European Systemic Risk Board's report on AI and systemic risk identifies AI as creating new financial-stability channels distinct from prior threats. The four channels flagged are (Source: [[2026-04-22-gap-finder-possible-ai-fueled-global-job-loss-reces]]):

- **Overreliance** — financial institutions delegating risk decisions to a small number of foundation models without independent validation
- **Complexity** — opacity of model behavior under stress, complicating supervisory oversight
- **Liquidity cascades** — correlated AI-driven trading reactions converting localized shocks into market-wide forced selling
- **Model homogeneity** — many participants running similar models trained on similar data, eroding the diversity of views that normally provides market resilience

**The "AI Layoff Trap" (Wharton / Boston University, Falk & Tsoukalas)** — A peer-reviewed mathematical model showing that a competitive automation race between firms can trigger a demand-collapse spiral: each firm rationally automates to cut labor costs, but the aggregate effect destroys consumer demand, which forces further automation, and so on. The paper finds that only a Pigouvian automation tax — pricing in the externality of destroyed consumer demand — breaks the loop. This formalizes a systemic-risk mechanism in which the labor channel, not the credit channel, is the source of cascading failure (Source: [[2026-04-22-gap-finder-possible-ai-fueled-global-job-loss-reces]]). See [[ai-layoff-trap]].

**Citrini's "2028 Global Intelligence Crisis" scenario** — Citrini Research's February 2026 publication is now the named tail scenario in institutional discourse. The scenario specifies 10%+ unemployment combined with roughly a 40% equity decline if superintelligent AI deployment outpaces labor reabsorption. Its release coincided with a tech sell-off across services (finance, real estate, media, medical, legal). Unlike abstract warnings, the scenario is quantitative enough to be priced and hedged, and is now referenced by traders sizing tail-risk positions (Source: [[2026-04-22-gap-finder-possible-ai-fueled-global-job-loss-reces]]). See [[citrini-2028-global-intelligence-crisis]] and [[citrini-research]].

**Institutional adoption signal — Norges Bank Investment Management** — In April 2026 the world's largest sovereign wealth fund embedded LLMs into daily portfolio risk management, with portfolio managers receiving daily AI-generated risk assessments. This is significant because it both validates the productive use of AI in risk processes and instantiates the ESRB's "overreliance" and "model homogeneity" concerns at scale: when the largest allocators use the same handful of models to filter the same risk signals, idiosyncratic views thin out (Source: [[2026-04-22-gap-finder-possible-ai-fueled-global-job-loss-reces]]).

**Transmission mechanisms specific to AI labor displacement**:

- **Service-sector multiplier (3-5x, 2-4 quarter lag)** — each AI-displaced tech job propagates to 3-5 service jobs (restaurants, childcare, retail, transportation) on a delayed schedule, meaning [[employment]] data understates true stress in the early innings. See [[service-sector-multiplier]].
- **Capital-vs-labor asymmetry** — AI productivity gains accrue to capital immediately while labor losses materialize on a 2-4 quarter delay. Markets can rally on capex even as the labor channel is silently building toward a demand shock. See [[capital-vs-labor-asymmetry]] and [[ai-driven-demand-destruction]].
- **Geographic concentration** — losses concentrate in tech hubs ([[tech-hub-concentration-risk]]), creating regional fiscal stress that bleeds into [[bank-run]] vulnerability and muni bond markets in ways the 2008-era playbook does not anticipate.

Goldman Sachs' forecast of 11M US jobs displaced (6%+ of the workforce) provides the order-of-magnitude scale at which the labor channel becomes a [[fed-policy]]-relevant systemic concern (Source: [[2026-04-22-gap-finder-possible-ai-fueled-global-job-loss-reces]]). See also [[goldman-sachs]] and [[recession]].
