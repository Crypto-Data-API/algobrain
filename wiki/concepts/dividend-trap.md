A **dividend trap** (or **yield trap**) is a stock whose unusually high [[dividend-yield|dividend yield]] lures income investors in, even though the dividend is unsustainable and likely to be **cut**. When the cut comes, the investor is hit twice: the income they bought the stock for shrinks or disappears, *and* the share price typically falls on the announcement. It is the single most important risk in naive, yield-chasing [[dividend-investing|income investing]].

## Why a high yield can be a warning, not a gift

Yield is a fraction: `Yield = Dividend per share / Price`. Because the price sits in the denominator, a yield can spike for two very different reasons:

- **Good reason** — the company is genuinely generous and the payout is well covered.
- **Bad reason** — the **price has fallen** because the market already doubts the dividend. The falling price mechanically *inflates* the trailing yield, making a troubled stock look like a bargain.

The trap is mistaking the second case for the first. A yield far above sector peers is more often the market pricing in a probable cut than a free lunch. This is the income-investing cousin of the **value trap** (a stock that looks cheap because it deserves to be).

## Warning signs of an unsustainable dividend

| Signal | Healthy | Red flag |
|---|---|---|
| [[dividend-payout-ratio|Payout ratio]] (earnings) | < 60% | > 100% (paying out more than it earns) |
| FCF payout ratio | < 70% | > 100% (dividend funded by debt or asset sales) |
| [[dividend-coverage-ratio|Coverage ratio]] | comfortably > 1 | near or below 1 |
| [[dividend-yield|Yield]] vs sector | in range | far above peers |
| Revenue / earnings / FCF trend | stable or rising | declining |
| Balance sheet | conservative | rising debt, covenant pressure |
| Recent dividend history | steadily rising | frozen or just-cut |

A single flag is not damning; several together — especially a payout ratio over 100% *plus* falling cash flow *plus* a sky-high yield — is the classic trap signature.

## The dividend cut itself

A **dividend cut** is a reduction in the per-share payout; a **suspension** sets it to zero. Common triggers:

- A **recession** or industry downturn that collapses earnings (cyclicals, financials, energy are frequent culprits).
- **Balance-sheet stress** — debt covenants or refinancing needs force cash to be conserved.
- A **business model in structural decline**, where the old payout no longer fits the cash flows.
- A **large acquisition or capital-spending need** that management chooses to fund by trimming the dividend.

Because a steady dividend is a signal of confidence, **cutting it is read as a confession**. Share prices commonly drop sharply on a cut announcement, and the stock can be sold off by income funds whose mandates forbid holding non-payers — a forced-selling cascade that compounds the loss.

## Clearly-hypothetical example

A fictional "Hypothetical Telecom Ltd." trades at $40 paying a $4.00 annual dividend — a headline **10% yield** that screens as the most attractive income name in its sector (peers yield ~4%). Closer inspection shows earnings per share of only $3.00 (a 133% payout ratio), declining free cash flow, and rising debt used partly to fund the dividend. An income investor who buys for the 10% yield is walking into a trap: a year later the board halves the dividend to $2.00 to protect the balance sheet, the shares fall to $24 on the news, and the holder is left with both a ~40% capital loss and a yield-on-cost of just 5%. (Illustrative only — not a real company or data.)

## How to avoid dividend traps

- **Screen for sustainability, not yield.** Prioritise the [[dividend-payout-ratio|payout ratio]], [[dividend-coverage-ratio|coverage]], and free-cash-flow trend over the headline number.
- **Favour dividend *growth* over raw yield** — the documented quality premium attaches to growers, not high-yielders (see [[dividend-investing]] and [[dividend-aristocrats]]).
- **Treat an outlier yield as a question, not an answer.** "Why is the market demanding this much yield?" is usually the right first question.
- **Diversify** across sectors so one cut does not wreck the income stream.
- **Pre-commit to selling on a cut** — by then the thesis is already broken; holding "for the recovery" often compounds the loss.

## Limitations

- Not every high yield is a trap — some sectors (REITs, utilities, MLPs) *structurally* pay high yields; the test is coverage and sustainability, not the number alone.
- Even [[dividend-aristocrats|Aristocrats]] with decades-long streaks can cut; a long history lowers but does not eliminate the risk.
- Sustainability metrics are backward-looking; a sudden shock (pandemic, regulatory change) can force a cut on a previously well-covered payer.
