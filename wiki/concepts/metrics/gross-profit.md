**Gross profit** is the dollar amount a company earns after deducting the direct costs of producing its goods or services from revenue. It is the second line of the income statement and the first measure of how efficiently a company converts sales into profit before overhead, interest, and taxes.

## Formula

$$\text{Gross Profit} = \text{Revenue} - \text{COGS} \qquad \text{Gross Margin} = \frac{\text{Gross Profit}}{\text{Revenue}} \times 100\%$$

Where [[cost-of-goods-sold|COGS]] includes only the direct costs of production: raw materials, direct labour, and manufacturing overhead. It excludes indirect costs like SG&A, R&D, marketing, interest, and taxes.

## Worked Example (illustrative round numbers)

A hardware company over two years:

| Line item | Year 1 | Year 2 | Change |
|-----------|--------|--------|--------|
| [[revenue|Revenue]] | $1,000M | $1,300M | +30% |
| [[cost-of-goods-sold|COGS]] | $700M | $845M | +20.7% |
| **Gross profit** | **$300M** | **$455M** | **+51.7%** |
| [[gross-margin|Gross margin]] | 30.0% | 35.0% | +5.0 pts |

Gross profit grew 51.7% while revenue grew only 30% — gross profit is *outpacing* revenue, and margin expanded 5 points. That is the signature of **operating leverage and pricing power**: each new dollar of sales is dropping more to the gross line, usually a leading indicator of [[earnings-per-share|EPS]] upgrades. The opposite pattern (gross profit lagging revenue, margin compressing) is an early erosion warning even when the top line still looks healthy.

## Gross Profit vs Gross Margin (Terminology)

Australian and US accounting differ in terminology:
- **Australia**: "Gross Profit" can refer to either the dollar amount or the percentage
- **United States**: "Gross Profit" is strictly the dollar amount, while "[[gross-margin|Gross Margin]]" is the percentage (Gross Profit / Revenue × 100%)

This wiki uses the US convention: gross profit = dollars, gross margin = percentage.

## "The Oxygen of Any Business"

Fred McNaught calls gross profit "the oxygen of any business" — without sufficient gross profit, a company cannot fund operations, pay overhead, service debt, or return cash to shareholders. The most desirable signal is gross profit that **stays constant or rises as revenue grows**, indicating pricing power and scale economies. (Source: [[fred-gross-profit-article]])

## Profit Hierarchy

Gross profit sits at the top of the profit hierarchy:

```
Revenue
  − COGS              → Gross Profit
  − Operating Expenses → Operating Income (EBIT)
  − Interest, Taxes   → Net Income
```

When operating margin sits close to gross margin, it indicates a business with low overhead — common in software and asset-light models.

## Catalysts Affecting Gross Profit

Per Fred's analysis, key drivers include: cost of materials and labour, production efficiency, pricing changes, new product launches, competition, market demand, supply chain disruptions, regulatory changes, and warranty/return claims. (Source: [[fred-gross-profit-article]])

## Typical Gross Margins by Industry

| Sector | Typical gross margin | Driver |
|--------|----------------------|--------|
| Software / SaaS | 70 – 90% | Near-zero marginal cost of a copy |
| Pharma (branded) | 60 – 80% | Patent-protected pricing power |
| Consumer brands | 40 – 60% | Brand premium over input cost |
| Industrials / manufacturing | 20 – 40% | Material- and labour-heavy |
| Retail | 20 – 35% | Thin markups, volume model |
| Grocery / distribution | 5 – 25% | Commodity goods, intense competition |

Compare gross margin only within a sector; a 30% margin is excellent for a grocer and poor for a software firm.

## The Gross-Profitability Factor (Novy-Marx)

The absolute dollar figure of gross profit is the building block of a well-documented academic quality factor. Robert Novy-Marx (2013) showed that **gross profitability = gross profit / total assets** predicts cross-sectional stock returns about as well as book-to-market predicts value, and that "profitable firms generate significantly higher returns than unprofitable ones, despite having higher valuations." Gross profit (above SG&A, R&D, interest and tax) is used rather than [[net-income]] precisely because it is the cleanest, least manipulated measure of true economic productivity. See [[gross-profitability-factor]].

## Trading Relevance

For active traders the **direction of dollar gross profit relative to revenue** matters: gross profit growing *faster* than revenue confirms operating leverage and pricing power and tends to precede [[earnings-per-share|EPS]] upgrades, while gross profit lagging revenue is an early erosion signal. Quality-factor screens (see [[gross-profitability-factor]]) rank the universe on gross profit scaled by assets and have historically outperformed. Because gross profit sits above SG&A, R&D, interest and tax, it is the income-statement line least exposed to discretionary accounting, so it is a cleaner basis for cross-company and time-series comparison than [[net-income]].

## Pitfalls

1. **COGS classification varies** — firms differ on what they push into COGS vs operating expenses (e.g. depreciation, fulfilment, support), which distorts cross-company gross-margin comparisons. Read the accounting policy notes.
2. **Mix shifts** — a blended gross margin can move purely from product/segment mix, masking a decline in the core product.
3. **Revenue recognition games** — channel stuffing or aggressive timing inflates revenue (and gross profit) ahead of returns.
4. **Dollar vs percent confusion** — see the terminology note above; always state whether you mean the dollar figure or the [[gross-margin|margin]].
