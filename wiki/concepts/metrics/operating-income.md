**Operating income** (also called Operating Profit or EBIT — Earnings Before Interest and Taxes) is the profit a company generates from its core business operations after deducting both direct costs (COGS) and indirect operating expenses (SG&A, R&D, depreciation). It represents profitability before financing decisions and tax effects.

## Formula

```
Operating Income (EBIT) = Revenue − COGS − Operating Expenses
                       = Gross Profit − Operating Expenses
```

Where Operating Expenses include: Selling, General & Administrative (SG&A), Research & Development (R&D), depreciation, amortisation, and other operating costs. EBIT excludes interest expense, interest income, taxes, and any non-operating items (gains/losses on asset sales, foreign exchange, etc.).

## Why It Matters

Operating income isolates the performance of the underlying business from the effects of how it is financed (debt vs equity) and the jurisdiction it operates in (tax rates). This makes it the best metric for comparing the operational efficiency of two companies in the same industry but with different capital structures.

It is the numerator in two important metrics:
- **[[operating-margin|Operating Margin]]** = Operating Income / Revenue
- **EBIT/Interest Coverage** = Operating Income / Interest Expense (a credit quality measure)

## EBIT vs EBITDA

[[ebitda|EBITDA]] adds back depreciation and amortisation to EBIT. EBIT is more conservative because it acknowledges that physical assets (PP&E) wear out and need to be replaced. Warren Buffett famously prefers EBIT over EBITDA, asking "does management think the tooth fairy pays for capital expenditures?"

## Profit Hierarchy

Operating income sits in the middle of the income statement profit ladder:

```
Revenue
  − COGS              → Gross Profit
  − Operating Expenses → Operating Income (EBIT)  ← you are here
  − Interest, Taxes   → Net Income
```

## Limitations

Operating income can be distorted by:
- One-off restructuring charges or impairments classified as operating
- Aggressive cost capitalisation (R&D, software development) reducing reported expenses
- Stock-based compensation not always treated consistently across companies

For a cleaner cash-based view, compare EBIT against [[free-cash-flow|Free Cash Flow]].

## Trading Relevance

Because EBIT strips out capital structure and tax jurisdiction, it is the profit line used in the valuation multiples traders rely on for **cross-company and M&A comparison** — most notably **EV/EBIT** and the [[ebitda|EV/EBITDA]] cousin. A low EV/EBIT versus peers is a classic value screen (and the profitability half of Joel Greenblatt's "Magic Formula," which ranks stocks by earnings yield (EBIT/EV) and return on capital). For directional trading, **operating leverage** is the key idea: a company whose EBIT grows faster than revenue is converting incremental sales into outsized profit, which tends to drive earnings upgrades and re-ratings; the same leverage cuts the other way in a downturn, making high-fixed-cost operators (airlines, semis, industrials) violent cyclical trades. EBIT is also the numerator of interest-coverage ratios, so a deteriorating EBIT trend is an early credit / distressed-equity short signal.
