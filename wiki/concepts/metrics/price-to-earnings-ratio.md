The **price-to-earnings ratio** (P/E ratio) is the most widely used [[valuation]] metric in equity analysis, calculated by dividing a company's stock price by its earnings per share (EPS). It tells investors how much they are paying for each dollar of earnings and serves as a quick gauge of whether a stock is cheap, fairly priced, or expensive relative to its earnings power.

## Overview

The P/E ratio has been central to [[fundamental-analysis]] since [[benjamin-graham|Benjamin Graham]] and [[david-dodd|David Dodd]] formalized [[value-investing]] in the 1930s. Graham famously advocated buying stocks with low P/E ratios as a margin of safety against overpaying. The ratio remains the first number most investors check when evaluating a stock.

A P/E of 15 means investors are paying $15 for every $1 of annual earnings. The inverse (E/P = 1/15 = 6.7%) is the "earnings yield," which can be compared directly to bond yields to assess relative value between stocks and bonds.

The S&P 500's historical average P/E is approximately 15-17x trailing earnings. During market bubbles (e.g., dot-com in 2000 with P/Es above 30x), stocks are generally overvalued; during crashes and recessions, P/Es compress to 10x or below, signaling potential value.

## How It Works

**Trailing P/E (TTM):** Stock price divided by the sum of the last four quarters' reported EPS. This uses actual, audited earnings and is the most common variant.

**Forward P/E:** Stock price divided by consensus analyst estimates of next year's EPS. Forward P/E is more forward-looking but depends on the accuracy of analyst forecasts, which can be wildly wrong.

**Shiller CAPE (Cyclically Adjusted P/E):** Developed by Robert Shiller, this divides the current price by the average of inflation-adjusted earnings over the past 10 years. By smoothing out business cycle fluctuations, CAPE provides a more stable long-term valuation measure. Historical CAPE above 30 has preceded poor long-term returns; below 15 has preceded strong returns.

**Interpreting P/E Ratios:**

| P/E Range | General Interpretation |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Below 10 | Deeply undervalued or distressed; earnings may be at risk |
| 10-15 | Potentially undervalued; classic Graham territory |
| 15-20 | Fair value for average-growth companies |
| 20-30 | Growth premium; justified only if earnings growth is strong |
| Above 30 | Expensive; requires exceptional growth to justify |

**Key Caveats:**
- P/E is meaningless for companies with negative earnings
- [[earnings-management|Accounting manipulation]] can distort reported EPS
- Different industries have structurally different P/E ranges (tech > utilities)
- High-growth companies warrant higher P/Es because future earnings will (presumably) be much larger
- One-time charges or gains can distort trailing P/E

## Trading Applications

**Value Screening:** [[value-investing|Value investors]] use low P/E ratios as a primary screen. Graham recommended buying stocks with P/Es below 15 and preferably below 10, combined with other [[financial-statement-analysis|financial quality]] checks. (Source: [[book-the-intelligent-investor]])

**Sector Comparison:** P/E is most useful when comparing companies within the same industry. A bank trading at 8x earnings vs. a peer at 12x may signal relative undervaluation. Cross-sector comparisons are less meaningful because growth rates and capital structures differ.

**Market Timing (Shiller CAPE):** While single-stock P/Es are noisy, the broad market's CAPE ratio has predictive power for long-term (10-year) returns. Investors use it to gauge whether overall equity exposure should be overweight or underweight.

**PEG Ratio:** The P/E-to-Growth ratio (PEG) normalizes P/E by the earnings growth rate. A PEG of 1.0 means the P/E equals the growth rate, considered "fair." [[peter-lynch|Peter Lynch]] popularized PEG as a way to find growth stocks at reasonable prices. (Source: [[book-one-up-on-wall-street]])

**Earnings Yield vs. Bond Yield:** Comparing E/P to the 10-year Treasury yield (the "Fed Model") offers a rough gauge of stock vs. bond attractiveness. When earnings yield significantly exceeds bond yield, stocks are relatively cheap, and vice versa.
