Return on Invested Capital (ROIC) measures the after-tax profit a business generates relative to the total capital — debt and equity — actively deployed in its operations. Among profitability metrics, ROIC is widely regarded as the purest gauge of underlying business quality because it is neutral to capital structure and strips out the effects of excess cash and one-off items.

## Formula

$$\text{ROIC} = \frac{\text{NOPAT}}{\text{Invested Capital}}$$

where:

$$\text{NOPAT} = \text{Operating Income} \times (1 - \text{Tax Rate})$$

$$\text{Invested Capital} = \text{Total Debt} + \text{Total Equity} - \text{Cash and Equivalents}$$

NOPAT (Net Operating Profit After Tax) strips out interest expense and one-time items so the numerator reflects the true operating return. Subtracting cash from invested capital removes idle balance-sheet cash that isn't actually deployed in the business. Return on Capital Employed (ROCE) is a close European cousin that typically uses EBIT in the numerator instead of NOPAT.

## Why ROIC Is the Gold Standard

[[return-on-equity]] is distorted by leverage. [[return-on-assets]] is distorted by excess cash and non-operating assets. ROIC corrects both: it asks "given every dollar of capital actually at work in operations, what after-tax return does the business earn?" That property makes it the favored metric of quality investors, McKinsey's valuation framework, and practitioners of [[intrinsic-value]] investing.

## ROIC vs. WACC — The Value Creation Test

The single most important comparison in corporate finance:

$$\text{Value Creation} \iff \text{ROIC} > \text{WACC}$$

A business creates shareholder value only when its return on invested capital exceeds its weighted average cost of capital. A firm with 8% ROIC and 10% WACC is actively destroying value as it grows — every new dollar invested earns less than it costs. Conversely, a firm with 25% ROIC and 8% WACC compounds intrinsic value ferociously. This spread (ROIC − WACC) is the foundation of **Economic Value Added (EVA)**.

## Quality Investing Screens

Quality and compounder-focused investors commonly screen for:

- ROIC > 15% sustained over 5–10 years
- Minimal year-to-year variance (stable competitive position)
- Rising or flat ROIC during reinvestment (incremental ROIC remains high)
- ROIC meaningfully above peers in the same industry

Sustained high ROIC is the statistical fingerprint of a durable competitive moat.

## Typical Ranges

- **Exceptional (wide-moat compounders)**: 20%+
- **Strong quality businesses**: 12–20%
- **Average**: 6–12%
- **Value-destroying**: below WACC (typically <7–8%)

## Limitations

- Sensitive to definition — practitioners disagree on whether to include operating leases, goodwill, or deferred taxes.
- Accounting ROIC can diverge from cash-based economic reality; cross-check with [[free-cash-flow]] yield on invested capital.
- High reported ROIC can reflect mature businesses with little reinvestment runway — growth matters too.
- Goodwill-heavy acquirers look artificially weak if goodwill is included in invested capital.
