**Capital expenditure** (CapEx) is the cash a company spends to acquire, build, or upgrade long-lived physical assets — property, plant, and equipment (PP&E) such as factories, machinery, servers, vehicles, and buildings. Because these assets deliver value over many years, CapEx is **capitalised** on the [[balance-sheet]] and expensed gradually through [[depreciation]], rather than being charged to profit all at once. It appears as an outflow in the **investing activities** section of the [[cash-flow-statement]].

## CapEx vs. operating expense

The defining distinction in accounting is between capital and operating spending:

| | Capital expenditure (CapEx) | Operating expense (OpEx) |
|---|---|---|
| **Benefit period** | Multiple years | Current period only |
| **Examples** | New plant, machinery, data-centre buildout | Rent, wages, utilities, marketing |
| **Income-statement treatment** | Spread over time via [[depreciation]] | Expensed immediately |
| **Where the full cash hits** | Investing section of the [[cash-flow-statement]] | Operating section / [[income-statement]] |

Because CapEx is capitalised, a large outlay does **not** depress reported profit in the year it is spent — only the year's depreciation slice does. This is why a fast-growing company can show healthy [[net-income]] while bleeding cash on construction.

## Maintenance vs. growth CapEx

Analysts split CapEx by purpose:

- **Maintenance CapEx** — spending needed just to keep the existing asset base running and revenue flat (replacing worn machines). A rough proxy is the period's [[depreciation]] charge.
- **Growth CapEx** — spending to *expand* capacity and drive future revenue.

The distinction matters because maintenance CapEx is an unavoidable cost of staying in business, whereas growth CapEx is discretionary investment whose payoff should be judged against the company's [[return-on-invested-capital]]. Companies rarely disclose the split, so analysts estimate it.

## Why it matters to a stock investor

- **It is what separates EBITDA from real cash.** [[ebitda]] deliberately ignores CapEx, which flatters capital-intensive businesses. Subtracting CapEx is exactly how you get to [[free-cash-flow]]:

```
Free Cash Flow = Cash Flow from Operations − Capital Expenditures
```

  A railroad, telecom, or chip foundry with huge CapEx can post strong EBITDA yet generate little free cash for shareholders.
- **Capital intensity defines the business model.** High CapEx relative to revenue (capital-intensive: utilities, airlines, semiconductors) means more cash must be reinvested just to compete; low CapEx (software, asset-light brands) leaves more cash for dividends and buybacks. A useful ratio is **CapEx ÷ Revenue** or **CapEx ÷ Operating Cash Flow**.
- **It is a forward signal of growth — and risk.** Rising growth CapEx signals management's confidence in future demand, but it ties up cash and can destroy value if the new capacity earns less than its cost of capital. Over-building near the top of a cycle is a classic [[capital-allocation]] failure.
- **CapEx vs. depreciation gap.** When CapEx persistently exceeds depreciation, the asset base is *growing*; when it runs below depreciation, the company may be under-investing and quietly shrinking its productive capacity.

## How it is used

- **Free-cash-flow valuation.** CapEx is the key subtraction in [[free-cash-flow]] and discounted-cash-flow models; small changes in the CapEx assumption swing intrinsic-value estimates materially.
- **Quality screening.** Comparing CapEx to depreciation and to operating cash flow over several years reveals whether reported profits are backed by genuine cash generation (see [[financial-statement-analysis]]).
- **Peer comparison.** CapEx intensity is compared across companies in the same industry to spot who is out-investing (or under-investing) relative to peers.
- **Lumpiness adjustment.** Because a single mega-project can distort one year, analysts average CapEx over a full cycle rather than annualising one print.

## Worked example (hypothetical)

Continuing with **Acme Co.** (an entirely hypothetical company), in one fiscal year all figures illustrative:

| Line | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Cash flow from operations | $147.50 |
| − Capital expenditures (CapEx) | $50 |
| **= Free cash flow** | **$97.50** |
| of which: maintenance CapEx (≈ depreciation) | ~$40 |
| of which: growth CapEx | ~$10 |

Acme spent **$50** building and replacing equipment. Only the **$40** depreciation slice hit the [[income-statement]] this year — the other portion sits on the [[balance-sheet]] as new PP&E and will be expensed in future years. Because roughly $40 of the $50 is just *maintaining* the asset base, only about **$10** is true expansion investment, which should be judged against the extra profit Acme expects the new capacity to earn.

## Limitations

- **No purpose disclosure.** The maintenance/growth split is rarely reported, so the most decision-relevant breakdown must be estimated.
- **Lumpy and cyclical.** A single year is a poor guide; trend it.
- **Capitalisation choices.** What counts as CapEx vs. an immediately-expensed cost (e.g. some software and R&D) varies and can differ under [[ifrs-vs-gaap|IFRS vs. US GAAP]], affecting comparability.
- **Not the same as acquisitions.** Buying whole businesses (M&A) also sits in the investing section but is reported separately from CapEx; do not conflate the two.
