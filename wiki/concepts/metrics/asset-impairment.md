**Asset impairment** is the accounting recognition that an asset is worth **less than the value at which it is carried** on the [[balance-sheet]]. When the carrying (book) value of an asset exceeds the amount the company can recover from using or selling it, the firm writes the asset down to its recoverable amount and books a one-time, usually **non-cash, impairment charge** that reduces [[net-income]] in that period. Impairments most commonly hit [[goodwill]], other [[intangible-assets]], property/plant/equipment, and long-lived assets after an acquisition sours or a business deteriorates.

## Impairment vs. depreciation/amortization

These are easy to confuse but distinct:

- **[[depreciation]] / amortization** is the *routine, predictable* spreading of an asset's cost over its useful life — small, recurring, planned.
- **Impairment** is a *lumpy, unplanned, one-off* write-down triggered when something goes wrong — the asset's value drops faster than the depreciation schedule assumed.

Both reduce the asset's carrying value and both are non-cash, but analysts treat them very differently: recurring D&A is part of normal operations, whereas an impairment is a discrete signal that an earlier investment (often an acquisition) is underperforming.

## When and how it is recognised

The mechanics differ slightly by regime (see [[ifrs-vs-gaap]]) but follow the same logic:

1. **Trigger / test.** Goodwill and indefinite-life [[intangible-assets]] are tested for impairment **at least annually**, and any asset is tested whenever there are *indicators* of impairment — a sharp drop in demand, a falling share price below book value, technological obsolescence, restructuring, or a souring acquisition.
2. **Compare carrying value to recoverable amount.** Under IFRS (IAS 36) the recoverable amount is the higher of fair-value-less-costs and value-in-use (discounted future cash flows). US GAAP uses a broadly similar test.
3. **Write down and charge to earnings.** If carrying value exceeds the recoverable amount, the difference is the **impairment loss**, recorded as an expense on the [[income-statement]] and as a reduction of the asset on the [[balance-sheet]].
4. **Reversals.** IFRS permits later reversal of impairments for most assets (not goodwill) if conditions improve; US GAAP generally **prohibits** reversals — another cross-border comparability issue.

## Why it matters to investors

- **A confession of overpayment.** A [[goodwill]] impairment is effectively management admitting it paid too much for a past acquisition. Large write-downs cluster after the peak of M&A cycles.
- **Non-cash, but not meaningless.** The charge moves no cash in the period — the cash already left when the asset was bought or built — but it flags poor capital allocation, central to [[financial-statement-analysis]].
- **Earnings quality.** Impairments are excluded from [[ebitda]] and are often added back in "adjusted earnings," so a company leaning heavily on adjusted figures while taking repeated impairments deserves scrutiny. They depress [[return-on-assets]] in the charge year by cutting net income.
- **Cash-flow neutrality.** Because it is non-cash, an impairment is added back in the cash-flow statement and does **not** reduce [[free-cash-flow]] — a key reason analysts look past the headline net-income hit to the cash picture.
- **Book-value signal.** A write-down brings reported equity closer to [[tangible-book-value]], sometimes belatedly confirming what the market already priced in.

## Worked example (hypothetical)

Suppose **Orion Industries** (hypothetical) carries a factory and related [[goodwill]] at **$500** combined. A downturn in its end-market means the unit's estimated recoverable value falls to **$340**. Orion books a **$160 impairment charge**: [[net-income]] for the period drops by $160 and the carrying value on the [[balance-sheet]] falls from $500 to $340. **No cash leaves** in the impairment period, so it is added back on the cash-flow statement and [[free-cash-flow]] is unaffected — the economic damage occurred earlier, when Orion deployed the capital. These figures are illustrative.

## Limitations

- **Subjective and often late.** Impairment timing depends on management's estimates of future cash flows and discount rates, so write-downs are frequently delayed past the point the economics clearly deteriorated.
- **A backward-looking signal.** By the time an impairment is booked, the market has usually already repriced the asset; it confirms bad news rather than predicting it.
- **Gameable through "big-bath" accounting.** Management may bundle large impairments into an already-bad quarter (often around a CEO change) to clear the decks and flatter future results.
- **Regime differences.** Reversibility, testing methods, and goodwill treatment differ under [[ifrs-vs-gaap|IFRS vs. US GAAP]], reducing comparability.
