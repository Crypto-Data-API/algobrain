**Operating margin** measures how much profit a company earns from its core business operations, per dollar of revenue, after accounting for both the direct cost of goods sold *and* operating expenses like R&D, sales & marketing, and general administration. It is the clearest single-number view of **operational efficiency** — how well management turns sales into profit before financing and tax decisions enter the picture.

## Formula

$$\text{Operating Margin} = \frac{\text{Operating Income (EBIT)}}{\text{Revenue}} \times 100\%$$

Operating income, also called **EBIT** (earnings before interest and taxes), is revenue minus COGS minus all operating expenses (SG&A, R&D, D&A). Both figures come from the income statement.

## Relationship to Gross Margin

Operating margin is a stricter cousin of [[gross-margin]]:

- **Gross margin** = (Revenue − COGS) / Revenue — deducts only the direct cost of producing the product.
- **Operating margin** = Gross margin further reduced by SG&A, R&D, and other operating expenses.

The gap between the two reveals *overhead intensity*. A company with a 70% gross margin but only a 10% operating margin is spending 60% of revenue on SG&A and R&D — which could be sound investment for future growth or an efficiency problem, depending on whether revenue is compounding in response.

## Typical Ranges by Industry

- **Software / SaaS**: 20 – 40%+ (very scalable; best-in-class > 35%)
- **Pharma / big biotech**: 25 – 35%
- **Luxury goods**: 20 – 30%
- **Semiconductors**: 15 – 35% (highly cyclical)
- **Mature industrials**: 8 – 15%
- **Retail**: 3 – 10% (high-volume, low-margin)
- **Grocery and supermarkets**: 1 – 4%
- **Airlines**: 0 – 10%, frequently negative in downturns

Comparisons are only meaningful within an industry.

## Interpretation

- **Expanding operating margins** are a leading signal of **economies of scale**, pricing power, or cost discipline — often a precursor to upward earnings revisions.
- **Contracting operating margins** signal cost inflation, competitive pressure, or sales mix shift — and often precede downward EPS revisions even when revenue is still growing.
- Watch the **trend over 8–12 quarters** rather than a single data point; one-off restructuring charges can distort a given quarter.

## Role in DuPont Decomposition

Operating margin (or its sibling, net margin) is the profitability leg of the DuPont identity:

$$\text{ROE} = \text{Net Margin} \times \text{Asset Turnover} \times \text{Equity Multiplier}$$

This decomposition lets analysts separate **how** a firm earns its [[return-on-equity]] — through fat margins, high asset efficiency, or leverage. Two firms with the same ROE can have completely different risk profiles depending on which lever is doing the work.

## Trading Relevance

Operating margin is one of the highest-signal forward indicators in [[fundamental-analysis]] because it captures the *full* cost base of the core business yet still sits above financing and tax noise. For the **Rule of 40** SaaS screen, operating margin is half the equation (revenue growth + operating margin ≥ 40%), letting growth investors trade off expansion against profitability. Margin-expansion stories — a software company crossing into GAAP profitability, an industrial cutting fixed costs — are among the most reliable multi-quarter long setups, since the operating leverage compounds into accelerating [[earnings-per-share|EPS]]. Conversely, margin contraction while revenue still grows is a textbook short tell that demand is being bought with discounts or cost inflation cannot be passed through. Traders watch the 8–12 quarter trend rather than a single print, and pair the read with [[free-cash-flow]] to confirm the margin is cash-backed rather than driven by capitalising costs.

## Limitations

1. **Accounting flexibility** — classification of R&D, capitalized software development, and restructuring charges can shift expenses in and out of operating income.
2. **Industry-specific** — raw cross-industry comparisons are meaningless.
3. **Excludes capital intensity** — two firms can have identical operating margins but very different [[return-on-invested-capital]] if one requires far more capital to generate each dollar of sales.
4. **Masks mix shifts** — stable overall margin can hide declining core business offset by higher-margin new segments.
