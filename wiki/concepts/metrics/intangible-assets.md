**Intangible assets** are identifiable, non-physical assets a company controls and expects to generate future economic benefit from — things like patents, trademarks, copyrights, licences, customer lists, software, and acquired technology. They sit on the [[balance-sheet]] alongside tangible assets (property, plant, and equipment) and, separately, [[goodwill]]. In a modern economy dominated by brands, software, and intellectual property, intangibles are often a company's most valuable resources — yet accounting rules capture them inconsistently, which makes them a frequent source of valuation distortion.

## Identifiable intangibles vs. goodwill

A key distinction in [[financial-statement-analysis]]:

- **Identifiable intangible assets** can be separated and valued on their own — a patent, a brand name, a broadcasting licence, a customer contract. These are recorded individually when acquired.
- **[[goodwill]]** is the *unidentifiable* residual premium paid in an acquisition that cannot be pinned to any specific asset. Accounting treats it as a distinct line, not a normal intangible.

Both are non-physical, but only identifiable intangibles are "intangible assets" in the strict sense; goodwill is reported and tested separately.

## The recognition gap: acquired vs. internally generated

This is the single most important thing to understand about intangibles:

- **Acquired intangibles are recorded.** When a company *buys* a brand, patent, or whole business, the purchase price is allocated to the identifiable intangibles at fair value, and they appear on the balance sheet.
- **Internally generated intangibles usually are not.** Under US GAAP (and largely under IFRS), most internally developed brands, customer relationships, and research costs are **expensed as incurred**, not capitalised. Coca-Cola's own brand, for example, is among the most valuable assets on earth but is almost absent from its balance sheet — see [[ifrs-vs-gaap]] for the partial exception IFRS makes for development (as opposed to research) costs.

The consequence: two companies with identical economics can look very different on paper purely because one *grew* its intangibles and the other *bought* them. This is why [[price-to-book-ratio]] and book-value comparisons are unreliable for brand- and IP-heavy firms.

## Amortization and impairment

- **Finite-life intangibles** (a patent with 12 years left, a fixed-term licence) are **amortised** — written down systematically over their useful life, conceptually the same as [[depreciation]] for physical assets.
- **Indefinite-life intangibles** (some brands, [[goodwill]]) are **not** amortised but are tested for **[[asset-impairment|impairment]]** at least annually. If the asset's recoverable value falls below its carrying value, the company books a non-cash impairment charge that reduces [[net-income]].

## How intangibles are used in analysis

- **Book-value quality.** A balance sheet stuffed with acquired intangibles and goodwill inflates reported equity; [[tangible-book-value]] strips them out to give a more conservative floor.
- **Return distortion.** Capitalised intangibles raise the asset base, *lowering* reported [[return-on-assets]] for acquisitive firms; conversely, firms with huge *unrecorded* internal intangibles can show implausibly high returns on a thin asset base.
- **Moat evidence.** Patents, proprietary technology, and brands are the balance-sheet footprints of an [[economic-moat]] — though the most durable moats are often the *internally built* ones that never appear as assets (see [[qualitative-analysis]]).
- **Adjusted analysts' views.** Some investors capitalise R&D and advertising themselves to "fix" the accounting and compare firms on a consistent basis.

## Worked example (hypothetical)

Suppose **NovaSoft** (hypothetical) acquires a smaller rival for **$300**. Of that, **$120** is allocated to identifiable intangibles — **$80** of acquired software (10-year life) and **$40** of a customer list (8-year life) — with the unallocated **$60** premium recorded as [[goodwill]]. NovaSoft will **amortise** the $120 of identifiable intangibles over their lives (e.g. the software at $8/year), reducing [[net-income]] each year, while the $60 of goodwill is left on the books and tested annually for [[asset-impairment]]. Meanwhile NovaSoft's *own* internally written software — possibly worth far more — never appears as an asset because those costs were expensed as incurred. All figures are illustrative.

## Limitations

- **Inconsistent recognition.** The acquired-vs-internal asymmetry means the balance-sheet figure understates the true economic value of intangibles for most organically grown companies.
- **Subjective fair-value estimates.** Allocating an acquisition price across intangibles relies on management and appraiser judgement.
- **Amortization is non-cash and somewhat arbitrary.** Useful-life assumptions are estimates; aggressive or conservative choices shift reported earnings.
- **Cross-border comparability.** Treatment differs under [[ifrs-vs-gaap|IFRS vs. US GAAP]], complicating international comparisons.
