**Shareholder yield** measures the *total* cash a company returns to its owners relative to its market value — not just dividends, but [[share-buybacks|share repurchases]] (and sometimes net debt reduction) as well. It exists because focusing only on [[dividend-yield|dividend yield]] understates how much a company is really giving back: two firms can return identical cash, one as dividends and one as buybacks, yet only the first shows up in a dividend-yield screen.

## Formula

```
Shareholder Yield = (Dividends + Net Buybacks [+ Net Debt Paydown]) / Market Capitalisation
```

- **Net buybacks** = gross shares repurchased − new shares issued. Subtracting issuance matters because heavy [[share-based-compensation|stock-based compensation]] quietly dilutes holders; a company can "buy back" stock yet still grow its share count.
- The **net-debt-paydown** term is optional. Some definitions add it (returning capital to debt holders also benefits equity); the stricter "total payout yield" uses only dividends + net buybacks.

A company paying a 2% [[dividend-yield|dividend yield]] while also retiring 3% of its net shares has a ~5% shareholder yield.

## Why income and total-return investors use it

- **Captures the whole capital return.** Many companies — especially in the US — deliberately favour buybacks over dividends, so a dividend-only view makes them look stingy when they are not.
- **More tax-efficient income.** Buybacks return cash by shrinking the share count, lifting future earnings and dividends per share, and the gain is deferred into **capital gains** rather than taxed as income each year. For a high earner this can beat the same cash paid as an [[qualified-vs-ordinary-dividends|ordinary dividend]].
- **Apples-to-apples comparison.** Comparing a dividend payer with a buyback-heavy peer on dividend yield alone is misleading; shareholder yield puts them on the same scale.
- **A value/quality factor.** High-shareholder-yield baskets have been studied as a value-style factor and a more complete cousin of the dividend-yield factor.

## What to watch for

- **Gross vs net buybacks.** The headline repurchase figure can be cosmetic if the company simultaneously issues lots of stock to employees. Always net out issuance — net buybacks are what reduce your dilution.
- **Buybacks are discretionary.** Unlike a dividend (which boards are reluctant to cut), buybacks can be quietly halted in a downturn — exactly when the share price is low and repurchases would be most valuable. A high shareholder yield is *less reliable* income than the dividend portion alone.
- **Buying back at high valuations destroys value.** Repurchasing overvalued shares transfers value from continuing holders to sellers. A high buyback yield is only good if the shares are not expensive.
- **Debt-funded returns.** Buybacks or dividends financed by borrowing inflate shareholder yield while weakening the balance sheet — sustainable only if [[free-cash-flow-yield|free cash flow]] genuinely covers them.
- **Net-debt-paydown ambiguity.** Including debt reduction can flatter a deleveraging company; know which definition a screen uses before comparing names.

## Clearly-hypothetical example

A fictional "Hypothetical Hardware Inc." has a $10bn market cap. Over the year it pays $200m in dividends (a 2% [[dividend-yield|dividend yield]]) and spends $500m on buybacks while issuing $100m of new stock to employees — so **net buybacks are $400m** (a 4% net buyback yield). Its shareholder yield is therefore ($200m + $400m) / $10bn = **6%**, three times its headline dividend yield. An income-oriented total-return investor would judge it on the full 6%, while noting that only the 2% dividend portion is a firm commitment — the 4% from buybacks could be paused in a recession. (Illustrative only — not a real company or data.)

## Limitations

- Buyback data can be **lumpy and announced-but-not-executed**; a board can authorise a repurchase programme it never fully uses, so trailing actuals beat forward promises.
- The metric says nothing about *whether the price paid was sensible* — value-destroying buybacks still raise the yield.
- It mixes a reliable stream (dividends) with a discretionary one (buybacks); for pure income needs, decompose it rather than reading the single number.
- Definitions differ (with or without net debt paydown; gross vs net of issuance), so cross-source comparisons need care.
