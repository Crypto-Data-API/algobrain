**Market share** is a company's percentage of total revenue (or units sold) within its sector or industry. It indicates competitive positioning and is a key component of assessing an [[economic-moat]]. Companies with rising market share are generally preferable to those losing ground, even if current earnings look similar.

## Formula

$$\text{Market Share (revenue)} = \frac{\text{Company Revenue}}{\text{Total Industry Revenue}} \times 100\%$$

$$\text{Market Share (unit)} = \frac{\text{Company Units Sold}}{\text{Total Industry Units Sold}} \times 100\%$$

Revenue-based share is more common in [[fundamental-analysis]] because it captures pricing power — a company can grow unit share while *losing* revenue share if it competes solely on price. The two diverging (units up, revenue share down) is itself a warning that growth is being bought with discounts, which usually shows up later as [[gross-margin]] compression.

## Why Dominant Share Matters

Fred McNaught applies a **"10% rule"**: a company needs greater than 10% market share in its sector to be considered dominant and to benefit from the pricing power and economies of scale that dominance affords. Dominant market share confers structural advantages that tend to compound:

- **Economies of scale** reduce per-unit costs, widening margins versus subscale rivals.
- **Brand recognition** lowers customer-acquisition cost and supports premium pricing.
- **Supplier bargaining power** improves with purchasing volume.
- **Distribution and network effects** raise switching costs for customers.

These advantages are self-reinforcing, which is why market leaders in stable industries often hold their positions for decades. Sustained high share is one of the most visible fingerprints of a durable [[economic-moat]].

## The Trend Matters More Than the Level

For investors, **tracking changes** in market share over time is more informative than the absolute level. A company gaining 1–2 percentage points of share per year is likely executing well and taking business from weaker competitors, which often precedes [[revenue-growth|revenue acceleration]] and margin expansion. Conversely, a company losing share — even while growing revenue in an expanding market — may be a warning of competitive deterioration that the top-line growth temporarily masks.

## How Analysts Measure It

Reported revenue alone does not reveal share; analysts triangulate company filings against third-party industry data:

- **Technology**: IDC, Gartner, Counterpoint, Canalys
- **Consumer goods / retail**: Circana (IRI), NielsenIQ
- **Automotive**: JATO Dynamics, registration data
- **Pharma**: IQVIA

In concentrated industries where a few players dominate, even small share shifts can have outsized effects on profitability and [[valuation]].

## Trading Relevance

Market-share data is a leading fundamental indicator that often moves *ahead* of reported financials, making it valuable for both directional theses and as an alternative-data edge:

- **Share-gainer longs**: companies steadily taking share in a structurally growing market combine two tailwinds (market growth + share growth) and tend to deliver the most durable [[revenue]] compounding — the core of many growth-investing theses.
- **Share-loser shorts**: eroding share is one of the earliest tells that a moat is breaking, frequently visible in channel-check / scanner / app-download data months before it hits the income statement.
- **Alternative-data front-running**: hedge funds buy syndicated share datasets (IDC, NielsenIQ, credit-card panels) specifically to estimate quarterly share moves before earnings, an informational edge over price-following participants.
- **Pricing-power confirmation**: rising *revenue* share (not just unit share) corroborates pricing power and supports a margin-expansion thesis, whereas unit share bought with price cuts is a value trap signal.

Always cross-check share trends against [[gross-margin]] and [[peer-comparison]] so that "share gains" funded by destroying profitability are not mistaken for genuine competitive strength.
