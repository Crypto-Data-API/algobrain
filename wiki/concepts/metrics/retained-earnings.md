Retained earnings (RE) are the cumulative net income a company has kept in the business over its entire life, rather than paid out to shareholders as [[dividends]]. They are a component of shareholders' [[equity]] on the [[balance-sheet]] — the portion of book value that has been built up from reinvested profits instead of contributed by investors buying shares. Retained earnings are also called accumulated earnings, and when the cumulative figure is negative they are labelled an *accumulated deficit*.

## What it is

Every accounting period, a firm earns net income on its [[income-statement]]. Management then chooses how much of that profit to return to owners as dividends and how much to keep. Whatever is kept flows into the retained-earnings account, which lives inside the equity section of the balance sheet. Retained earnings therefore represent the running, all-time total of *profits earned minus profits distributed* — the accumulated result of the reinvestment-versus-payout decision made period after period.

Because it is a cumulative, all-time balance, retained earnings is a stock (a balance-sheet figure at a point in time), whereas net income and dividends are flows measured over a period. RE is the bridge that carries earnings from the income statement into equity.

## The roll-forward

Retained earnings connect one period's balance sheet to the next through a simple roll-forward, usually presented in the **statement of changes in equity** (or a standalone statement of retained earnings):

**Ending RE = Beginning RE + Net Income − Dividends Declared**

- **Beginning RE** — the closing balance carried over from the prior period.
- **Net Income** — the current period's profit (a loss reduces RE).
- **Dividends** — *both* cash dividends and stock dividends reduce retained earnings when declared. A stock dividend capitalises a portion of RE by transferring it into paid-in capital; a cash dividend transfers value out to shareholders.

The identity closes the loop between the income statement and equity: each period's profit that is not paid out accumulates in RE and raises book equity. Prior-period adjustments and certain accounting-standard transitions can also flow through the opening RE balance.

## What it signals

- **Reinvestment versus payout.** A firm that retains most of its earnings is signalling that it has (or believes it has) attractive internal opportunities to redeploy profit — organic growth, capex, R&D, acquisitions, debt reduction. A firm that pays most of it out as dividends is signalling maturity and limited high-return reinvestment options.
- **Long-run profitability history.** A large positive RE balance generally reflects a long track record of cumulative profitability that has been ploughed back into the business.
- **Accumulated deficit.** A negative RE balance means cumulative losses (net of any distributions) exceed cumulative profits. This is common and often unalarming for early-stage or high-growth firms that are deliberately spending ahead of revenue; it is a warning sign for a mature company, where it points to persistent losses or a history of distributions exceeding earnings.

## Uses

- **Feeds book value and [[return-on-equity|ROE]].** Retained earnings are a building block of shareholders' equity, and equity is the denominator of ROE and the basis of [[book-value]] per share. Reinvested profits that earn a good return compound equity over time; profits reinvested poorly inflate the equity base without adding commensurate earnings, dragging ROE down.
- **The RE / Total-Assets ratio in the [[altman-z-score]].** In Altman's bankruptcy model this ratio (the X₂ term) proxies for a firm's *age and cumulative profitability* — how much of the asset base has been self-funded from retained profit over the company's life. Young or chronically loss-making firms score low here, which raises their assessed distress risk.
- **Capital-allocation lens.** Retained earnings are, in effect, capital that management chose to keep and reinvest on shareholders' behalf. The key question is whether those retained profits are earning a return above the firm's [[wacc|cost of capital]] — a judgement best made with [[return-on-invested-capital]], which measures return on all invested capital rather than being flattered by leverage. Retaining earnings only creates value when the incremental return on them beats what shareholders could have earned with the cash themselves.

## Caveats

- **RE is not cash.** This is the most common misreading. Retained earnings are an accounting and historical-cost figure describing cumulative reinvested profit — not a reserve of money sitting in a bank account. The profits that built up RE have typically already been reinvested in inventory, receivables, plant, acquisitions, or debt repayment. A company can have billions in retained earnings and very little cash; the cash position is on the asset side of the balance sheet, not in the RE line.
- **Distorted by buybacks and write-offs.** [[share-buybacks]] reduce equity and, depending on jurisdiction and accounting, can reduce retained earnings directly, so heavy repurchasers can show modest or even negative RE despite strong profitability. One-off write-offs, impairments, and restructuring charges hit net income and therefore drag RE down in the period they are recognised.
- **Book versus market value.** Because RE is carried at historical cost and reflects accounting profit rather than economic value, it can diverge sharply from a company's market value. A business with intangible-driven value (brands, network effects, IP) may create enormous market value while accumulating comparatively little retained earnings, whereas an asset-heavy firm can carry large RE that the market discounts. RE describes accounting history, not forward-looking worth.
