Franking credits (also known as imputation credits) are tax credits attached to dividends paid by Australian companies, representing the corporate tax already paid on the profits from which the dividend was distributed. Australia's dividend imputation system, introduced in 1987, prevents the **double taxation of corporate profits** — a feature that fundamentally shapes Australian investment behaviour, corporate payout policy, and market structure. For Australian investors, franking is the single largest driver of the gap between an equity's headline yield and its actual after-tax return, and the central reason that domestic equities are weighted so heavily in [[smsf|SMSF]] and [[superannuation]] portfolios. See [[australian-investor-tax]] for the broader tax framework in which franking sits.

> **Not tax advice.** This page is general educational information about how the Australian dividend imputation system works. All tax rates, thresholds, and dollar figures below are **indicative** — corporate and personal rates, brackets, the Medicare levy, the small-shareholder threshold, and refund rules change with legislation and over time, and individual circumstances vary widely. Confirm current rules with the ATO and a registered tax agent before acting. Figures are illustrative and used to show the *mechanics*, not to state your liability.

## Overview

In most countries (including the United States), corporate profits are taxed twice: once at the corporate level (corporate tax) and again when distributed to shareholders as dividends (personal income tax). Australia's imputation system eliminates this double taxation by allowing shareholders to claim a credit for the corporate tax already paid.

This seemingly technical tax feature has profound effects:

- Australian companies pay out a much higher percentage of earnings as [[dividend|dividends]] than US companies (~70% payout ratio vs ~35%)
- Income-focused investing is more prevalent in Australia than almost any other market
- [[superannuation]] funds and [[smsf|SMSFs]], particularly in pension phase, receive **cash refunds** of franking credits — creating one of the most tax-advantaged investment structures in the world
- The demand for fully franked dividends influences stock prices, ex-dividend trading patterns, and [[portfolio-construction]] for Australian investors

## How the System Works

### Step by Step

1. **Company earns profit**: Company XYZ earns $100 in pre-tax profit
2. **Company pays corporate tax**: 30% corporate tax = $30, leaving $70 after-tax profit
3. **Company declares dividend**: $70 dividend with $30 franking credit attached (this is a "fully franked" dividend)
4. **Shareholder receives dividend**: $70 cash arrives in your account
5. **Tax return — gross up**: You declare $100 as assessable income ($70 dividend + $30 franking credit)
6. **Tax return — apply credit**: The $30 franking credit is applied as a tax offset against your personal tax liability
7. **Net result**: You effectively pay tax on the $100 at your marginal rate, but receive credit for the $30 already paid by the company

### Worked Examples at Different Tax Brackets

The table below uses an *indicative* 30% corporate rate and round marginal rates to show the mechanics. Real marginal rates include the Medicare levy and shift with bracket indexation, so treat every cell as illustrative, not as a current schedule (see disclaimer above).

| Marginal Tax Rate | Gross Income | Tax Owed | Franking Credit | Net Tax Payable | Cash Dividend Received | Effective After-Tax Income |
|-------------------|-------------|----------|-----------------|-----------------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| **0%** (retiree/pension SMSF) | $100 | $0 | $30 | **-$30 (refund)** | $70 | **$100** |
| **15%** ([[superannuation|super fund]]) | $100 | $15 | $30 | **-$15 (refund)** | $70 | **$85** |
| **19%** (low income) | $100 | $19 | $30 | **-$11 (refund)** | $70 | **$81** |
| **30%** (middle income) | $100 | $30 | $30 | **$0** | $70 | **$70** |
| **32.5%** | $100 | $32.50 | $30 | **$2.50** | $70 | **$67.50** |
| **37%** | $100 | $37 | $30 | **$7** | $70 | **$63** |
| **45%** (top bracket) | $100 | $45 | $30 | **$15** | $70 | **$55** |

The key insight: anyone with a marginal rate **below 30%** (the corporate tax rate) receives a **cash refund** from the ATO for the difference. This makes fully franked dividends extraordinarily valuable for retirees, low-income earners, [[superannuation]] funds (taxed at 15%), and [[smsf|SMSFs]] in pension phase (taxed at 0%).

## Franking Levels

Not all dividends carry full franking credits:

| Franking Level | Meaning | Example |
|----------------|---------|---------|
| **Fully franked** | 100% of the dividend has franking credits attached (company paid full corporate tax on profits) | CBA, BHP, Telstra — most Big Four bank dividends |
| **Partially franked** (e.g., 50%) | Only part of the dividend carries credits (company may have foreign income not subject to AU tax) | Some resource companies with significant offshore earnings |
| **Unfranked** | No franking credits attached (profits weren't subject to Australian corporate tax) | Companies with tax losses, foreign income, or specific structures |

### Franking Credit Formula

For a fully franked dividend:

**Franking Credit = Dividend Amount × (Corporate Tax Rate / (1 - Corporate Tax Rate))**

At 30% corporate rate: Franking Credit = $70 × (0.30 / 0.70) = $30

For small companies (25% tax rate): Franking Credit = $75 × (0.25 / 0.75) = $25

## Effective Yield — Why It Matters

The headline [[dividend]] yield of an Australian share understates the true return for investors who can use franking credits. The "grossed-up yield" reflects the total value including the credit:

**Grossed-Up Yield = Headline Yield / (1 - Corporate Tax Rate)**

### Real-World Example: Commonwealth Bank (CBA)

- CBA share price: ~$130
- Annual dividend: ~$4.50 per share (fully franked)
- Headline yield: 3.5%
- Grossed-up yield: 3.5% / 0.70 = **5.0%**
- For a 0% tax entity (SMSF in pension phase): effective yield = **5.0%** (the full grossed-up amount is received as cash)
- For a 15% tax entity (super fund): effective yield = 5.0% × 0.85 = **4.25%** — still higher than the headline 3.5%

This grossed-up yield effect is why Australian banks and other high-dividend payers trade at premium valuations relative to their international peers on a headline yield basis.

## How Franking Affects After-Tax Equity Returns

For an Australian investor, total equity return decomposes into **price appreciation + cash dividend + franking-credit value**. The third term is invisible in a headline total-return chart but real in an after-tax sense, and its size depends entirely on the holder's tax position. This is the practical heart of why franking matters for [[portfolio-construction]] and [[fundamental-analysis]] in Australia.

### The decomposition

For a fully franked payer at an indicative 30% corporate rate, every $70 of cash dividend carries $30 of credit. The credit is worth its full face value only to an entity whose marginal rate is at or below the corporate rate; above it, the credit offsets but does not fully cover the dividend tax. The after-tax return on the dividend leg therefore *fans out by holder type* even though the pre-tax cash is identical:

| Holder type | Indicative rate | Value of $30 credit | After-tax outcome on a $70 fully franked dividend |
|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|---------------------------------------------------|
| SMSF in pension phase | 0% | Full $30 refunded as cash | $100 retained — the credit *adds* ~43% to the cash dividend |
| [[superannuation]] fund (accumulation) | 15% | $30 credit, $15 tax | ~$85 retained |
| Mid-bracket individual | ~30% | $30 credit ≈ tax due | ~$70 retained (no extra benefit, no extra cost) |
| Top-bracket individual | ~45%+ | $30 credit, ~$45 tax | ~$55 retained — still better than an unfranked equivalent |
| Foreign investor | n/a | Credit unusable | $70 less any withholding tax |

The asymmetry is the point: the *same security* delivers a materially different after-tax return depending on the wrapper it is held in. A franking-rich Australian equity is a far better after-tax holding inside a [[smsf|low-tax SMSF]] than inside a high-bracket individual's name — one of the strongest arguments for holding domestic dividend payers in the most tax-advantaged structure available.

### After-tax vs pre-tax total return

Two stocks can have identical pre-tax total returns but very different after-tax returns once franking is layered in:

| Scenario (illustrative) | Pre-tax total return | After-tax for 0% SMSF | After-tax for 47% individual |
|-------------------------|----------------------|-----------------------|------------------------------|
| Fully franked high-payout (e.g. a Big Four bank profile) | ~9% | Highest — full credit refunded | Reduced but credit-supported |
| Unfranked / low-payout growth name | ~9% | No credit boost; relies on capital gains | Capital gains may be taxed at a lower effective rate via the [[capital-gains-tax|CGT discount]] |
| Foreign equity (e.g. US name) | ~9% | No franking; US withholding applies | No franking; foreign-income complexity |

For a low-tax entity, the franked payer's after-tax return is meaningfully higher; for a high-bracket individual, the unfranked-growth name's CGT-discounted capital gains can close or reverse the gap. This is why **franking interacts with [[capital-gains-tax]] and the CGT discount**: the optimal after-tax mix of dividends vs capital growth is holder-specific, not universal.

### Implications for screening and valuation

- When screening Australian equities, compare **grossed-up yields** across names, not headline yields — otherwise a partially franked or unfranked high payer looks artificially competitive against a fully franked one.
- A [[discounted-cash-flow|DCF]] or required-return model built for an AU investor should value the credit stream explicitly for low-tax holders; ignoring it understates the after-tax value of franked payers.
- The franking *balance* on a company's balance sheet (its franking account) limits how much it can frank future dividends. Companies with large unfranked offshore earnings, or with depleted franking accounts, cannot deliver the full benefit even at a high payout ratio.

## Impact on Market Behaviour

### Higher Payout Ratios

Australian companies distribute a much larger share of their profits as dividends compared to US companies:

- **ASX 200 average payout ratio**: ~70%
- **S&P 500 average payout ratio**: ~35%

This is directly driven by franking credit demand. Shareholders (especially super funds and retirees) prefer dividends over retained earnings because franking credits are only distributed via dividends, not buybacks or capital appreciation.

### Ex-Dividend Price Behaviour

On the ex-dividend date, a share's price typically drops by approximately the dividend amount. However, research has consistently shown that ASX share prices drop by **less than the grossed-up dividend** (dividend + franking credit). This is because:

- Not all shareholders can fully utilise franking credits (foreign investors, tax-exempt entities)
- The market prices the "average" value of the credit across all holder types
- Typical ex-dividend drop: approximately the cash dividend amount, not the grossed-up amount

This creates subtle trading dynamics around ex-dividend dates. See the 45-day rule section below.

### Bias Toward Dividend Stocks

The franking credit system creates a structural demand for dividend-paying Australian equities, particularly among:

- [[smsf|SMSF]] trustees seeking income and credit refunds
- Retirees in pension phase
- [[superannuation]] funds in accumulation phase (15% tax makes credits very valuable)
- [[value-investing|Value investors]] who screen on effective yield

This demand can push valuations of high-dividend stocks above what [[fundamental-analysis]] alone would justify.

## The 45-Day Holding Rule

To prevent "dividend stripping" — buying shares shortly before ex-dividend solely to capture the franking credit, then selling — the ATO enforces the **45-day holding rule** (also called the "qualified person" rule):

- You must hold shares **"at risk"** for at least **45 days** (excluding the acquisition and disposal dates) within the qualifying period around the ex-dividend date
- "At risk" means you have genuine economic exposure — hedging the position (e.g., buying a put) during this period can disqualify you
- The rule only applies if your total franking credit claims exceed **$5,000** in a financial year (the "small shareholder exemption")
- For preference shares: the holding period is **90 days**

### What "At Risk" Means

You are NOT at risk if you hold an offsetting position that eliminates downside — for example:

- Holding a put option over the shares
- Having a short position in the same or similar security
- A contract to sell at a fixed price

This rule is particularly relevant for [[covered-call]] and options strategies — SMSF trustees should ensure holding periods are met before relying on franking credits.

## Cash Refunds for Excess Franking Credits

Since 2000 (introduced by the Howard government), excess franking credits are refundable as cash. If your tax liability is less than your franking credits, the ATO sends you a cheque for the difference.

**Who benefits from cash refunds**:

| Entity | Tax Rate | Gets Cash Refund? |
|--------|----------|-------------------|
| Retirees (age 60+, no other income) | 0% | **Yes** — full credit refunded |
| [[smsf]] in pension phase | 0% | **Yes** — full credit refunded |
| [[superannuation]] fund (accumulation) | 15% | **Yes** — difference between credit and 15% tax |
| Low-income individuals (<$18,200) | 0% | **Yes** — full credit refunded |
| Charities, not-for-profits | 0% | **Yes** |
| Companies | 30% | **No** — credit exactly offsets corporate tax |
| High-income individuals (45%+) | 45% | **No** — must pay additional tax above the credit |
| Foreign investors | N/A | **No** — cannot use franking credits (withholding tax applies instead) |

## Political Risk

Franking credit cash refunds have been a political flashpoint:

- **2019 federal election**: Labor proposed eliminating cash refunds for individuals and SMSFs not paying tax (while retaining credits as offsets). The estimated annual saving was widely reported in the multi-billion-dollar range (indicative). The policy was widely seen as contributing to Labor's unexpected election loss — dubbed the "retiree tax" by opponents
- **Ongoing debate**: The cost of franking credit refunds to the federal budget runs into the billions annually (indicative figure; the exact number is regularly re-estimated by Treasury), which means future governments may revisit the policy
- **Legislative risk**: Any change to refund rules would disproportionately affect retirees and SMSFs in pension phase — a core consideration for [[risk-management]] in SMSF portfolio construction. A concentrated, franking-driven tilt toward domestic banks is itself a single-policy-dependent risk: an investor whose after-tax thesis rests entirely on cash refunds is exposed to legislative change in a way a price-return investor is not

## International Comparison

| Country | Imputation System | Cash Refunds? |
|---------|-------------------|---------------|
| **Australia** | Full imputation (since 1987) | Yes (since 2000) |
| **New Zealand** | Full imputation | No (credits only offset tax, no refunds) |
| **United Kingdom** | Abolished imputation in 1999 | N/A |
| **United States** | No imputation — double taxation | N/A |
| **Canada** | Partial — gross-up and dividend tax credit | Partial refund mechanism for low-income |
| **Chile** | Full imputation | Yes |
| **Malta** | Full imputation | Yes (for some shareholders) |

Australia and New Zealand are the only major markets with full imputation. Australia's addition of cash refunds in 2000 made its system uniquely generous.

## Franking Credits in Practice

### For Individual Investors

- Check franking status in dividend announcements (company ASX announcements or your broker's dividend calendar)
- Fully franked dividends from Big Four banks (CBA, WBC, NAB, ANZ), Telstra, Wesfarmers, Woolworths, BHP are staples of income portfolios
- Consider your marginal tax rate: at 45%, franking credits reduce but don't eliminate dividend tax; at 0–19%, they generate cash refunds

### For Super Funds and SMSFs

- [[superannuation]] funds in accumulation (15% tax) receive a net benefit from every fully franked dividend
- [[smsf|SMSFs]] in pension phase receive the maximum benefit: 0% tax + full cash refund of credits
- Franking credits are a primary driver of SMSF asset allocation toward Australian equities
- See [[smsf#Dividend and Franking Credit Strategy]] for detailed SMSF-specific strategies

### Tracking Franking Credits

- Your broker provides an annual tax summary showing all dividends and franking credits received
- ATO pre-fills dividend and franking credit information in tax returns (from July each year)
- SMSFs must track franking credits as part of the fund's tax return

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Comparing headline yields across franking levels.** A 6% unfranked yield is not better than a 5% fully franked yield for a low-tax holder — grossed-up, the franked stock can be worth more. Always compare on a grossed-up, holder-specific basis.
2. **Assuming everyone values the credit equally.** Foreign investors, charities, and high-bracket individuals capture very different fractions of the credit. The market price reflects a blended "average" holder, so the *marginal* value of franking to a 0%-tax SMSF is higher than the price implies — and lower for a foreigner.
3. **Chasing franking into concentration.** The biggest, most reliably fully franked payers are clustered in banks and a few large caps. A franking-maximising income portfolio can quietly become a concentrated bet on Australian financials — a [[diversification]] and [[correlation-breakdown]] risk, not just a tax question.
4. **Breaching the 45-day "at risk" rule with hedges.** Buying a put, writing certain [[covered-call|covered calls]] deep in the money, or otherwise neutralising downside during the qualifying window can disqualify the credit if claims exceed the small-shareholder threshold. Options overlays and franking capture interact.
5. **Ignoring the company's franking account balance.** A high payout ratio does not guarantee full franking. Firms with large offshore (unfranked) earnings or depleted franking accounts can only partly frank — the headline "fully franked" history is not a forward guarantee.
6. **Treating franking as risk-free yield.** The credit is real, but the *underlying equity* still carries full market and dividend-cut risk. A bank that cuts its dividend in a downturn delivers less cash *and* less franking exactly when the income investor needs it most.
7. **Mistaking grossed-up yield for total return.** Grossing up captures the dividend leg only. Total after-tax return still depends on price movement and, for high-bracket holders, the trade-off against CGT-discounted capital gains (see [[capital-gains-tax]]).
8. **Forgetting legislative risk.** Cash refundability is a policy choice, not a law of nature — see Political Risk above.
