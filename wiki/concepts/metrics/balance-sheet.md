The **balance sheet** is one of the three core financial statements (alongside the income statement and the cash flow statement). It is a *point-in-time* snapshot of what a company owns (assets), what it owes (liabilities), and what is left over for shareholders (equity) at the close of a reporting period. Unlike the income statement, which covers a span of time, the balance sheet captures a single instant — the last day of the quarter or fiscal year.

## The Accounting Identity

Every balance sheet must balance, by definition:

$$\text{Total Assets} = \text{Total Liabilities} + \text{Shareholders' Equity}$$

This is the fundamental accounting equation. Equity is the residual claim: whatever assets remain after all liabilities are settled belongs to shareholders. It also means equity can be read as the company's accumulated net worth.

## Structure

### Assets (what the company controls)
- **Current assets** — cash, marketable securities, accounts receivable, inventory, prepayments. Expected to convert to cash within 12 months.
- **Non-current assets** — property, plant and equipment (PP&E), intangibles, goodwill, long-term investments. Held for longer than a year.

### Liabilities (what the company owes)
- **Current liabilities** — accounts payable, short-term debt, accrued expenses, the current portion of long-term debt, deferred revenue. Due within 12 months.
- **Non-current liabilities** — long-term loans, bonds, lease obligations, pension and deferred-tax liabilities.

### Shareholders' Equity (the residual)
- Share capital (par + paid-in capital) + retained earnings + reserves − treasury stock.

**Working capital** = current assets − current liabilities, a quick read on near-term operating liquidity.

## Trading Relevance

The income statement drives the headline numbers investors react to, but the balance sheet tells you whether a company can *survive* — and it is where most solvency and liquidity signals live. Key ratios derived from it:

| Metric | Formula | Reads |
|--------|---------|-------|
| [[return-on-equity\|ROE]] | Net Income / Shareholders' Equity | Capital efficiency for owners |
| [[return-on-assets\|ROA]] | Net Income / Total Assets | Efficiency of the whole asset base |
| [[debt-to-equity\|D/E]] | Total Debt / Shareholders' Equity | Leverage and balance-sheet risk |
| [[current-ratio\|Current ratio]] | Current Assets / Current Liabilities | Short-term liquidity |
| [[quick-ratio\|Quick ratio]] | (Current Assets − Inventory) / Current Liabilities | Liquidity excluding slow inventory |
| [[cash-conversion-cycle\|CCC]] | DIO + DSO − DPO | Working-capital efficiency |

Practical balance-sheet reads:
- **Net debt** (total debt − cash) reveals true leverage that gross debt hides; pair with net debt / EBITDA.
- **Goodwill and intangibles** as a large share of assets flag acquisition risk and possible future write-downs.
- **Quality of equity** — equity inflated by capitalised costs or unimpaired goodwill overstates ROE. As Fred McNaught put it, while EPS and dividends drive his buy decisions, "you must always look also to the balance sheet" (Source: [[fred-sam-session-2024-01-02]]); he flagged Northern Star's D/E of 15.6 as "low as it should be for a commodity stock" (Source: [[fred-sam-session-2024-04-25]]).
- **Point-in-time risk** — because it is a single-day snapshot, the balance sheet can be window-dressed at quarter-end (paying down debt, delaying payables). Trend across several quarters, not one print.
