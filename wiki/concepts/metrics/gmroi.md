Gross Margin Return on Investment (GMROI) is a management metric primarily used in the retail sector to evaluate how effectively a company converts its inventory investment into gross profit. [[fred-mcnaught|Fred McNaught]] uses this as a key metric when analysing retail businesses.

## Formula

```
GMROI = Gross Margin % × Stock Turn
```

Or equivalently:

```
GMROI = Gross Profit / Average Inventory Cost
```

A common benchmark: **Stock Turn × Gross Profit % > 100** indicates a business is generating adequate return on its inventory investment.

The two-component decomposition makes GMROI a more honest profitability measure for retailers than gross margin alone: a low-margin, high-turnover model (a discount grocer) and a high-margin, low-turnover model (a luxury jeweller) can produce identical GMROI, capturing the genuine trade-off between margin and velocity that defines retail strategy.

## Interpretation

| GMROI | Interpretation |
|-------|---------------|
| > 3.0 | Excellent — strong inventory management |
| 2.0-3.0 | Good — healthy retail operation |
| 1.0-2.0 | Marginal — may need improvement |
| < 1.0 | Poor — losing money on inventory |

## Fred McNaught's Application

Fred uses GMROI as a management quality indicator, particularly for retail and consumer companies. It reveals whether management is:
- Pricing products effectively (gross margin component)
- Turning over inventory efficiently (stock turn component)
- Generating sufficient return to cover operating costs and provide shareholder returns

### Case Study: Coles vs Woolworths

Fred applies GMROI to compare Australian grocery retailers, examining stock turn rates and gross margins to assess which management team is running a more efficient operation.

## Trading Relevance

For equity analysts covering retail, consumer-discretionary, and consumer-staples names, GMROI is a management-quality and operational-efficiency screen that complements valuation multiples. A retailer whose GMROI is rising relative to peers is converting working capital into profit more effectively — often a leading indicator of expanding [[return-on-invested-capital|ROIC]] and improving same-store economics before it shows up in headline earnings. Conversely, a falling GMROI can signal inventory bloat (markdown risk ahead) or margin pressure, both of which precede earnings disappointments. Because inventory is the largest invested asset on most retail balance sheets, GMROI trends feed directly into assessments of capital efficiency that drive long/short pair trades within the retail sector.
