Gross Margin is the percentage of revenue that remains after subtracting the direct cost of producing a company's goods or services. It is the first profitability line on the income statement and a powerful signal of pricing power, production efficiency, and competitive positioning.

## Formula

$$\text{Gross Margin} = \frac{\text{Revenue} - \text{COGS}}{\text{Revenue}} \times 100\%$$

Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) includes direct materials, direct labor, and manufacturing overhead — the costs that scale directly with units sold. It excludes operating expenses like R&D, sales & marketing, and general administration, which are captured further down the income statement at the [[operating-margin]] level.

## What Gross Margin Reveals

Gross margin is the cleanest read on **pricing power**. A company that can consistently charge more than its production costs — especially in the face of inflation or competitive pressure — has something customers value that rivals cannot easily replicate. Rising or stable gross margin through an input-cost spike indicates real pricing power; compressing gross margin during the same period reveals a commoditized business with no moat.

Gross margin also captures **production efficiency**. Manufacturers improving yields, software companies scaling server costs across more users, or retailers negotiating better supplier terms all show the improvement in this line first.

## Typical Ranges by Industry

Gross margin is only meaningful when compared within an industry:

- **Software / SaaS**: 70–90% (near-zero marginal cost of distribution)
- **Pharmaceuticals (branded)**: 60–80% (IP-protected pricing)
- **Luxury goods & consumer brands**: 50–70%
- **Semiconductors**: 45–65% (cyclical)
- **Retail (specialty)**: 30–50%
- **Retail (broadline)**: 20–40%
- **Supermarkets**: 20–25%
- **Commodities, airlines, trucking**: 5–15% (price-takers)

A 30% gross margin is outstanding for a grocer and catastrophic for a software firm.

## Buffett's Preference and Competitive Moats

Warren Buffett has long cited high and stable gross margins as one of the clearest fingerprints of a durable competitive advantage. Businesses with sustained 40%+ gross margins tend to possess brand power, network effects, switching costs, or proprietary technology — the classic moat sources. Margin **stability** over a full economic cycle often matters more than the absolute level.

## Margin Compression as a Warning

A gradual, multi-quarter decline in gross margin is one of the earliest warning signs in [[fundamental-analysis]]. Causes include: new entrants forcing price cuts, input cost inflation the company cannot pass through, product mix shifting toward lower-margin lines, or loss of scale. By the time compression reaches [[operating-margin]] or [[earnings-per-share]], the thesis is usually already broken.

## Gross Profitability as a Quality Factor

Robert Novy-Marx's 2013 paper "The Other Side of Value" showed that **gross profits / total assets** is a powerful and robust quality factor — firms with high gross profitability delivered significant excess returns, rivaling traditional value signals. The insight: gross profit is the "cleanest" measure of economic profitability because it is furthest from discretionary accounting choices below the COGS line.

## Limitations

- Companies classify costs differently between COGS and operating expenses, distorting peer comparisons.
- Service and software firms may have minimal "COGS" by construction, making the metric less informative in isolation.
- Gross margin ignores SG&A, R&D, interest, and taxes — a high gross margin doesn't guarantee net profitability.
- Mix shifts can mask underlying product-level margin compression.

## Trading Relevance

Gross margin trend is a tradeable signal, not just an analyst's checkbox. Quarterly gross-margin beats and misses move stocks sharply because they reveal pricing power *before* it shows up in [[earnings-per-share|EPS]] — sell-side models that anchor on revenue growth often miss a quiet margin roll-over. Practical uses:

- **Quality/value screens**: gross-profits-to-assets (Novy-Marx) is a robust long-leg factor; combine high gross profitability with cheap [[price-to-earnings-ratio|P/E]] for a GARP-style tilt.
- **Margin-inflection trades**: a company that has spent two to three quarters compressing margin under input-cost pressure can re-rate violently when costs normalise — the inflection often leads the price.
- **Short candidates**: persistent gross-margin erosion in a "growth" name is one of the cleanest early tells that the growth is being bought with price cuts (a [[fundamental-analysis]] short thesis).

Because gross margin sits high on the income statement and far from discretionary below-the-line accounting, it is harder to manage for one quarter than [[net-income]] — making it a more reliable forward indicator.
