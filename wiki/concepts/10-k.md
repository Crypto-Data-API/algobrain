The **Form 10-K** is the comprehensive annual report that U.S. public companies file with the [[sec|SEC]]. It is the single most authoritative, audited disclosure a company produces — far more detailed than the glossy "annual report to shareholders" mailed to investors — and it is the primary-source document fundamental analysts read first. A 10-K covers a full fiscal year and bundles the audited financial statements together with a narrative description of the business, its risks, and management's own discussion of performance.

## Overview

The 10-K is filed once per fiscal year and is **audited** by an independent accounting firm, which distinguishes it from the lighter, unaudited quarterly [[10-q|10-Q]]. It is filed electronically through [[edgar|SEC EDGAR]], where it is free to read. Because it is a legal filing signed by the CEO and CFO under penalty of personal liability (Sarbanes-Oxley certifications), it is generally more conservative and complete than press releases, investor decks, or the marketing-oriented "annual report" cover document.

Investors often confuse three things:
- The **glossy annual report** — a designed shareholder brochure (optional, marketing-led).
- The **10-K** — the mandatory, audited SEC filing (the real document).
- The **earnings release / 8-K** — the headline press release of results (see [[earnings]], [[8-k]]).

The 10-K is the ground truth. The others summarize or spin it.

## What It Contains

The 10-K follows a fixed SEC-mandated structure of numbered Items across four Parts:

**Part I — the business and its risks**
- **Item 1 — Business**: what the company does, its segments, customers, competition, supply chain, employees, and regulation.
- **Item 1A — Risk Factors**: a ranked list of what could go wrong. Year-over-year *changes* to this section are themselves a signal (new risks added, old ones dropped).
- **Item 1C — Cybersecurity**: cyber risk-management and governance (required since the SEC's 2023 cybersecurity disclosure rule).
- **Item 2 — Properties**; **Item 3 — Legal Proceedings**; **Item 4 — Mine Safety**.

**Part II — the numbers and management's narrative**
- **Item 5 — Market for the stock**: share price history, dividends, [[share-buybacks|repurchase]] activity.
- **Item 7 — Management's Discussion and Analysis (MD&A)**: management explains *why* the numbers moved — the most-read narrative section.
- **Item 7A — Market Risk**: interest-rate, FX, and commodity exposures.
- **Item 8 — Financial Statements**: the audited [[income-statement|income statement]], [[balance-sheet|balance sheet]], [[cash-flow-statement|cash-flow statement]], statement of equity, and the all-important footnotes.
- **Item 9A — Controls and Procedures**: internal-control effectiveness and any "material weakness."

**Part III — governance** (often incorporated by reference to the proxy statement): directors, executive compensation, and ownership.

**Part IV — exhibits**: material contracts, subsidiaries list, and certifications.

## What an Investor Looks For

- **The footnotes to Item 8** — revenue-recognition policy, debt maturity schedule, lease obligations, segment breakdowns, stock-based compensation, and contingencies. The footnotes routinely contain more decision-relevant information than the headline statements.
- **Changes in the Risk Factors (Item 1A)** versus last year's 10-K — new language often previews a deteriorating situation before the numbers show it.
- **MD&A (Item 7)** — does management's explanation match the cash-flow reality, or is it explaining away weakness? Compare the narrative to the [[cash-flow-statement|cash-flow statement]].
- **The auditor's opinion** — an "unqualified" (clean) opinion is normal; any qualification, "going concern" doubt, or **material weakness** in internal controls is a major red flag.
- **Reconciliation of non-GAAP numbers** — the 10-K forces a reconciliation of adjusted EPS back to GAAP, exposing how aggressive the "adjustments" are (see [[earnings]]).

## Timing and Cadence

The filing deadline depends on the company's filer category (set by public float):
- **Large accelerated filers** (public float ≥ $700M): within **60 days** of fiscal year-end.
- **Accelerated filers** ($75M–$700M): within **75 days**.
- **Non-accelerated / smaller reporting companies**: within **90 days**.

A fiscal year need not be a calendar year — many retailers end in late January or early February. The 10-K replaces the fourth-quarter 10-Q, so a full year produces three 10-Qs and one 10-K.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Reading the press release instead of the filing.** The headline beat (see [[earnings]]) can hide a deteriorating balance sheet that only the 10-K footnotes reveal.
- **Skipping the footnotes.** Off-balance-sheet obligations, customer concentration, and debt covenants live in the footnotes, not the headline tables.
- **Ignoring "incorporated by reference."** Part III governance and compensation are frequently pulled from the separate proxy statement; you have to read both.
- **Treating a late or delayed filing (Form NT 10-K) as routine.** A "Notification of Late Filing" often signals accounting problems or an auditor dispute.
- **Comparing across companies without normalizing fiscal years** — a January fiscal-year-end retailer is not directly comparable to a December-end peer in a fast-moving cycle.

## A Hypothetical Example

Suppose **XYZ Corp** (a fictional mid-cap manufacturer) files its 10-K 58 days after its December fiscal year-end. The headline release touted record revenue. Reading Item 8, an analyst notices a new footnote disclosing that a single customer now represents 41% of revenue (up from 22%), and Item 1A added a fresh risk factor about that customer renegotiating terms. Item 9A also discloses a newly remediated material weakness in revenue-recognition controls. None of this appeared in the press release. The 10-K, not the headline, is where the real risk surfaces — exactly why fundamental and [[information-arbitrage|information-driven]] investors read the filing line by line.
