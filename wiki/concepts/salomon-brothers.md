Salomon Brothers was a New York investment bank, founded in 1910, that became the most powerful bond-trading house on Wall Street during the 1980s. It pioneered the mortgage-backed securities market, dominated US Treasury dealing, and built the proprietary fixed-income arbitrage culture that later spawned [[ltcm|Long-Term Capital Management]]. Its 1991 Treasury auction scandal nearly destroyed the firm, and after a series of acquisitions it was absorbed into Citigroup, the Salomon name retired by 2003.

## Overview

Salomon Brothers rose from a money-brokerage partnership into a fixed-income powerhouse, distinguished by an unusually aggressive, trading-driven culture rather than the relationship-banking style of rivals. In the 1980s it was, by trading revenue, frequently the most profitable firm on Wall Street, and its arbitrage and proprietary desks were the training ground for a generation of quantitative bond traders.

Key chapters:

- **Mortgage-backed securities.** Under Lewie Ranieri, Salomon effectively created the modern securitized [[mortgage-backed-securities|MBS]] market in the late 1970s and early 1980s, packaging home loans into tradable bonds — one of the most consequential financial innovations of the era.
- **Treasury dominance.** Salomon was the leading primary dealer in [[us-treasury-bonds|US Treasuries]], with enormous influence over auctions and the [[on-off-the-run-treasury-arbitrage|on-the-run/off-the-run]] basis.
- **The Arbitrage Group.** Run by [[john-meriwether|John Meriwether]], Salomon's fixed-income arbitrage desk practiced relative-value and convergence trading at scale, generating a large share of firm profits and developing the playbook that Meriwether and several colleagues (including future Nobel laureates Myron Scholes and Robert Merton) carried into [[ltcm|LTCM]] in 1994.

## The Trading-Floor Culture (Liar's Poker)

Salomon's 1980s trading floor is the most documented in financial history thanks to Michael Lewis's memoir *[[liars-poker|Liar's Poker]]* (1989), written after Lewis worked there as a bond salesman. The book's title comes from a high-stakes bluffing game (based on the serial numbers of dollar bills) that traders played on the floor — CEO **John Gutfreund** reputedly once challenged Meriwether to a single hand for $1 million. Lewis coined or popularized the floor's vocabulary — "Big Swinging Dick," "Equities in Dallas" (the worst job assignment), and "feeding frenzy" — and portrayed a culture of aggressive risk-taking, machismo, and outsized bonuses that defined the bond-trading boom. The portrait is essential context for understanding how a single rogue trader's auction bidding could later imperil the entire firm.

## The 1991 Treasury auction scandal

In 1990-1991, Salomon's chief government bond trader **Paul Mozer** submitted fraudulent customer-name bids in several US Treasury auctions to evade the 35% per-bidder limit and effectively [[corner-the-market|corner]] portions of issues. When discovered, the scandal triggered a regulatory crisis that nearly forced the firm into bankruptcy. **Warren Buffett**, Salomon's largest shareholder, stepped in as interim chairman to stabilize it and rebuild relations with regulators. The episode is a landmark case in auction-market manipulation and dealer-conduct regulation — see [[1991-salomon-treasury-auction-scandal]] for the full account.

## Decline and absorption

Weakened by the scandal and by volatile proprietary results, Salomon was acquired by Travelers Group in 1997, merged with Smith Barney to form **Salomon Smith Barney**, and then folded into **Citigroup** after the 1998 Travelers-Citicorp merger. Citigroup retired the Salomon Brothers name in 2003. The firm's lasting legacy is twofold: the institutionalization of fixed-income securitization and arbitrage, and — through the LTCM lineage — a cautionary tale about leveraged convergence trading.

## Significance and Legacy

Salomon Brothers' influence on modern markets is disproportionate to its eventual disappearance:

- **Securitization.** By turning illiquid home mortgages into tradable [[mortgage-backed-securities|MBS]], Salomon created an entire asset class and the template for later securitization of auto loans, credit cards, and — eventually — the subprime instruments at the center of the 2008 crisis. The plumbing of structured credit traces back to Ranieri's desk.
- **Fixed-income arbitrage as a discipline.** The relative-value, convergence, and basis trades industrialized in the Arbitrage Group became the core of quantitative bond trading. Understanding [[ltcm|LTCM]] — its strategies, its leverage, and its 1998 collapse — is impossible without understanding the Salomon culture from which Meriwether and his Nobel-laureate partners emerged.
- **Regulatory landmark.** The 1991 scandal reshaped the rules governing Treasury auctions and primary-dealer conduct, and stands beside later episodes as a canonical case in [[corner-the-market|market cornering]] and auction manipulation. Buffett's congressional testimony — "Lose money for the firm and I will be understanding; lose a shred of reputation for the firm and I will be ruthless" — became a touchstone for corporate-conduct ethics.
- **People.** The Salomon diaspora seeded Wall Street: Meriwether and the arbitrage team to LTCM; Lewis to a writing career that shaped popular understanding of finance; Bloomberg co-founder Michael Bloomberg, a former Salomon partner, used his payout after being pushed out in the 1981 partnership reshuffle to build the Bloomberg terminal.

The recurring theme is *concentration of risk*: a firm whose immense profits and immense vulnerabilities both flowed from the same aggressive, trading-driven culture.

## Trading relevance

Salomon Brothers matters to a trading wiki less as a defunct bank than as the **origin point of modern fixed-income arbitrage and securitization**. The relative-value, convergence, and basis trades that define quantitative bond trading today were industrialized on Salomon's desks; understanding [[ltcm|LTCM's]] strategies and collapse requires understanding the Salomon Arbitrage Group culture from which they came. The 1991 scandal is also a primary case study in [[corner-the-market|market cornering]], auction manipulation, and the regulatory limits on primary-dealer behavior. Michael Lewis's *Liar's Poker* (1989), based on his time at Salomon, remains the canonical inside portrait of the trading-floor culture of the era.
