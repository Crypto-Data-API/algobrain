A **penny stock** is the common shorthand for a very low-priced, small-company stock that trades at a few dollars or less per share. In US regulatory usage (SEC Rule 3a51-1), a penny stock is generally an equity security priced **below $5** that does not trade on a major national exchange. The label answers the common ALFRED question *"why is this stock so cheap, and is it safe?"* — the short answer is that low price usually signals a tiny [[market-capitalization|market cap]], thin [[liquidity]], limited disclosure, and elevated risk of [[market-manipulation|manipulation]], not a bargain.

## What defines a penny stock

There is no single universal cutoff, but penny stocks share a recognizable profile:

- **Low share price** — typically under $5, often under $1, and sometimes "sub-penny" (fractions of a cent). Price alone is the loosest criterion; a low price plus the traits below is what matters.
- **Tiny [[market-capitalization|market cap]]** — most are **micro-cap** (roughly under a few hundred million) or **nano-cap** (tens of millions or less). A low *price* with a huge share count can still be a large company, so cap matters more than the headline price.
- **Off-exchange listing** — many trade on the [[over-the-counter-markets|OTC markets]] (OTCQB, Pink) rather than the [[nyse|NYSE]] or [[nasdaq|Nasdaq]], where listing and disclosure standards are lighter.
- **Thin [[liquidity]]** — low daily volume, wide [[bid-ask-spread|bid-ask spreads]], and small float, so even modest orders move the price.
- **Limited disclosure and coverage** — little or no analyst coverage, sparse or irregular financial reporting, and sometimes no audited statements.

> **Note on price vs. value.** A $0.50 stock is not "cheaper" than a $500 stock in any meaningful sense. Cheapness is about [[valuation]] (price relative to earnings, assets, cash flow), not the nominal price tag. A high share count means a low price can still imply a multi-billion-dollar company, and vice versa.

## Characteristics and risk/return profile

| Dimension | Penny stock | [[blue-chip|Blue-chip]] benchmark |
|---|---|---|
| Price | Often < $1-$5 | Tens to hundreds of dollars |
| [[market-capitalization]] | Micro / nano-cap | Large / mega-cap |
| [[liquidity]] | Thin; wide spreads | Deep; tight spreads |
| [[volatility]] | Extreme (can double or halve intraday) | Moderate |
| Disclosure | Sparse, sometimes none | Audited, frequent |
| Typical outcome | Lottery-like; most underperform | Steady compounding |

Penny stocks have a **lottery-like payoff**: a small chance of a large gain and a large chance of a slow (or fast) loss — the same skew documented in the [[lottery-stock-anomaly|lottery-stock anomaly]], where low-priced, high-volatility names systematically underperform on average because buyers overpay for the dream of a jackpot. The extreme [[volatility]] cuts both ways, and the thin float means moves are easy to start and hard to exit.

## The SEC "penny stock rules"

Because penny stocks are so fraud-prone, US securities law imposes special **broker-dealer obligations** (Section 15(g) of the Securities Exchange Act and Rules 15g-1 through 15g-9) before a broker can sell a penny stock to a retail customer. In practice the broker must:

- **Deliver a standardized risk-disclosure document** (the "**Schedule 15G**" penny-stock disclosure) describing the risks of the penny-stock market.
- **Make a suitability determination** and get the customer's **written agreement** approving the transaction — the customer must be formally approved for penny-stock trading.
- **Disclose the current quoted bid and offer** (or the fact that none exists) and the broker's **compensation** and any market-maker role in the trade.
- **Send monthly account statements** showing the market value of each penny stock held.

These rules do not make penny stocks safe — they exist precisely because the venue is dangerous, and they create paperwork friction that some legitimate investors find surprising when first buying a sub-$5, off-exchange name.

## What to watch for

- **Pump-and-dump schemes.** Penny stocks are the classic vehicle for [[market-manipulation|pump-and-dump]] fraud: promoters hype a thinly traded name (newsletters, social media, "hot tip" emails), retail buyers pile in, and insiders dump into the spike. The thin float that makes the pump easy makes the exit brutal.
- **Liquidity traps.** A wide [[bid-ask-spread|spread]] means you can lose 10-30% instantly just on the round-trip, and a name that gaps up can be impossible to sell at the quoted price.
- **Dilution.** Many micro-caps fund themselves by issuing new shares (toxic convertibles, at-the-market offerings), steadily diluting existing holders.
- **Disclosure gaps and [[delisting]].** Irregular filings, going-concern warnings, and reverse splits to maintain a listing are red flags; some end in [[delisting]] to the lowest OTC tiers.
- **Reverse stock splits.** A company near the $1 listing minimum may do a reverse split, which props up the price cosmetically without changing the underlying value.

## Hypothetical example

Suppose a fictional micro-cap, **NanoFusion Labs**, trades at **$0.40** with 50 million shares — a **$20 million** [[market-capitalization|market cap]] — on the OTCQB, with average daily volume of only 80,000 shares and a quoted spread of $0.38 bid / $0.42 ask (~10%). A promotional email blast claims a "breakthrough contract," and volume spikes; the stock runs to $1.20 in a day. A trader buying the hype at $1.20 finds that when the promotion stops, volume evaporates, the bid drops to $0.55, and exiting the position pushes the price down further. The same thinness that produced the 200% spike makes the round-trip a large realized loss. This pattern — illustrative, not a real company — is the textbook penny-stock trap.
