Scott Patterson's *The Quants* is a narrative history of the quantitative trading revolution and the August 2007 quant meltdown, told through the stories of four central figures: [[ken-griffin]] ([[citadel]]), Peter Muller (PDT / Morgan Stanley), Cliff Asness (AQR Capital Management), and Boaz Weinstein (Deutsche Bank). The book traces how physicists, mathematicians, and computer scientists conquered Wall Street with statistical models — and how their collective success created new, hidden forms of systemic risk. The August 2007 event, in which dozens of quant funds suffered simultaneous catastrophic losses, serves as the book's climax and its central cautionary lesson about crowding, leverage, and model overconfidence.

## Key Claims

1. [HIGH] The August 2007 quant meltdown occurred when multiple statistical arbitrage funds simultaneously deleveraged, creating a cascade of forced selling that had nothing to do with the fundamental value of the underlying securities. The event lasted several days and caused billions in losses across the industry. (Source: Scott Patterson)

2. [HIGH] Crowding risk — when many quantitative funds hold similar positions built from similar models and similar data — means that forced liquidation by one fund triggers losses for all others holding the same trades. This hidden correlation only becomes visible during crises. (Source: Scott Patterson)

3. [HIGH] [[ken-griffin]] built [[citadel]] from a Harvard dorm room, starting with convertible bond arbitrage. He used a satellite dish to receive real-time quotes and grew the fund into one of the largest and most diversified hedge funds in the world. (Source: Scott Patterson)

4. [HIGH] Peter Muller's PDT (Process Driven Trading) at Morgan Stanley was one of the most consistently profitable internal quantitative trading groups on Wall Street, generating hundreds of millions in annual profits while remaining largely unknown outside the industry. (Source: Scott Patterson)

5. [HIGH] Cliff Asness, a student of Eugene Fama at the University of Chicago, founded AQR Capital Management and brought academic [[factor-investing|factor research]] — particularly value and momentum — to institutional asset management at scale. (Source: Scott Patterson)

6. [HIGH] The August 2007 quant crisis foreshadowed the broader 2008 financial crisis. The same structural vulnerabilities — extreme [[leverage]], hidden correlation, and model overconfidence — that caused the quant meltdown caused the global financial system's near-collapse one year later. (Source: Scott Patterson)

7. [HIGH] [[leverage]] amplifies both returns and systemic risk. The quant meltdown demonstrated that when multiple leveraged participants unwind similar positions simultaneously, the speed and severity of the market impact far exceeds what any individual fund's risk model predicted. (Source: Scott Patterson)

8. [HIGH] Quantitative strategies that appear diversified on the surface may share hidden factor exposures that cause them to correlate strongly during market stress. Stat-arb portfolios across different funds loaded on the same underlying factors — a vulnerability invisible in calm markets. (Source: Scott Patterson)

9. [HIGH] The Gaussian copula model, used widely to price collateralized debt obligations (CDOs), catastrophically underestimated tail risk and default correlation. Its assumption of stable, low correlations between defaults proved disastrously wrong during the subprime crisis. (Source: Scott Patterson)

10. [HIGH] The quant revolution transformed markets — bringing greater liquidity, tighter spreads, and faster price discovery — but also created new forms of systemic risk from algorithmic herding, model monoculture, and the concentration of leveraged positions in similar strategies. (Source: Scott Patterson)
