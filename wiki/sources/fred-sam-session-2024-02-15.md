Fred and Sam discuss forward PE ratios using consensus analyst estimates, the [[adobe|Adobe]] investment case, the [[burns-philp|Burns Philp]] career story including Papua New Guinea and tax-advantaged compensation, the [[discounted-cash-flow|DCF]] valuation method and its limitations, gross profit discrepancies in [[propel-funeral-partners|PFP]] data, and the impact of interest rate expectations on stock valuations. Fred emphasizes that DCF is only a rough guide and that PE compression/expansion drives real-world price movement.

## Key Claims

### Forward PE and Consensus Earnings

1. [HIGH] The forward PE ratio uses the current price divided by the consensus earnings estimate for the next four quarters (gathered from 7-10 analysts). Fred considers this far more reliable than a single company's own forward guidance.

2. [HIGH] Always use the median of analyst estimates, not the average, because one outlier analyst "spruiking the company" can skew the average. The median eliminates that distortion.

3. [HIGH] Fred bought additional [[adobe|Adobe]] shares when the forward PE based on consensus estimates came in at 27 (versus a trailing PE that was much higher). This tipped him over the edge to buy at $499.75.

### Adobe Investment Case

4. [HIGH] Adobe's five-year average ROE is approximately 35%. Everything about the company is excellent except that it does not pay dividends — a common feature of US stocks because US capital gains tax is lower than most marginal tax rates, so companies manage for capital gain rather than dividends.

5. [HIGH] Fred bought Adobe at approximately $398 originally. It rose above $600. He set a buy at $550, bought again, and then bought at $499.75 and $497.25 when the market pulled back on revised interest rate expectations.

6. [MEDIUM] Fred receives over $30,000 per year in franking credit refunds from the Australian government because he pays no tax while on the pension — a significant income stream from dividend-paying Australian stocks.

### Interest Rates and Market Impact

7. [HIGH] In early 2024, analysts predicted 3-4 US interest rate cuts. Fred believed there was no logical reason to cut: the economy was performing, unemployment was low. The market then shifted to predicting only one cut (September), and stocks fell accordingly — pure PE compression, with no change to underlying earnings.

8. [HIGH] PE compression and expansion is the primary driver of short-term stock price movement. "Nothing fundamentally changed with the company... but the PE ratio shrunk." The morning after an interest rate announcement, all employees are still working, revenue still flowing — only the multiple changes.

### Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

9. [HIGH] The DCF method takes future earnings and discounts them to present value. A dollar in hand now is worth more than a dollar five years from now. The critical input is the interest rate used for discounting — different rates produce wildly different valuations.

10. [HIGH] When interest rates were near zero, DCF produced high valuations that pushed share prices up. As rates rose, future values declined and shares fell. "As simple as that."

11. [HIGH] Fred does not include a "terminal growth rate" in his DCF formula because it is a guess. "Good luck with that."

12. [HIGH] DCF assumes the company continues in the same business sector as the past 10 years — no acquisitions, no pivots. These are large hidden assumptions.

13. [HIGH] Fred uses DCF only as a rough guide. He would never give it "pride of place" in analysis. The real market value is determined by the PE ratio people are willing to pay.

14. [HIGH] For international (US) stocks, use the 10-year US Treasury rate as the risk-free rate. For domestic (ASX) stocks, use the RBA cash rate. The US Treasury market is the deepest and most liquid in the world, making it the global standard.

### Gross Profit Discrepancies in PFP

15. [HIGH] PFP's gross profit showed wildly different values across platforms: Self Wealth at 64%, Alfred at 30%, Trading View at 30%, and PFP's own financial report at 70%. The EBIT line across all sources was nearly identical, suggesting the discrepancy is in how cost of goods sold vs. cost of revenue is defined.

16. [HIGH] "Cost of revenue" is a broader metric than "cost of goods sold" — some platforms include sales, marketing, and admin expenses in cost of revenue, which halves the gross profit figure. The company's own report uses strict COGS, producing the higher figure.

17. [HIGH] For service businesses (unlike goods businesses), value judgments about fixed vs. variable cost allocation make gross profit calculations inherently ambiguous. What matters is consistency in methodology across time.

### Burns Philp Story and Real Estate

18. [HIGH] Fred joined Burns Philp through a newspaper ad that attracted 322 applicants for 1-2 positions. He was appointed projects manager and led the Queensland steel distribution integration.

19. [HIGH] In PNG, Fred's compensation included: 50% salary increase, 25% of salary set aside annually in US dollars in Hong Kong (tax-free), 20% employer super contribution in Hong Kong (tax-free), all Australian entitlements converted to US dollars. The Kina was protected at a fixed rate. Average exchange rate was $1.76 AUD.

20. [HIGH] Fred bought investment flats using the 1987 crash as an opportunity: "Sheer luck — no greater analytics on my part. I just felt the market had overheated. The prices of real estate were so low I couldn't afford to pass it up."

### Management Insights

21. [HIGH] A senior manager at Burns Philp taught Fred: "When you look at numbers, try and form an image of the company in your mind." Fred still practices this — using financial figures to build a mental picture of the business before making investment decisions.

22. [HIGH] Investors, including Fred, are prepared to pay extra for companies with an existing customer base that will buy more from them, pay monthly, and have a low churn rate. [[adobe|Adobe]] and [[microsoft|Microsoft]] with Office 365 are prime examples.

23. [HIGH] When Morgans analyst recommended selling [[cloudflare|Cloudflare]] (citing unprofitability and debt), Fred overrode the advice based on his own image of the company. The stock subsequently more than doubled.

24. [MEDIUM] Strategic planning (Boston Consulting Group methodology, which Fred learned firsthand in 1980): Year 1 projections are pretty accurate, Year 2 reasonable, and all others are "bullshit, just crap." In today's disruptive environment, forward projections are even less valuable.
