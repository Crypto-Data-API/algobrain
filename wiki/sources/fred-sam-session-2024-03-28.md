Wide-ranging session covering Fred's restructuring of the [[alfred]] stock report format, a detailed case study of [[northern-star-resources]] (NST) and gold sector peer analysis, Fred's legendary North Flinders gold mine investment story from 1979-1985, analysis of [[perseus-mining]] (PRU) with sovereign risk in Africa, and review of the [[close-the-loop]] (CLG) report with discussion of DCF valuation and acquisition risk.

## Key Claims

### Alfred Report Structure & One-Pager Concept

1. [HIGH] The stock report snapshot should include: number of shares on issue, key valuation metrics (PE, ROE, D/E), profitability metrics, and solvency ratios -- these core metrics apply universally across all sectors and companies.
2. [HIGH] Fred advocates a "one-pager" format for quick screening -- if the one-pager metrics do not look good, you can stop analysis there without producing a full report.
3. [HIGH] The full report should add sector-specific metrics beyond the core universal ones (e.g., mine life and production costs for materials sector, banking-specific ratios for financials).
4. [HIGH] Always date every report printout and note the number of years of data shown (e.g., 5-year averages) and whether it uses quarterly or annual figures.
5. [MEDIUM] Fred recommends including the last quarter's data alongside historical averages, because for commodity companies like gold miners, the most recent quarter reflects the current commodity price impact on the share price.

### Gold Sector Peer Comparison

6. [HIGH] When comparing companies, you must compare like-with-like: companies at the same stage of development in the same industry sub-sector -- not the entire materials sector. For gold, this means comparing only producing gold miners, excluding explorers and developers. (Source: Fred's professional methodology)
7. [HIGH] Negative-earnings companies should be removed from peer group averages as they distort the comparison.
8. [MEDIUM] Australian miners are excellent on a worldwide basis, so there is less need to look overseas for gold investments, though Canadian and US-listed miners are accessible through CommSec and SelfWealth.
9. [MEDIUM] Fred sees gold, copper, and uranium as the key commodities with tailwinds in the materials sector.

### North Flinders Gold Mine Story (1979-1985)

10. [HIGH] Fred bought North Flinders Mines shares at approximately $1.10 average (starting at ~80 cents) in 1979-1980, based on intelligence from Rupert Murdoch's casual recommendation at a cocktail event in Darwin and subsequent investigative journalism confirming the rich gold deposit in the Tanami Desert would proceed despite native title negotiations.
11. [HIGH] The shares reached approximately $27-30 by 1985 -- roughly a 25x return. Fred sold half immediately at ~$27 and the balance at ~$29 about six months later. The key factor was the very small number of shares on issue combined with an extraordinarily rich gold deposit (150 oz/tonne vs typical 30 oz/tonne).
12. [HIGH] Fred allocated approximately $4,000-5,000 to this speculative position, which represented about three months of after-tax savings on a $50,000 salary. He had a mental target of $10 per share (10x) as his expectation.
13. [MEDIUM] The principle demonstrated: a small number of shares on issue combined with a high-value underlying asset creates explosive upside for shareholders.

### BHP vs Microsoft / Tech vs Gold Allocation

14. [HIGH] Fred's view on [[bhp]]: bought at ~$12.45, would not buy more, will hold existing shares for dividends. BHP trades in a $45-55 range and is essentially a dividend play with low growth upside. He does not like BHP's heavy dependence on China for iron ore revenue.
15. [MEDIUM] Fred's capital allocation principle: do not allocate a lot of capital to BHP in the materials sector when you can buy [[microsoft]] instead. Tech has superior growth characteristics for capital allocation.
16. [HIGH] Gold stocks are not buy-and-hold -- there are many factors that cause them to underperform gold itself over time. Half of the top 10 global gold producers have underperformed the gold price over five years.

### Perseus Mining (PRU) Sovereign Risk Analysis

17. [HIGH] For mining companies operating in developing countries (especially Africa), the key additional data needed before investing is: (a) remaining mine life for each operation, and (b) revenue split by country/mine. Without these, you cannot properly assess the risk.
18. [HIGH] Fred would not invest in Sudan under any circumstances due to extreme sovereign risk from rebel groups and political instability. He is cautious but not entirely negative on Ghana (considered relatively stable) and Ivory Coast.
19. [MEDIUM] PRU had a PE of ~5.5-7.4 at the time of analysis. Fred suspected something undisclosed was keeping the PE unusually low -- possibly government policy risk in the operating countries.
20. [HIGH] Gold share prices lag gold price movements. External events (wars, crises) cause gold to spike immediately, but gold mining shares rise slowly as the market gains confidence the price movement will persist. The correlation between gold price and gold share price is approximately 0.8-0.9, not 1:1.

### Evolution Mining (EVN) Concerns

21. [MEDIUM] Fred holds EVN with an average cost of ~79 cents and will not sell because it is in his super (no capital gains concern) and EVN management is committed to paying dividends. However, recent acquisitions (including Canadian mines) have not been well executed, and the stock's rise is driven purely by gold price, not management performance.
22. [HIGH] When a company's all-in production cost is high relative to peers, and it operates only in high-cost jurisdictions (Australia, US), its share price will structurally underperform peers with lower-cost operations.

### Close the Loop (CLG) Analysis

23. [HIGH] Fred's benchmark for debt-to-equity: generally favors below 50%. CLG was reporting net debt-to-equity of 38% but gross debt-to-equity was actually ~80% -- the difference coming from subtracting cash on hand. Net debt is an accepted practice but can present the best possible picture.
24. [HIGH] When debt-to-equity appears high, the first check is the cash flow statement. If the company has strong, regular, predictable cash flow that can service and replenish the debt, a higher D/E ratio is more acceptable. If cash flow is volatile or unproven (as with CLG's new acquisitions), it is a red flag.
25. [HIGH] Fred's main concern with CLG: despite multiple acquisitions and strong revenue growth, there has been no increase in earnings per share -- this is a critical failing. Acquisitions must eventually translate into EPS growth to justify the share dilution.
26. [MEDIUM] For companies making frequent "bolt-on acquisitions" across different industries: Fred is 85% concerned this is a weakness, as it means the company is not "sticking to the knitting." It can be a strength or weakness depending on execution.
27. [HIGH] When evaluating DCF valuations, the most important variable to verify is the discount rate used. Fred considers this the critical assumption that can make any stock look under or overvalued.
28. [HIGH] Fred is a long-term holder who does not try to time buy-backs: "Warren Buffett and Fred McNaught don't sell and try to buy back." Over the medium term, stock price is determined by company performance, not short-term technicals.
