The foundational text of security analysis and value investing, first published in 1934 by Benjamin Graham and David Dodd of Columbia Business School. Written in the aftermath of the 1929 crash and the Great Depression, it established the intellectual framework for systematically evaluating stocks and bonds based on financial statement analysis, intrinsic value estimation, and margin of safety. The book covers bond analysis, preferred stock evaluation, common stock valuation, and the detection of accounting manipulation. It remains the bible of [[value-investing]] and the direct ancestor of modern [[fundamental-analysis]] and [[factor-investing]].

## Key Claims

1. [HIGH] **Intrinsic value can be estimated from financial statements — price eventually converges to intrinsic value**: The analyst's primary task is to determine what a business is worth based on its assets, earnings power, dividends, and definite prospects. Market price may diverge from intrinsic value for extended periods, but eventually the weighing-machine mechanism corrects the discrepancy. (Source: [[benjamin-graham]], David Dodd)

2. [HIGH] **Margin of safety is the central principle — buy only when price is well below conservative value estimate**: The gap between the purchase price and the estimated intrinsic value is the margin of safety. It protects against errors in analysis, deterioration in fundamentals, and unforeseen adverse events. Without margin of safety, investment becomes speculation. (Source: [[benjamin-graham]], David Dodd)

3. [HIGH] **Fixed-charge coverage ratio is the primary test of bond/debt safety**: A company must earn its fixed charges (interest, rent, preferred dividends) by a comfortable margin under both normal and adverse conditions. Graham and Dodd specify minimum coverage ratios by industry and security type, establishing quantitative thresholds for credit quality. (Source: [[benjamin-graham]], David Dodd)

4. [HIGH] **Earnings must be evaluated over a full business cycle (5-10 years), not a single year**: Single-year earnings are distorted by cyclical conditions, one-time events, and accounting choices. Normalized earning power — the average earnings capacity over a complete business cycle — is the proper basis for valuation. (Source: [[benjamin-graham]], David Dodd)

5. [HIGH] **Balance sheet analysis reveals hidden assets and liabilities that earnings alone miss**: Real estate carried at historical cost, undervalued subsidiaries, excess cash, off-balance-sheet obligations, pension underfunding, and contingent liabilities all affect true enterprise value. Earnings-only analysis is incomplete without balance sheet examination. (Source: [[benjamin-graham]], David Dodd)

6. [HIGH] **Book value provides a floor for stock valuation — stocks trading below book may be opportunities**: Net asset value, and especially net current asset value (current assets minus all liabilities), provides a liquidation floor for stock valuation. Companies trading below net current asset value are potentially deep-value bargains if they are solvent and operationally viable. (Source: [[benjamin-graham]], David Dodd)

7. [HIGH] **Qualitative factors modify quantitative analysis**: Industry conditions, competitive position, management quality, and future prospects must adjust the quantitative assessment derived from financial statements. Quantitative analysis provides the foundation; qualitative judgment determines whether that foundation is sound or deteriorating. (Source: [[benjamin-graham]], David Dodd)

8. [HIGH] **Speculative excess creates opportunities — securities become most attractive when sentiment is worst**: Periods of market panic, forced selling, and extreme pessimism create the widest gaps between price and intrinsic value. The disciplined analyst buys when others are selling in distress and sells when euphoria drives prices above conservative value estimates. (Source: [[benjamin-graham]], David Dodd)

9. [HIGH] **Preferred stocks are neither fish nor fowl — they combine bond limitations with equity risk**: Preferred stocks have limited upside (like bonds) but bear subordination risk and dividend suspension risk (like equities). Graham and Dodd argue that most preferred stocks are poorly designed securities that serve issuers' interests more than investors'. (Source: [[benjamin-graham]], David Dodd)

10. [HIGH] **Depreciation, amortization, and non-recurring charges require careful scrutiny for earnings quality**: Companies manipulate reported earnings through aggressive depreciation schedules, capitalization of expenses, non-recurring charge abuse, and reserve accounting. The analyst must reconstruct economic earnings from reported accounting earnings. (Source: [[benjamin-graham]], David Dodd)

11. [HIGH] **Comparative analysis within an industry reveals relative value**: Comparing financial ratios, growth rates, margins, and valuations across similar companies within the same industry identifies which are overvalued and which are undervalued relative to peers. Industry-specific benchmarks are more informative than market-wide averages. (Source: [[benjamin-graham]], David Dodd)

12. [HIGH] **The analyst's job is to be approximately right rather than precisely wrong**: Intrinsic value is inherently an estimate, not a precise calculation. A conservatively estimated range of values, combined with a margin of safety, is more useful and intellectually honest than a false-precision discounted cash flow yielding a single number to two decimal places. (Source: [[benjamin-graham]], David Dodd)
