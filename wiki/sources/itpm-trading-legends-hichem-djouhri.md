[[anton-kreil]] interviews [[hichem-djouhri|Hichem Djouhri]], a senior trading mentor at itpm based in Dubai. Djouhri is described as one of the best-performing portfolio managers in the Middle East / North Africa (MENA) region over the preceding decade, having won best-performing asset manager of the region for seven consecutive years. The interview covers his career, MENA market dynamics, hedging strategies in commodity-correlated markets, and his observations on retail vs. professional trader mentality. This is part of ITPM's "Trading Legends" promotional series for its paid courses; biographical and performance claims are first-person and single-source, so confidence is MEDIUM.

> **Note on confidence:** Promotional interview for a paid trading-education business. Career details, the "seven consecutive years best-performing" award, and opinions are the interviewee's own statements, not independently verified — treat as [MEDIUM] single-source.

## Career Background

- [MEDIUM] Educated at the University of New Brunswick in Canada. Grew up in Dubai and returned there to begin his career.
- [MEDIUM] Started as an equity derivatives trader for a couple of years before moving to the buy side at an investment bank, where he began managing approximately $500 million — described as a decent amount for someone just starting in the industry.
- [MEDIUM] Career focused on the MENA region, covering approximately 14 different stock markets. Mandates were mainly long-only, with funds covering the whole MENA region and country-specific funds (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, etc.).
- [MEDIUM] Also managed discretionary mandates on behalf of institutions and ultra-high-net-worth individuals, which were more tailored and could include shorter-term tactical trading strategies.
- [MEDIUM] Was in his early thirties at the time of the interview.

## MENA Market Characteristics

- [MEDIUM] MENA market [[volatility]] is approximately three times the VIX. "Volatility is a trader's best friend or worst enemy if you don't know how to use it."
- [MEDIUM] Approximately 70% of the MENA market is made up of retail money, which is completely different from more developed markets. Retail investors in the region are driven by news, highly speculative (trading in and out daily), frequently misuse margin leading to constant margin calls, and exhibit strong herd mentality following blogs and tips.
- [MEDIUM] Retail investors tend to drive fundamentally strong companies lower during sell-offs, providing institutions with liquidity to buy at discounted prices. Retail then buys when the market is expensive, at which point institutions sell. "It's a cycle that has happened time and time again."

## Portfolio Management Methodology

- [MEDIUM] Key driver of outperformance: identifying companies driven by domestic demand rather than oil prices. Example: telecoms grow as population grows and data becomes more expensive — these stocks outperform regardless of oil price movements.
- [MEDIUM] Counter-intuitive oil correlation trade: when oil prices drop, market participants reflexively short all oil-related entities (especially in Saudi Arabia). However, many of these companies actually benefit from lower oil prices because oil is their raw material input — their margins expand. The market misses this because it focuses solely on the correlation.
- [MEDIUM] Track record shows "beautiful negative correlation" to benchmark: as market falls, portfolio stays flat or rises slightly, then continues to outperform — driven entirely by understanding fundamentals.

## Hedging and Risk Management

- [MEDIUM] Multiple hedging approaches in the MENA region: (1) short individual stocks, (2) buy covered calls, (3) allocate to high-dividend-yielding stocks (5-6% yield with quarterly payments) that are not of high interest to retail investors but have strong fundamentals — the compounding effect adds significant alpha, (4) short oil futures as a hedge when long the market, (5) cross-market correlation trades — long one MENA market while short another, as money flows between MENA markets quite frequently.

## Lessons Learned — Worst Trade

- [MEDIUM] Describes his worst trade: Mobily (Etihad Etisalat) in Saudi Arabia, a telecom that was the "crown jewel" across all portfolios. The stock had approximately 7% annual dividend yield, strong positive cash flows, and predictable growing earnings. Then the company disclosed that it had falsely reported earnings for the past year and a half.
- [MEDIUM] The stock did not go to zero but nearly did. Due to Saudi Arabia's 10% daily circuit breaker limits, if you did not sell in the very first seconds the market was open, the stock was limit-down with no bids for multiple days. Exited with a 30% loss. It was a core position across all portfolios — "nobody was spared."
- [MEDIUM] Key lesson: "Audit your numbers, look at the fundamentals, understand the company, sell with measurement, and there is no such thing as a stock that is going to remain great forever. Stories change and you need to adapt very quickly."

## Retail vs. Professional Trader Mentality

- [MEDIUM] The number one issue with retail traders is the lack of an investment process. Without discipline in your investment process, "you're just a cowboy, shooting everywhere, hoping one of them is going to be a bull's-eye."
- [MEDIUM] Emphasizes two pillars: (1) having a systematic, repeatable investment process, and (2) focusing on fundamentals — not what a friend said, not what was read in a blog, not that a stock "just broke a certain line." Dig into the company and understand the business.
- [MEDIUM] Diversification is essential: investors in the MENA region who only focus on their regional market without understanding global macro drivers (US, China, Europe GDP) are "completely lost." No country is an island. Even to be a good MENA trader, you need to understand the world first, which then naturally expands your investment horizon.
