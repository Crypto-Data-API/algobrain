The investment classic by Peter Lynch, legendary manager of Fidelity's Magellan Fund (1977-1990), where he averaged 29.2% annual returns. Lynch argues that individual investors possess advantages over Wall Street professionals because they encounter great investment opportunities in their daily lives — as consumers, employees, and industry participants. The book provides a practical framework for classifying stocks, evaluating growth, and building a concentrated portfolio of deeply understood holdings. Lynch's emphasis on [[earnings-momentum]], the PEG ratio, and qualitative business analysis has influenced generations of growth-at-a-reasonable-price (GARP) investors.

## Key Claims

1. [HIGH] **Individual investors have advantages over professionals**: Retail investors encounter great investments in daily life — products they use, companies they work for, trends they observe in their industry — before Wall Street analysts discover them. Institutional constraints (career risk, committee approval, position size minimums) prevent professionals from acting on many opportunities. (Source: Peter Lynch)

2. [HIGH] **"Buy what you know" — personal knowledge provides edge**: An investor who works in healthcare, technology, retail, or any industry has firsthand knowledge of which companies are gaining market share, launching superior products, or facing headwinds. This informational edge, when combined with financial analysis, is more valuable than Wall Street research. (Source: Peter Lynch)

3. [HIGH] **Six stock categories require different strategies**: Lynch classifies all stocks into slow growers (low growth, high dividend), stalwarts (steady 10-12% growers), fast growers (20%+ earnings growth), cyclicals (tied to economic cycles), turnarounds (recovering from distress), and asset plays (hidden balance sheet value). Each category has different expected returns, holding periods, and sell criteria. (Source: Peter Lynch)

4. [HIGH] **PEG ratio is superior to P/E alone for growth stocks**: The Price/Earnings-to-Growth ratio (P/E divided by earnings growth rate) normalizes valuation for growth rate. A PEG below 1.0 indicates a stock is attractively priced relative to its growth; a PEG above 2.0 is expensive. This is more informative than P/E alone because it penalizes slow growers with high P/Es and rewards fast growers appropriately. (Source: Peter Lynch)

5. [HIGH] **Earnings growth is the primary driver of stock price appreciation**: Over any meaningful time period, a stock's price trajectory follows its earnings trajectory. Short-term price movements are noise; long-term price movements are earnings. This makes earnings analysis the single most important research activity. (Source: Peter Lynch)

6. [HIGH] **Do your homework before buying**: Read the annual report, understand the business model, check the balance sheet for excessive debt, verify the competitive position, and know the company's growth strategy. A two-minute tip should lead to two hours of research, not a market order. (Source: Peter Lynch)

7. [HIGH] **Own 3-10 stocks you understand deeply, not 50 you don't**: Overdiversification ("diworsification") dilutes returns and makes it impossible to track positions properly. A concentrated portfolio of deeply researched holdings outperforms a scattered collection of superficially understood names. (Source: Peter Lynch)

8. [HIGH] **Tenbaggers come from fast growers with sustainable competitive advantages**: The 10x returns that transform portfolios originate in companies with strong earnings growth (20%+), defensible market positions, and long growth runways — typically discovered before institutional investors pile in and drive the P/E to expensive levels. (Source: Peter Lynch)

9. [HIGH] **Buy when the story is intact but the stock is down on temporary bad news**: The best buying opportunities arise when a fundamentally strong company's stock declines due to temporary factors — a weak quarter, sector-wide selling, macro fears — while the long-term growth thesis remains unchanged. (Source: Peter Lynch)

10. [HIGH] **Wall Street consensus is often wrong — contrarian opportunities arise when institutions ignore a stock**: By the time a stock is widely covered by analysts, recommended by brokers, and owned by institutions, the easy money has been made. The best returns come from identifying great companies before Wall Street discovers them. (Source: Peter Lynch)
