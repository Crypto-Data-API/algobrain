The intellectual manifesto of George Soros, founder of the [[quantum-fund]] and the most successful macro trader in history. Published in 1987, the book presents Soros's "theory of reflexivity" — the argument that financial markets are inherently unstable because participants' biased perceptions influence the fundamentals they are trying to evaluate, creating self-reinforcing feedback loops that drive boom-bust cycles. The book includes a real-time trading diary from 1985-1986 showing how Soros applied reflexivity to positions in currencies, bonds, and equities. It directly challenges the efficient market hypothesis and equilibrium-based economic models.

## Key Claims

1. [HIGH] **Reflexivity: market participants' biased perceptions influence fundamentals, creating feedback loops**: Investors do not passively observe reality — their actions based on imperfect beliefs change the reality they observe. When investors believe a stock/currency/market will rise, their buying causes it to rise, confirming their belief and attracting more buyers. This feedback loop drives trends far beyond what fundamentals alone would justify. (Source: [[george-soros]])

2. [HIGH] **Markets are NOT efficient — they are inherently unstable due to reflexive feedback**: The efficient market hypothesis assumes that prices reflect all available information and tend toward equilibrium. Soros argues the opposite: reflexive feedback between perception and reality makes disequilibrium the natural state and equilibrium a temporary, unstable condition between booms and busts. (Source: [[george-soros]])

3. [HIGH] **Boom-bust sequences follow a recognizable pattern**: The typical reflexive cycle progresses through: unrecognized trend, self-reinforcing phase (trend and bias align), testing (temporary setback), acceleration (trend resumes stronger), twilight (growing doubts), crisis point (reversal triggers), and violent reversal (self-reinforcing decline). Identifying the current phase is the key to macro positioning. (Source: [[george-soros]])

4. [HIGH] **The prevailing bias and the underlying trend interact to create market dynamics**: Every market has a dominant narrative (prevailing bias) and an actual fundamental trajectory (underlying trend). When the two reinforce each other, markets trend powerfully. When the prevailing bias diverges from the underlying trend, the setup for reversal is forming. (Source: [[george-soros]])

5. [HIGH] **Soros's theory of reflexivity challenges the equilibrium assumptions of classical economics**: Standard economic theory models rational agents in equilibrium systems. Soros argues that agents are fallible, their fallibility affects outcomes, and the resulting feedback loops invalidate equilibrium as a useful concept for financial markets. Financial markets require a different theoretical framework than natural sciences. (Source: [[george-soros]])

6. [HIGH] **Macro trading requires understanding policy makers' likely responses, not just economic data**: Central banks, finance ministers, and regulators react to market events and economic data, and their reactions change the fundamentals. Successful macro trading means anticipating the policy response as much as the underlying economic dynamics. (Source: [[george-soros]])

7. [HIGH] **Currency markets are particularly reflexive — speculative flows affect fundamentals**: In foreign exchange markets, speculative capital flows influence trade balances, inflation rates, and interest rate policy — the very fundamentals that are supposed to determine exchange rates. This makes currency trends more persistent and reversals more violent than equilibrium models predict. (Source: [[george-soros]])

8. [HIGH] **Short selling during the reversal phase of boom-bust cycles captures the fastest price declines**: The unwinding of a reflexive boom produces the most violent price movements because the self-reinforcing process operates in reverse: selling triggers more selling, credit contracts, margin calls force liquidation, and the prevailing bias flips from euphoria to panic. (Source: [[george-soros]])

9. [HIGH] **Risk management for macro trades: size positions to survive being wrong, cut losses when thesis is invalidated**: Soros sizes positions to survive inevitable errors — ensuring that no single trade can threaten the portfolio's viability. When the investment thesis is proven wrong by market action, cut the position immediately rather than hoping for recovery. When the thesis is confirmed, increase size aggressively. (Source: [[george-soros]])

10. [HIGH] **Real-time journaling of investment thesis and revisions is essential for self-correction**: Soros maintained a contemporaneous diary recording his investment theses, the logic behind positions, and real-time revisions as new information arrived. This practice enforced intellectual honesty, prevented rationalization of losses, and enabled rapid adaptation when market behavior invalidated the thesis. (Source: [[george-soros]])
