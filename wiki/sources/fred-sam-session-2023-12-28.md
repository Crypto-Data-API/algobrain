Fred walks through his complete buy process for [[propel-funeral-partners|Propel Funeral Partners (PFP)]], examining each fundamental metric — revenue growth, EPS, dividends, ROE, debt-to-equity, and PE ratio — and explaining his flag system (green, orange, black). This session is one of the most methodical recordings of Fred's stock evaluation framework and is essential training data for [[alfred]].

## Key Claims

### Sector Selection

1. [HIGH] The first thing that attracted Fred to PFP was the sector — people are always dying and the population is ageing, so customer demand is structurally guaranteed (Source: sector analysis)
2. [HIGH] Fred categorises it "almost in the healthcare sector" — it is in a mature sector but the company is still growing through market share gains (Source: sector classification)

### Revenue & Market Share

3. [HIGH] The only way a company beats the market growth rate is by taking market share from competitors — if deaths grow 5% but PFP revenue grows 10-12%, they must be stealing share (Source: revenue growth analysis)
4. [HIGH] You can infer market share gains by comparing company revenue growth to sector/market growth rates — you do not need granular competitor data (Source: top-level inference method)

### Earnings Per Share (EPS)

5. [HIGH] PFP's EPS growth over five years was 41% cumulative — Fred considers this "more than reasonable" (Source: EPS evaluation)
6. [HIGH] EPS should be evaluated over long periods — five years preferred, three years sometimes, but five is better (Source: EPS timeframe principle)
7. [HIGH] EPS growth and revenue growth together were the two primary motivators for buying PFP (Source: dual growth criteria)

### Dividends

8. [HIGH] PFP was paying 100% franking credit on its dividend — this ticked a box in Fred's current strategy of prioritising franked dividend income (Source: dividend analysis)

### Return on Equity (ROE)

9. [HIGH] PFP's ROE was 7.6% — Fred considers this "just acceptable" with above 10% preferred (Source: ROE evaluation)
10. [HIGH] ROE measures how much the company earns on the money shareholders have lent it — if they earn 10 cents on a dollar of equity, that is 10% ROE (Source: ROE definition)
11. [HIGH] Fred compares equity ROE against risk-free alternatives: if an ANZ hybrid note pays ~7% fully franked with near-zero risk, a stock's ROE needs to meaningfully exceed that to justify the equity risk (Source: ROE vs hybrid comparison)
12. [HIGH] Higher debt-to-equity inflates ROE — a company with $50k equity and $150k debt earning the same absolute profit will show much higher ROE than one with $100k equity and $100k debt (Source: leverage effect on ROE)
13. [MEDIUM] Fred accepted 7.6% ROE for PFP because he believed the growth phase may be temporarily suppressing net profit as the company invests in expansion (Source: growth-phase ROE adjustment)

### Debt-to-Equity

14. [HIGH] PFP's debt-to-equity was 50% — Fred considers this "at the top end" of acceptable. Many companies run at about 35% (Source: debt-to-equity evaluation)
15. [HIGH] Fred's hard cutoff: above 60% debt-to-equity is a "black flag" — he would not buy (Source: debt-to-equity red line)
16. [HIGH] Between 50-60% is an "orange flag" — a warning zone requiring extra scrutiny (Source: debt-to-equity warning zone)
17. [HIGH] Exception: if a company has a clear plan to reduce debt (e.g. borrowed for a specific acquisition and will pay it down to ~45%), Fred may accept temporarily elevated debt (Source: temporary debt exception)
18. [HIGH] The debt-to-equity threshold decision comes directly from the board — if the board has committed to maintaining a certain ratio, that is a positive signal (Source: board debt commitment)

### Price-to-Earnings (PE) Ratio

19. [HIGH] PFP's PE ratio was 33 — Fred wrote in capitals "this is far too high" (Source: PE evaluation)
20. [HIGH] A funeral company in a mature business having a higher PE than [[microsoft|Microsoft]] (~26 at the time) is a "double red flag" (Source: PE comparison)
21. [HIGH] Because of the excessive PE, Fred cut his buy order to the minimum — buying only $2,500-$2,600 worth (~500 shares) instead of his normal minimum of $7,500 (Source: position sizing response to PE)
22. [HIGH] Fred's logic: "that 33 can't last" — the PE will come down either through price decline or earnings catching up. If it drops to $4.50 he will buy more (Source: PE mean reversion expectation)
23. [HIGH] A PE of ~22 would make Fred a "keen buyer" of PFP (Source: target PE for full position)
24. [HIGH] If PE drops because earnings are growing while price is flat, the stock can sustain its price — this is the outcome Fred was positioning for (Source: PE normalisation through earnings growth)
25. [HIGH] The chart showed PFP near all-time highs in a nearly straight-line uptrend — both Fred and Sam independently disliked the chart. Fred said the chart "is signalling clearly and unambiguously that the PE ratio is too high" (Source: chart confirmation of PE concern)

### Fred's Flag System for Stock Evaluation

26. [HIGH] Fred uses a mental scoring system: green flag (favourable), orange flag (warning), black flag (deal-breaker). If there are any black flags, he does not buy (Source: evaluation framework)
27. [HIGH] A stock at all-time highs is a red flag for Fred — he very rarely buys at all-time highs (Source: all-time high aversion)

### Portfolio Management & Strategy

28. [HIGH] Fred buys in round share lots (multiples of 100 or ending in 5/0) — partly historical convention (odd lots used to incur premium fees) and partly for mental accounting simplicity (Source: lot size preference)
29. [HIGH] By buying even a small position ($2,500), Fred starts receiving company announcements and reports, which he reads to learn the business — he opted into all email announcements (Source: small position as research tool)
30. [HIGH] Fred examines charts on 12-month timeframes primarily, sometimes 5-year — anything before that "could have happened, how would I know?" (Source: chart timeframe preference)
31. [HIGH] When evaluating a stock, Fred first reads only the financial results, not the commentary — "at the end of the day, only read the financial results" (per Rex Breedenberg's advice) (Source: results-first principle)
32. [HIGH] Morgans advised Fred he owns too many stocks — Fred estimates roughly 50 across Macquarie, ComSec, and SelfWealth. He acknowledges this is too many to properly monitor (Source: portfolio size concern)
