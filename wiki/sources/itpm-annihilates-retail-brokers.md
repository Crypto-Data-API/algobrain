Source summary of [[anton-kreil]]'s seminar exposing the structural conflicts of interest in the retail brokerage industry. Previously shared only in closed ITPM seminars for ~5 years, this was the first public version. Covers the "inversion narrative" — how the retail trading infrastructure is built against the trader — and contrasts professional vs. retail approaches in detail.

> **Confidence framing (read first):** This is a **promotional seminar by a trading-education vendor** (ITPM / [[anton-kreil|Anton Kreil]]). Its core argument — that the retail brokerage industry is structurally rigged against clients — is broadly corroborated by independent evidence (regulators' own CFD-loss disclosures, ESMA leverage restrictions, the 2010 US CFD ban), so the *direction* of the thesis is well-founded. However, the talk is also a **funnel for ITPM's paid programs**, and many specific figures are asserted from the stage without citation. Claims below are marked individually: structural/regulatory facts that can be independently verified are **[HIGH]**; plausible-but-uncited assertions and rhetorical figures are **[MEDIUM]**; promotional/self-serving claims are **[LOW]**. Treat unsourced statistics (e.g. exact financing-turn percentages, the precise "90/90/90" formulation) as indicative rather than authoritative. Overall source confidence: **medium**.

## Key Claims

### Industry Structure — Exchange Monopoly

1. [HIGH] Global exchanges are effectively a duopoly: ICE (owns NYSE, Euronext) and CME (owns CBOT, NYMEX, COMEX). This gives them pricing power and control over market structure. (Source: direct statement)

### The 90/90/90 Phenomenon

2. [MEDIUM] "90% of retail traders lose 90% of their deposited margin within 90 days." Presented as the industry-standard statistic for CFD/spread-betting brokers. The *direction* is corroborated by regulators (e.g. ESMA/FCA disclosures show 74-89% of retail CFD accounts lose money), but the exact "90/90/90" formulation is a rhetorical device, not a sourced figure. (Source: direct statement)
3. [HIGH] CFDs (Contracts for Difference) are derivative contracts that mirror the price of an underlying asset — the trader never owns the actual stock. (Source: direct statement; definitionally correct)
4. [HIGH] CFDs are not offered to US retail clients; the seminar attributes this to "too many people lost too much money too quickly." (US securities/CFTC rules effectively prohibit retail OTC CFDs.) (Source: direct statement)
5. [MEDIUM] Introducing Broker (IB) contracts were banned in Singapore in 2010 due to massive losses from educators teaching "absolute nonsense" and funneling people onto platforms. Specific regulatory detail asserted from stage, not independently cited here. (Source: direct statement)

### Four Broker Conflicts of Interest

6. [HIGH] **Conflict 1 — Spread**: Broker buys at bid, sells at offer, capturing the spread on every trade. (Source: direct statement; structurally accurate)
7. [HIGH] **Conflict 2 — Commission**: Percentage charged on entry and exit in equities, on top of spread. (Source: direct statement; structurally accurate)
8. [MEDIUM] **Conflict 3 — Financing Turn**: Brokers borrow from investment banks at ~2.75%, lend to retail at ~3.75%, taking a ~1% financing turn; retail deposits are collateralized as part of the revolving credit facility. The *mechanism* (a financing spread on margin) is real; the exact rates are stage figures, not sourced, and the inflammatory phrasing is rhetorical. (Source: direct statement)
9. [MEDIUM] **Conflict 4 — OTC Gain**: With most clients losing, the broker (acting as market-maker / B-book) takes the other side of trades. The B-book conflict is real and regulator-documented; the specific "~90% win rate" figure is an uncited rhetorical estimate. (Source: direct statement)
10. [MEDIUM] All four conflicts create a single super-incentive: maximize volume, frequency, and size of client trading — the stated reason brokers offer 100x leverage in forex. Plausible synthesis; an interpretive claim rather than a verified fact. (Source: direct statement / interpretation)

### Leverage Reality

11. [HIGH] Forex CFD: 100x leverage standard (in 2017, pre-ESMA caps) = 1% variable margin; a 1% adverse move wipes the account. (Source: direct statement; arithmetic and pre-2018 leverage norms accurate)
12. [HIGH] Equities/Commodities CFD: 10x leverage standard = 10% variable margin. (Source: direct statement; consistent with pre-ESMA norms)
13. [MEDIUM] Average retail trading account balance in UK/Europe: approximately £2,000. Uncited stage figure; plausible order of magnitude. (Source: direct statement)
14. [MEDIUM] Professional trader leverage norms: 4-5x equities/commodities, 6-8x currencies. ITPM house figure, consistent with the Investment Banks Destroyed series but not independently sourced. (Source: direct statement)

### Professional vs. Retail Trader Comparison

15. [MEDIUM] Professional approach (per ITPM): systematic long-short-equity portfolio management across multiple asset classes, 80% fundamental-analysis / 20% [[technical-analysis-overview|technical analysis]], 10-20 positions, 5-10% position sizes. Reflects ITPM's house methodology — one professional style, presented as *the* professional style. (Source: direct statement / ITPM methodology)
16. [MEDIUM] Retail approach (per ITPM): short time frames (1-minute to 1-day candles), 1-3 positions, usually favorite forex pair + short S&P + one cheap long stock, seeking quick returns. Characterization/generalization. (Source: direct statement)
17. [HIGH] Professional consistency: risk-adjusted returns with volatility caps. Example: 10% annual risk for 16% return beats 50% risk for 25% return because the first is more investable. The risk-adjusted-return principle is sound (cf. [[sharpe-ratio]]). (Source: direct statement)
18. [MEDIUM] Professional outcome: capital preservation then growth, "get rich slowly," trade forever. Retail outcome: "big money quickly," capital destruction. Rhetorical framing aligned with [[capital-preservation]] orthodoxy. (Source: direct statement)

### The "Inversion Narrative"

19. [LOW] The entire retail infrastructure is built to maximize the probability of client losses; brokers have only ~90 days to extract money from each account. This is the seminar's central rhetorical thesis ("inversion narrative") — directionally supported by B-book conflicts and CFD-loss data, but stated as an absolute and unfalsifiable claim. (Source: direct statement / interpretation)
20. [MEDIUM] 30-50% of broker revenue goes to marketing to replace blown-up accounts; growth requires opening more accounts than close. Uncited estimate; plausible for high-churn CFD brokers. (Source: direct statement)
21. [HIGH] Account opening requires only ID/proof of address plus a risk-disclosure acknowledgement, after which the broker extends high leverage. (Onboarding/KYC + risk-warning practice is accurate; the framing is rhetorical.) (Source: direct statement)
22. [LOW] "I am a forex day trader — can the Institute help me?" is called "the most stupid question" because it self-limits to one asset class and one time horizon. Opinion / sales framing. (Source: direct statement)

### Volatility and Market Dynamics

23. [MEDIUM] Post-2009 volatility in major asset classes was "crushed" via: (1) QE suppressing yields, (2) more participants/liquidity from technology access, (3) algorithmic trading compressing short-term opportunities. The low-vol regime of 2012-2017 is real; the causal attribution is the presenter's interpretation. (Source: direct statement / interpretation)
24. [LOW] Day trading/scalping "has not existed for a good six-seven years" (as of 2017). Overstated rhetorical claim — day trading demonstrably persisted; intended to steer students away from short time frames. (Source: direct statement / opinion)
25. [MEDIUM] The "whisper number" phenomenon: pros anticipate macro releases; retail traders reacting to NFP are "providing liquidity for the smart guys to get out." The adverse-selection dynamic around data releases is well-documented; the "month in advance" specifics are rhetorical. (Source: direct statement)

### Pension Crisis Context

26. [MEDIUM] Average pension balance: UK ~£30,000, US ~$67,000, Europe ~€40,000, Singapore ~$330,000; "no one can retire" in the Western world. Uncited stage figures; broadly consistent with the well-documented retirement-savings shortfall, but the round numbers are indicative. (Source: direct statement)
27. [MEDIUM] The pension crisis drives middle-class people into trading, and a predatory education/brokerage industry has grown to cater to them. Interpretive thesis; plausible and partly corroborated by regulator action against trading "educators." (Source: direct statement / interpretation)

### Self-Regulation Imperative

28. [MEDIUM] Regulators are "always passive and reactionary" — they act after the damage is done; "as a retail trader you have to regulate yourself." Opinion with a kernel of truth (regulation often lags); stated as an absolute. Notably, ESMA *did* later (2018) impose binding retail leverage caps, partly contradicting the "purely reactionary/useless" framing. (Source: direct statement / opinion)

### Charlatan Educators

29. [LOW] Free seminars by brokers and educators teach the "reverse of what professional traders do," specifically to generate volume/turnover for the broker. Self-serving claim that positions ITPM against competitors; directionally plausible for broker-funded "education" but unfalsifiable as stated. (Source: direct statement / competitive positioning)
30. [LOW] ITPM's mission: first show how the industry works to prevent losses, then teach professional methodology for making money. Explicit sales pitch for ITPM's paid programs. (Source: promotional context)
