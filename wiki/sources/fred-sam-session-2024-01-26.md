Fred announces he has sold his personal crypto holdings (both Bitcoin and Ethereum) while retaining positions in the self-managed super fund. Extensive discussion of interest rate dynamics, two types of inflation (demand-pull and cost-push), the Cairns sugar boom as a real-world example of money flow velocity, the housing market, [[dollar-cost-averaging]] both down and up, and the discounted cash flow framework for understanding forward-looking asset pricing.

## Key Claims

### Fred Selling Crypto

1. [HIGH] Fred sold all Bitcoin and Ethereum in his personal name after BTC passed its previous all-time high and approached $100K AUD — he did not sell the holdings in the self-managed super fund (Source: crypto sale decision)
2. [HIGH] Fred's sell rationale: "All bull runs come to an end... momentum has overtaken reality. This is now what I would call a momentum trade" (Source: momentum characterisation)
3. [HIGH] The first warning signal was Bitcoin passing its previous all-time high — Fred treats this as a cautionary marker rather than a bullish one (Source: sell signal)
4. [HIGH] Fred's selling philosophy: "You can never pick the peak and never pick the drop" — he decided to "take some money off the table" on a day when BTC had gone up and come down (Source: selling discipline)
5. [HIGH] Fred sold when BTC was going down, not up: "It's going down, but I am not confident this is going to go back up again. And if it does, I'm not confident it will go up in any meaningful way" (Source: sell timing)

### Interest Rates & Inflation

6. [HIGH] The market rally in late 2023/early 2024 was driven by belief that next interest rate move would be downward — "risk premiums built into the market disappeared because people said interest rates are going down" (Source: risk-off scenario)
7. [HIGH] Fred believes the risk-off scenario is "starting to wear thin" — US inflation went up in January and remained elevated in February, making rate cuts unlikely soon (Source: inflation persistence)
8. [HIGH] [[larry-summers|Larry Summers]] "does not agree" with consensus on rate cuts — he remains concerned about embedded inflation in the US (Source: macro view)
9. [HIGH] Two types of inflation: (1) **Demand-pull inflation** — "too much money chasing too few goods"; (2) **Cost-push inflation** — when costs (especially wages) rise, prices must rise to maintain gross profit margins (Source: inflation types)
10. [HIGH] The current inflation cycle started as demand-pull (government COVID stimulus + migration) and transitioned to cost-push as workers demanded higher wages to keep pace with rising prices, creating a "terrible cycle" (Source: inflation cycle)
11. [HIGH] The 2021 inflation was predictable: Biden immediately spent ~$870 billion on stimulus upon taking office, ignoring Trump's Treasury Secretary's formula that interest expense should not exceed 22% of tax revenue collected — "politics and ideology overrode economic sense" (Source: fiscal policy critique)
12. [HIGH] Fred predicted inflation in February 2021 and warned a friend to be cautious with investments — he has a witness (Source: inflation prediction)

### Cairns Sugar Boom — Money Flow

13. [HIGH] Real-world example of money multiplier: when Cairns sugar farmers got 12 cents/pound (unprecedented), the money flowed through the entire economy — farmer buys tractor, tractor dealer buys car, car dealer spends, and so on down the chain (Source: economic multiplier)
14. [MEDIUM] The economic multiplier effect means one dollar of initial spending creates approximately $2.20 of total economic activity — Fred attributes this concept to a British economist whose name he couldn't recall (Source: multiplier estimate)

### Interest Rates & Asset Pricing

15. [HIGH] Ethereum staking return vs bank interest rates: if you earn ~4% on ETH staking and bank rates exceed 5%, there's less incentive to hold the riskier asset. Lower interest rates make crypto yields relatively more attractive (Source: opportunity cost)
16. [HIGH] "Every second word is interest rates" on Bloomberg — the entire financial system is heavily influenced by central bank rate decisions, which act as levers controlling the economy (Source: interest rate dominance)
17. [HIGH] The Federal Reserve's dual mandate is to keep inflation around 2-3% — "inflation in an economy is like having a cancer" (Source: central bank mandate)
18. [HIGH] Higher interest rates serve dual purpose: make saving more attractive (encouraging less spending) and make borrowing more expensive (reducing demand) — both work to bring demand in line with supply (Source: monetary policy mechanism)
19. [HIGH] Fred's podiatrist asked about buying a house — Fred advised waiting because employment will eventually rise and the government will cut immigration, reducing housing demand. He verified the podiatrist's savings were earning 5.5% at ING (secured by government up to $250K) before offering any guidance (Source: practical advice)

### Dollar Cost Averaging — Up and Down

20. [HIGH] "People think they all want to dollar cost average down. Now you have to learn to dollar cost average up" — Fred bought [[microsoft]] starting at $252, then continued buying at $286+ as it rose because of growing conviction (Source: DCA up principle)
21. [HIGH] Fred's Microsoft buy was triggered by reading a Fortune/Forbes article about Microsoft's AI investment while flying back from KL — he immediately decided to buy more upon returning (Source: Microsoft thesis)
22. [HIGH] Fred acknowledges his mistake with Microsoft: "I didn't buy enough" — he was worried about the weak Australian dollar and potential currency loss if AUD recovered to 75 cents (Source: position sizing regret)

### Discounted Cash Flow & Forward Pricing

23. [HIGH] DCF analysis can make a share worth "anything I like simply by altering the assumptions" — the main unknown is future interest rates over 5-10 years, which nobody can predict (Source: DCF limitations)
24. [HIGH] As interest rates go up, the present value of future cash flows (growth + dividends) goes down; as rates go down, future values go up. The market tries to predict and "get in front of itself" — first movers profit most if their assumption proves correct (Source: DCF mechanics)
25. [HIGH] Fred follows [[warren-buffett]]: macro predictions are "nothing more than PR consultants for the companies" — instead, pick quality companies at the best price and hold (Source: macro skepticism)

### Bitcoin Future Scenarios

26. [MEDIUM] Sam outlines three possible Bitcoin outcomes: (1) goes to zero from hack/flaw/supersession; (2) becomes digital gold / new asset class at ~$500K per coin matching gold's market cap; (3) becomes global unit of account used by AI for micro-transactions. Fred thinks outcome is "two and a bit" — store of value with some utility (Source: Bitcoin scenarios)
27. [HIGH] Fred on Bitcoin vs gold: gold since the late 1700s has only kept pace with inflation — if Bitcoin is truly "digital gold," that implies it will merely break even with inflation long-term (Source: gold comparison)
28. [HIGH] For Bitcoin to function as a mature financial instrument, volatility must decrease dramatically: "If it's being used to pay wages or pay off loans, it can't function with that degree of volatility" — if it matures, expect utility-level returns, not speculative gains (Source: maturity thesis)
