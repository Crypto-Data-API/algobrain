Fred and Sam discuss key fundamental analysis metrics including GMROI (Gross Margin Return on Investment), market share thresholds, and economic moats. The session uses [[propel-funeral-partners|PFP]] as a case study for applying these metrics, and covers how companies borrow to increase [[return-on-equity|ROE]]. Fred also shares his views on [[tesla|Tesla]] as a cautionary tale and [[nvidia|NVIDIA]] as an example of a temporary moat.

## Key Claims

### Market Share — The 10% Rule

1. [HIGH] A business must have at least 10% market share in its sector; below that threshold, larger players can squeeze you out at will. Fred enforced this rule as projects manager at Burns Philp. (Source: Fred's direct professional experience)

2. [HIGH] When Fred was tasked with achieving 10% of the Queensland steel distribution market, he was given two years but accomplished it in under 12 months. Once at 10%, expansion becomes much easier — below that, you cannot get the best supplier deals or generate enough cash flow to grow.

3. [HIGH] Market share can be calculated by comparing a company's revenue to total industry revenue. For [[propel-funeral-partners|PFP]], this means looking at total deaths in their operating areas and the number of funerals they handle. PFP had approximately 7% market share at the time of discussion.

4. [MEDIUM] Even private companies must submit financial information (though it is often years late and sometimes deliberately obscured, as Fred notes James Packer does legally).

### GMROI — Gross Margin Return on Investment

5. [HIGH] The GMROI metric (stock turn multiplied by gross profit percentage) must be greater than 100 for a successful retail business. Fred learned this when Burns Philp employed consultants to analyze Coles vs. Woolworths. Every successful retailer had GMROI above 100; struggling ones were all below.

6. [HIGH] GMROI indicates that management is doing a great job controlling stock levels and maintaining gross profit margins. If GMROI exceeds 100, high operating margins almost certainly follow.

7. [MEDIUM] [[bunnings|Bunnings]] achieves a gross profit above 30%, which Fred considers very high for retail. In earlier decades, investors looked for 40% gross profit, but increased competition and price transparency have made that unachievable for most retailers now.

### PFP Analysis — Financial Metrics

8. [HIGH] Fred's benchmark for current ratio is 2.0; quick ratio should be approximately 1.0. PFP's current ratio of 1.1 and quick ratio of 0.48 look weak, but 100% earnings stability (people always die) provides some comfort.

9. [HIGH] PFP's debt-to-equity was 68 cents in the dollar. Fred's maximum tolerance is 70% for a company with 100% earnings stability. The sector average was a healthier 48%. The high debt is driven by acquisition-funded growth.

10. [MEDIUM] Fred gives PFP's management the benefit of the doubt on high debt because the company is acquiring competitors to increase market share. The key risk is management growing too fast without properly integrating acquisitions.

11. [HIGH] Organic growth (increasing funerals per outlet, analogous to "same store sales" at retailers like Coles/Woolworths) is a critical metric that Fred could not find in PFP's reporting.

12. [HIGH] Financial data on platforms like CommSec can be lagging by 9+ months (data from June 30 when it is already March). This is why Fred values getting the latest quarterly data directly from company reports.

### ROE — Companies Borrow to Increase Return on Equity

13. [HIGH] Approximately 98% of companies borrow money. Two reasons: (1) they need capital for expansion/acquisitions, and (2) using debt increases [[return-on-equity|ROE]]. A company using 50% debt and 50% equity will have a much higher ROE than one using equity alone.

14. [HIGH] Microsoft borrows money despite having enormous cash reserves, specifically because not using debt would drag down its return on equity.

15. [HIGH] At 50% debt, most companies are safe. The risk increases when debt gets too high, but ROE is the metric shareholders care about most — it measures how well management deploys shareholder capital.

### Moat Calculations

16. [HIGH] A moat is not simply high market share. Indicators of a moat include: high ROE (suggesting limited competition allows pricing power), ability to raise prices in all conditions, and barriers preventing new competitors from entering.

17. [HIGH] [[nvidia|NVIDIA]] has a temporary moat (estimated 3-5 years) because it is the sole supplier of a critical chip for AI. Temporary moats arise from new inventions/products but erode as competitors catch up.

18. [MEDIUM] PFP has a small moat — not from competitive advantage per se, but from being the only publicly listed funeral company in Australia, which attracts investment by default.

19. [HIGH] When evaluating [[nvidia|NVIDIA]] as a moat investment: if demand exists and supply is from a single source, the price must go up. Fred identifies the moat, confirms single-source supply, and buys.

### Tesla — Cautionary Example

20. [HIGH] Fred never invests in the automotive industry. He considered EVs overhyped from the start because demand was driven by government subsidies rather than genuine customer need. Revenue not driven by customers is unsustainable.

21. [MEDIUM] Tesla's moat has eroded: BYD outsold Tesla in Q4 2023 in China, Tesla is giving up gross profit to maintain sales, and resale values of older models have collapsed.

22. [HIGH] When investing in a stock with blue sky potential (like Tesla's AI/self-driving narrative), you must assign a PE value to the blue sky and subtract it from the current PE. If the remainder is still too high, it is not a buy. Fred: "When you're investing, not spruiking, you say that's blue sky."
