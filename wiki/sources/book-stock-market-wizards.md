The third volume in Jack Schwager's Market Wizards series, published in 2001 and focused exclusively on equity traders. Conducted during and immediately after the late-1990s tech boom, the interviews capture a generation of stock traders who navigated the most extreme bull market in modern history — and, in many cases, the dotcom unwind that followed. The book is the most psychology-heavy of the four volumes, with Schwager dedicating substantial space to Dr. Ari Kiev (the psychiatrist who consulted to Steve Cohen and other top traders) and Dr. Van K. Tharp (the [[expectancy]] / R-multiple framework that became the foundation of modern position-sizing pedagogy). For an options-trader audience the book is less directly applicable than [[book-the-new-market-wizards|The New Market Wizards]], but the psychology and expectancy framework here is foundational for anyone evaluating any trading system.

## Core Themes

### Trader psychology as the dominant variable
The book's center of gravity is psychology rather than methodology. Schwager devotes more space to the inner game — beliefs, self-sabotage, drawdown response, identity — than in any prior volume. Kiev's chapter in particular argues that elite traders have systematically different relationships with uncertainty, loss, and identity than typical retail traders. (Source: Ari Kiev interview)

### Expectancy and R-multiples
Tharp's contribution is methodological: every trade can be measured in R-multiples, where 1R is the initial risk taken on the trade. A system's [[expectancy]] is its average R-multiple over many trades, and [[position-sizing]] determines how that expectancy translates to portfolio returns. This framework — which originates in Schwager's interview with Tharp — has become near-universal in modern trader education. (Source: Van K. Tharp interview)

### Cutting losers fast as the universal rule
Across virtually every interview the same rule appears: minimize loss per trade, accept being wrong, never average down on a loser. Minervini's "8% stop loss" rule, Cook's intraday risk-budget discipline, and Walton's emphasis on not letting losses compound are all variations on the same principle. (Source: multiple interviewees)

### Concentration vs. diversification
Several interviewees (Watson, Okumus, Walton) ran concentrated long-equity books — substantially more concentrated than the textbook recommendation. The argument: if you've done the research and have an edge, dilution destroys returns. The counter-argument from the systematic side (Lescarbeau, Tharp): diversification is the only free lunch when edge is small.

### How short-sellers think differently
Dana Galante's interview is the most unusual in the book — a dedicated short-seller in a near-uninterrupted bull market. Her framework: shorts require different position management (positions go *against* you when they work, since they shrink as a percentage of the book), different time horizons (catalyst-driven), and different psychology (tolerating extended periods of being wrong before vindication). [[short-selling|Short-selling]] is structurally harder than going long and demands higher conviction. (Source: Dana Galante interview)

### Systems vs. discretion
The book continues the Wizards-series tradition of profiling both discretionary and systematic traders, with Schwager refusing to declare a winner. Lescarbeau's systematic mutual-fund timing and Tharp's framework sit alongside Cohen, Minervini, Walton, and Watson — all of whom rely heavily on discretionary judgment.

## Most Cited Lessons

1. [HIGH] **Trading is psychology applied to a method, not the other way around**: Kiev's central claim is that the trading method is necessary but secondary; the durable edge is the trader's psychological architecture — their relationship with loss, uncertainty, and identity. (Source: Ari Kiev interview)

2. [HIGH] **Expectancy = (Win% × Avg Win) – (Loss% × Avg Loss)**: Tharp's R-multiple framework is the standard for evaluating any trading system. A system with positive expectancy is profitable in the long run; a system with negative expectancy is not, regardless of hit rate. (Source: Van K. Tharp interview)

3. [HIGH] **Position sizing is more important than entry timing**: Tharp explicitly argues that a mediocre system with optimal sizing outperforms an excellent system with poor sizing. The sizing decision determines whether the expectancy of the system actually compounds in the account. (Source: Van K. Tharp interview)

4. [HIGH] **Cut losses at a pre-defined level, period**: Minervini's 8% rule, Cook's intraday loss caps, and Walton's emphasis on closing losers quickly all reflect the same principle: the largest losses are the ones that compound, and a small mechanical stop is the only reliable defense against the rationalization that will otherwise occur. (Source: multiple interviewees, especially Mark Minervini and Mark D. Cook)

5. [HIGH] **The market doesn't care about your opinion**: Across interviews, traders emphasize that being "right" about a thesis is irrelevant if price moves against you. The market is the arbiter; the trader's job is to manage exposure, not to be vindicated. (Source: multiple interviewees)

6. [HIGH] **Concentration when conviction is high**: Walton, Watson, and Okumus all ran concentrated long equity books and explicitly argued that diluted positions destroy returns when the edge is real. This contradicts standard portfolio theory but matches the practice of many great equity traders. (Source: Stuart Walton, Steve Watson, Ahmet Okumus interviews)

7. [HIGH] **Short-selling is structurally harder than going long**: Galante's interview is the canonical articulation of why dedicated short-only is so rare: positions shrink as they work (reducing winning-trade impact), positions can move infinitely against you (vs. capped downside on longs), and the long-term equity drift is upward. Sustained short-only success requires both methodology and unusual psychological tolerance. (Source: Dana Galante interview)

8. [HIGH] **Identify and exploit your own edge — don't copy**: Multiple interviewees argue that copying another trader's method without their psychology, risk tolerance, and execution speed is a route to mediocrity. Each trader must adapt methods to their own temperament. (Source: multiple interviewees)

9. [HIGH] **Drawdowns are inevitable; the question is how you respond**: Kiev's framework treats drawdown response as the single most diagnostic trader behavior. Average traders chase losses, revenge-trade, and increase size after losing streaks; elite traders reduce size, return to process, and treat drawdowns as information rather than insult. (Source: Ari Kiev interview)

10. [HIGH] **Tape reading and tape feel still matter even in modern equity markets**: Cohen's interview emphasizes that despite all the quantitative advances, intraday equity flow has a "feel" that experienced traders develop and rely on. This is the modern descendant of Livermore-era [[tape-reading|tape reading]] applied to electronic order books. (Source: Steve Cohen interview)

11. [HIGH] **The U.S. Investing Championship contestants demonstrate that triple-digit returns are achievable, briefly, via concentration and momentum**: Minervini and Schwartz both won the championship with extraordinary annual returns. The lesson is not that such returns are sustainable — they aren't, on a fund scale — but that the methodology (concentrated growth-momentum) works in trending markets. (Source: Mark Minervini, Buzzy Schwartz interviews)

12. [HIGH] **Belief systems determine results**: Tharp and Kiev both emphasize that the trader's beliefs about markets — whether markets are random, whether the trader is "supposed" to make money, whether losses are punishments — directly shape behavior and outcomes. Belief work is part of trader development, not optional. (Source: Van K. Tharp, Ari Kiev interviews)

## Critiques and Caveats

- **Era-specific lineup**: The book was written during and immediately after the dotcom bubble. Several profiled traders had outsized returns from the bubble itself, and not all maintained those returns in the bear market that followed.
- **Survivorship bias**: As with all Wizards books, the lineup is selected for success. Many traders using identical methods failed during the same period; their experience is absent.
- **Subsequent regulatory issues**: At least one trader profiled (Michael Lauer) became the subject of significant regulatory action after publication. The book should be read as a snapshot of the time, not as endorsement of all subsequent track records.
- **Less options content than the prior volume**: Unlike [[book-the-new-market-wizards|The New Market Wizards]] (which contains the Yass and Joe Ritchie interviews), this volume has minimal options-specific methodology. Cook is the only options-leaning interviewee, and his use of options is secondary to his equity work.
- **Heavy reliance on psychology consultants**: Critics note that Kiev and Tharp are arguably given disproportionate space relative to the active traders. Defenders argue the psychology framework is the lasting contribution.

## Reading Recommendations for Options Traders

This volume is less directly applicable to options traders than the prior one, but the following interviews are still high-value:

1. **Van K. Tharp** — the R-multiple / expectancy framework translates directly to options-position evaluation. Required foundational reading.
2. **Ari Kiev** — drawdown psychology and the trader's relationship with uncertainty apply identically to options trading.
3. **Mark D. Cook** — closest to a options-leaning interview; intraday risk discipline and the Cumulative Tick indicator are directly useful for short-dated options traders.
4. **Dana Galante** — short-selling psychology informs how to think about short-volatility options positions, which share the "limited gain, large potential loss" structure.
5. **Mark Minervini** — growth-momentum stock selection has obvious analogs in long-call momentum options trading.
