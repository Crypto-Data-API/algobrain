A comprehensive research framework covering the full commodity analysis workflow: market classification, fundamental supply/demand analysis, technical indicators, COT positioning, macro context, geopolitical risk, risk management, and data sources. Compiled from 31 referenced sources including academic papers, government agencies, and professional research platforms.

## Key Claims

### Market Classification [MEDIUM]
1. Commodities span six major asset classes: energy, precious metals, base/industrial metals, agricultural grains, soft commodities, and livestock -- each with distinct price drivers requiring tailored analytical approaches
2. Energy commodities are the most heavily traded by dollar volume

### Fundamental Analysis [HIGH]
3. Supply-demand balance (Total Supply - Total Demand) is the core output of fundamental analysis; persistent surplus pushes prices lower, deficit creates upward pressure
4. USDA WASDE report is the single most market-moving data release for agricultural commodities -- equivalent to earnings reports in equities
5. Capital expenditure cycles create multi-year supply gaps (new mines take 5-10 years)
6. Normalized excess supply models show strong inverse correlation to spot and futures prices

### Technical Analysis [MEDIUM]
7. Commodity markets are especially suited to technical analysis due to sharp moves from supply shocks
8. 200-day moving average is key support/resistance across all commodities
9. RSI useful for mean-reverting commodities; MACD works well across energy and metals
10. Seasonal price patterns are systematic and tradeable: natural gas peaks in winter, grain prices fall post-harvest

### Futures Term Structure [HIGH]
11. Backwardation signals tight near-term supply; contango implies oversupply or high storage costs
12. Roll yield is a critical return component for passive commodity investors

### COT/Market Positioning [HIGH]
13. COT report published weekly by CFTC (Friday, reflecting prior Tuesday's positions)
14. Commercial hedgers often move against price (fading rallies, buying dips)
15. Extreme speculative long positioning relative to historical norms = potential top/reversal risk
16. Extreme speculative short positioning = potential bottom/squeeze risk
17. COT positioning data goes back to 1986, disaggregated data from 2006

### Macro Context [MEDIUM]
18. Most commodities are USD-denominated; rising dollar historically pressures commodity prices
19. Rising real rates compress gold and silver; falling real rates support them
20. China's Caixin PMI is the key indicator for base metals demand
21. Commodities often lead inflation; CPI breakevens signal real-asset demand

### Geopolitical Risk [MEDIUM]
22. OPEC+ production decisions are the single largest discretionary supply variable for crude oil
23. Geopolitical uncertainty is increasingly priced as a permanent cost component rather than transitory
24. Geopolitical Risk Index (GPR) negatively affects returns of crude oil, gold, platinum, and silver
25. Gold functions as a hedge against geopolitical uncertainty and USD debasement
26. Critical supply choke points: Strait of Hormuz (oil), Red Sea shipping, Black Sea grain corridor

### Risk Management [HIGH]
27. Commodity futures require careful position sizing due to higher volatility than equities
28. Basis risk (spot vs futures divergence) is particularly relevant in physical delivery markets
29. Roll yield can be positive (backwardation) or negative (contango) -- critical for long-term positions
30. Commodities offer portfolio diversification; cross-asset correlations change during stress periods

### Data Sources [HIGH]
31. EIA: weekly petroleum stocks, natural gas storage, refinery inputs, crude production (free)
32. USDA ERS/FAS: WASDE reports, production, supply, distribution, export data with API access (free)
33. CFTC: COT reports with weekly positioning data back to 1986 (free)
34. USGS: global minerals supply, demand, and flow data (free)
35. World Bank: worldwide commodity prices and quarterly forecasts (free)
36. CME Group: real-time and historical futures pricing across energy, metals, agriculture
37. LME: global benchmark pricing for base metals and warehouse stock levels
38. S&P Global Platts: price benchmarks used in physical commodity contracts
39. Baker Hughes: weekly US and international drilling rig count

### AI/ML in Commodity Research [MEDIUM]
40. ML-based supply/demand forecasting using weather data, satellite imagery, and shipping data
41. Satellite imagery for counting oil tankers, measuring crop greenness (NDVI), monitoring mine activity
42. Commodity Fundamentals API offers CFTC + EIA + USDA data through single authenticated endpoint
