Fred McNaught recording his core investing principles across 8 short clips. This is the purest distillation of Fred's philosophy — recorded solo, with no conversational digressions. Many principles overlap with [[fred-share-investing-guide]] but include additional verbal nuance and emphasis.

## Key Claims

### Investment Philosophy

1. [HIGH] There are two parts to investing: (1) identify the best stocks, (2) endeavour to buy them for less than they are worth (Source: Clip 1)
2. [HIGH] Nothing will ever stop prices from reaching excessive levels up or down — people cannot be prevented from making excessively optimistic or pessimistic assumptions (Source: Clip 1)
3. [HIGH] The goal is to sell when optimism prevails and buy when pessimism is at its peak (Source: Clip 1)
4. [HIGH] Always keep cash as an asset — you need it to take advantage of buying opportunities (Source: Clip 1)
5. [HIGH] In every boom, a dominant theme emerges that allows valuations to disengage with reality — these themes are always false (Source: Clip 1)
6. [HIGH] "You go for growth to eat, but dividends to sleep" (Source: Clip 1)

### Buying Principles

7. [HIGH] Go with value, not momentum when buying shares (Source: Clip 2)
8. [HIGH] Know the difference between speculating and investing (Source: Clip 2)
9. [HIGH] Demergers are always worth a good look — when a large company divests a division, almost every instance is worth buying (Source: Clip 2)
10. [HIGH] Management quality matters — when you buy equity, you are also buying the current management team (Source: Clip 2)
11. [HIGH] The market isn't always right in determining the price of your stock — this is why you buy when the market dips (Source: Clip 2, Clip 5)
12. [HIGH] Look for companies with predictable earnings (Source: Clip 2)
13. [HIGH] Low PE stocks are not always the most attractive (Source: Clip 2)

### Core vs Trading Portfolio

14. [HIGH] Fred divides his portfolio into two categories: core (long-term hold) and speculating/trading (~15-20% of portfolio) (Source: Clip 3)
15. [HIGH] Core portfolio stocks are sold only when structural changes occur that no longer make them attractive (Source: Clip 3)
16. [HIGH] Trading stocks are bought for cyclical reasons with intent to sell for capital gain (Source: Clip 3)
17. [HIGH] Fred holds trading stocks with ComSec and core portfolio with Macquarie Private Wealth (Source: Clip 3)

### Wealth Creation & Key Metrics

18. [HIGH] [[earnings-per-share|EPS]] growth and [[franking-credits|franked]] [[dividend-yield|dividends]] per share are "one of the main keys" Fred looks at (Source: Clip 4)
19. [HIGH] If EPS grows over time, dividends (paid from EPS) can also grow, leading inevitably to a higher share price (Source: Clip 4)
20. [HIGH] Always look at the balance sheet — companies survive on cash flow (Source: Clip 4)
21. [HIGH] [[return-on-equity|ROE]] is a good measure of economic performance — a business generating high ROE on retained earnings is tantamount to reinvesting interest at a very high rate (Source: Clip 4)
22. [HIGH] [[compounding|Commonwealth Bank]] cited as a good example of high ROE over time (Source: Clip 4)

### Borrowing & Risk

23. [HIGH] Borrowing to invest is an excellent idea — [[negative-gearing]] applies (interest on borrowings to purchase shares is tax deductible) (Source: Clip 5)
24. [HIGH] "Debt properly managed and understood is a great way to build wealth. Debt along with compound interest, the eighth and ninth wonders of the world." (Source: Clip 5)

### Selling Discipline

25. [HIGH] Before you buy a share, know why you are buying it. Make a list of reasons. Only consider selling if those initial reasons change (Source: Clip 6)
26. [HIGH] Timing matters — "it's not timing, but time in the market that counts" is only partially true. You wish to buy at the lowest possible price, so buy when the market dips (Source: Clip 6)
27. [HIGH] Diversify between asset classes AND within each asset class. Invest both domestically and internationally (Source: Clip 6)

### Moats & Specific Holdings

28. [HIGH] Companies that establish significant barriers to entry can be very profitable — a [[economic-moat|moat]] lasting 20 years is a "wide moat" and those stocks are "well worth owning" (Source: Clip 2, Clip 8)
