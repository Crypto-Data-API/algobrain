Sebastian Mallaby's *More Money Than God* is the most comprehensive history of the hedge fund industry, tracing its evolution from Alfred Winslow Jones's invention of the hedged fund structure in 1949 through the modern era of quantitative, macro, and activist strategies. The book profiles key figures — [[george-soros]], Julian Robertson, [[paul-tudor-jones]], [[jim-simons]], and others — while interrogating a central analytical question: do hedge funds make markets better or worse? Mallaby's balanced conclusion is that hedge funds contribute to price discovery and liquidity but also amplify systemic risk through leverage and herding. The book serves as both narrative history and analytical framework for understanding professional trading strategies.

## Key Claims

1. [HIGH] Alfred Winslow Jones invented the hedge fund structure in 1949, combining long/short equity positions with leverage and a 20% performance fee. Jones also pioneered manager co-investment — requiring the fund manager to invest his own capital alongside clients. This basic structure remains the industry template. (Source: Sebastian Mallaby)

2. [HIGH] [[george-soros]]'s [[quantum-fund]] made approximately $1 billion in profit shorting the British pound in September 1992, exploiting the unsustainability of the UK's Exchange Rate Mechanism (ERM) peg. Soros's reflexivity-based analysis identified that the Bank of England's defense of the peg was politically and economically untenable. (Source: Sebastian Mallaby)

3. [HIGH] Julian Robertson's Tiger Management pioneered fundamental long/short equity with a global macro overlay. Robertson trained dozens of analysts who later founded their own funds (the "Tiger Cubs"), creating a diaspora that shaped the modern hedge fund industry. (Source: Sebastian Mallaby)

4. [HIGH] [[paul-tudor-jones]] predicted and profited from the October 1987 stock market crash, making approximately 200% returns that month. His approach combined Elliott Wave theory, historical chart pattern analysis (specifically analogies to 1929), and aggressive risk management — demonstrating that pattern recognition and position management can protect capital during extreme market events. (Source: Sebastian Mallaby)

5. [HIGH] [[jim-simons]]'s [[renaissance-technologies]] achieved returns that no other fund has matched — the Medallion Fund generated roughly 66% annualized returns before fees over multiple decades. Simons's approach treated markets as a data science problem, hiring physicists, mathematicians, and computer scientists rather than finance professionals. (Source: Sebastian Mallaby)

6. [HIGH] The efficient market hypothesis in its strong form is contradicted by the sustained alpha generation of multiple hedge fund strategies across decades. Skilled, disciplined participants using diverse strategies — macro, quant, event-driven, activist — have consistently extracted returns above market benchmarks after adjusting for risk. (Source: Sebastian Mallaby)

7. [HIGH] Leverage and short-selling, properly managed, are risk reduction tools rather than purely speculative instruments. Jones's original insight was that a hedged portfolio (long + short) is less risky than a long-only portfolio. Leverage within a hedged framework can improve risk-adjusted returns. The danger lies in unhedged or excessive leverage. (Source: Sebastian Mallaby)

8. [HIGH] Short sellers serve a critical market function by accelerating price discovery for overvalued securities, identifying corporate fraud, and punishing capital misallocation. Markets without short selling are empirically more prone to asset bubbles. (Source: Sebastian Mallaby)

9. [HIGH] Macro hedge funds — [[george-soros|Soros]], Druckenmiller, [[paul-tudor-jones|Tudor Jones]] — profit primarily from identifying unsustainable government and central bank policies and positioning for their eventual reversal. The largest profits come from policy errors, not from predicting economic data. (Source: Sebastian Mallaby)

10. [HIGH] The 2008 financial crisis demonstrated that some hedge fund strategies provide genuine portfolio diversification. While equity long/short and credit-focused funds suffered, macro funds and managed futures (trend-following) funds generated positive returns during the crisis, proving that strategy diversification across uncorrelated approaches is real and valuable. (Source: Sebastian Mallaby)
