Focused session on [[gross-profit]] as the fundamental metric for evaluating businesses, the GMROI (Gross Margin Return on Investment) formula that Fred's team at Burns Philp developed to evaluate retailers, [[stock-turn]] mechanics, what drives changes in gross profit margin, and the hierarchy of profitability metrics (gross profit margin, operating profit margin, net profit margin). Includes Fred's practical business stories from running a steel/plate distribution business.

## Key Claims

### Gross Profit as "Oxygen of Business"

1. [HIGH] Tony O'Reilly (Heinz CEO) stated: "Gross profit is the oxygen of business." Fred considers this 100% correct and has used it as a guiding principle throughout his management career. Every business Fred managed achieved at least top-quartile industry gross profit.
2. [HIGH] Fred's hierarchy of profitability metrics: (1) Gross Profit Margin is the most important -- it reflects pricing power and management skill; (2) Operating Profit Margin adds in operating expenses; (3) Net Profit Margin adds interest and tax. Gross profit is the surface metric, and you can dig deeper with the others.
3. [HIGH] "Any fool can cut back expenses. Any idiot can do it. But only smart managers can maximize the gross profit." This is Fred's central thesis -- operating profit can be improved by cost-cutting (which any mediocre manager can do), but gross profit improvement requires genuine management skill in pricing, product mix, and customer relationships.
4. [HIGH] People will pay more for reliability and quality of service than for the lowest price. Fred proved this running a steel/plate distribution business where his gross profit margin was 20% versus the industry average of 12% and the top performers at 14%. He achieved this through next-day delivery and zero-error fulfillment.

### GMROI Formula (Gross Margin Return on Investment)

5. [HIGH] GMROI = Gross Margin x Stock Turn. This is a metric Fred's team at Burns Philp identified in the early 1970s when evaluating whether to acquire [[coles]]. They found that every successful retailer on the ASX had a GMROI greater than 100. No unsuccessful retailer met this threshold.
6. [HIGH] "Successful" was defined as EBIT-to-Net-Operating-Assets exceeding 15% (similar to a management-level return on capital metric). Every company meeting this criteria also had GMROI > 100. The correlation was perfect across their sample.
7. [HIGH] Example: Coles at the time had approximately 12-13% gross profit margin and 8-9x stock turn, giving a GMROI of approximately 96-117. The low GP was offset by high stock turn.
8. [HIGH] For small businesses, a target of 40% gross profit margin with a 3x stock turn gives GMROI of 120 -- generally indicating a profitable business. The 40% GP target is not a hard rule but a proven practical benchmark for smaller operations.
9. [HIGH] The formula reveals a fundamental tension in retail: you can have high GP with low stock turn, or low GP with high stock turn. Either can produce a viable business if GMROI exceeds 100, but the management challenges differ.

### Stock Turn Mechanics

10. [HIGH] Stock turn measures how many times a business turns over its entire inventory in 12 months. Higher stock turn means less capital tied up in inventory and faster cash conversion.
11. [HIGH] Stock turn is primarily a retail and inventory-based business metric. It does not appear in fundamental analysis of technology or service businesses because they carry minimal physical inventory. This is why Fred notes it is rarely seen in standard stock analysis.
12. [HIGH] Fred's observation on [[kogan]]: CEO Ruslan Kogan claimed he was running a software company, not a retailer. Fred immediately recognized this as wrong -- Kogan is a retailer with inventory risk. When stock built up and did not sell, Kogan had to cut prices repeatedly, sacrificing gross profit to improve stock turn. Fred correctly predicted trouble.
13. [HIGH] Fred's Kogan shopping strategy demonstrates stock turn in action: watch an item's price drop from $129 to $109 to $99 as Kogan tries to clear inventory. When it drops to a suspected cost price (~$75), buy immediately. Each price reduction is the retailer sacrificing GP to improve stock turn.
14. [HIGH] Amazon and Kogan both shifted to third-party marketplace models to solve the stock turn problem -- by not carrying inventory at all (sellers ship directly), they eliminate inventory risk and improve stock turn infinitely. But they sacrifice gross margin for commission income.

### Drivers of Gross Profit Change

15. [HIGH] Fred identifies competition as the primary driver of gross profit changes: "It's only basically ever competitive forces that force prices down and the gross profit down. Virtually no other reasons." This includes both direct competition (same product) and disruptive competition (different technology attacking the market).
16. [HIGH] For companies with no competition or limited competition (like [[microsoft]]), cost increases from materials or regulation can simply be passed on to customers through price increases, maintaining gross profit margin. Strong competitive positions protect GP.
17. [MEDIUM] A recession or economic downturn can affect gross profit, but does not have to. If a company maintains its gross profit margin through a recession (as Microsoft has done), that is an excellent signal of competitive strength and pricing power.
18. [MEDIUM] Supply chain disruptions tend to increase gross profit (counterintuitively) because supply shortages allow companies to raise prices. During COVID, retailers saw gross profit increases as prices rose faster than costs in a supply-constrained environment.
19. [HIGH] Changes in production efficiency (better machines, better processes, AI) that reduce costs create three options for management: (1) maintain prices and keep the extra margin, (2) pass some savings to workers through higher wages, (3) reinvest in the business, or (4) increase dividends. At Burns Philp, they split productivity gains three ways between staff, shareholders, and reinvestment.

### Specific Gross Profit Drivers (Alfred Checklist)

20. [MEDIUM] Potential causes of GP change that [[alfred]] should investigate: (a) competitive intensity changes, (b) technology disruption, (c) raw material cost changes, (d) demand shifts from recession, (e) production efficiency changes, (f) product additions or removals from the product line, (g) increased returns/warranty claims, (h) regulatory changes, (i) supply chain disruptions.
21. [HIGH] Product line changes affect GP: removing a product with below-average GP will raise the overall GP margin; removing one with above-average GP will lower it. This is a mechanical effect that does not reflect management quality.
22. [HIGH] New product pricing principle: "When you introduce a new product, put it out at the highest price you can get. Then if it doesn't sell well, you can drop back." Start high and adjust down -- never start low hoping to raise prices later.

### Fred's Steel/Plate Business Story

23. [HIGH] Fred ran a steel plate distribution business (bought from someone named Jason) and achieved a 20% gross profit margin when the ATO-reported industry average was 12% and the best performers were at 14%. His competitive advantage: next-day delivery and zero errors. Customers willingly paid a premium for reliability.
24. [HIGH] Real-world example of pricing power: Fred quotes a plate order to ANI (part of Union Steel's parent company) at a higher price than their internal transfer price from Union Steel. The customer accepted immediately because Fred delivered next day while Union Steel took 1-2 weeks. The customer's internal calculation: faster delivery = lower inventory carrying cost = net savings despite higher unit price.
25. [HIGH] Gross profit in Australia conventionally refers to the dollar amount, while gross margin refers to the percentage. Fred always converts to percentages for analysis: "It has to look better as a percentage." [[alfred]] should display gross margin (%) as the primary metric, with gross profit ($) as secondary.
