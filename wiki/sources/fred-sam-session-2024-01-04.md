Fred's most detailed session on his crash-buying strategy — the single most significant wealth creator over his lifetime. He recounts the [[crash-of-1987|1987 crash]] and copying Charles Barrett's buy list, explains the mechanics of how/when/how much to buy during crashes, discusses cash allocation for crash buying, the distinction between quality and non-quality stock declines, and ends with a macro warning about US government debt levels potentially causing a future major crash. He also references [[larry-summers|Larry Summers]] as the smartest macroeconomist to follow.

## Key Claims

### The 1987 Crash Story

1. [HIGH] During the [[crash-of-1987|1987 crash]], Fred went to see his broker Rex Breedenberg at Macquarie intending to ask whether to sell — instead he witnessed Charles Barrett (then the richest man in Queensland, who made all his wealth through shares) sending a cab driver to Macquarie with a message because the phone lines were jammed (Source: 1987 anecdote)
2. [HIGH] Barrett was buying aggressively during the crash — Rex wrote down Barrett's buy list and Fred copied approximately a third of the stocks, the ones he knew (Source: 1987 buy list)
3. [HIGH] Fred spent about $10,000 buying crash stocks; within 12-18 months most had doubled; he still holds approximately 60% of them decades later (Source: 1987 outcome)
4. [HIGH] Barrett was not a trader — he was a long-term buy-and-hold investor who simply bought the crashes and held (Source: Barrett strategy)

### Crash-Buying as Wealth Strategy

5. [HIGH] Fred states "categorically, [crash buying] is the most significant wealth maker for me over my lifetime" (Source: direct quote)
6. [HIGH] You must be patient and disciplined — but it is impossible to avoid all trading while waiting for a crash, so the practical approach is to keep cash on hand (Source: patience requirement)
7. [HIGH] Cash for crash-buying should be held in a cash management account (e.g. Macquarie) earning ~3-4% while waiting (Source: cash parking)
8. [HIGH] Stock market crashes occur roughly every 5-8 years (Source: crash frequency estimate)
9. [HIGH] After every crash, the market reaches a new high within a couple of years — the dip shakes out overleveraged participants and the economy resets (Source: recovery pattern)

### Buffett-Munger Philosophy

10. [HIGH] [[charlie-munger|Charlie Munger]] changed [[warren-buffett|Buffett's]] approach from buying undervalued companies to buying quality companies — "we go about this the wrong way; we've got to look for quality companies and buy those" (Source: Buffett-Munger history)
11. [HIGH] Fred has followed this quality-buying rule — using [[csl|CSL]] as his prime example, buying every time it dipped (Source: quality buying execution)
12. [HIGH] Rex Breedenberg told Fred: "up to 70% of the value of your stock could depend on the market" — meaning the market often does not trade on intrinsic value and overshoots both at the top and bottom (Source: Rex principle on market pricing)

### How to Buy During Crashes

13. [HIGH] The market typically drops ~20% in a crash, but quality stocks only drop about 15% — if the market is down 20% and your target stocks are down 15%, that is the buy signal (Source: quality stock discount)
14. [HIGH] You have approximately 2-4 weeks of optimal buying window — crashes never last longer than 3-6 months before the market stabilises (Source: crash buying window)
15. [HIGH] Buy the lower-risk quality stocks first (e.g. [[microsoft|Microsoft]], [[adobe|Adobe]]) and save cash for higher-risk stocks (e.g. [[cloudflare|Cloudflare]]) which will overshoot downward more and present better opportunities later (Source: crash buying order)
16. [HIGH] Never commit all cash at once — Fred's approach is to deploy in stages (Source: staged deployment)

### Cash Allocation for Crash Buying (Practical Example)

17. [HIGH] Example with $50k cash reserve: Microsoft gets ~$10k (in 2 trades), Adobe gets ~$8k, another quality holding ~$4k — roughly half deployed in the early stage on quality names (Source: allocation example)
18. [HIGH] Keep the remaining ~$25k in reserve for: (a) a potential second wave down, (b) higher-risk stocks that overshoot later, (c) capital raise participation from existing holdings (Source: reserve rationale)
19. [HIGH] Fred always retains a base of ~$15k minimum in cash as conservative reserve, never deploying everything — partly because companies he owns will likely need to raise equity during crashes at very attractive prices (Source: minimum cash floor)
20. [HIGH] During crashes, capital raise prices are deeply discounted — e.g. Fred bought [[commonwealth-bank|Commonwealth Bank]] shares at $19 during the GFC via a share purchase plan; they are ~$115 fifteen years later with fully franked dividends throughout (Source: GFC CBA purchase)

### Quality Stock Behaviour vs Junk

21. [HIGH] Quality stocks never drop 50% in a crash — if something drops 50%, it is not quality (Source: quality stock floor)
22. [HIGH] During a crash, the business of quality companies keeps operating — e.g. CBA still opens its doors, processes mortgages, collects interest. The market price drop is irrelevant to the business fundamentals (Source: business continuity principle)
23. [MEDIUM] Non-profitable growth stocks like [[cloudflare|Cloudflare]] carry much higher crash risk because they need equity or debt to survive and a downturn could affect their sales growth — "the risk surrounding Cloudflare is up here, the risk surrounding Microsoft is here" (Source: crash risk hierarchy)

### When to Sell

24. [HIGH] Fred has zero selling during crashes — he never sells during a downturn (Source: no crash selling)
25. [HIGH] Fred sold two stocks that "weren't going anywhere" to cash up, redeploying proceeds toward higher-conviction names (Adobe, Microsoft) — this is not crash selling but strategic portfolio rotation (Source: portfolio rotation)
26. [HIGH] Once Fred sells a stock, he never looks at it again — "I will never look at it again in case it goes up" (Source: selling discipline)

### CSL Case Study

27. [HIGH] Fred bought [[csl|CSL]] initially at $53, then at $68, then up to $100, with an average price around $72 — it subsequently rose to $330 (Source: CSL cost basis)
28. [HIGH] When CSL's heart surgery drug failed FDA final-stage approval, shares dropped to $254 — Fred considered this exceptional luck and bought at $254, $259, and $263 (Source: CSL drug failure buying)
29. [HIGH] Fred's CSL thesis for buying the dip: the management had a proven track record of successful acquisitions and integration; the market was overreacting to one failed drug (Source: CSL management quality)
30. [HIGH] CSL also functions as a currency hedge — most of its revenue is in USD and dividends are paid in USD (Source: CSL currency exposure)

### Currency and International Buying

31. [HIGH] Over time, the AUD has gone from $1.10 to $0.65 against the USD — having some USD-earning stocks provides currency diversification (Source: AUD long-term depreciation)
32. [HIGH] Fred monitors the AUD/USD exchange rate when funding his SelfWealth account for US stock purchases — he prefers to convert when AUD is at or above $0.645-0.650 (Source: currency timing for US purchases)

### Macro Warning: US Debt Crisis

33. [MEDIUM] Fred predicts a future crash triggered by unsustainable US government debt — "there is so much debt held by governments that it is going to cause a collapse at some time" (Source: US debt thesis)
34. [MEDIUM] The trigger will be when buyers of US Treasury bills demand 6%+ yields and no one will finance the government at lower rates — this will cause credit rating downgrades that cascade through every asset class globally (Source: debt crisis mechanism)
35. [MEDIUM] This crash would be massive — potentially 50%+ drops, some stocks going to zero, lasting approximately 3 years (Source: severity estimate)
36. [MEDIUM] For this specific crash type, cash should be held in Swiss francs or Singapore dollars rather than USD — because the USD itself would be crashing (Source: safe haven currencies)
37. [MEDIUM] Fred does not believe he will be alive when this happens, but positions Matthew and Helen to have no debt so they can ride it out (Source: generational planning)

### Larry Summers & Information Sources

38. [HIGH] [[larry-summers|Larry Summers]] is "the smartest guy in the world on macroeconomics" — former Treasury Secretary under Bill Clinton, currently Harvard economics professor (Source: Summers endorsement)
39. [HIGH] Fred recommends watching "Wall Street Week" on Bloomberg — Summers appears roughly 3 times per episode and it is the best macro-economics program available (Source: information source)
40. [HIGH] At the time of recording, Summers was predicting at most one interest rate cut (November), with a 15% chance of a rate rise — contrasting with market consensus of 3-4 cuts (Source: Summers rate prediction)

### BHP Management Lesson

41. [HIGH] [[bhp|BHP]] is cited as a negative management example: strong profits were wasted on overpriced acquisitions in Zimbabwe and the US because "managers thought they were too clever by half" — the share price could never recover until institutional shareholders forced a board change and a new American CEO turned the company around (Source: BHP management failure)

### Property During Crashes

42. [HIGH] Fred borrowed at 17.5% interest rates in the early 1980s recession to buy property at $90,000 that should have been $200,000 — he fixed the rate for 3 years, knowing rates would eventually fall. The property crash created the opportunity; the high interest rate depressed prices enough to offset the borrowing cost (Source: 1980s property purchase)
