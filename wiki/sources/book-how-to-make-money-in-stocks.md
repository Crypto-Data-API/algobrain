The definitive presentation of the CAN SLIM investment system by William O'Neil, founder of Investor's Business Daily. Now in its fourth edition (2009), the book describes a rules-based methodology combining [[fundamental-analysis]] (earnings growth, institutional sponsorship) with [[technical-analysis]] (chart bases, relative strength, volume) to identify high-growth stocks at optimal entry points. O'Neil developed CAN SLIM through an exhaustive historical study of every major stock market winner from 1880 to the present, identifying the common characteristics shared by great stocks before their major advances. The system includes specific quantifiable thresholds for every criterion and a strict 7-8% stop-loss rule.

## Key Claims

1. [HIGH] **CAN SLIM system integrates fundamentals and technicals into a complete investment methodology**: The acronym stands for Current earnings (25%+ quarterly growth), Annual earnings (3-5 year growth with acceleration), New products/management/highs (catalysts), Supply and demand (volume confirmation), Leader or laggard (relative strength), Institutional sponsorship (smart money), Market direction (overall trend). All seven criteria must be satisfied for a buy signal. (Source: William O'Neil)

2. [HIGH] **Current quarterly earnings growth of 25% or more is the minimum threshold**: The best-performing stocks in history showed at least 25% quarterly earnings growth before their major price advances, with many showing 50-100%+ acceleration. Stocks with flat or decelerating earnings should be avoided regardless of other criteria. (Source: William O'Neil, historical study of market winners)

3. [HIGH] **Annual earnings growth over 3-5 years should show consistent acceleration**: Companies whose annual earnings growth is not only consistent but accelerating demonstrate expanding competitive advantages and growing market opportunity. Three to five years of accelerating earnings is a hallmark of stocks that go on to make major advances. (Source: William O'Neil)

4. [HIGH] **Buy stocks making new highs from proper chart bases — cup-with-handle, flat base, double bottom**: Price consolidation patterns lasting 5-26 weeks represent periods of institutional accumulation where supply is absorbed by informed buyers. The breakout from a well-formed base on above-average volume is the optimal entry point. Buying breakouts from proper bases, rather than trying to catch bottoms, was the entry method of the vast majority of historical winners. (Source: William O'Neil)

5. [HIGH] **Relative price strength rating of 80 or higher identifies market leaders**: A stock's relative strength rating compares its price performance to all other stocks in the market. Stocks with a rating of 80+ have outperformed 80% of all stocks. Buying market leaders (high RS) rather than laggards (low RS) dramatically improves the probability of success. (Source: William O'Neil)

6. [HIGH] **Volume surge on breakout day confirms institutional accumulation**: Breakout-day volume should be at least 50% above the stock's average daily volume. This confirms that institutional investors — mutual funds, pension funds, hedge funds — are driving the breakout, providing the sustained buying power necessary for a continued advance. Low-volume breakouts frequently fail. (Source: William O'Neil)

7. [HIGH] **Cut all losses at 7-8% below purchase price — no exceptions**: This mechanical stop-loss rule is the risk management foundation of CAN SLIM. It ensures that no single position can inflict catastrophic damage to the portfolio. The 7-8% threshold is calibrated from historical data: most breakouts that fail do so within this range, while successful breakouts rarely pull back this far. (Source: William O'Neil)

8. [HIGH] **Market direction determines whether to be in stocks at all**: The M in CAN SLIM is the most important variable. Three out of four stocks follow the general market direction, so even the strongest individual stocks struggle in bear markets. O'Neil's system uses "follow-through days" (strong gains on heavy volume after a decline) to confirm new uptrends and distribution days (heavy-volume selling) to identify market tops. (Source: William O'Neil)

9. [HIGH] **Most big winners show tight, orderly price consolidation before breakout**: Tight, low-volatility consolidation patterns indicate that holders are unwilling to sell — supply is drying up. Loose, wide, volatile bases indicate institutional distribution and weak holder commitment. The quality of the base predicts the quality of the breakout. (Source: William O'Neil)

10. [HIGH] **Institutional sponsorship provides the buying power for sustained stock advances**: Individual investors cannot drive a stock from $30 to $300. Only sustained institutional buying — mutual funds building positions over weeks and months — provides the capital to power major advances. Increasing institutional ownership (rising number of funds holding the stock) confirms accumulation. (Source: William O'Neil)

11. [HIGH] **Three out of four stocks follow the general market direction — trade with the market, not against it**: O'Neil's historical research shows that approximately 75% of stocks decline during market corrections and bear markets, regardless of their individual fundamentals. This makes market timing — identifying major tops and bottoms — the single most important variable in the system. (Source: William O'Neil)

12. [HIGH] **Historical study of greatest stock market winners reveals common pre-advance characteristics**: O'Neil's study of every major stock winner since 1880 demonstrates that the vast majority shared the same characteristics before their moves: strong earnings acceleration, new products or catalysts, tight chart bases, relative strength leadership, and institutional accumulation. These patterns repeat across eras because human behavior and institutional dynamics are constant. (Source: William O'Neil)
