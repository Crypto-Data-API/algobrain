The definitive comprehensive reference on technical analysis, originally published as *Technical Analysis of the Futures Markets* (1986) and substantially revised in 1999. [[john-murphy]] — former technical analyst for CNBC and director of Merrill Lynch's Technical Analysis Futures Division — systematically covers every major branch of TA from basic chart construction through intermarket analysis. The book is the primary study text for the CMT (Chartered Market Technician) designation and has sold over a million copies. Its core premise: price discounts everything, prices move in trends, and history repeats itself.

## Key Claims

1. [HIGH] **Trend is the most important concept in technical analysis**: All technical tools and indicators exist to answer one question — what is the trend and when will it change? Murphy defines uptrends as successive higher highs and higher lows, downtrends as successive lower highs and lower lows. Trading in the direction of the prevailing trend is the single most important principle. (Source: [[john-murphy]])

2. [HIGH] **Support and resistance are the foundation of all chart pattern analysis**: Prices tend to stop and reverse at levels where supply (resistance) or demand (support) has previously clustered. All chart patterns — head and shoulders, double tops, triangles — are manifestations of support and resistance dynamics. Broken support becomes resistance and vice versa. (Source: [[john-murphy]])

3. [HIGH] **Moving averages are the most versatile and widely used technical indicator**: Moving averages smooth price data, define trend direction, and generate trading signals through crossovers. The 50-day and 200-day simple moving averages are the most universally followed. The "golden cross" (50 crossing above 200) and "death cross" (50 crossing below 200) are widely recognized trend signals. (Source: [[john-murphy]])

4. [HIGH] **Volume confirms price movements**: Volume should expand in the direction of the prevailing trend. Rising prices on increasing volume confirm bullish conviction; rising prices on declining volume warn of potential reversal. Volume leads price — volume changes often precede price changes. (Source: [[john-murphy]])

5. [HIGH] **RSI overbought/oversold levels identify potential reversals**: The Relative Strength Index, developed by Welles Wilder, oscillates between 0 and 100. Readings above 70 indicate overbought conditions; readings below 30 indicate oversold conditions. RSI divergences (price making new highs while RSI makes lower highs) are among the most reliable reversal signals. (Source: [[john-murphy]])

6. [HIGH] **MACD measures momentum through moving average convergence and divergence**: The Moving Average Convergence Divergence indicator, developed by Gerald Appel, uses the difference between 12-period and 26-period exponential moving averages to measure momentum. Signal line crossovers and histogram divergences identify trend changes and momentum shifts. (Source: [[john-murphy]])

7. [HIGH] **Bollinger Bands measure volatility dynamically**: Developed by John Bollinger, these bands plot two standard deviations above and below a 20-period moving average. Price tends to revert to the middle band. Band width contractions ("Bollinger squeezes") precede explosive moves. Approximately 95% of price action occurs within the bands. (Source: [[john-murphy]])

8. [HIGH] **Candlestick patterns provide visual price action signals**: Japanese candlestick patterns — including doji, hammer, engulfing, morning/evening star, and harami — provide specific bullish and bearish implications based on the relationship between open, high, low, and close within one or more periods. (Source: [[john-murphy]])

9. [HIGH] **Fibonacci retracement levels identify probable support and resistance zones**: The 38.2%, 50%, and 61.8% retracement levels of a prior move consistently attract price during corrections. These ratios, derived from the Fibonacci sequence, appear across markets and timeframes. The 61.8% level is the most important. (Source: [[john-murphy]])

10. [HIGH] **Intermarket analysis: bonds, currencies, commodities, and stocks are interconnected**: Rising bond prices (falling yields) are bullish for stocks. A falling dollar is bullish for commodities and commodities-related stocks. Rising commodity prices signal inflation, which is bearish for bonds. These intermarket relationships provide macro context that single-market analysis misses. (Source: [[john-murphy]])

11. [HIGH] **Chart patterns have measurable reliability and price targets**: Head and shoulders, double tops and bottoms, triangles, flags, and pennants have documented success rates and specific measurement rules for price targets. The measured move technique projects targets based on the pattern's height. (Source: [[john-murphy]])

12. [HIGH] **Multiple timeframe analysis provides better context than single-timeframe trading**: A daily chart signal is more reliable when the weekly chart confirms the direction. Murphy recommends analyzing at least three timeframes — one for trend, one for signals, and one for timing. (Source: [[john-murphy]])

13. [HIGH] **Open interest in futures provides additional confirmation**: Rising open interest alongside rising prices confirms new money entering a bullish trend. Declining open interest during price advances suggests short covering rather than genuine buying — a bearish warning. (Source: [[john-murphy]])

14. [HIGH] **No single indicator should be used in isolation**: The most effective technical analysis combines trend-identifying tools (moving averages, trendlines) with oscillators (RSI, MACD, stochastics), confirmed by volume, and contextualized by intermarket relationships. Over-reliance on any single tool produces false signals. (Source: [[john-murphy]])
