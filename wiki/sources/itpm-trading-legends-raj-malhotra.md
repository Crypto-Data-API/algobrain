[[anton-kreil]] interviews [[raj-malhotra]], a senior trading mentor at itpm, in New York. Raj had a career spanning three continents (Europe, Asia, America) as a professional [[options]] trader, culminating as head of all institutional options trading at bank-of-america. The interview covers his career, the 2008 financial crisis from inside a major Wall Street bank, and his insights on how retail traders misunderstand [[volatility]]. This is part of ITPM's "Trading Legends" promotional series for its paid courses; biographical and philosophy claims are first-person and single-source, so confidence is MEDIUM.

> **Note on confidence:** Promotional interview for a paid trading-education business. Career details and opinions are the interviewee's own recollection, not independently verified — treat as [MEDIUM] single-source.

## Career Background

- [MEDIUM] Started trading stocks in high school using Lotus 1-2-3 spreadsheets. Attended the Wharton School of Business at the University of Pennsylvania, graduating in 1998.
- [MEDIUM] Joined a hedge fund owned by BNP Paribas after graduation, trading S&P 500 index [[options]]. In 2000, moved to their New York office trading options for the firm and customers until 2002.
- [MEDIUM] Hired by bank-of-america in 2002 as head of index options. In 2006, named head of all institutional options trading. Made managing director before the age of 30, which he describes as "unheard of."
- [MEDIUM] Left Bank of America in 2009 after the Merrill Lynch merger (Merrill kept their own employees; Bank of America Securities staff became redundant). Moved his team to nomura to help launch their US presence. Stayed at Nomura for approximately three years until retirement in 2012.
- [MEDIUM] Career spanned three continents: Europe, Asia, and America.

## Financial Crisis Experience

- [MEDIUM] Saw cracks appearing around 2006: the assumption that all real estate would go up forever, and banks' balance sheets carrying bad bets they could not move. The hard part was getting the timing right — it took a year to eighteen months before everything crumbled.
- [MEDIUM] At Bank of America, weekly risk meetings brought together all heads of products to discuss positions and observations across asset classes. This cross-asset intelligence from mortgages, structured products, and credit markets helped the equity options desk position defensively before the equity market showed cracks — "the equity market was almost the last one to see the cracks."
- [MEDIUM] Describes the crisis as chaos across all Wall Street firms, not just Bank of America. Notes that the CEO made poor acquisitions (Countrywide at the wrong time, Merrill Lynch "when Merrill Lynch was really worthless") but "the professionals definitely knew what was going on" and were well positioned.
- [MEDIUM] First lesson in financial markets was about greed: a CEO's girlfriend had 200,000 shares at ~$220, was offered a collar with a $150 put (guaranteeing ~$30 million minimum). She got greedy waiting for the stock to return to $220; it went to zero and she walked away with nothing.

## Options Trading Philosophy

- [MEDIUM] Key insight on [[volatility]]: "There's a difference between being low and being cheap. When vol is low, doesn't make it cheap. Usually when vol is high, that's when it's cheap. And when it's low, that's when it's expensive." This is opposite to most retail traders' instincts.
- [MEDIUM] Retail traders should buy vol when it is going up (momentum) and sell vol when it is going down — same directional principle as stocks. Buying vol simply because it is at a low level leads to major time decay losses.
- [MEDIUM] Uses options primarily to express a stock view with better leverage and limited downside, rather than seeking options mispricing: "Most options markets aren't mispriced. I'm just looking at the stock itself and then using the options to express my view."

## Idea Generation — Worldview-First Approach

- [MEDIUM] Advises starting with a worldview: look around at what is happening in the world — what apps kids are using, how behavior is changing, how the world differs from five years ago — then identify companies that will profit from those changes. References Polaroid, Blockbuster as examples of once-relevant companies replaced by new ones like Snapchat.
- [MEDIUM] Follows a top-down approach: (1) develop a worldview, (2) identify which sectors will benefit, (3) drill down to individual company selection. "If the company you want to buy is doing great but in a sector that's declining, that doesn't make sense."
- [MEDIUM] Looks down the value chain for second and third derivative beneficiaries: if Apple says they will sell more iPhones, who benefits? Battery suppliers, component makers, etc. — "that's where you want to look."

## Retail Trader Mistakes

- [MEDIUM] Classic mistakes: overtrading, trading without doing proper research, playing catalysts (like earnings) without having a genuine edge or understanding that news is already priced in.
- [MEDIUM] Advises: if you don't know anything about a company or sector, look elsewhere — there are so many stocks and sectors to choose from. Trade what you understand.
