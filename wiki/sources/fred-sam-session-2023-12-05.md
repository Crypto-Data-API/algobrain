Fred discusses the [[evolution-mining|Evolution Mining (EVN)]] capital raise and why he was sceptical of it, strategies for participating in owned-stock capital raises, and a detailed forex margin trade story from Singapore involving the AUD/USD pair. The session also touches on gold stocks as portfolio components, the role of private loan suppliers (shadow banking), and currency risk when buying US-listed stocks.

## Key Claims

### Capital Raise Strategy (EVN)

1. [HIGH] When a capital raise comes out, the offer price normally reflects the dilution from increased share count, plus a small discount to sweeten the pot — Fred evaluates whether the discount adequately compensates for the risk of the underlying transaction (Source: EVN example)
2. [HIGH] EVN's capital raise at $3.80 was to fund a mine acquisition; Fred believed the company was underestimating the assimilation risk and the price should have been ~$3.50, representing a ~8% gap (Source: EVN analysis)
3. [MEDIUM] Fred's scepticism was partly intuition based on long experience with the company — "the price did not reflect the risk involved in the transaction" (Source: EVN analysis)
4. [HIGH] Companies often release negative information after the capital raise closing date — EVN released negatives to the [[asx|ASX]] that were never mentioned in the original prospectus (Source: EVN post-raise disclosure)
5. [HIGH] It is very difficult to prove this is illegal because companies will claim the information "only came to light" after the raise, and they are advised by lawyers (Source: EVN legal discussion)
6. [HIGH] Capital raises are a way for individual shareholders to make money because you pay no brokerage and get a discount — but the proviso is the company must be successful and grow earnings beyond current EPS (Source: general capital raise principle)
7. [HIGH] Fred's general strategy is to wait until right up to the deadline to participate, in case the price dips and you can buy cheaper on market — exception: if the raise is clearly attractive, waiting risks it being filled early and missing out (Source: capital raise timing)
8. [HIGH] If the market price falls below the capital raise offer price, it is better to buy on market (e.g. through [[comsec|ComSec]]) rather than participate in the raise (Source: EVN price action)
9. [MEDIUM] When share prices crash after a capital raise closes, institutional underwriters are obliged to buy if there is a shortfall, which can cause short-term price spikes — Fred suspects this happened with EVN when shares suddenly shot to $3.80 (Source: EVN institutional buying)
10. [HIGH] Fred chose the minimum $2,500 participation in the EVN raise — his normal minimum would be $7,500 but the red flags kept the commitment low (Source: EVN position sizing)

### Capital Raise — NAB Example

11. [HIGH] With [[national-australia-bank|NAB]], Fred was allowed to buy $30,000 maximum in a capital raise but declined because his bank/financial sector allocation was already at its limit — he did not want to increase his exposure beyond what his portfolio allocation allowed (Source: NAB capital raise)
12. [HIGH] Fred stopped all DRP (Dividend Reinvestment Plan) in his banking/financial sector because he did not want that sector to become a bigger percentage of his portfolio (Source: portfolio allocation discipline)

### Gold Stocks

13. [HIGH] You must have at least one and possibly two gold stocks in your portfolio (Source: gold allocation principle)
14. [MEDIUM] Fred was considering buying [[barrick-gold|Barrick Gold]] in the US/Canada at ~$15-16 per share through [[selfwealth|SelfWealth]] at the time of recording (Source: gold stock interest)
15. [HIGH] If you bought gold bars in the early 1800s and held them, all you would have done is keep pace with inflation — gold only preserves purchasing power, it does not grow it (Source: gold as inflation hedge only)
16. [HIGH] What makes EVN attractive as a gold stock is that it pays a dividend — this is unusual for a gold company and adds value beyond pure gold price exposure (Source: EVN dividend)
17. [HIGH] EVN also provides a currency hedge because gold is priced in USD (Source: EVN currency exposure)
18. [HIGH] Fred's first buy price of EVN was $0.79, so despite capital raise concerns, the long-term position has done extremely well including dividends (Source: EVN cost basis)

### Forex Trading Story (AUD/USD)

19. [HIGH] Fred participated in a leveraged forex trade with three associates in Singapore — each contributed $25,000 USD for a $100,000 pool (Source: forex trade structure)
20. [HIGH] They borrowed at 10x leverage to trade $1,000,000 USD worth of AUD, betting on a rebound in the Australian dollar driven by iron ore prices being higher than the market expected (Source: forex leverage and thesis)
21. [HIGH] The information came from a contact in Japan who knew the negative iron ore forecasts were incorrect — when iron ore exports at high prices flowed into Australia, the AUD would strengthen (Source: forex information edge)
22. [HIGH] Risk management: if the AUD dropped 10%, the stop loss would trigger and they would lose their entire $100,000 capital with no ifs or buts (Source: forex risk discipline)
23. [HIGH] Exit rule: 3 of 4 participants had to agree to sell — Fred was the holdout wanting to go higher (Source: forex exit governance)
24. [HIGH] The AUD went up ~18% and they took profits, with Fred wanting to hold for 20%+ — the trade lasted approximately six months (Source: forex outcome)
25. [HIGH] The trade was conducted through Singapore's shadow banking system — no KYC, no names on accounts, unregulated, but legal in Singapore (Source: shadow banking)
26. [HIGH] The risk with shadow banking is that the counterparty could theoretically abscond with funds — mitigated by reputation and ongoing business relationships (Source: shadow banking risk)

### Currency Risk and ASX Stock

27. [HIGH] When buying offshore stocks in another currency, you take a currency risk that can work for or against you — Fred's [[cloudflare|Cloudflare]] purchase was made when AUD was $0.74 to USD, so currency movement partially offset the share price decline (Source: currency hedging through stocks)
28. [HIGH] Rex Breedenberg, Fred's long-time broker/mentor at Macquarie, recommended [[asx|ASX Ltd]] shares as his single best stock pick when he retired — Fred bought at ~$9.80 and the shares went to $80-90, roughly 10x over ~15-20 years, plus fully franked dividends every six months (Source: ASX Ltd investment)
