Session covering the impact of AI on banking bad debts at [[commonwealth-bank]], Fred's strategy for managing super fund withdrawals using franked dividends versus bank deposits, a comprehensive review of a gold commodity research report, and discussion on gold supply-demand dynamics including central bank buying.

## Key Claims

### AI Impact on Banking

1. [HIGH] [[commonwealth-bank]] (CBA) used AI to scan its entire loan book and identify customers at risk of default as interest rates rose. They proactively contacted these customers from a call centre, advising them to sell or restructure before defaulting. As a result, bad and doubtful debts as a percentage of loans barely moved despite rising interest rates -- breaking a historical pattern where bad debts always rose with rates.
2. [MEDIUM] No analysts are making provision for the positive benefits that AI will deliver to bank performance. They are using backward-looking models that assume bad debts rise with interest rates, but AI has changed this relationship.

### Growth vs Dividend Strategy / Tax Efficiency

3. [HIGH] Fred's strategy for super fund withdrawals: invest in dividend-paying stocks with 30% franking credits rather than bank deposits. At a 40% personal tax rate, franked dividends mean you effectively pay only 10% tax (40% rate minus 30% franking credit), versus paying full 40% on bank interest income.
4. [HIGH] The government's projections for increased tax revenue from super fund withdrawals assume retirees will put money in bank deposits at 5-6%. Fred calls this naive -- experienced investors will use franked dividends and other tax-efficient structures instead.
5. [HIGH] Fred's principle: as you age and move from accumulation to distribution phase, shift from growth stocks to income-paying (dividend) stocks for tax efficiency. Growth stocks are for building wealth; dividend stocks are for drawing income.

### Alfred Report Development / Benchmarks / Peer Groups

6. [HIGH] For the [[alfred]] report, the verdict/conclusion should go at the end, not the beginning. Reading the conclusion first pre-disposes you to a view before reviewing the evidence. Do the analysis first, then arrive at the conclusion.
7. [HIGH] When analyzing a commodity sector (e.g., gold), the report structure should include: commodity overview, price performance, supply and demand (current and historical), peer comparisons of producing companies, sector-specific forecasts, and then conclusions.
8. [HIGH] Fred distinguishes between sector, industry group, industry, and sub-industry (per GICS classification). For peer comparison, you want the sub-industry level (e.g., "gold" not "materials") and further filtered to like-for-like companies at the same development stage.
9. [HIGH] The peer group should show key metrics for each company in the sub-industry, allowing you to circle the 3-4 best candidates and then do deep-dive analysis on each. This is the most efficient screening workflow.

### Gold Market Analysis

10. [HIGH] Gold has historically maintained its purchasing power against inflation since the 1800s. However, since 2000, gold has dramatically outperformed inflation -- driven primarily by massive US government debt accumulation.
11. [HIGH] Fred warns against getting swept up in bullish gold reports. After initially planning to go heavily into gold, he sought a second opinion from a more fact-based report and moderated his position. Always seek contrarian analysis before committing.
12. [HIGH] Central banks, particularly China, have been major gold buyers pushing up the price. However, at current high prices, China has pulled back its buying. If central bank demand drops off, the gold price could face headwinds.
13. [MEDIUM] China is reportedly shifting some buying from gold to silver as gold prices have risen.
14. [HIGH] Gold supply and demand have been remarkably stable over the past 10 years -- approximately equal. The dramatic price increase since 2000 is driven more by monetary factors (US debt, dollar debasement) than supply-demand imbalance.
15. [HIGH] Approximately 58% of gold demand goes to jewellery, with electronics demand growing. Switzerland processes 50-70% of the world's unrefined gold, acting as the main global hub for minting bullion.
16. [HIGH] Because easy-to-find gold has been mined, production costs everywhere are rising as miners must go deeper and to more difficult locations. This provides a structural floor under gold prices.
17. [HIGH] Gold is not a buy-and-hold investment. Fred emphasizes that many external factors affect gold stocks, and roughly half of the top 10 global gold producers underperformed the gold price itself over 5 years. You are better off buying physical gold or a gold ETF if you want pure gold exposure.

### Interest Rates & Inflation Outlook

18. [HIGH] The era of ultra-low inflation is over. Fred considers it "inevitable" that inflation will return higher due to US government debt levels. Every three months, the US must borrow trillions, and lenders will demand progressively higher interest rates as debt grows.
19. [HIGH] Fred believes interest rates now have a structural floor of approximately 5-6%, much higher than the near-zero era. Risk premiums will be layered on top of this floor.
20. [MEDIUM] CPI inflation figures bear "no relationship to reality" according to Fred -- true experienced inflation is significantly higher than official statistics.

### Northern Star & Gold Fields

21. [HIGH] [[northern-star-resources]] is the 7th largest gold producer globally but has underperformed because its all-in production cost is high relative to peers, owing to operations solely in high-cost jurisdictions (Australia and US). Over 5 years, NST returned ~51% vs gold's ~94%.
22. [MEDIUM] [[gold-fields]] has always been well-managed in Fred's experience. He owned it at one point and retained that positive impression.
23. [HIGH] Life of existing mining assets is a "critical metric" for evaluating gold miners. If a mine has only 2-3 years of production left, the stock could drop significantly without warning.
24. [HIGH] When evaluating a commodity stock, you are investing in two things: (a) the correlation with the commodity price, and (b) the quality of the company's management and operations. Both matter, but management quality determines whether the stock outperforms or underperforms the commodity.
