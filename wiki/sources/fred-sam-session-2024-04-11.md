Extensive session covering the [[alfred]] report structure refinements (valuation, profitability, solvency sections), the importance of liquidity in stock selection, Fred's story about [[steve-jobs]] and [[apple]], the mechanics of buying international stocks in pre-internet days, tips for shareholders receiving buyout offers, the sophisticated investor qualification process in Australia, and initial discussion of [[macquarie-technology]] (MAQ:ASX).

## Key Claims

### Alfred Report Structure

1. [HIGH] Fred's preferred report sections: Valuation, Profitability (Profit & Loss), Solvency (liquidity metrics like debt-to-equity, current ratio), and Management. The term "solvency" is preferred over "financials" for the section covering D/E, current ratio, quick ratio, and related metrics.
2. [HIGH] The report should include the number of shares on issue prominently. Share dilution from management options and acquisition payments is a critical concern -- every share issued to management dilutes existing shareholders.
3. [HIGH] Fred's view on CEO compensation via shares: "What you hope is that his expertise and knowledge will get a higher return because he's there, so everyone will be better off." The dilution must be offset by superior performance.
4. [MEDIUM] Government regulations have made executive remuneration reporting so complex (20+ pages in some reports) that it becomes impenetrable. Fred gave up reading them after the first page in some cases.

### Liquidity and Market Crashes

5. [HIGH] Fred's principle on stock liquidity: you need sufficient daily trading volume to be able to exit your position in a reasonable timeframe. For small-cap stocks with thin volume, you can be trapped during a market crash because there are no buyers.
6. [HIGH] During the 1987 crash, Fred observed stocks that literally had no buyers at any price. Liquidity evaporates precisely when you need it most. This is a structural risk for small-cap and micro-cap positions.
7. [HIGH] Fred's rule: always consider "What happens if I need to sell this tomorrow?" before buying. If the answer is "I can't sell it quickly without destroying the price," the position size must be adjusted accordingly.

### Steve Jobs / Apple Story

8. [HIGH] Fred tells the story of [[apple]]'s near-death in the 1990s when Steve Jobs was fired, and the company nearly went bankrupt. Jobs returned, simplified the product line, and transformed it into the world's most valuable company. The lesson: management quality is the single most important factor in a company's success.
9. [MEDIUM] Fred's observation on [[apple]]: the stock's trajectory from near-bankruptcy to world's largest company demonstrates that great management can overcome almost any structural challenge.

### Buying International Stocks (Historical)

10. [HIGH] Before electronic trading, buying international stocks required phone calls to brokers, physical share certificates sent by mail, and settlement times measured in weeks. Fred had to physically arrange share transfers through mail when working in Papua New Guinea.
11. [MEDIUM] Fred notes that the ease of modern international stock trading (through CommSec, SelfWealth) should not be taken for granted -- it creates opportunities the previous generation could not access easily.

### Buyout Tips for Shareholders

12. [HIGH] When a buyout offer arrives for a stock you hold, Fred's first principle: do not accept the first offer. Historically, the initial bid is almost always below the final price. Wait for competing bids or upward revisions.
13. [HIGH] Fred's buyout framework: assess (a) is the offer price fair relative to your fundamental valuation of the company? (b) are there likely competing bidders? (c) what is the board recommending and why? The board must provide an independent expert's report to shareholders.
14. [HIGH] If the bidder already holds a controlling interest (>50%), individual shareholders have limited negotiating power. If the bidder is below 50%, hold out -- they need your shares and will likely raise the offer.
15. [MEDIUM] Fred's personal experience: in several buyout situations, he held out for a better price and was rewarded with 10-30% above the initial offer. Patience during buyouts is typically rewarded.

### Sophisticated Investor Process

16. [HIGH] In Australia, to qualify as a "sophisticated investor" (allowing access to pre-IPO placements, unlisted investments, and other opportunities), you need to demonstrate either: (a) $2.5M in net assets, or (b) $250,000 in gross annual income for the last two years.
17. [HIGH] The qualification process requires an accountant to certify your financial position. Once certified, you can receive offers for investments not available to retail investors -- but Fred warns these carry significantly higher risk and many are garbage.
18. [MEDIUM] Fred advises being extremely selective with sophisticated investor opportunities. The vast majority of pre-IPO and private placement deals fail. Only consider them if you have deep expertise in the specific sector.

### Macquarie Technology (MAQ:ASX)

19. [HIGH] Fred and Sam begin analyzing [[macquarie-technology]] -- a data centre company. Fred notes it has strong tailwinds from cloud computing and AI infrastructure demand.
20. [HIGH] Fred's principle for tech/infrastructure companies: look for long-term government and enterprise contracts that provide predictable recurring revenue. This is the quality of revenue that justifies premium valuations.
21. [MEDIUM] Data centre companies benefit from a structural moat: once a customer's data and operations are hosted in your facility, switching costs are enormous. This creates sticky, long-term revenue streams.

### General Investment Principles

22. [HIGH] Fred's report metric hierarchy: ROE and D/E must always be viewed together because they are mechanically linked -- higher debt (D/E) at the same net profit level will produce higher ROE. A high ROE with high D/E is riskier than a moderate ROE with low D/E.
23. [HIGH] Return on Assets (ROA) is deliberately excluded from Fred's core metrics because it is not comparable across sectors: Microsoft with few physical assets will always have much higher ROA than a mining company with massive physical plant and equipment.
24. [HIGH] Free float versus closely held shares: free float matters most in takeover situations. Fred focuses on the total number of shares on issue as the key figure for day-to-day analysis.
25. [HIGH] Institutional ownership percentage matters because institutions can provide capital support (equity raisings) if a company needs it, and their involvement provides a degree of validation of the company's fundamentals.
26. [MEDIUM] Fred notes that insiders (founders/management) will always try to keep their percentage high and will only reluctantly dilute their holdings over time. This is natural behavior, similar to his own experience founding businesses.
