Two-part session covering sector PE comparison methodology, how companies are classified on the [[asx|ASX]], the distinction between domestic and international PE, Fred's views on [[moats]], demergers, government regulation risk, financial report timing and reliability, third-party audits, data source discrepancies, global macroeconomics, and reserve bank monetary policy. Extensive discussion of practical challenges in finding and interpreting company data.

## Key Claims

### Sector Averages & PE Comparison

1. [HIGH] Every listed company is assigned to a sector category when it lists on the ASX or NYSE — this classification determines which sector average it is compared against (Source: sector classification)
2. [HIGH] PE ratio should be compared to the company's specific sector average, not the total market — "the tech sector will always have a higher PE than the total" (Source: PE methodology)
3. [HIGH] If a company's PE is well above sector average, it flags investigation: "All I've got to tell you is that this particular company is well above the average. All that's flagging there is check this out" (Source: PE screening)
4. [HIGH] There is generally some correlation between domestic and international sector PEs, but Australian companies often trade at lower PE than US counterparts because the US market assigns more growth potential (Source: domestic vs international PE)
5. [HIGH] Fred used Incitec Pivot (fertiliser/explosives company) as an example — it always traded at a lower PE than its American competitor, and they could be compared directly since both were in the same business (Source: direct comparison example)

### Are All Companies Unique?

6. [HIGH] Companies exist on a spectrum — some are "snowflakes" (wide [[moats|moat]], truly unique) and some are "marbles" (interchangeable within a sector). Banks tend to be marbles; [[wesfarmers|Brambles]] is a snowflake with a very wide moat that's difficult to compete against (Source: moat analogy)
7. [MEDIUM] Historical company comparisons were more valid in the past — today, digital-age companies listing on exchanges are "almost completely new" and harder to compare to predecessors (Source: market evolution)

### Gross Profit Emphasis

8. [HIGH] Fred puts more emphasis on [[gross-profit]] percentage than most analysts — it was the factor that pushed him to buy [[microsoft|Microsoft]] despite a PE of ~35 (Source: GP methodology)
9. [HIGH] Microsoft's gross profit percentage was increasing even as revenue grew rapidly — this is rare, as most businesses sacrifice GP% for growth (Source: Microsoft analysis)
10. [HIGH] "The gross profit is the oxygen of the business" — attributed to Tony O'Reilly, CEO of Heinz; Fred adopted this as a primary analytical focus after hearing it firsthand (Source: GP philosophy)

### Demergers

11. [HIGH] Chris Chadley principle: when a company demerges a division, "it's always worth a look" — Coles improved significantly after being separated from [[wesfarmers]] because management could focus solely on that business (Source: demerger thesis)
12. [HIGH] In a conglomerate, divisions compete for capital allocation from the board — after demerger, the independent company's board can secure all necessary capital for growth (Source: capital allocation)
13. [HIGH] [[endeavour-group|Endeavour Group]] was demerged from Woolworths — Fred bought it as a long-term hold because the gambling/liquor sector is resilient during economic downturns: "People are going to continue to have a beer at the pub and continue to gamble" (Source: Endeavour thesis)

### Government Regulation Risk

14. [HIGH] "You can't beat City Hall" — Fred's cardinal rule on regulatory risk. The Victorian government imposed overnight restrictions on poker machines, tanking Endeavour from $6.50 to ~$5.40 (Source: regulation impact)
15. [HIGH] Fred's investment rule: if a company is in a legal activity and he likes it, he buys it regardless of moral judgments — "a lot of people say I'm not going to buy Philip Morris... not me" (Source: moral investing)
16. [HIGH] Government regulation can be unpredictable even in sectors where the government profits from taxation — Victoria left poker machines alone for years because of tax revenue, then acted suddenly (Source: regulatory timing)
17. [MEDIUM] Governments have never historically regulated away technology (robots, internet) — Japan had the most robots and internet usage yet only 3% unemployment. However, non-college-educated workers will face the most disruption from AI (Source: technology and jobs)

### Company Lifespan

18. [HIGH] Company lifespan is a sector-specific concern — mining companies with a single resource may have short lifespans; Fred sold all coal and most gas holdings due to finite life expectancy under climate policy (Source: lifecycle risk)

### Finding & Interpreting Company Data

19. [HIGH] Primary data sources Fred uses: Selfwealth ($240/year subscription), Macquarie research, ComSec (which sources from Morningstar), Yahoo Finance, and company websites (Source: data sources)
20. [HIGH] The company's own website is "much underestimated" as a data source — in Australia, look for the "Investors" tab for quarterly reports (Source: primary data)
21. [HIGH] Selfwealth is excellent but slow — reports may not appear for weeks after release. Yahoo Finance has some useful information but "after that it was just nonsense" (Source: data reliability)
22. [HIGH] When data sources show discrepancies, Fred generally goes with the source he has a view on — company website as primary truth, then ComSec/Morningstar, then Selfwealth (Source: conflict resolution)
23. [MEDIUM] Macquarie research is generally reliable but they have a conflict of interest — after completing legal work for companies, they tend to push those stocks in subsequent recommendations (Source: analyst bias)

### Financial Reports & Deadlines

24. [HIGH] ASX companies must report within a deadline set by the exchange — failure to report on time can result in delisting (Source: reporting rules)
25. [HIGH] Companies release their reporting date to the ASX in advance and are held to that date — they choose dates before the deadline (Source: reporting timeline)
26. [HIGH] ASX continuous disclosure regulation: all companies must disclose relevant information as and when they become aware of it (Source: ASX rules)
27. [HIGH] Unaudited management reports are acceptable to the ASX but the post-30 June report must be properly audited — Fred trusts unaudited management reports because companies report conservatively (e.g., reporting 30% GP when expecting 32%) to avoid being sued (Source: report reliability)
28. [HIGH] Bad news in unaudited reports requires extra caution — companies may only release 70% of bad news initially, cover themselves with "under investigation" language, then release full impact a month later after the market is "softened up" (Source: bad news strategy)

### Third-Party Audits

29. [HIGH] For mining companies, drilling results must be certified by a designated person within the company and then verified by an independent third-party auditor before release to the market (Source: audit process)
30. [HIGH] "Buy the rumour, sell the news" — Fred has used positive speculation around pending audit results to sell stocks he was planning to reduce (Source: trading wisdom)
31. [HIGH] Bad news from audits comes out quicker than good news — positive results must go through full verification procedures, so good news takes longer to reach the market (Source: information asymmetry)

### Macroeconomics & Reserve Banks

32. [HIGH] Each country has three key macro levers: interest rates (monetary policy via reserve bank), fiscal policy (government spending), and its currency — these are country-specific, not global (Source: macro framework)
33. [HIGH] Fred is "in the camp" that interest rates will hold — contrasting with market consensus expecting cuts. Larry Summers is his most trusted macro voice (Source: interest rate view)
34. [HIGH] Fred recommends tracking Larry Summers and Paul Krugman as representing two poles of macro opinion — "Summers on that case was dead set right" regarding inflation persistence vs Krugman's "temporary blip" prediction (Source: macro experts)
35. [HIGH] When evaluating macro commentary, always ask: "What self-interest does the person have in spruiking this position?" — brokerage houses predicting rate cuts to boost trading should be ignored; independent consultancies are more trustworthy because "if they get it wrong there's a penalty for them" (Source: source evaluation)
