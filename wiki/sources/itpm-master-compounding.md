A webinar presented by [[anton-kreil]] with junior trading mentor Philip Klein (January 2025), covering the theory and practice of [[compounding]] as an active trader. Phil Klein demonstrates his broker statements, equity curve, and trade examples from his TradeStation account to illustrate how compounding works in a real options portfolio over 32 months. **Confidence note:** the mathematics of compounding (claim 1) is a [HIGH] arithmetic fact; descriptions of the ITPM *method* (option spread mechanics, equity-curve-as-business framing) are faithful method summaries marked [HIGH]. The performance figures — Klein's own returns, the showcased student case studies, and the vendor return *targets* — are self-reported and shown on ITPM's own promotional webinar; even though broker screenshots were displayed, they are single-source, unaudited, and selection-biased (winners are shown), so they are marked [MEDIUM].

## Key Claims

### Compounding Theory Applied to Trading

1. [HIGH] Compounding is making money on a regular basis and reinvesting it. At $100,000 deposited at 3% per month over 5 years (60 months), with no additional deposits or withdrawals, the account grows to ~$589,000. The curve goes exponential around year 6-7. (Source: [[anton-kreil]])

2. [MEDIUM] Monthly compounding targets by year (on $100K at 3%): Year 1: $3,000-$4,200/month. Year 2: $4,200-$5,900/month. Year 3: $5,900-$8,500/month. Year 4: $8,500-$12,000/month. Year 5: $12,000-$17,700/month. (Source: [[anton-kreil]] — target/illustration assuming a sustained 3%/month; the arithmetic follows but the assumed return is aspirational)

3. [HIGH] The returns are the **outcome** of implementing the process. "If you focus on the returns and not the process, the returns will never come." Focus on the process first; returns follow. (Source: [[anton-kreil]])

### Trading vs Investing Distinction

4. [HIGH] Traders are NOT investors. Investors hold positions for 3-10+ years with unrealized (mark-to-market) P&L that can suffer massive drawdowns. Traders realize profits on a regular basis within the 20-60 day time horizon. (Source: [[anton-kreil]])

5. [HIGH] Long-only strategies (pensions) are mark-to-market and suffer drawdowns every ~7 years. That "doesn't happen to traders" because traders are long-short and realize profits regularly. (Source: [[anton-kreil]])

6. [HIGH] Buy-and-hold, day trading, and long-only pension strategies are all explicitly rejected. Day trading has a ~99% failure rate for retail traders. (Source: [[anton-kreil]])

7. [HIGH] ITPM traders are "20-60 day [[long-short-equity]] options portfolio managers" — trading fundamental positioning, leveraging directional [[volatility]] through options, managing probabilistic payoffs, and running trading as a business. (Source: [[anton-kreil]])

### The Equity Curve as a Business

8. [HIGH] The [[equity-curve]] is the trader's business. The job is to make it go up every month by **realizing** profits, not watching mark-to-market P&L. Each month creates a new "node" on the equity curve — booking more winners than losers makes the node go up. (Source: [[anton-kreil]])

9. [HIGH] The ideal equity curve is a 45-degree line from bottom-left to top-right. In reality, there will be variation, but the goal is to minimize that variation. (Source: [[anton-kreil]])

10. [HIGH] Example of monthly compounding in practice: In month 6, year 1, target $3,000-$4,000 net booking. Exit $7,500 of winners and $3,500 of losers = $4,000 net profit, R-core of 2.14. (Source: [[anton-kreil]])

### Key Trading Statistics

11. [MEDIUM] Annual return target: 50-100% trading options (can achieve much more). (Source: [[anton-kreil]] — vendor performance target, aspirational)

12. [MEDIUM] Win/loss rate target: 60/40, ideally 60.5/35, with some traders reaching the 70s. (Source: [[anton-kreil]] — vendor performance target)

13. [MEDIUM] R-core (dollar winners / dollar losers) target: minimum 1.5, ideally with a "2 in front of it" for top traders. (Source: [[anton-kreil]] — vendor performance target)

14. [MEDIUM] Underlying portfolio volatility: 20-30% annualized. Options leverage this by 3-4x, so a 20% underlying vol portfolio should produce 60-80% returns; 30% underlying vol should produce 90-120%. (Source: [[anton-kreil]] — vendor performance target)

15. [MEDIUM] Average days in trade: within the 20-60 day range. Some trades are 5-9 days (quick credits), many are 30-60 days. The average tends to settle at 20-25 days. (Source: [[anton-kreil]] — stated method/target)

### Compounding Through Credits

16. [HIGH] A primary compounding mechanism is **collecting credits** by selling shorter-dated options against longer-dated long positions (calendar spreads, vertical spreads). This adds stable, near-linear value to the portfolio every month. (Source: [[anton-kreil]], Philip Klein)

17. [HIGH] The credit equity curve (short legs only, where credits were collected) forms a nearly perfect 45-degree line — stable monthly additions with minimal variation. This anchors the total equity curve even during flat or difficult trading periods. (Source: Philip Klein)

18. [HIGH] Credits provide **asymmetric returns**: during good periods, credits add on top of trading profits. During struggling periods, credits save you from losing money — your downside is protected. (Source: [[anton-kreil]])

### Trade Examples from Phil

22. [MEDIUM] **ON Holdings calendar spread (call structure)**: Long October $42.50 calls (64 contracts at $2.97), Short August $43 calls (32 contracts at $1.39 — 2:1 ratio). Collected $3,800 credit (85% of max) after 18 trading days by buying back short leg at $0.20. Then the stock moved in his favor and he booked $30,000 on the long leg. Total: ~$33,800. (Source: Philip Klein)

23. [MEDIUM] **Snowflake calendar spread (put structure)**: Long October $110 puts (30 contracts at $6.22), Short September $108 puts (15 contracts at $2.55 — 2:1 ratio). Collected $2,900 credit (75% of max) after 6 days. Trade went against him — full write-off on long leg (-$18,669), but short leg credit reduced total loss to -$15,770. (Source: Philip Klein)

24. [MEDIUM] **Coinbase vertical spread (call structure — Trump trade)**: Long December $190 calls (11 contracts at $17.70), Short December $260 calls (11 contracts at $4.50 — 1:1 ratio). Pro-crypto Trump catalyst. Short leg lost $72K but long leg made $122K. Net profit: ~$50,000 in 50 days. Risk-reward at entry: 4.5:1. (Source: Philip Klein)

25. [MEDIUM] **Boeing vertical spread (put structure)**: Long $140 puts / Short $125 puts (1:1, 39 contracts each). Trade went against him — nearly full loss on long leg. But the short leg reduced loss from $18K to $12,380. (Source: Philip Klein)

26. [MEDIUM] Over 32 months, Phil collected **$254,000 in total credits** across 102 credit-collecting trades, averaging ~$2,500 per credit and ~$8,000 per month in credits. Credits are targeted at 10-20% of the long leg value. (Source: Philip Klein, spreadsheet data — self-reported)

### Options Structuring Methodology

27. [HIGH] **Calendar ratio spreads**: Buy longer-dated option, sell shorter-dated option at 2:1 ratio. For calls: sell a higher strike. For puts: buy a higher strike, sell a lower strike. Aim to collect 75-85% of the maximum credit before closing the short leg. (Source: Philip Klein)

28. [HIGH] **Vertical spreads**: Same expiration, same ratio (1:1). Used when directional conviction is high. The short leg caps the upside but reduces cost basis and lowers maximum loss. (Source: Philip Klein)

29. [HIGH] When the short leg of a spread is losing money (because the trade is going in your favor), the long leg is making even more money due to the ratio — the overall structure is profitable. (Source: Philip Klein, [[anton-kreil]])

### Managing the Trading Business

30. [HIGH] The trader's job is to make money in all market conditions — up, down, or sideways — within the 20-60 day directional volatility time horizon. Most time is spent outside the trading account generating ideas and managing risk. Actual trading happens a couple of times per week. (Source: [[anton-kreil]])

31. [HIGH] Monthly options expiry management is critical — learn to make the equity curve go up every month by controlling outcomes through risk management. (Source: [[anton-kreil]])

32. [HIGH] Phil traded with a full-time job (project management in Austrian aviation industry public sector). ITPM trading can be done alongside regular employment. (Source: Philip Klein)
