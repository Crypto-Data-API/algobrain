Deep-dive session covering [[propel-funeral-partners|Propel Funeral Partners (PFP)]] as a PE ratio case study, ASX listing and IPO mechanics, tax system effects on wealth building, [[tesla|Tesla]] and the automotive industry, competitive [[moats]], [[capital-raises]], the six C's of commodity inflation indicators, earnings per share dilution at [[close-the-loop|CLG]], forecast metrics (forward PE vs trailing PE), shareholder ownership analysis, and Fred's views on technical analysis metrics (all-time high, moving averages, RSI, volume). Session also covers Fred's "one-pager" framework for stock analysis.

## Key Claims

### PE Ratio — PFP Case Study

1. [HIGH] [[propel-funeral-partners|Propel Funeral Partners (PFP)]] has excellent fundamentals across all metrics except PE ratio of ~40, which is "more like a tech stock PE than one associated with a funeral parlour" (Source: PFP analysis)
2. [HIGH] At a PE of 40, "the price risk is to the downside — you don't expect that to expand to 50 and push the price up. You expect it to go back to 26 and push the price downwards" (Source: PE risk assessment)
3. [HIGH] Fred bought only 500 shares of PFP at $5.17 as a small initial position — strategy is to learn the company and buy more when PE compresses, either through a company-specific issue or a general market decline (Source: position sizing)
4. [HIGH] "The market will overshoot on the downside" — Fred knows with certainty the market will overreact to negative events, and this is his buying opportunity (Source: market psychology)
5. [HIGH] High-PE stocks suffer the most during market declines — this amplifies both risk and buying opportunity (Source: PE and drawdowns)
6. [HIGH] PE ratio is recorded every day as the price changes — it is not static between reports (Source: PE mechanics)
7. [MEDIUM] For PFP specifically: PE below 20 is green (bullish), 20-27 is orange, above 27-30 is red — but Fred emphasises this is highly company and sector specific (Source: PE thresholds)
8. [HIGH] "A good company can become a bad investment if you pay too much for it" — quoting [[warren-buffett]] (Source: valuation discipline)

### Market Types & PE Context

9. [HIGH] Fred classifies markets into three types: high growth (e.g., NVIDIA, Microsoft), growth, mature (chugs along, doesn't grow much), and low growth — this classification affects acceptable PE ranges (Source: market classification)
10. [HIGH] PFP's fundamental thesis: Australia's aging population drives increasing death rates, which drives revenue for funeral companies. A sector-specific metric (death rate growth) would enable more accurate PE assessment (Source: sector-specific metrics)

### ASX Listing & IPOs

11. [HIGH] There is generally only one reason companies list on the ASX: to raise capital. The listing process is expensive, requires additional staff, mandated auditing, and puts pressure on directors (Source: IPO rationale)
12. [HIGH] Listing brings more discipline, generally attracts more professional management, and provides access to capital — this has a positive trickle-down effect on business performance (Source: listing benefits)
13. [HIGH] IPOs in the 1980s were "a way to make money" with over 80% success rate — a limited number of merchant banks controlled the process and offered undervalued shares to trusted clients (Source: historical IPOs)
14. [HIGH] Modern IPOs are risky: 3-5 banks compete for each listing, offering increasingly high valuations to win the mandate, so IPO prices are often inflated — "Getting in on IPOs today is a risky business" (Source: modern IPO risk)
15. [HIGH] The underwriting bank guarantees the IPO price to the company — if shares sell for less, the bank covers the difference. Competition forces banks to guarantee higher prices (Source: IPO mechanics)

### Tax & Capital Gains

16. [HIGH] The absence of capital gains tax in earlier decades enabled upward socioeconomic mobility — "you could take a risk and whatever capital you made, you kept" — Fred sees CGT as a barrier for non-wealthy people climbing the ladder (Source: tax philosophy)
17. [MEDIUM] Fred's solution: the first $50-60K of capital gains should be tax-free to enable wealth accumulation at the lower end (Source: policy suggestion)

### Tesla & Automotive Industry

18. [HIGH] Fred would never buy Tesla — citing [[warren-buffett]]: "I know auto companies are going to make money. I just don't know which one." The auto sector is cyclical and unpredictable (Source: auto sector view)
19. [HIGH] Tesla relied on government subsidies (California state + federal) for profitability rather than car production itself — in a high-debt environment, this was "always a risky proposition" (Source: Tesla critique)
20. [HIGH] EV market share is "stagnant and falling slightly" — manufacturers who spent billions gearing up for EV production are now cutting prices to clear inventory, crushing margins (Source: EV market)
21. [MEDIUM] Fred sees Tesla as 100% a trade, not an investment — "if you didn't look at the PE and seen it, it wouldn't have read, it was a triple red flag... 70" (Source: Tesla classification)

### Moats — Temporary & Permanent

22. [HIGH] The first question to ask about any high-performing stock: "What moat does it have against its competitors?" (Source: moat framework)
23. [HIGH] [[nvidia|NVIDIA]] has a moat on generative AI chips, but the duration and depth are uncertain — "Every man and his dog now is doing it. Now he's got a time frame where they've got to catch up" (Source: NVIDIA moat)
24. [MEDIUM] NVIDIA releasing successively better chips may extend the moat beyond Fred's initial estimate — "the moat may be bigger than I think" (Source: NVIDIA reassessment)

### Capital Raises

25. [HIGH] Capital raises via share issuance (rather than debt) lower risk but dilute existing shareholders — Fred considers this a management decision with tradeoffs: "it lowers the risk but lowered the return for existing shareholders" (Source: capital structure)
26. [HIGH] Hybrid notes (e.g., ANZ example): pay ~3% above bank bill swap rate (~7.5% total), fully franked. A way for banks to raise capital without diluting equity — "return on equity is not affected" but P&L carries interest expense (Source: hybrid instruments)

### CLG — EPS Dilution Analysis

27. [HIGH] CLG's EPS dropped from $1.97 to $1.62 despite excellent operating metrics because management issued shares for bolt-on acquisitions — "the number of shares exploded, dragged down the earnings per share" (Source: CLG EPS analysis)
28. [HIGH] In Fred's era, boards prioritised managing for shareholders first: "we would not have given those shares away... this will lower the earnings per share which was critical to us" (Source: governance standards)
29. [HIGH] CLG dividend suspended: "the board has decided not to pay a dividend this half" — combined with EPS dilution, this pushed Fred to hold rather than buy more (Source: CLG hold decision)
30. [MEDIUM] Fred's CLG target remains $1.00 by early 2026 if the Melbourne recycling factory and acquisitions produce projected returns (Source: CLG price target)

### Commodity Trading — Six C's

31. [HIGH] Peter Borish (Wall Street Week) uses six commodity indicators to predict inflationary pressure: copper, coffee, corn, cocoa, cotton, and crude oil — if all six are high, inflation is likely increasing and rate cuts may not come (Source: six C's framework)
32. [HIGH] Fred has never traded commodities directly (except twice in Singapore with inside information): "it's a zero-sum game — there's a winner and a loser" and requires constant monitoring with institutional-grade information (Source: commodity avoidance)
33. [HIGH] Commodities are "a trader's market pure and simple" — Fred would never put a commodity in his investment portfolio. However, he owns three copper companies as a way to gain commodity exposure through equities (Source: commodity philosophy)
34. [HIGH] Federal Reserve announced commitment to three 25bp interest rate decreases — this surprised Fred who had factored in no cuts, causing him to miss a buying opportunity as stocks rallied (Source: Fed pivot)

### Forecast Metrics & Shareholder Analysis

35. [HIGH] Fred glances at both forward PE and trailing PE — forward PE is based on analyst assumptions and the 12-month forward projections are "generally reasonably accurate." Fred bought Microsoft partly because the forward PE was ~26 (Source: forward PE use)
36. [HIGH] EPS adjusted (ongoing businesses) vs EPS with abnormals: Fred looks at the adjusted figure excluding one-offs — "the company's trying to get rid of those businesses" or there may be non-recurring currency losses (Source: EPS types)
37. [HIGH] Shareholder registry analysis: Fred occasionally checks top shareholders — seeing a respected investor (e.g., James Packer buying Adobe, or the founder of a company buying heavily) gives confidence but is not a primary decision factor (Source: shareholder signals)
38. [MEDIUM] Dividend payout ratio: Fred prefers ~70% payout; 90% is a concern because it leaves insufficient cash for reinvestment. Sometimes high payouts are used to distribute excess franking credits before potential regulatory changes (Source: payout ratio)

### Technical Analysis Metrics

39. [HIGH] All-time high: Fred agrees — if a stock is at its all-time high, "I immediately arrive at the conclusion I'm too late" and would put it on a watchlist for a pullback (Source: ATH rule)
40. [HIGH] Moving averages: "of minus significance to me" — Fred does not use them for investment decisions, though he acknowledges they may be useful for traders (Source: MA dismissal)
41. [MEDIUM] RSI (Relative Strength Index): Fred would "certainly use it for a buying situation" — an RSI of 15 (oversold) could add confidence, but PE ratio remains far more important: "All I need to know is the PE ratio, nothing more" (Source: RSI limited use)
42. [HIGH] Volume: Fred does not base decisions on trading volume, though he acknowledges it can indicate momentum. He cites Bloomberg CEO interviews where the standard answer is "we can't control the share price, all we can do is control what we do in the company" (Source: volume dismissal)

### Fred's One-Pager Framework

Fred's ideal stock analysis one-pager includes these metrics in order of importance:

1. Stock name, price, sector classification
2. **EPS** — must be going up; compared to itself over time
3. **Return on equity** — must exceed bank interest rate by at least 3% margin to justify risk
4. **Dividends** — directly related to EPS; check if franked and if DRP is offered
5. **Debt-to-equity ratio** — prefer under 50%; stable or declining is good
6. **PE ratio** — compared to sector competitors (not total market)
7. **Current ratio** — current assets / current liabilities; benchmark is 2.0; below 2.0 is a red flag
8. (Optional) RSI and all-time high as secondary checks
