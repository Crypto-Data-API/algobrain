Fred and Sam discuss a wide range of topics including Ethereum node hedging strategy, Fred's views on crypto as an asset class, the importance of [[gross-profit]] as a fundamental metric, [[close-the-loop|Close the Loop (CLG)]] EPS analysis, reasons to pay higher PE than sector average, and detailed analysis of Australian bank stocks and automotive stocks. Fred repeatedly emphasises patience, discipline, and fundamental analysis over speculation.

## Key Claims

### Ethereum Node & Hedging

1. [HIGH] When you earn bonus coins/shares from staking, only the original staked amount continues earning — bonus tokens just sit there not generating income (Source: ETH node discussion)
2. [HIGH] Hedging involves selling a portion of gains to protect against downside: "If you've made money, you can hedge what you've made" — Fred cites MYX medical supply stock as an example of selling portions on the way up (Source: hedging discussion)
3. [MEDIUM] Fred's plan for ETH node: exit completely at $7,500 AUD per ETH; personally would hold to $10,000 AUD as he believes it will reach that level in the bull run (Source: ETH price targets)

### Crypto as an Asset Class

4. [HIGH] "Nothing will ever stop prices from reaching excessive levels up or down simply because people cannot be prevented from making excessively optimistic or pessimistic assumptions that result in risky behaviour and a misallocation of capital" (Source: market philosophy)
5. [HIGH] Time in market principle: as long as you believe in the fundamentals and they haven't changed, a dip/pullback is the time to buy (Source: buying philosophy)
6. [HIGH] Fred admits his mistake was not buying Bitcoin during the first major dip because he didn't have it on his watch list as a quality asset (Source: crypto regrets)
7. [HIGH] "The first time you hear somebody say 'this time is different,' I suggest you press the sell button" — a sell signal for momentum-driven markets (Source: sell discipline)
8. [MEDIUM] Fred believes [[ethereum]] has utility value (global transfers, smart contracts) but is uncertain about long-term AI implications for which blockchain wins (Source: crypto views)
9. [HIGH] Bitcoin and crypto markets are cyclical: "It goes up and comes down and the next peak will be higher than the previous one" — same pattern as stock market crashes (Source: cycle theory)
10. [MEDIUM] Fred concerned about ETH's centralisation risks — Vitalik can be "held hostage" while Bitcoin's decentralisation is its core strength (Source: crypto comparison)

### Selling Strategy & Trading vs Investing

11. [HIGH] Fred sells on pullbacks/dips rather than waiting for green days, because if a dip turns into a correction, you've missed your exit (Source: sell strategy)
12. [HIGH] For a 10-unit portfolio in a momentum trade, Fred would sell 1.5 units on first pullback (~15%), then 1.5 at $100K, then progressively smaller amounts at higher prices — selling amounts decrease as price rises (Source: position sizing exit)
13. [HIGH] Sharp distinction between trading account (ComSec) and core portfolio (Macquarie): trading account stocks are actively traded; core portfolio stocks are held through volatility (Source: portfolio structure)
14. [HIGH] "When you find a truly wonderful business, stick with it. Patience pays and one wonderful business can offset many mediocre decisions" — quoting [[warren-buffett]] (Source: investment philosophy)
15. [HIGH] Fred maintains ~$300K (~8% of total portfolio) in his trading account; he's prepared to risk it and accept some losses (Source: portfolio allocation)

### Gross Profit — "The Oxygen of the Business"

16. [HIGH] Tony O'Reilly (CEO of Heinz) said "the gross profit is the oxygen of the business" — Fred adopted this as a major focus for company analysis (Source: gross profit philosophy)
17. [HIGH] When analysing [[microsoft]], Fred was initially worried about the PE (~35) but was pushed to buy when he found that gross profit percentage was actually increasing as revenue grew — most companies sacrifice gross profit for growth, but Microsoft did not (Source: Microsoft analysis)
18. [HIGH] Gross profit percentage is relevant for every sector, though absolute levels differ — supermarkets have low GP% with high volume; other companies have lower sales but higher GP% (Source: sector comparison)
19. [HIGH] [[wesfarmers|Wesfarmers/Bunnings]] achieves ~32% gross profit while claiming lowest prices — explained by superior buying power due to scale and historically never being out of stock (Source: Bunnings analysis)
20. [HIGH] Fred's own business (tech trades) achieved 22% GP vs 14% sector average — the ATO used the sector average as a benchmark to flag businesses with suspiciously low GP (Source: personal experience)

### Demergers & Company Lifespan

21. [HIGH] Chris Chadley principle: when a company demerges a division, it's "always worth a look" — Wesfarmers sold off Coles, and Coles performed much better independently because management could focus and allocate capital without competing against Bunnings and other divisions (Source: demerger thesis)
22. [HIGH] In conglomerates, divisions compete for capital allocation from the board — an independent company can access all capital needed for growth (Source: capital allocation)
23. [HIGH] Company lifespan is a concern primarily in specific sectors — mining companies may have finite resource life; coal/gas companies face existential risk from climate policy (Source: sector risk)
24. [HIGH] "You can't beat City Hall" — if government decides against an industry (coal, gas), exit because regulatory risk is insurmountable (Source: government risk)

### Close the Loop (CLG) — EPS Deep Dive

25. [HIGH] Fred owns 50,000 CLG shares at average cost $0.36; despite excellent revenue, profit, and cash flow growth, the share price dropped after results because the company issued new shares for bolt-on acquisitions, diluting EPS from $1.97 to $1.62 (Source: CLG analysis)
26. [HIGH] CLG management paid for acquisitions half in cash and half in newly issued shares — this diluted existing shareholders and dragged down EPS even though operating metrics improved (Source: share dilution)
27. [HIGH] Fred's view: it would have been better to borrow the cash rather than issue shares, as borrowing adds risk but preserves shareholder equity; issuing shares lowers risk but dilutes returns (Source: capital structure opinion)
28. [HIGH] CLG placed on temporary hold — Fred will wait for June half-year results to see if Melbourne factory (on time and on budget) produces income that lifts EPS back above $2.00 (Source: investment decision)
29. [MEDIUM] Fred's target: CLG stock could reach $1.00 by early 2026 if the new Melbourne recycling factory and acquisitions come to fruition (Source: price target)

### PE Ratio & Sector Comparison

30. [HIGH] The PE ratio is company-specific and must be compared to the sector average — "Why should you be paying twice the sector average for that company? There has to be a reason. If you like the reason, buy it. If you don't, stay away" (Source: PE guidance)
31. [HIGH] Companies often trade at different PEs domestically vs internationally — Australian companies typically trade at lower PE than US counterparts because the US market assigns more growth potential (Source: domestic vs international PE)
32. [HIGH] [[commonwealth-bank|Commonwealth Bank]] has consistently maintained a higher PE than other Australian banks because of better management, stronger technology focus, and greater market confidence (Source: bank PE analysis)
