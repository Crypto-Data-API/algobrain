Three segments from a single session. Fred recounts the [[poseidon-bubble|Poseidon nickel bubble]] of the late 1960s and the devastating personal lesson of not taking profits on GME (Gold Mineral Exploration). Matthew's [[solana|Solana]] story provides a modern parallel. The Brambles segment delivers Fred's framework for evaluating company management teams, including the cautionary tale of an outside-industry CEO hire and the [[telstra|Telstra]] management decline.

## Key Claims

### Poseidon Bubble — Fred's GME Story (1966-67)

1. [HIGH] The [[poseidon-bubble|Poseidon boom]] was a massive mining bull run in Australia in the late 1960s — Poseidon (a nickel company) went from ~$0.20-0.40 to approximately $300 (Source: Poseidon history)
2. [HIGH] Fred did not buy Poseidon itself — he bought other stocks in the buzzing market and made ~$10,000 profit from an initial investment of ~$2,000 (aged approximately 19-20 at the time) (Source: Fred's market profits)
3. [HIGH] There was no capital gains tax effectively enforced at that time — investors signed a form declaring they were "investors not traders" and the ATO never followed it up (Source: historical tax context)
4. [HIGH] Fred had a plan to use the $10,000 to buy two investment houses: one in Toowong (~$5,000) and one at Surfers Paradise (~$4,000) (Source: original exit plan)
5. [HIGH] Fred received a tip from a high school friend (Ian Prentice) about GME (Gold Mineral Exploration), which held the exploration licence adjacent to Poseidon in Western Australia — a geologist boyfriend of a GME employee had allegedly seen drill results showing nickel and copper (Source: GME tip origin)
6. [HIGH] Fred bought 1,000 GME shares at $9.80 each ($9,800 total — his entire profit), despite his broker Rex Breedenberg subtly trying to talk him out of it. Rex could not be told the insider information source (Source: GME purchase)
7. [HIGH] The shares rose from $9.80 to $15.50 within approximately two weeks — hitting Fred's original profit target (Source: GME price action)
8. [HIGH] Fred's friend Ian Prentice told him "only wimps sell at $15, it's going to $25" — Fred was persuaded not to sell (Source: social pressure to hold)
9. [HIGH] Fred was then transferred to Sydney for work (ICI), became busy with his new role and university studies, and lost track of the investment for 3-4 months (Source: lost attention)
10. [HIGH] Fred eventually saw a newspaper headline: "GME crashes, directors charged with fraud" — the drill results had been forged, the directors sold their own shares at ~$15, and the stock went to zero (Source: GME fraud and collapse)
11. [HIGH] Fred lost his entire $9,800 investment plus the opportunity cost of the two houses he planned to buy (Source: total loss)

### Lessons from the GME Loss

12. [HIGH] **Lesson 1 — Take your profit.** This is the "first and foremost" lesson Fred learned, and he has stuck with it ever since (Source: direct quote)
13. [HIGH] **Lesson 2 — Keep your eye on trading positions daily.** When buying a share for a trade (not a long-term dividend hold), monitor it every day (Source: monitoring discipline)
14. [HIGH] **Lesson 3 — Risk only half your capital.** Fred should have bought a maximum of 500 shares (not 1,000) and at $15 should have sold half, recovering approximately his original capital (Source: position sizing retrospective)
15. [HIGH] "Greed overcame common sense and experience" — Fred's own words on why he invested the full $10,000 instead of half (Source: self-assessment)

### Matthew's Solana Story (Modern Parallel)

16. [HIGH] Matthew (Fred's son) bought [[solana|Solana]] at approximately $2-3 and watched it rise to ~$311 — Fred bought 3 SOL at ~$280 "to learn something" (Source: Solana purchase)
17. [HIGH] Fred told Matthew at $300 to sell at least half — "this will not last" — Matthew reportedly had ~$160,000 in unrealised gains but never sold (Source: Fred's advice to Matthew)
18. [HIGH] Solana subsequently dropped to ~$10, and Matthew held the entire way down — the same pattern as Fred's GME loss decades earlier (Source: Solana decline)
19. [HIGH] Matthew still held the Solana position at the time of recording (price had recovered to ~$100+), never having taken any profit (Source: ongoing hold)
20. [MEDIUM] Months later, Matthew bought another crypto coin and Fred again advised to sell at least a third — Matthew sold 50%, showing some learning (Source: partial lesson learned)
21. [HIGH] Fred's view: "The only way to learn this is to experience it" for complete novices — people from wealthy families with investing as part of family conversation have a built-in advantage (Source: learning through experience)

### Sam's Crypto Losses

22. [MEDIUM] Sam lost ~1.5 Bitcoin locked in [[zipmex|Zipmex]] exchange (worth ~$100,000 AUD at time of recording) when it went under — illustrating counterparty risk with crypto exchanges (Source: Zipmex loss)

### Brambles — Company Management Evaluation

23. [HIGH] [[brambles|Brambles]] was recommended by Rex Breedenberg as a long-term hold and dividend payer — Fred bought at approximately $2 and the shares rose above $13 over time (Source: Brambles history)
24. [HIGH] Fred also bought Brambles as a currency hedge — approximately 65% of its earnings come from US operations (Source: Brambles currency exposure)
25. [HIGH] When a Chinese manager was appointed, the company started going downhill — the board eventually sacked him and appointed Brian Fletcher (former CEO of Coles), who had great charisma but no experience in the pallet/logistics industry (Source: Brambles management change)
26. [HIGH] Fred's management rule: "When you're looking at management, be wary. If they're in the same industry and they've performed well, okay to buy. If they've come in from outside the industry, wait." (Source: direct quote — management principle)
27. [HIGH] Fred waits for two reporting periods (minimum 9 months) to evaluate a new CEO — the first report may not truly reflect their work, but by the second (typically 31 December for a March starter), "that's his result" and you can make decisions (Source: CEO evaluation timeframe)
28. [HIGH] If a new CEO comes from outside the industry and the stock price pops 5% on the announcement, that initial bump is "generally enough" — Fred does not chase it further until the CEO proves themselves (Source: announcement pop avoidance)
29. [HIGH] If a company with a failed outside-industry CEO then appoints someone with relevant industry experience while the stock is depressed, it is "certainly worth a look" but not a "screaming buy" because you must ask why the company failed in the first place and whether the culture can be turned around (Source: turnaround assessment)

### Telstra Management Decline

30. [HIGH] [[telstra|Telstra]] had a capable CEO (described as Mexican, with engineering background) whom Fred graded an "A" using his old Bernstorp evaluation methods — but the board replaced him and started appointing "commercial people" instead of engineers. Fred believes Telstra needs someone with an engineering background but has instead appointed finance-background executives "to be fashionable." This has been going on for a decade with deteriorating results (Source: Telstra management critique)

### Santos — Positive Management Example

31. [HIGH] [[santos|Santos]] is cited as a positive example of an industry-experienced outsider: an oil and gas executive came in and within 12 months the "vast improvement" was visible — because he came from the same industry (Source: Santos management success)

### General Management Principles

32. [HIGH] Gender and age are irrelevant to Fred's management assessment — the only question is "did this person get the job because they are the best person, or for some other reason?" and "experience counts" (Source: management evaluation criteria)
