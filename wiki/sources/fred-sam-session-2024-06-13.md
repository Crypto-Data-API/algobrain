Session with detailed analysis of [[cloudflare]]'s Q1 2024 earnings report, discussion comparing SEC (US) and ASX data availability for fundamental analysis, the importance of EPS and revenue forecasting, deep dive into [[macquarie-technology]] (MAQ), Fred's sector allocation framework with specific percentage allocations, and commodity market views on iron ore, copper, gas, and power.

## Key Claims

### Cloudflare Q1 2024 Earnings Analysis

1. [HIGH] Cloudflare showed ~2% increase in gross profit alongside a 30% increase in sales. Fred was surprised because normally a 30% sales increase comes with a slight decrease in gross profit (companies sacrifice GP margin to drive volume). Increasing both sales and GP simultaneously is unusual and a strong positive signal.
2. [HIGH] Fred's principle: "An increase in sales normally comes with a decrease in the gross profit. That doesn't go hand in glove." When both rise together, it suggests pricing power and/or economies of scale -- both bullish indicators.
3. [HIGH] For large companies like Cloudflare (~$1B+ revenue), even a 2% GP improvement translates to massive absolute dollar gains. The significance of a GP improvement depends on the revenue base it applies to.
4. [HIGH] Fred introduces the concept of [[elasticity-of-demand]]: the relationship between price changes and demand changes. In a meeting at Burns Philp, the general manager demonstrated that for their product, a small price increase lost minimal volume but significantly increased gross profit -- inelastic demand is a sign of pricing power.
5. [HIGH] Fred's test for pricing power: "If you can raise the price by 10% and lose less than 10% of volume, you have pricing power. The gross profit in dollars will increase even if volume drops slightly." This is the essence of inelastic demand.

### SEC vs ASX Data Availability

6. [HIGH] US stocks (SEC filings) provide vastly more standardized and accessible fundamental data than ASX-listed companies. The SEC's EDGAR system offers machine-readable filings that enable automated analysis. ASX data is more fragmented and harder to systematically collect.
7. [HIGH] Fred notes this as a competitive advantage for US-listed stocks in the [[alfred]] system: automated fundamental analysis will be easier and more accurate for US stocks because the data quality is superior.
8. [MEDIUM] The difference in regulatory disclosure requirements between SEC and ASX means that some metrics readily available for US companies (quarterly EPS, segment revenue breakdowns) may be unavailable or inconsistently reported for Australian companies.

### Fundamentals Forecasts / EPS / Revenue

9. [HIGH] Fred's hierarchy of fundamental metrics: (1) EPS growth -- the single most important metric for a growth investor; (2) Revenue growth -- the top-line driver; (3) Gross profit margin -- the quality of revenue; (4) ROE and D/E -- the financial structure. EPS growth is the "bottom line" that drives stock prices.
10. [HIGH] Consensus forecasts for EPS and revenue are valuable as a benchmark: if a company beats consensus, the stock typically rises; if it misses, it falls. Fred recommends tracking consensus forecasts from SelfWealth and CommSec.
11. [HIGH] Fred warns against relying solely on consensus forecasts: "These forecasts are produced by analysts who are often wrong. Use them as a reference point, not a decision tool." The company's actual trajectory matters more than analyst predictions.

### Macquarie Technology Group (MAQ)

12. [HIGH] Fred sees [[macquarie-technology]] as a compelling data centre play with strong tailwinds from cloud computing, AI infrastructure, and government digital transformation. The company has significant government contracts providing recurring revenue.
13. [HIGH] For data centre companies, Fred focuses on: (a) contract duration and quality of tenants, (b) expansion pipeline (new data centre capacity coming online), (c) utilization rates of existing capacity, and (d) debt used to finance construction.
14. [MEDIUM] Fred values MAQ's strategy of targeting government and enterprise tenants with long-term contracts. Government contracts, while lower-margin, provide predictable cash flows that support debt financing of new facilities.
15. [HIGH] Fred's concern with MAQ: the company must execute its expansion plans on time and on budget. Construction risk for data centres is significant -- delays mean lost revenue and increased interest costs on the debt used to build them.

### Sector Allocation Framework

16. [HIGH] Fred's sector allocation approach: define which sectors to invest in, which to avoid, and allocate a target percentage of total portfolio to each. The allocation should reflect both conviction (tailwinds) and risk management (diversification).
17. [HIGH] Fred's sectors to invest in: Information Technology (mega trends: cloud, AI, robotics), Healthcare (specifically biotech and pharma -- avoid hospitals and high-debt health companies), Utilities (small allocation for diversification, captured through ETFs), Real Estate (small allocation, favor REITs with quality tenants and government contracts).
18. [HIGH] Fred's sectors to avoid: Industrials (except waste management), Financials (specifically big banks -- "growth days are over," though holding existing positions for dividends is acceptable), Consumer Staples and Consumer Discretionary (too much competition, hard to pick long-term winners -- "except the champion").
19. [HIGH] Fred's concept of "paradigm advantage": invest in sectors with a structural shift (paradigm change) in their favor. Technology has the paradigm advantage now; traditional industrials do not.
20. [MEDIUM] For utility exposure, Fred prefers ETFs over individual stocks because utilities are relatively commoditized and an ETF captures the sector return without single-stock risk.

### Commodities: Iron Ore, Copper, Gas, Power

21. [HIGH] Fred's view on [[iron-ore]]: heavily dependent on Chinese demand, which makes it structurally risky as China's economy slows. He would not increase exposure to iron ore and sees it as a mature commodity with limited upside.
22. [HIGH] [[copper]] is Fred's preferred base metal because of electrification tailwinds -- EVs, renewable energy, grid infrastructure all require massive amounts of copper. Supply is constrained and new mine development takes years.
23. [MEDIUM] Fred views natural gas as a transition fuel with a 10-20 year demand runway. Countries moving away from coal will use gas as a bridge to renewables. LNG demand from Asia is a specific tailwind for Australian producers.
24. [MEDIUM] Power sector companies (generators, transmission) benefit from electrification trends but are often regulated with capped returns. Fred prefers unregulated power producers that can capture price spikes during peak demand.

### General Investment Principles

25. [HIGH] Fred's principle on revenue quality: "Not all revenue is equal." Revenue from long-term government contracts is worth more than revenue from one-off project work. Recurring revenue commands a premium valuation.
26. [HIGH] When a company's gross profit margin increases despite higher sales volume, investigate whether this comes from (a) pricing power, (b) economies of scale, (c) mix shift toward higher-margin products, or (d) cost reductions. Each driver has different implications for sustainability.
27. [HIGH] Fred's view on sector rotation: do not chase the hot sector after it has already run. Identify the tailwind before the market fully recognizes it, build a position, and hold through the volatility. By the time CNBC is talking about it, you are already too late.
28. [MEDIUM] Fred notes the importance of understanding sub-industry dynamics within sectors: within Technology, cloud infrastructure companies have different economics than software companies, which differ from semiconductor companies. Treat each sub-industry as a separate investment thesis.
