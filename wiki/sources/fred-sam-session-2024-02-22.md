Fred and Sam discuss Fred's investing edge (intuition combined with fundamentals), the importance of revenue growth as a sell signal, PFP data discrepancies, how to form an "image" of a company from its numbers, interest rate uncertainty, and the dilemma between investing and trading for cash flow. Fred shares his decision framework for buying and selling stocks, including detailed examples from [[integrated-research|IRI]] and [[cloudflare|Cloudflare]].

## Key Claims

### Intuition as Fred's Edge

1. [HIGH] Fred's edge is the combination of intuition and financial data: "If I see the figures and my intuition tells me something, and those two coincide, then that is the edge I'm looking for." [[cloudflare|Cloudflare]] was one example where both aligned.

2. [HIGH] When intuition says buy but the numbers say no, Fred limits himself to small positions. When both are green, he goes in decisively: "If it's a game of poker, you've got to be fearless. And the boys around the table have got to know it."

3. [HIGH] Fred aims for a 7 out of 10 win rate on conviction trades. Henry Ford said any executive right 50% of the time should be immediately promoted.

### The Number One Edge for Individual Investors

4. [HIGH] "The edge to have is to buy in those down times." Fred considers buying during market downturns the single greatest edge for individual investors. Institutions panic more than individuals during downturns — Warren Buffett being the exception — and sell into the decline.

5. [HIGH] Individual investors' advantage is that they deal with their own money, have no quarterly performance reporting, and can take a longer-term view. "We can take a longer term view and prosper from others' errors."

6. [HIGH] Fred is prepared to put 75% of his cash into the market during a major downturn.

### Revenue Growth as the Critical Signal

7. [HIGH] Fred's experience with [[integrated-research|IRI]]: he bought at approximately $3, it rose to $6 on exceptional revenue growth (15-20% annually, with major US clients like AT&T and Bank of America). When revenue growth slowed and then turned negative, the stock collapsed to 38 cents.

8. [HIGH] "Know why you bought it" — Fred bought IRI for revenue growth and its customer base. When that growth reversed, he should have sold immediately. His mistake was not paying attention; he should have had it on his watch list.

9. [HIGH] If the reason you bought a stock changes, sell. Fred envisions telling Alfred: "I'm buying this because of X and Y. If it changes, let me know."

10. [HIGH] When revenue growth stalls, PE compression follows quickly. "If you've got revenue growth, your PE is high. As soon as the bad news comes, it drops."

### Structural vs. Cyclical Revenue Decline

11. [HIGH] Analysts distinguish between structural and cyclical revenue downturns. If they determine the downturn is structural (not temporary), the stock will get hammered because revenue growth drives the PE multiple.

### Selling Discipline

12. [HIGH] Fred is liquidating underperforming stocks ("duds") to raise cash for the next downturn. He made a list of stocks to sell while the market is buoyant, noting: "Hopeless ones go down the most" in downturns.

13. [HIGH] Fred's approach to losing positions: there are only two choices — reduce drastically or buy more to lower the average price. "There's no in between."

14. [MEDIUM] Fred sold Alibaba, Tencent, and PayPal due to Chinese government interference — "How can you buy in a market where one man can say something and all others fit in?" Xi Jinping's ability to unilaterally change rules makes Chinese stocks uninvestable for Fred.

### Interest Rates and Monetary Policy

15. [HIGH] "Nobody knows" the future of interest rates — Fred repeats this emphatically. All TV analysts pushing predictions are "pushing their own book." The market only wants rates to go down; stable is merely tolerable.

16. [HIGH] Fred's investment strategy against interest rate uncertainty: "Buy shares that are least affected by macroeconomic conditions. If it goes down, we have a win. If it goes up, we lose less than others."

17. [HIGH] Companies like [[microsoft|Microsoft]] and [[nvidia|NVIDIA]] are effectively immune to interest rate changes — nobody stops using Office 365 because rates went up, and NVIDIA's demand outstrips supply regardless of macro conditions.

### Cash Flow Business Considerations

18. [MEDIUM] Fred's experience with TechTrade (bought for ~$50,000, eventually worth nearly $1M): successful because he worked extremely hard in the business. His subsequent granite importing venture (Monte Grinetti) failed because nobody — including Fred — put in the effort.

19. [HIGH] "It's very, very difficult to get a cash flow business if you're not prepared to put in the work." This is Fred's core principle on business ownership.

### Earnings Per Share Trend

20. [HIGH] Early in Fred's career, he only looked at earnings per share trends: "If they were going up like that, I bought it." The logic: if EPS is rising, dividends can rise, and the share price must follow. Revenue going up and EPS going up = happy days.

21. [HIGH] Fred only started looking at balance sheets and ROE in the late 1980s or early 1990s. Previously, the P&L statement (specifically EPS trend) was his sole focus.

### PFP Data Discrepancies (Continued)

22. [HIGH] Fred suspects PFP's higher reported gross profit (70% vs. 30%) may be because acquisition costs (legal fees, integration, refurbishment) are being capitalized rather than expensed through cost of goods sold — temporarily inflating gross profit.

23. [HIGH] The first thing Fred checks with gross profit is whether the company has been able to increase sales while maintaining gross profit, because sometimes companies sacrifice gross profit to drive sales growth.

### Singapore and International Investing

24. [MEDIUM] Fred is interested in Singapore markets but has not found a way to trade there directly. He would consider buying Swiss francs as a long-term currency hedge.

25. [HIGH] Fred's approach to ASX stocks with international exposure (e.g., [[csl|CSL]], Brambles): buying an Australian-listed stock that sells primarily to the US gives you geographic and currency diversification within a single ASX position.

26. [HIGH] Fred started buying [[csl|CSL]] at $60, specifically for its international exposure — biggest market is the US, with Europe and China as secondary markets.
