Fred and Sam review the first [[alfred|Alfred]] one-page snapshot and full report for [[northern-star-resources|NST]], discuss management effectiveness metrics for gold miners, BHP's copper thesis, share buybacks, the importance of EPS growth, sovereign risk in African mining stocks, and the broader macro environment including US debt, stagflation risk, and interest rate policy. Fred provides detailed feedback on Alfred's report format and identifies core metrics for quick screening.

## Key Claims

### Alfred Report Feedback

1. [HIGH] Fred's key feedback on the Alfred one-page snapshot: drop the five-year cumulative EPS and replace it with annual EPS figures to see the trend. "The trend of the EPS is one of the most critical issues."

2. [HIGH] Fred recommends using a trimmed mean rather than a simple average for sector data, agreeing with Sam's approach of calculating the mean then cutting 10% off each extreme. This handles the problem of small unprofitable gold miners with massively negative PEs skewing the average.

3. [HIGH] For quick screening, Fred identifies core metrics across categories: (a) P&L: revenue, gross profit, EBIT; (b) Management effectiveness: ROE; (c) Balance sheet: current ratio, cash flow coverage. If a quick glance shows problems in these areas, Fred does not dig deeper.

4. [HIGH] Fred wants a "current" column alongside the sector average for instant comparison, and quarterly data between annual figures to get the latest trends — this is where Alfred can gain an edge over platforms with lagging data.

### Northern Star Resources (NST) Analysis

5. [HIGH] NST has 84% price correlation to gold — this means to evaluate the stock, you must also evaluate the gold market (supply/demand, who is buying, stockpiles).

6. [HIGH] For gold mining stocks, the critical management question is: when the gold price goes down, do managers cut expenses, reduce capex, and do what is needed to maintain EPS? That is the management issue, not the gold price itself (which is out of their control).

7. [HIGH] NST is doing share buybacks to increase EPS. When a company buys back shares, the board is signaling: "The market is not valuing our business properly. The share price should be higher." The board invests in its own company at what it believes is a cheap price.

8. [MEDIUM] NST had approximately 35% gold sector market share on the ASX — a significant position with few comparable competitors.

9. [HIGH] "Nameplate production" is what the plant is designed to produce when properly run and managed (e.g., 200,000 ounces per year). If actual production is well below nameplate, management needs to improve productivity. This metric is measured in physical output, not dollar terms.

10. [HIGH] New gold mines prioritize extracting the highest-value gold first to pay down debt quickly. During this debt-repayment phase, shareholders receive nothing — "that's just how it works."

### Share Buybacks

11. [HIGH] Share buybacks are both positive and negative. Positive: the company believes the market undervalues it and invests accordingly. Negative: some companies buy back shares because they have run out of ideas for growth.

12. [HIGH] Banks are currently over-capitalized, which drags down ROE. They are buying back shares to reduce excess capital and improve return on equity.

13. [HIGH] It would be unusual and frowned upon for a company to buy back shares and then reissue them later. The standard practice is to cancel bought-back shares.

14. [MEDIUM] Apple's extensive buybacks are likely funded from their enormous cash pile, not borrowing. Fred is "definitely not a buyer" of Apple due to over-investment in China and Xi Jinping's ban on Apple phones for government employees.

### EPS Growth

15. [HIGH] Fred finds 15% compound annual EPS growth "impressive" — he considers it a critical metric. Even big tech companies growing EPS at 15% are beloved by investors. If Fred sees EPS growing at 15%, it is a strong buy signal regardless of other factors.

16. [HIGH] EPS trend was Fred's sole screening metric early in his career: "If they were going up like that, I bought it. If the earnings per share is going up, the dividend can go up. If the EPS is going up, the share price has to follow."

### BHP and Copper

17. [HIGH] BHP's attempted takeover of Anglo American was for one reason only: copper assets. BHP is "absolutely 100% convinced that copper is the metal of the future." If they win, they will sell all non-copper assets.

18. [MEDIUM] Fred dismisses alternatives to copper (graphite, lab-produced metals) as too far in the future to be relevant to current investment decisions.

### Sovereign Risk

19. [HIGH] Mining stocks in Africa carry sovereign risk — the risk that government actions (policy changes, nationalization, instability) can destroy value. This must be discounted when valuing African-based miners versus Australian-based ones.

20. [MEDIUM] Sam identified PRU (Perseus Mining) as having better fundamental metrics than NST and EVN, but its African mine locations introduce sovereign risk despite 52-54% institutional ownership (Fidelity, iShares).

### Small Cap Mining — Cash on Hand and Burn Rate

21. [HIGH] For small-cap miners, Fred's first check is always the balance sheet: cash on hand, burn rate, and whether a capital raise will be needed. If the company can "sneak through" without raising more equity, ROE will rise substantially as existing equity becomes more productive.

22. [HIGH] Fred bought MCI (small copper miner) at 48.5 cents, specifically because it had approximately $100M cash on hand and was burning approximately $10M per year — enough to fund its copper production ramp-up without a dilutive capital raise.

### Gold Production Costs

23. [HIGH] Two critical metrics for gold miners: cash cost per ounce and all-in sustaining cost (AISC) per ounce. Both are important; AISC is slightly more important but includes items that management can quickly adjust if the gold price drops. Cash cost is more "forever linked" — harder to change.

24. [HIGH] Gold equivalent ounces: when a gold mine also produces other metals, the output is converted to gold-equivalent ounces at the current gold price for comparison purposes.

### Interest Rates, Debt, and Stagflation

25. [HIGH] Fred's investment philosophy on interest rates: "We cannot and will not make our decisions based on what's happening with interest rates. We want good quality companies that can raise their prices in most circumstances and are well managed."

26. [HIGH] Stagflation (inflation + no growth) is a real possibility that cannot be ruled out. The best way out is to increase productivity. The last episode was in the 1970s. Australian interest rates reached 18%.

27. [HIGH] Revenue decline is the single most important sell signal. If the CEO says "we're in a bit of a slump" — the market will not believe recovery promises. If analysts determine the revenue decline is structural (not cyclical), the stock gets hammered.

28. [MEDIUM] Fred on the 2024 US election and rates: Biden keeps flooding the economy with borrowed money to keep unemployment low, while the Fed wants unemployment to rise to cool inflation. "He keeps flooding the US economy with borrowed money to keep it afloat. That's a predicament." Trump would be slightly better for portfolios because of discretionary spending discipline, but neither candidate will address entitlement programs.
