[[anton-kreil]] interviews [[jason-mcdonald]], a senior trading mentor at itpm with an 18-year proprietary trading career across multiple investment banks in London. The interview covers his career progression, trading through major market cycles including the Global Financial Crisis, and his transition to mentoring retail traders. This is part of ITPM's "Trading Legends" promotional series for its paid courses; biographical and philosophy claims are first-person and single-source, so confidence is MEDIUM.

> **Note on confidence:** Promotional interview for a paid trading-education business. Career details and opinions are the interviewee's own recollection, not independently verified — treat as [MEDIUM] single-source.

## Career Background

- [MEDIUM] Graduated university in 1992 at age 21; entered investment banking immediately via the graduate trainee program at Barclays de Zoete Wedd (BZW, now Barclays Capital).
- [MEDIUM] Went straight from the graduate rotation onto the [[event-driven-trading|event-driven]] / risk arbitrage proprietary trading desk at BZW as the number two trader. This was pure [[proprietary-trading]], trading the bank's own capital.
- [MEDIUM] Risk arbitrage work included: announced takeover situations (playing the spread between acquirer and target), pre-announced takeover identification ("ratr"), dualistic company spreads (e.g., Shell/Royal Dutch, Unilever PLC vs NV — playing the discount between London and Amsterdam listings after FX adjustment), and cash extractions using warrants (long warrant, short underlying stock).
- [MEDIUM] Moved to Credit Suisse First Boston (CSFB) when CSFB acquired BZW's equities business (~1997). Became part of an internal hedge fund called Modal Capital at CSFB. Was given his own book of approximately $250 million, continuing in [[event-driven-trading]].
- [MEDIUM] Left CSFB around the dot-com implosion (~2000) to set up a volatility and event-driven hedge fund at Mako, a firm specializing in options market making. Ran that fund for three and a half years.
- [MEDIUM] Returned to prop trading at Commerzbank with a team of six, running a fundamentally driven long-short-equity book of 2-3 billion euros, covering UK, Europe, North America, developed Asia (Singapore, Hong Kong, Australia), and structured trades in South Africa.
- [MEDIUM] Moved to Lehman Brothers briefly (~2006), then to Toronto Dominion (TD) where he set up an equity [[proprietary-trading]] book with three people working under him, including his brother as analyst. Finished his City career at TD in 2010.
- [MEDIUM] At TD, he was part of the credit products group but running an equity long-short-equity portfolio — a unique cross-divisional positioning that gave early intelligence on credit market stress ahead of the equity market.

## Trading Through Market Cycles

- [MEDIUM] Traded through the 1994 Fed rate shock (broke even that year despite event-driven focus), the tech bubble and bust (2000-2003), and the Global Financial Crisis (2007-2009).
- [MEDIUM] Considers the Global Financial Crisis the most interesting and enlightening cycle of his career. Describes witnessing people queuing outside Northern Rock near the Bloomberg offices in the City — something unprecedented in his lifetime, comparable to the 1930s.
- [MEDIUM] Got short the banks early during the GFC, particularly Australian banks on a valuation basis, informed by what the credit desk was seeing. Went too early — was losing money on bank shorts in late 2007 — but ultimately profited while others were "getting carried out."
- [MEDIUM] Believes the post-GFC unwinding is still ongoing and that investors should be long volatility in the current environment.

## Trading Philosophy and Process

- [MEDIUM] Event-driven trading instills extreme discipline and process orientation because you must be aware of minutiae that could cause a deal to break — this provides an excellent foundation for fundamental investing.
- [MEDIUM] Prop trading is pure meritocracy: "It's you against the market... only one way to keep score" — your P&L is the sole measure of performance with nowhere to hide behind client flow.

## Mentoring Retail Traders

- [MEDIUM] Identifies two main struggles for retail traders: (1) time management — fitting 8-12 hours of weekly commitment around existing careers, and (2) adopting a systematic process and methodology. Most retail traders who come to the program have tried trading before and failed largely because they lacked a proper process.
- [MEDIUM] Notes that the internet has been "the great leveler" — retail traders can now access nearly as much information as institutional traders, while being free from compliance restrictions like restricted stock lists. Barriers to entry have come down significantly.
- [MEDIUM] Observes that retail traders can generate "really original" forward-looking ideas that rival or exceed what some professional traders produce, because they come from diverse backgrounds and think differently once given a proper process.
- [MEDIUM] Maintains contact with mentees long after the formal program ends, noting their progression from nervous beginners to confident traders who understand measured risk-taking within 2-3 months.
