The definitive account of the rise and collapse of Long-Term Capital Management, published in 2000. Roger Lowenstein — former Wall Street Journal reporter — reconstructs how a hedge fund founded by Nobel laureates Myron Scholes and Robert Merton, legendary trader John Meriwether, and a team of PhDs produced four years of extraordinary returns using convergence arbitrage strategies before losing $4.6 billion in less than four months during the 1998 Russian debt crisis and global liquidity panic. [[ltcm]]'s collapse nearly triggered a systemic financial crisis, prompting the Federal Reserve to organize a $3.6 billion private-sector bailout. The book is the definitive case study in leverage risk, model risk, liquidity risk, and the dangers of intellectual arrogance in quantitative finance.

## Key Claims

1. [HIGH] **LTCM's Nobel Prize-winning founders built models assuming normal distributions — the models failed catastrophically**: The fund's risk models, built on Gaussian assumptions and calibrated on historical data from relatively calm periods, assigned near-zero probability to the events of August-September 1998. The models predicted maximum daily losses of ~$35 million; the fund lost $553 million on a single day in August 1998. The gap between model prediction and reality was not incremental — it was existential. (Source: Roger Lowenstein)

2. [HIGH] **Extreme leverage (25:1 to 100:1) turns small losses into existential threats**: LTCM operated at approximately 25:1 balance sheet leverage, with notional derivatives exposure far exceeding even that. At 25:1 leverage, a 4% decline in asset value wipes out 100% of equity. The fund's strategy required near-perfection in predicting the magnitude and timing of convergence — any sustained adverse move consumed the thin capital cushion. (Source: Roger Lowenstein)

3. [HIGH] **Correlations spike toward 1.0 during crises — diversification fails precisely when needed most**: LTCM held positions across dozens of markets and strategies that exhibited low correlation during normal conditions. During the 1998 crisis, correlations across all positions converged toward 1.0 as a generalized flight to quality and liquidity caused losses in every position simultaneously. The portfolio's apparent diversification was an illusion that existed only in calm markets. (Source: Roger Lowenstein)

4. [HIGH] **Liquidity evaporates in panics — positions easily exited in normal times become impossible to unwind**: LTCM's positions included large, concentrated bets in markets with limited liquidity (off-the-run Treasuries, European bond spreads, equity volatility). When the fund needed to liquidate, the very act of selling moved prices against it, and counterparties who would normally absorb supply withdrew from the market entirely. Liquidity risk was systematically underestimated by the models. (Source: Roger Lowenstein)

5. [HIGH] **Convergence trades can diverge further before converging**: Many of LTCM's individual bets were eventually proven correct — mispricings between related securities did converge to fair value. But convergence happened after the fund was already insolvent. Being right about the fundamental thesis is worthless without the capital to survive the path to convergence. "The market can remain irrational longer than you can remain solvent." (Source: Roger Lowenstein)

6. [HIGH] **Model risk: all models are simplifications — the gap between model and reality widens in stress**: LTCM's models assumed continuous markets, constant liquidity, stable correlations, and normally distributed returns. None of these assumptions held during the crisis. The models were useful approximations in normal conditions but became actively misleading in exactly the conditions where accurate risk assessment mattered most. (Source: Roger Lowenstein)

7. [HIGH] **Counterparty risk cascades: one firm's distress becomes every connected firm's problem**: LTCM had derivatives positions with every major Wall Street bank. The fund's failure would have forced counterparties to crystallize massive losses and replace hedges in illiquid markets, potentially triggering a chain reaction of failures. Modern systemic risk is about interconnectedness — a single firm's collapse can threaten the entire financial system. (Source: Roger Lowenstein)

8. [HIGH] **The Fed organized LTCM's bailout to prevent systemic collapse — "too interconnected to fail"**: The Federal Reserve Bank of New York convened 14 major banks and brokerage firms, who collectively invested $3.6 billion to recapitalize LTCM and prevent a disorderly liquidation. While the Fed insisted this was not a government bailout, its role in organizing the rescue established the principle that systemically important financial institutions would be supported — creating moral hazard that contributed to the 2008 crisis. (Source: Roger Lowenstein)

9. [HIGH] **Intellectual arrogance is the most dangerous trait in risk management**: LTCM's partners — including two Nobel laureates — genuinely believed their models captured financial reality. This certainty discouraged internal questioning, dismissed external warnings, and led to position sizes that left no room for the possibility that the models might be wrong. The most dangerous risk manager is the one who is certain their model is correct. (Source: Roger Lowenstein)

10. [HIGH] **Risk management must account for scenarios the model says are "impossible"**: LTCM's Value at Risk models computed the 99th percentile loss and sized positions accordingly. They did not seriously contemplate what would happen in the remaining 1% — which is precisely where the fund blew up. Position sizing and leverage limits must be set based on survival analysis (what is the worst that could happen?), not probability-weighted expected outcomes. (Source: Roger Lowenstein)
