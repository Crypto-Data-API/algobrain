[[anton-kreil]] interviews [[ben-berggreen|Ben Berggreen]], the newest senior trading mentor at [[itpm]] at the time of recording, who is based in Los Angeles and still actively trades daily. Berggreen's career is notable for its unusual starting point (aviation) and his experience spanning the full spectrum from $200+ billion long-only funds to small startup hedge funds. The interview provides sharp commentary on the shift toward quantitative/algorithmic trading in hedge funds and why this creates opportunities for retail traders. This is part of ITPM's "Trading Legends" promotional series for its paid courses; biographical and philosophy claims are first-person and single-source, so confidence is MEDIUM.

> **Note on confidence:** Promotional interview for a paid trading-education business. Career details and opinions are the interviewee's own recollection, not independently verified — treat as [MEDIUM] single-source.

## Career Background

- [MEDIUM] Education in aeronautical science; started career as a cargo pilot flying planes in the Caribbean. Transitioned to finance in the mid-1990s after developing an interest in trading.
- [MEDIUM] First finance role at [[franklin-templeton|Franklin Templeton]] (~$200 billion fund) in Fort Lauderdale as a global long-only asset manager. Started in operations with no business background, worked his way up to the trading desk within a year, and traded European assets for the firm for a couple of years. Received orders from legendary investors including Mark Hoeschele and Mark Mobius.
- [MEDIUM] Moved to London after 9/11 when his current firm was shut down and no one was hiring. Joined Head Capital, a quantitative [[long-short-equity]] hedge fund startup in Chelsea. The fund used chaos mathematics and computer programming. Set up and managed their trading operation starting in 2001. Fund eventually shut down because the backers were real estate people who lacked the network/infrastructure to raise assets beyond the friends-and-family stage (~$40-50 million ceiling without major allocator access).
- [MEDIUM] Returned to the US, traded futures, then joined [[ge-asset-management|GE Asset Management]] in Connecticut, trading the entire Asian region (from Australia open to India close — roughly 11 PM to 6 AM). This was GE's full pension fund (~$250 billion). He and one other person covered all of Asia.
- [MEDIUM] Moved to the West Coast (lifestyle move + easier hours for Asia trading) and joined Tradewinds Global Investors (a Nuveen affiliate, a major mutual fund in Chicago with close to $40 billion). Ran their Asian desk for seven years; the Asian portion was approximately a quarter of the total ($10 billion).

## Large Funds vs. Small Funds

- [MEDIUM] Large funds have liquidity constraints that force longer holding periods (5-7 years). It can take months to build or exit a position in smaller markets (e.g., small-cap stocks in Malaysia). Compliance is much more intense. New positions must be approved by investment boards — extensive process with many "hoops to go through."
- [MEDIUM] Large funds are like "super tankers" that take a long time to turn around. Much of the trading at big firms is actually trading around core positions — overweight, underweight, neutral relative to the portfolio — rather than initiating new positions.
- [MEDIUM] Small funds are like "speedboats" — much more flexible, can access smaller companies that big funds cannot or will not invest in due to compliance/regulatory constraints.
- [MEDIUM] Prefers working for smaller funds due to the flexibility and speed advantages.

## Industry Shift Toward Quantitative/Algorithmic Trading

- [MEDIUM] Observes a definitive industry shift toward AI and quantitative processes. Hedge funds now prioritize programming skills (C++, multiple coding languages) over investment knowledge when hiring. This has caused a "brain drain" where the skill set to enter investment management has changed, and pay scales have been reduced.
- [MEDIUM] The old-school skill set of finding investment opportunities is "almost non-existent" now in hedge fund hiring. It is all about algorithms and coding.
- [MEDIUM] Quant/algo approaches dominate very short-term time frames. These firms have rooms full of math/programming PhDs, thousands of systems running simultaneously, constantly updating strategies. Edges slip away quickly due to fierce competition. Most retail traders cannot compete in this arena ("David competing against Goliath").
- [MEDIUM] Most large long-only funds are "closet indexers" — pursuing beta rather than alpha, charging high fees while barely keeping up with benchmarks. Returns have declined.

## Opportunity for Retail Traders

- [MEDIUM] The gap between short-term quant trading and long-term closet indexing creates a sweet spot for retail traders: the 1-6 month "swing trading" horizon where qualitative, subjective judgment matters more than algorithms.
- [MEDIUM] Over longer time periods, variables become more qualitative and less quantitative, giving human judgment an edge over algorithmic approaches.
- [MEDIUM] Retail traders now have access to the same information as professionals ("super fast... probably the best and quickest information in history"), creating a near-level playing field. Additionally, being small is an actual edge: invest in smaller companies big funds cannot access, move faster, no compliance overhead.
- [MEDIUM] The institutional bar is low — retail traders who come through proper education can perform as well as or better than many current professionals. "There's an intellectual snobbery on the professional side that's been caught out in terms of returns."

## Trading Philosophy

- [MEDIUM] Must have a systematic process/framework but it does not need to be rigid like a hedge fund's thousand-line codebase. Without a process, "you're just winging it."
- [MEDIUM] Must match your trading approach to your personality. If you are not comfortable taking much risk, you should not be trading the same way as someone who is. "You're not going to be successful until you match your trading approach to your personality." This took him a long time to learn.
- [MEDIUM] Discipline is the most important thing in trading. Simulated/demo accounts miss the emotional impact, which is "huge." Real money losses trigger reactions that fundamentally change decision-making.
- [MEDIUM] Retail traders need mentoring in the beginning because mistakes compound like a snowball rolling downhill — "before you know it you blew out half your account." The learning curve can be very expensive without guidance.
- [MEDIUM] References Peter Lynch's approach — keeping it simple, observing the world, seeing what works — as a philosophy that would no longer fly in institutional settings obsessed with pedigree and 200-page investment process documents, but which remains effective for retail traders.
