# Commodities

Commodities are raw materials and primary goods -- energy, metals, and agricultural products -- traded on global exchanges. Commodity markets are among the oldest in existence and remain critical for global trade, [[inflation]] dynamics, and portfolio [[diversification]]. Commodity trading is a core component of [[global-macro]] strategies and offers diversification benefits due to historically low correlation with equity markets.

### Energy

Crude oil (WTI, Brent), [[natural-gas]], [[heating-oil]], [[gasoline]], and [[coal]]. Energy commodities are the most heavily traded by dollar volume and are deeply tied to geopolitics and global economic cycles. OPEC production decisions are the single largest discretionary supply variable for crude oil.

### Precious Metals

[[gold|Gold]], [[silver]], [[platinum]], and [[palladium]]. Gold in particular serves as a traditional safe-haven asset and [[inflation]] hedge. Precious metals are sensitive to [[real-interest-rates]] and USD strength via the [[dxy-commodity-correlation|DXY-commodity inverse relationship]].

### Industrial Metals

[[copper|Copper]], [[aluminum]], [[nickel]], and [[zinc]]. Industrial metals track Chinese PMI and global manufacturing activity. Copper is known as "Dr. Copper" for its role as an economic bellwether. The EV transition is driving structural demand growth for copper, nickel, and [[lithium]].

### Agriculture

Grains ([[corn]], [[wheat]], [[soybeans]], [[rice]]), livestock ([[live-cattle|cattle]], [[lean-hogs|hogs]]), and oilseeds. Agricultural commodities are influenced by weather, crop reports ([[usda-data|WASDE]]), and government subsidies.

### Soft Commodities

Tropical and specialty agricultural products including [[coffee]], [[cocoa]], [[sugar]], [[cotton]], and [[orange-juice]]. These markets tend to be more volatile due to concentrated growing regions and weather sensitivity.

## How Commodities Trade

Most commodity trading occurs through [[futures-overview|futures]] contracts on exchanges like [[cme-group|CME Group]], ICE, and [[london-metal-exchange|LME]]. Futures allow producers to hedge price risk and speculators to take directional positions. Commodity ETFs and ETNs provide exposure without futures account requirements.

Key concepts in commodity trading include [[contango]] (futures price above spot), [[backwardation]] (futures below spot), and the [[roll-yield]] that results from continuously holding futures positions. [[convenience-yield]] and [[storage-economics]] determine the shape of the futures curve. [[trend-following-cta|Managed futures (CTA)]] strategies have historically performed well in commodity markets due to persistent supply-demand imbalances and policy-driven trends.

## Key Drivers

- **Supply and demand**: Weather, geopolitics, production decisions (OPEC for oil), and inventory levels. The [[supply-demand-balance]] is the core output of commodity fundamental analysis.
- **U.S. dollar**: Commodities are USD-priced, so dollar strength generally pressures commodity prices ([[dxy-commodity-correlation]]).
- **Inflation**: Commodities are both a cause and a hedge for inflation ([[commodity-inflation-link]]). Gold is viewed as the primary inflation hedge.
- **Central bank policy**: [[interest-rates]] and [[quantitative-easing]] affect the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding commodities.
- **[[capex-cycle|Capital expenditure cycles]]**: Long lead-time projects (new mines take 5-10 years); capex cuts create future supply gaps that drive [[commodity-super-cycle|super-cycles]].
- **[[geopolitical-risk-premium|Geopolitical risk]]**: Sanctions, export bans, supply route disruptions (Strait of Hormuz, Red Sea, Black Sea grain corridor).

## Market Positioning

The [[cot-report-analysis|Commitments of Traders (COT) report]], published weekly by the [[cftc|CFTC]], breaks down futures positioning by trader type. [[commercial-hedger-positioning|Commercial hedgers]] (producers and consumers) often fade rallies and buy dips. Extremes in [[speculative-positioning]] can signal crowded trades and potential reversals.

## Contango and Backwardation

Futures curves slope upward ([[contango]], when future prices > [[spot-price|spot]]) or downward ([[backwardation]], when spot > future prices). This term structure affects the returns of passive commodity investors through [[roll-yield]]. [[backwardation]] signals tight near-term supply; [[contango]] implies oversupply or high [[storage-economics|storage costs]]. Understanding [[futures-curve-structure-analysis|curve structure]] is essential for any commodity trading approach.

## Risk Management

Commodity markets demand explicit risk frameworks due to higher [[volatility]] than equities and leverage inherent in futures trading:

- **[[basis-risk]]**: Difference between spot and futures price, particularly relevant in physical delivery markets
- **[[roll-yield]]**: The return from rolling expiring contracts; critical in [[contango]] markets where it erodes returns
- **Position sizing**: Must adjust for commodity volatility; a 1-lot crude oil futures contract controls ~$70,000 of notional
- **Correlation analysis**: Commodities offer portfolio diversification; correlations spike during stress periods

## Market State (2025-2026)

Commodities saw a powerful bull run into 2026, led by precious metals:

- **Gold**: broke through the long-watched $3,000/oz and $4,000/oz psychological levels and surged to a record all-time high of roughly **$5,589/oz on 2026-01-28**, trading around **$4,100/oz in June 2026**. The rally was driven by central-bank buying, [[real-interest-rates]] dynamics, and safe-haven demand amid geopolitical stress. The 2026 Reuters analyst poll was the first to forecast an annual average above $4,000/oz.
- **Crude oil (WTI)**: traded near **$90-92/bbl in June 2026**, spiking on Middle East conflict (US strikes on Iran raising Strait of Hormuz disruption fears). Forecasts placed H2 2026 WTI in a roughly $67-97/bbl range.
- **Copper**: traded in the **$9,000-10,000/tonne** range through 2026, supported by EV/grid demand and the structural [[capex-cycle|supply deficit]].
- **Uranium (U3O8)**: spot in the **high-$60s to high-$70s/lb** through 2026 (around $69/lb in May 2026), reflecting reactor restarts and supply concentration.

## Why It Matters for Traders

Commodities provide diversification, inflation protection, and [[global-macro]] signals. [[crude-oil|Oil]] and [[copper]] are leading economic indicators; [[gold]] reflects risk sentiment and [[real-interest-rates]] expectations. Understanding commodity dynamics enriches analysis across all asset classes.
