Corn is the **largest US crop by acreage** and one of the most actively traded agricultural commodities globally. Traded on the [[cme-group|CBOT]] as corn futures (ZC), each contract represents 5,000 bushels. Corn has two dominant demand drivers: **animal feed** (~36% of US production) and **ethanol** (~34%), with the remainder split among exports, food/industrial use, and ending stocks. The US is the world's largest producer (~35% of global output), followed by China and Brazil. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Production and Supply

The **"Corn Belt"** -- Iowa, Illinois, Indiana, Nebraska, Minnesota -- is where US production concentrates. These five states account for roughly 55% of US corn output. The production cycle follows a predictable calendar:

- **April-May:** Planting season. [[usda-data|USDA Prospective Plantings]] (late March) gives the first major acreage signal
- **June-July:** Pollination -- the most critical weather window. Hot, dry conditions during July pollination can reduce yields dramatically (the 2012 drought cut US yields ~27%)
- **August-September:** Grain fill. [[usda-data|USDA Crop Progress]] reports track condition ratings weekly
- **October-November:** Harvest. Supply pressure typically depresses prices

Global production is roughly 1.2 billion metric tonnes annually. After the US (~35%), China (~23%) and Brazil (~10%) are the next largest producers, though Brazil's production has been growing rapidly. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Demand Drivers

### Animal Feed (~36% of US use)
Corn is the primary energy source in livestock rations. Demand is driven by [[live-cattle|cattle]], [[lean-hogs|hog]], and poultry herd sizes. The cattle-on-feed report and USDA livestock inventory data provide demand-side signals.

### Ethanol (~34% of US use)
The **Renewable Fuel Standard (RFS)** mandates blending ethanol into gasoline, creating a policy-driven demand floor. Approximately 5.0-5.5 billion bushels of corn are used annually for ethanol. Changes to the RFS, E15 waivers, or biofuel policy can shift demand materially. Ethanol plant margins (crush margin) connect corn to [[gasoline]] and energy markets.

### Exports (~14% of US use)
China, Mexico, Japan, and South Korea are the largest importers of US corn. Chinese import demand has been a significant swing factor since 2020. Export inspections data (weekly) and export sales reports provide real-time tracking.

### Food/Industrial (~10%)
Corn sweeteners (HFCS), corn starch, and other industrial uses provide relatively stable baseline demand.

## Key Reports and Data

The [[usda-data|USDA]] publishes the most market-moving data for corn:

| Report | Frequency | Release | Why It Matters |
|--------|-----------|---------|----------------|
| **WASDE** | Monthly | ~10th of month | Ending stocks-to-use ratio is the key price metric |
| **Prospective Plantings** | Annual | Late March | First major acreage estimate; often moves corn sharply |
| **Acreage** | Annual | Late June | Updated acreage based on actual planting data |
| **Crop Progress** | Weekly | Monday 4pm ET (May-Nov) | Condition ratings: Excellent/Good/Fair/Poor/Very Poor |
| **Grain Stocks** | Quarterly | Late month | Actual measured stocks; can deviate from WASDE implied |
| **Export Inspections** | Weekly | Monday morning | Real-time demand tracking |

The **ending stocks-to-use ratio** is the single most important fundamental metric. Below 10% signals tightness and typically supports prices above $5/bushel; above 15% signals surplus. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Price Drivers

1. **Midwest weather during pollination (July):** The single largest price driver in any given year. Hot/dry conditions during the critical pollination window can reduce yields 20-30%
2. **Ethanol mandates and policy:** RFS blending requirements, E15 waivers, SAF (Sustainable Aviation Fuel) credits
3. **Chinese import demand:** China's shift to large-scale corn imports starting in 2020 added a new structural demand source
4. **Fertilizer costs:** Corn is the most fertilizer-intensive major crop. Nitrogen fertilizer prices (linked to [[natural-gas]]) affect planting economics and acreage decisions
5. **Corn-soybean price ratio:** Farmers decide spring acreage based on relative profitability. A ratio above 2.4:1 (soybeans/corn) typically pulls acres toward [[soybeans]]; below 2.2:1 favors corn
6. **US dollar strength:** Corn is priced in USD; a strong dollar makes US corn more expensive for foreign buyers
7. **South American competition:** Brazil's *safrinha* (second-crop) corn has grown dramatically, now rivaling the US as an exporter

## Seasonal Patterns

Corn exhibits well-documented [[commodity-seasonality-patterns|seasonal patterns]]:

- **February-June:** Prices tend to rally as weather uncertainty builds ahead of the planting and growing season ("weather premium")
- **July:** Peak volatility -- pollination weather makes or breaks the crop
- **September-November:** Harvest pressure typically depresses prices as new supply hits the market
- **December-February:** Post-harvest consolidation; demand data becomes the focus

The 15-year seasonal average shows prices are typically lowest in September/October and highest in June/July. [[seasonal-spread-trading|Seasonal spread traders]] often focus on the July-December spread. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Key Relationships

- **[[crush-spread]]:** Corn's demand as ethanol feedstock connects it to the ethanol crush margin (corn in, ethanol + distillers grains out)
- **Corn-soybean ratio:** Key acreage allocation indicator watched by all ag traders
- **Feed cost index:** Corn + [[soybeans|soybean meal]] together determine livestock feeding costs, linking corn to [[live-cattle]] and [[lean-hogs]] markets
- **Fertilizer-corn ratio:** When fertilizer prices spike (as in 2022), marginal acres may not be planted, reducing supply

## Trading Considerations

- **Limit moves:** CBOT corn has $0.25/bushel ($1,250/contract) daily price limits, expanding to $0.40 after a limit day
- **Delivery:** Physical delivery at Chicago and several other locations; basis (cash vs. futures) varies by location
- **Options:** Active options market on ZC futures; implied volatility typically peaks in May-July
- **Mini contracts:** CBOT offers mini corn (XC) at 1,000 bushels for smaller accounts
- **COT positioning:** [[cot-report-analysis|Commitment of Traders]] data shows commercial hedger and managed money positioning; extreme managed money longs often precede corrections

## Historical Price Events

- **2012 US Drought:** Prices surged from ~$5.50 to $8.49/bushel (all-time high at that time) as the worst drought since the 1950s hit the Corn Belt
- **2020-2021 Bull Run:** Chinese demand + La Nina drought in South America pushed corn from $3.20 to over $7.50
- **2022 Russia-Ukraine War:** Corn spiked above $8.00 on Black Sea export disruption fears (Ukraine is a major corn exporter)
- **1996 Peak:** $5.54/bushel on tight stocks, the high watermark until 2007

## Advantages of Trading Corn

- Deep liquidity and tight bid-ask spreads on ZC
- Well-defined seasonal patterns with decades of data
- Extensive publicly available fundamental data ([[usda-data|USDA reports]], export data, ethanol production)
- Active options market enables sophisticated strategies
- Multiple related spread trades ([[crush-spread]], corn-soybean, corn-wheat, calendar spreads)

## Disadvantages and Risks

- Heavily influenced by weather -- inherently unpredictable beyond 7-10 days
- Policy risk: ethanol mandates, trade tariffs (US-China), export controls
- Concentration risk: US production concentrated in a few states; a single weather event can move the market
- Contract size ($25,000-$40,000 notional) may be large for retail accounts (mini contracts available)
- Physical delivery mechanics add complexity for futures traders
