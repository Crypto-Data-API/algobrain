Rice is a **global staple food** for over half the world's population, particularly in Asia. Unlike other major grains, rice is predominantly consumed in the country where it is produced, with only about 9% of global output entering international trade. CBOT rough rice futures (ZR) exist but are significantly less liquid than other grain contracts, making rice one of the least financialised major food commodities.

## Key Facts

- **Major producers:** China (~28%), India (~24%), Indonesia, Bangladesh, Vietnam
- **Unique trade dynamics:** Only ~9% of global rice production is traded internationally -- the vast majority is consumed domestically, making it fundamentally different from [[wheat]], [[corn]], or [[soybeans]]
- **Geopolitical sensitivity:** India's 2023 export ban on non-basmati white rice (India accounts for ~40% of global rice exports) demonstrated how quickly food commodity policy can disrupt markets
- **Futures:** CBOT Rough Rice (ZR), 2,000 hundredweight per contract. Low open interest and volume make it difficult to trade in size
- **Varieties:** Indica (long-grain, tropical), Japonica (short-grain, temperate), and specialty varieties command very different prices
- **Food security:** Governments in major producing countries (India, Thailand, Vietnam, Philippines) maintain rice stockpiles and intervene heavily in pricing and trade

## Pricing and Benchmarks

The primary international benchmark for rice is the **Thai 5% broken** white rice price, quoted FOB Bangkok. This benchmark serves as the reference for global rice trade. Unlike [[wheat]] or [[corn]], where [[futures]] markets dominate price discovery, rice prices are largely determined through bilateral government-to-government contracts and tenders. The CBOT rough rice contract (ZR) covers US-produced long-grain rice, which represents a small fraction of global trade. Rough rice futures average daily volume is typically under 5,000 contracts, compared to over 300,000 for corn -- making it impractical for institutional trading and largely irrelevant for global rice price discovery.

## Weather and Climate Dependency

Rice production is exceptionally weather-dependent. Paddy rice requires standing water for much of its growing cycle, making it vulnerable to both drought and flooding. El Nino and La Nina patterns significantly affect production in Southeast Asia. Climate change poses long-term risks to rice production through rising sea levels (saltwater intrusion into coastal paddies), increased frequency of extreme weather events, and rising temperatures (rice yields decline above 35C). These supply-side vulnerabilities, combined with growing demand from population growth in Asia and Africa, create the potential for periodic price spikes despite the market's low liquidity.

Rice is included for completeness as a global food commodity, but its thin futures market makes it impractical for most trading strategies. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])
