# Nuclear Power

Nuclear power is the generation of electricity using nuclear fission, currently providing roughly 9–10% of the world's electricity from over 440 reactors across more than 30 countries. For traders, nuclear power matters chiefly as the **demand engine for the [[uranium]] market**: reactor builds, restarts, life extensions, and the growing power needs of AI data centres define the structural demand side of the uranium investment thesis. It is not itself a directly traded commodity, but it sits at the centre of a tradeable ecosystem of uranium spot/term prices, miners, utilities, and enrichment/conversion bottlenecks.

## Why It Matters to Traders

The investment case for nuclear power flows through several tradeable channels:

- **[[uranium]] fuel** — physical uranium (U3O8 / "yellowcake"), the uranium investment trusts (e.g., Sprott Physical Uranium Trust), and uranium-focused ETFs.
- **Uranium miners** — Kazatomprom, Cameco (CCJ), NexGen, and the ASX complex including paladin-energy, boss-energy, Deep Yellow, and Lotus Resources.
- **Utilities and reactor operators** — long-life cash-flow plays increasingly signing power-purchase agreements (PPAs) with tech firms.
- **Fuel-cycle bottlenecks** — conversion and enrichment capacity, where Russia's Rosatom holds outsized market share, creating supply-chain risk premia for Western utilities.

## How It Works

Nuclear fission splits heavy atoms (uranium-235, and in some reactors plutonium-239) inside a reactor core, releasing heat that boils water into steam to drive turbines. The fuel passes through a multi-stage cycle:

1. **Mining** — uranium ore extracted (conventional, ISR/in-situ recovery, or by-product).
2. **Conversion** — yellowcake (U3O8) converted to uranium hexafluoride (UF6).
3. **Enrichment** — UF6 enriched to raise the fissile U-235 fraction (typically to ~3–5% for power reactors).
4. **Fuel fabrication** — enriched material formed into fuel pellets and rods.

Each stage is a distinct industry with its own pricing and capacity constraints; enrichment in particular is concentrated and politically sensitive.

## Demand Drivers

- **New reactor builds and restarts** — over 60 reactors are under construction globally (China leading), with dozens more planned, creating multi-year fuel demand growth and a structural uranium supply deficit (Source: [[uranium]]).
- **Reactor life extensions** — existing reactors granted licence extensions keep baseload demand for fuel.
- **AI data-centre power demand** — the new secular driver: major tech companies have signed PPAs and restart deals (e.g., interest in restarting shuttered plants) to secure firm, low-carbon baseload power for data centres.
- **Decarbonisation policy** — nuclear is increasingly classified as clean/low-carbon (including in some green taxonomies), supporting policy tailwinds toward net-zero targets.
- **Small modular reactors (SMRs)** — a potential next-generation demand catalyst, though commercialisation timelines remain uncertain and largely beyond the late 2020s.

## Catalysts and Risks

- **Policy shifts** — government nuclear endorsements, subsidies, or reversals (Germany's phase-out vs. the pro-nuclear "Sapporo-5" alliance of the US, France, UK, Japan, and Canada).
- **Accidents** — Fukushima (2011) triggered a global retrenchment and a decade-long uranium bear market; tail risk remains the single biggest sentiment threat to the thesis.
- **Geopolitics / fuel-cycle dependence** — Russia's Rosatom controls a large share of global enrichment; the 2024 US ban on Russian uranium imports (with a carve-out for existing contracts to 2028) accelerated Western efforts to onshore the fuel cycle.
- **SMR execution risk** — delays or cost overruns would push out the next demand leg.

## Competing Power Sources

Nuclear competes with [[natural-gas]], [[coal]], and renewables for baseload and dispatchable generation. Its advantages are high capacity factor, near-zero operating carbon emissions, and the highest energy density per unit of land; its disadvantages are high upfront capital cost, long build times, waste handling, and public-perception risk after accidents.
