Coffee is the **second most traded commodity by physical volume** after crude oil and one of the world's most widely consumed beverages. ICE Arabica Coffee futures (KC) serve as the global pricing benchmark -- known as the "C market." Robusta coffee trades on ICE Europe (London). Brazil produces ~35% of the world's coffee (predominantly Arabica), followed by Vietnam (~15%, predominantly Robusta). Coffee is extremely weather-sensitive, with Brazilian frost events and droughts capable of causing historic price spikes. Demand is relatively **price-inelastic** -- people continue drinking coffee regardless of price, making supply shocks the dominant driver of volatility. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Arabica vs Robusta

| Characteristic | Arabica | Robusta |
|---------------|---------|---------|
| **Flavor** | Mild, aromatic, complex | Strong, bitter, earthy |
| **Caffeine** | ~1.2% | ~2.2% |
| **Growing altitude** | 600-2,000m | 0-800m |
| **Disease resistance** | Low (coffee leaf rust vulnerable) | High |
| **Price** | Higher (~60-70% of market value) | Lower (~30-40%) |
| **Primary producers** | Brazil, Colombia, Ethiopia, Central America | Vietnam, Brazil, Indonesia, India |
| **Primary use** | Specialty, premium blends | Instant coffee, espresso blends |

The KC (Arabica) contract is the more important for most traders, as it sets the global pricing benchmark. Robusta's importance has grown with Vietnam's emergence as a major producer and increased demand for instant coffee and espresso blends. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Production and Supply

### Brazil: The Dominant Producer

Brazil produces roughly 60-65 million 60-kg bags annually (~35% of global), making it the single most important country for coffee pricing:

- **Minas Gerais** state alone produces more coffee than most entire countries
- **Biennial bearing cycle:** Coffee trees naturally alternate between "on-years" (high yield) and "off-years" (low yield), creating a 2-year production cycle
- **Frost risk:** Southern Brazilian coffee regions (Parana, Sao Paulo) are vulnerable to frost events from June-August (Southern Hemisphere winter). The 1975 "Black Frost" destroyed ~70% of Brazil's crop and caused prices to surge over 300%
- **Drought risk:** Prolonged dry seasons during the critical flowering period (September-October) reduce yields the following year

### Other Key Origins

- **Colombia:** Third-largest Arabica producer; two harvest cycles per year
- **Vietnam:** Largest Robusta producer; production surged from near-zero in the 1980s to ~30 million bags today
- **Ethiopia:** Birthplace of coffee; diverse heirloom varieties
- **Central America (Guatemala, Honduras, Costa Rica):** Quality Arabica, vulnerable to coffee leaf rust

### The ICE Certified Stocks

ICE-certified coffee stocks (warehouses in US, Europe, and occasionally origin) are a key indicator of available physical supply. Drawdowns in certified stocks signal tight supply and often precede price rallies.

## Demand Dynamics

- **Global consumption:** ~170 million bags annually, growing at ~2-3% per year
- **Inelastic demand:** Coffee consumption is habitual; price elasticity of demand is estimated at -0.1 to -0.3 (a 10% price increase reduces consumption only 1-3%)
- **Specialty coffee premium:** The "third wave" specialty movement has created a bifurcated market where premium single-origin coffees trade far above the C market
- **Emerging market growth:** Per-capita consumption growth in China, India, and Southeast Asia is the long-term demand story
- **Seasonal demand:** Roasting activity peaks ahead of the Northern Hemisphere winter (October-December)

## Price Drivers

1. **Brazilian weather (frost and drought):** The single largest price driver. A severe frost or drought can add $0.50-$1.50/lb to prices within days
2. **Biennial bearing cycle:** On-year/off-year production swings of 5-15% create systematic supply variability
3. **Vietnamese Robusta production:** Affects the Arabica-Robusta spread and overall global supply
4. **ICE certified stocks:** Declining stocks signal physical tightness and are a bullish catalyst
5. **Brazilian real (BRL):** A weak BRL makes Brazilian coffee cheaper in USD, incentivizing exports and pressuring prices; a strong BRL does the opposite
6. **Shipping logistics:** Coffee ships in containers; global shipping disruptions (2021-2022 container shortage) can affect delivery and pricing
7. **Coffee leaf rust (roya):** A fungal disease that devastated Central American production in 2012-2013, destroying an estimated 50% of the region's crop
8. **Speculation and fund flows:** Coffee attracts significant speculative interest; [[cot-report-analysis|COT]] managed money positioning can amplify moves

## Seasonal Patterns

Coffee follows [[commodity-seasonality-patterns|seasonal patterns]] tied to Brazil's harvest and frost calendar:

- **April-June:** Brazilian harvest begins (April for Robusta, May-June for Arabica). New supply typically pressures prices
- **June-August:** Brazilian frost window. The market prices in "frost premium" from June through early August
- **September-October:** Brazilian flowering period. Rainfall during this window determines the next year's crop potential
- **November-January:** Northern Hemisphere peak consumption season; roaster buying can support prices
- **February-March:** Pre-harvest anticipation; market assesses Brazilian crop size for the upcoming harvest

The biennial cycle overlays this: off-year seasons (lower Brazilian production) tend to have tighter markets and higher average prices. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Historical Price Events

- **1975 "Black Frost":** A catastrophic freeze destroyed ~70% of Brazil's coffee trees, sending prices from ~$0.65/lb to over $3.00/lb
- **1994 Double Frost:** Two frosts within weeks (June and July 1994) pushed prices from $0.80 to $2.75/lb
- **2011 Supply Squeeze:** Colombian production collapse + Brazilian off-year pushed Arabica to $3.08/lb
- **2021-2022 Rally:** Brazilian drought + frost (July 2021) combined with global shipping disruptions; prices surged from $1.20 to $2.60/lb
- **2024-2025 Bull Run:** Vietnamese Robusta supply concerns + Brazilian drought pushed KC above $4.00/lb, approaching all-time highs in nominal terms

## Trading Considerations

- **Volatile market:** Coffee is one of the most volatile soft commodities; daily moves of 3-5% are not uncommon
- **Brazilian weather premium:** The market routinely prices in risk premium during the frost window (June-August), which can deflate rapidly if no frost materializes
- **Spread trading:** The Arabica-Robusta spread is a key relative value trade; calendar spreads reflect the biennial cycle
- **COT positioning:** Managed money can build large speculative positions; extremes often precede reversals
- **Options:** Active options market on KC; implied volatility tends to peak during the frost window
- **Physical market complexity:** Certification, quality, origin, and shipping logistics create significant basis risk between futures and physical coffee

## Advantages of Trading Coffee

- Extreme weather sensitivity creates large, tradeable moves
- Well-defined seasonal patterns (frost window, biennial cycle)
- Relatively predictable supply dynamics (Brazil dominant, known growing regions)
- Active options market enables structured trades
- Inelastic demand provides a fundamental floor
- Growing global consumption provides a structural demand tailwind

## Disadvantages and Risks

- High volatility can cause large drawdowns; daily moves of 5-10% occur during weather events
- Brazilian weather forecasting beyond 7-10 days is unreliable
- Biennial cycle creates year-to-year supply swings that complicate trend following
- Physical coffee market has complex quality, origin, and certification dynamics
- Less liquid than energy or grain futures; wider spreads, especially in deferred months
- Currency risk (BRL, VND) adds a macro overlay to fundamental analysis
