Sugar is a globally traded agricultural commodity with a unique **dual-use** profile: it serves as both a food sweetener and an **ethanol feedstock**. ICE Sugar No. 11 (SB) is the global benchmark for raw cane sugar. Brazil is both the largest producer (~20% of world output) and largest exporter, and Brazilian mills can switch between producing sugar and ethanol based on relative prices -- creating a distinctive "sugar-ethanol parity" that acts as a price floor/ceiling mechanism found in no other commodity. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Key Specifications

> Contract terms below reflect publicly documented exchange specifications and may be updated by the exchanges; tick *values* are derived from contract size and quotation unit, not live prices. Confirm current specs with ICE before trading.

| Attribute | Raw Sugar No. 11 (SB) | White Sugar No. 5 (QW) |
|-----------|-----------------------|------------------------|
| **Exchange** | ICE Futures US | ICE Futures Europe (London) |
| **Underlying** | Raw centrifugal cane sugar, FOB | Refined white sugar |
| **Contract size** | 112,000 lbs (50.8 metric tonnes) | 50 metric tonnes |
| **Quotation** | US cents per pound | US dollars per metric tonne |
| **Tick size** | $0.0001/lb = $11.20 per contract | $0.10/tonne = $5.00 per contract |
| **Trading months** | Mar (H), May (K), Jul (N), Oct (V) | Mar, May, Aug, Oct, Dec |
| **Settlement** | Physical delivery, FOB at origin port | Physical delivery |
| **Typical price range** | $0.10–$0.28/lb (2015–2025, historical) | (tracks SB plus the white premium) |

**Note on US domestic sugar:** the **No. 16 (SF)** contract on ICE US covers US domestic raw cane sugar, which trades at a premium to world (No. 11) prices because of the US sugar program's tariff-rate quotas. Most global price discovery happens in No. 11.

### CME vs ICE

While ICE is the home of the benchmark soft-commodity sugar contracts, traders frequently note that the broader agricultural complex (grains, oilseeds, and competing ethanol feedstock [[corn]]) lists on **CME Group**. Sugar itself is an ICE product; the CME linkage is via the corn-ethanol vs cane-ethanol substitution that drives part of sugar's demand thesis (see Price Drivers).

## Raw Sugar vs White Sugar

- **Raw sugar (No. 11, SB):** Unrefined cane sugar; the global benchmark. Traded on ICE US. This is what most producers export
- **White sugar (No. 5, QW):** Refined sugar; traded on ICE London. Commands a premium ("white premium") reflecting refining costs. The raw-white spread is a key trade
- **Beet sugar:** Produced primarily in EU, Russia, US. Cannot be used for ethanol. EU beet sugar production is protected by domestic policy

## Production and Supply

### Brazil: The Sugar Superpower

Brazil's position in sugar is unrivaled among agricultural commodities:

- Produces ~570-620 million tonnes of sugarcane annually
- Brazilian **Center-South (CS) region** (Sao Paulo state and surroundings) accounts for ~90% of Brazil's production and ~20% of global sugar
- **The mill allocation decision:** Brazilian mills can allocate sugarcane to either sugar or ethanol production. In a typical year, roughly 45-55% goes to sugar and 45-55% to ethanol. This ratio is the single most important variable in global sugar supply
- **Harvest season:** April-November (Center-South). Peak crushing is June-September
- **UNICA** (Brazilian Sugarcane Industry Association) publishes bi-weekly crush data -- the most important near-term supply indicator

### India: The Policy-Driven Swing Producer

India is the world's second-largest sugar producer but exhibits extreme policy-driven variability:

- Production swings between 28-36 million tonnes depending on monsoon and acreage
- Government sets **minimum support prices** and controls exports through subsidies, quotas, or bans
- India has become the world's third-largest ethanol producer as part of its E20 blending mandate (20% ethanol by 2025-26)
- The Indian sugar cycle (surplus → low prices → reduced planting → deficit → high prices → increased planting) runs roughly 3-4 years

### Other Key Producers

- **Thailand:** #2 exporter; sensitive to El Nino drought
- **EU:** Major beet sugar producer; mostly consumed internally
- **China:** #3 consumer; imports ~5-6 million tonnes annually; maintains strategic reserves

## Demand and Consumption

Sugar demand is famously **price-inelastic** in the short run — people do not stop sweetening food when prices rise — which is why supply shocks drive most of the volatility.

| Demand segment | Characteristics |
|---|---|
| **Direct food/beverage** | The largest, most stable bucket; grows roughly with population and income in developing economies |
| **Industrial (confectionery, processed food)** | Steady; large buyers hedge forward |
| **Ethanol feedstock (cane)** | The swing factor — Brazilian mills divert cane to ethanol when fuel economics favor it (see parity below) |
| **High-fructose corn syrup (HFCS) substitution** | In some markets (notably the US), corn-based HFCS substitutes for sugar in soft drinks, linking sugar demand to [[corn]] economics |
| **Sweetener substitution** | Artificial/low-cal sweeteners and sugar taxes create slow structural headwinds in developed-market per-capita consumption |

Key demand themes: rising emerging-market consumption (India, Africa, Southeast Asia) offsetting flat-to-declining developed-market per-capita intake; sugar taxes in numerous jurisdictions; and the steady pull of biofuel mandates on the supply available for food.

## Global Supply/Demand Balance Framework

The market trades the **global balance** — production minus consumption — and the resulting stocks-to-use ratio:

| Indicator | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| **Global surplus** | Production > consumption → stocks build → bearish (e.g., 2016/17 trough) |
| **Global deficit** | Consumption > production → stocks draw → bullish (e.g., 2023 rally) |
| **Stocks-to-consumption < 40%** | Signals tightness; supports prices |
| **Stocks-to-consumption > 50%** | Signals surplus; pressures prices |
| **ICE certified stocks** | Near-term proxy for deliverable physical supply |

A useful mental model: the **balance** sets the level, the **Brazilian sugar/ethanol mix** and **Indian policy** set the near-term direction, and the **BRL** modulates the floor via the ethanol-parity mechanism described next.

## The Sugar-Ethanol Parity

This is sugar's most distinctive fundamental feature:

- **Mechanism:** Brazilian mills can switch between sugar and ethanol output based on which is more profitable
- **Parity price:** At roughly $0.14-$0.17/lb (depending on exchange rates and ethanol prices), sugar and ethanol reach economic parity for Brazilian mills
- **Floor effect:** When sugar falls below parity, mills divert more cane to ethanol, reducing sugar supply and supporting prices
- **Ceiling effect:** When sugar rises above parity, mills maximize sugar output, increasing supply and capping upside
- **Brazilian real (BRL):** A weak BRL lowers the parity in USD terms (Brazilian costs are in BRL, sugar is priced in USD), making the floor lower. A strong BRL raises the floor
- **Gasoline price link:** Brazilian ethanol competes with gasoline at the pump. Petrobras gasoline pricing policy affects ethanol demand, which feeds back to sugar allocation

This parity mechanism makes sugar fundamentally different from most commodities -- it has a semi-structural price band anchored by the ethanol alternative. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Price Drivers

1. **Brazilian CS sugar-ethanol mix:** The allocation decision is the most important near-term supply variable. UNICA bi-weekly data moves markets
2. **Indian production and policy:** Government export subsidies, bans, and minimum support prices create unpredictable supply swings
3. **Weather (El Nino/La Nina):** El Nino tends to reduce production in Asia (Thailand, India) while benefiting Brazil. La Nina is roughly the opposite
4. **Brazilian real (BRL):** Directly affects the sugar-ethanol parity price and Brazilian export competitiveness
5. **Global stocks-to-consumption ratio:** Below 40% signals tightness; above 50% signals surplus
6. **Oil prices:** Affect the relative attractiveness of sugarcane ethanol vs sugar production
7. **Chinese imports:** China maintains strategic reserves and adjusts import quotas; restocking/destocking moves prices
8. **Thai production:** Thailand is the second-largest exporter; drought or policy changes affect global supply
9. **Freight rates:** Sugar is a bulky, low-value-per-tonne commodity; shipping costs matter more than for most commodities

## Seasonal Patterns

Sugar follows [[commodity-seasonality-patterns|seasonal patterns]] driven by the dual-hemisphere crushing calendar:

- **April-May:** Brazilian CS crushing begins. Initial UNICA data sets the tone for the new crop year
- **June-September:** Peak Brazilian crushing. Sugar supply increases; prices often under pressure
- **October-December:** Northern hemisphere beet and cane crushing begins (India, Thailand, EU). Brazilian CS crush winds down
- **January-March:** Indian export window (if government allows). Thai crushing peaks. Brazilian inter-crop period (lower supply)

The **marketing year** for sugar typically runs October-September globally. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## EM Currency Impact

Sugar is uniquely sensitive to **emerging market currencies** because production is concentrated in developing countries:

- **Brazilian real (BRL):** The most important single currency for sugar. A 10% BRL depreciation can lower the effective sugar floor by $0.01-$0.02/lb
- **Indian rupee (INR):** Affects Indian production economics and export competitiveness
- **Thai baht (THB):** Similar dynamics for Thai exports
- When EM currencies weaken simultaneously (risk-off episodes, Fed tightening), it can create significant headwinds for sugar prices even if fundamentals are neutral

## Spread Trading

- **Raw-white premium (SB-QW):** Reflects refining capacity utilization. Widens when refining capacity is tight, narrows when surplus
- **Sugar-ethanol spread:** The relative value trade that drives Brazilian mill allocation
- **Calendar spreads:** March-May, May-July spreads reflect near-term supply dynamics
- **Inter-commodity:** Sugar-corn spread can reflect relative ethanol feedstock economics (US corn ethanol vs Brazilian cane ethanol)

### Curve Structure

- **Contango** (deferred > nearby) typically signals comfortable near-term supply and carrying cost — common during peak Brazilian crushing.
- **Backwardation** (nearby > deferred) signals near-term tightness — common during deficit years or inter-crop periods when physical supply is scarce.
- The **harvest calendar** imprints a recurring shape on the curve: the Oct (V) contract often prices the transition between the Brazilian CS crush and the northern-hemisphere crop. See [[commodity-seasonality-patterns]].

## Trading Considerations

- **Policy risk is paramount:** Indian export policy, Brazilian ethanol mandates, EU beet subsidies, and Chinese import quotas all affect global supply/demand in ways that are difficult to forecast
- **BRL hedging:** Many sugar traders simultaneously trade the BRL to hedge or express views on the currency component
- **Contract size:** 112,000 lbs ($11,200-$31,000 notional) is manageable for most futures accounts
- **Delivery complexity:** Physical delivery requires port loading at origin; the delivery process is complex
- **Less liquid than grains:** Lower open interest and volume than [[corn]], [[wheat]], or [[soybeans]]

## Historical Price Events

- **2010-2011 Spike:** Sugar surged from $0.13 to $0.36/lb on Indian production shortfall + Brazilian ethanol demand surge
- **2016 Trough:** Prices fell to $0.10/lb on global surplus, Brazilian recession, and weak BRL
- **2023 Rally:** Prices reached $0.28/lb on El Nino-related production concerns in India and Thailand, plus Brazilian drought affecting the 2024 crop outlook
- **1974 and 1980 Spikes:** Sugar reached $0.65/lb in 1974 and $0.45/lb in 1980 during commodity supercycles

## Advantages of Trading Sugar

- Well-understood fundamental framework (Brazilian allocation, Indian policy, global balance)
- The sugar-ethanol parity provides a structural price framework unlike other commodities
- Pronounced seasonal patterns tied to crushing calendars
- Active spread trading opportunities (raw-white, calendar, sugar-ethanol)
- Growing ethanol demand provides long-term structural support

## Disadvantages and Risks

- Heavy government intervention in major producing countries (India, China, EU, Thailand)
- Brazilian policy (ethanol mandates, Petrobras pricing) adds a layer of political risk
- Currency risk (BRL, INR, THB) can dominate fundamental signals
- Less liquid than grain or energy futures
- Complex delivery process for physical market participants
- Long and uncertain Indian policy cycle creates multi-year uncertainty
