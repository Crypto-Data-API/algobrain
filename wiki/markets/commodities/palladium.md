# Palladium

Palladium (Pd) is a Platinum Group Metal (PGM) with approximately 85% of its demand coming from gasoline-engine catalytic converters, making it the most automotive-dependent precious metal. It is the most supply-constrained of the four major precious metals ([[gold]], [[silver]], [[platinum]], palladium), with production concentrated in just two countries.

## How Palladium Is Traded

- **Futures**: NYMEX palladium futures (PA) on [[cme-group|CME Group]], with each contract representing 100 troy ounces.
- **Spot Market**: Priced in USD per troy ounce, with the [[lbma|LBMA]] Palladium Price set twice daily.
- **ETFs**: PALL (Aberdeen Standard Physical Palladium Shares) is the primary physical palladium ETF.
- **London Market**: OTC trading through LBMA-approved dealers.

## Supply

Russia (~40% of global supply, primarily from Norilsk Nickel) and South Africa (~35%) dominate palladium production. Unlike [[gold]] and [[silver]], there is virtually no above-ground stockpile of palladium — nearly all supply goes directly into industrial use. Palladium is primarily mined as a byproduct of nickel (in Russia) and platinum (in South Africa), making supply highly inelastic to price changes. This supply structure creates acute vulnerability to geopolitical disruption — particularly Russia-related sanctions or trade restrictions.

| Producer | Share | Notes |
|----------|-------|-------|

| South Africa | ~35% | Byproduct of PGM mining (Bushveld Complex) |

## Price History

Palladium underwent one of the most dramatic price moves in precious metals history:

- **2016**: ~$500/oz — undervalued, market in early deficit
- **2018**: Surpassed [[platinum]] price for the first time since 2001
- **2019**: Crossed $1,500/oz as structural deficit intensified
- **2020**: Briefly above $2,800/oz before COVID crash
- **2022**: Hit $3,000+/oz following Russia's invasion of Ukraine (supply fears)
- **2023-2024**: Declined back toward $1,000/oz as EV transition concerns mounted and substitution increased

This move was driven by a multi-year structural supply deficit: automakers needed palladium for tightening emissions standards (Euro 6, China 6), while supply was constrained by geography and byproduct economics.

## EV Transition: The Long-Term Headwind

Electric vehicles do not require catalytic converters, representing an existential long-term demand threat to palladium. As EV penetration grows:

- **Gasoline vehicle production peaks** — consensus estimates suggest global ICE vehicle production will peak between 2025-2030
- **Catalytic converter demand declines** — 85% of palladium demand is directly at risk
- **Substitution accelerates** — automakers are increasingly substituting cheaper [[platinum]] for palladium in remaining gasoline converters
- **Hydrogen optionality is limited** — unlike platinum, palladium has minimal role in fuel cell technology

This EV headwind is the primary reason palladium declined sharply from its 2022 highs despite supply remaining tight.

## Geopolitical Risk

Palladium carries outsized [[geopolitical-risk-premium]] due to Russian supply concentration. Sanctions, trade disruptions, or production issues at Norilsk Nickel can cause immediate price spikes. The 2022 Russia-Ukraine conflict demonstrated this dynamic, with palladium briefly spiking above $3,000/oz on supply fears before sanctions ultimately exempted most metal exports.
