# Coal

Coal is a combustible sedimentary rock and one of the oldest traded energy commodities. Despite declining usage in developed markets due to the renewable energy transition, coal remains a massive global market — responsible for approximately **36% of global electricity generation** and an essential input for steelmaking. China and India together account for over two-thirds of global coal consumption. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Types of Coal

### Thermal Coal (Steam Coal)

- **Use**: Electricity generation in coal-fired power plants
- **Benchmark**: Newcastle coal (Australia) — the primary seaborne thermal coal benchmark, traded on [[intercontinental-exchange|ICE]]
- **Other benchmarks**: Richards Bay (South Africa), API 2 (Northwest Europe)
- **Trend**: Declining in OECD nations as renewable energy and [[natural-gas]] displace coal for power generation. However, demand remains strong in China, India, and Southeast Asia.

### Metallurgical Coal (Coking Coal)

- **Use**: Essential input for blast furnace steelmaking — converted to coke which acts as fuel and reducing agent for [[iron-ore]]
- **Benchmark**: Premium hard coking coal (Australia) — traded OTC and on futures exchanges
- **Characteristics**: Higher quality requirements than thermal coal; fewer substitutes available
- **Demand driver**: Global steel production, particularly in China (which produces ~55% of world steel)

## Key Price Drivers

- **China policy**: As the world's largest consumer and producer, Chinese import policies, production targets, and environmental regulations dominate the market
- **India demand growth**: Rapidly increasing consumption as the economy industrializes
- **[[natural-gas]] substitution**: In power generation, gas-to-coal switching depends on relative fuel prices (the "spark spread" vs "dark spread" economics)
- **Renewable energy transition**: Long-term structural headwind in developed markets; coal plant retirements accelerating in US, EU, UK
- **Supply disruptions**: Mining accidents, weather (Australian floods), labor strikes, and export bans (Indonesia) can spike prices
- **Carbon pricing**: EU ETS, UK carbon price support, and other carbon pricing mechanisms increase the effective cost of coal-fired generation
- **Shipping costs**: Coal is heavy and bulky; freight rates significantly affect delivered prices

## Trading Instruments

- **ICE Newcastle Coal Futures**: Primary thermal coal benchmark, 1,000 metric tonnes per contract
- **CME/NYMEX coal futures**: U.S. Appalachian coal contracts (less liquid)
- **OTC swaps**: Active dealer market for physical coal swaps and options
- **Equity proxies**: Coal mining companies — Glencore, BHP (coking coal), Whitehaven Coal, Peabody Energy, Arch Resources, CONSOL Energy

## Structural Decline vs Cyclical Spikes

Coal presents a paradox for traders: the secular trend is bearish (energy transition, ESG divestment, carbon pricing), but cyclical supply shocks can create massive price spikes. Newcastle thermal coal traded below $60/tonne in early 2020, spiked above $400/tonne in 2022 (Russia-Ukraine supply disruption + European energy crisis), then retreated. This "declining asset with convex upside" profile makes coal interesting for tactical traders even as it becomes uninvestable for long-term buy-and-hold.

## ESG Divestment vs Asian Demand

Coal is at the centre of a structural tension in global markets. Western institutional investors, pension funds, and sovereign wealth funds are divesting from thermal coal under ESG mandates -- Norway's Government Pension Fund Global, for example, has excluded companies deriving more than 30% of revenue from thermal coal. Many banks have restricted lending to new coal projects. This divestment pressure reduces the available capital for coal production, constrains supply, and paradoxically supports higher prices for remaining producers. Meanwhile, coal demand in Asia continues to grow: China approved over 100 GW of new coal-fired generation capacity in 2022-2023, and India's coal consumption is rising 5-7% annually. This bifurcation creates a market where Western capital avoids coal while Asian demand sustains it, leaving well-positioned producers like [[whitehaven-coal|Whitehaven Coal]], New Hope Group, and Yancoal with strong margins and limited competition for capital.

## Key Australian Coal Producers

- **[[whitehaven-coal|Whitehaven Coal]]** (ASX: WHC) -- Pure-play coal producer (thermal and metallurgical), NSW operations. Acquired Daunia and Blackwater coking coal mines from BHP in 2024
- **New Hope Group** (ASX: NHC) -- Thermal coal producer, New Acland mine in Queensland. High-cost but positioned in the premium Australian thermal coal market
- **Yancoal Australia** (ASX: YAL) -- Major thermal and metallurgical coal producer, majority-owned by China's Yanzhou Energy. Operations across NSW and Queensland
- **Stanmore Resources** (ASX: SMR) -- Metallurgical coal producer, acquired BHP's 80% stake in BMA's South Walker Creek and Poitrel mines

## Seasonality

- **Northern-hemisphere winter (Q4-Q1)** — peak heating demand for thermal coal lifts power-sector consumption; cold snaps in China, Europe, and Northeast Asia are the main bullish catalysts.
- **Summer cooling (Q2-Q3)** — air-conditioning load in Asia provides a secondary demand peak.
- **Australian wet season (Dec-Mar)** — Queensland flooding can disrupt coking-coal exports, a recurring supply-side spike risk.
- **Restocking cycles** — utilities and steelmakers build inventory ahead of winter, front-loading seaborne demand in autumn.
