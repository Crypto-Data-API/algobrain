Brent crude is the world's most widely used benchmark for pricing crude oil, serving as the reference price for approximately two-thirds of globally traded oil. Originating from the North Sea fields off the coast of the United Kingdom and Norway, Brent was named after the Brent oilfield, itself named using Shell's convention of naming fields after geese (the Brent goose). As North Sea production has declined, the benchmark has evolved into "Brent blend," incorporating crude from the Forties, Oseberg, Ekofisk, and Troll fields (collectively known as BFOET) to maintain sufficient physical supply to underpin the benchmark's liquidity.

Brent [[futures]] are traded primarily on the Intercontinental Exchange (ICE) under the ticker symbol "B," with contracts denominated in US dollars per barrel and sized at 1,000 barrels. The contract is cash-settled against the ICE Brent Index, making it a financial rather than physical delivery contract -- a key distinction from [[wti|WTI]] (West Texas Intermediate) futures on the CME/NYMEX, which are physically deliverable at Cushing, Oklahoma. Brent typically trades at a premium or discount to WTI, with the Brent-WTI spread driven by differences in crude quality (Brent is slightly heavier and more sulfurous), transportation logistics, and regional supply-demand balances. When the spread widened to over $25/barrel in 2011 due to a glut at Cushing, it created significant arbitrage opportunities that attracted pipeline investment and eventually narrowed the differential.

For traders, the Brent-WTI spread is itself a tradeable instrument that reflects global vs. US-centric oil dynamics. A widening spread often signals stronger international demand or constrained non-US supply, while a narrowing spread reflects US oversupply or increased export capacity. [[commodity-seasonality-patterns|Seasonal patterns]], OPEC production decisions, geopolitical risk in the Middle East, and global refinery maintenance schedules all influence Brent pricing. The Brent market's deep liquidity -- daily trading volume regularly exceeds 1 million contracts on ICE -- makes it a preferred instrument for both hedging by oil producers and consumers and for speculative trading by commodity funds and macro traders.

## Contract Specifications

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Exchange** | Intercontinental Exchange (ICE) |
| **Ticker** | B (front-month: BRN) |
| **Contract size** | 1,000 barrels |
| **Quotation** | US dollars and cents per barrel |
| **Settlement** | Cash-settled vs the ICE Brent Index (vs WTI's physical delivery at Cushing) |
| **Listed months** | Consecutive months out to ~8 years; deep liquidity in front 6-12 months |
| **Quality** | Light, sweet; ~38 API gravity, ~0.4% sulphur (slightly heavier/more sour than WTI) |
| **Physical basis** | BFOET basket — Brent, Forties, Oseberg, Ekofisk, Troll |

## Key Drivers

- **OPEC+ supply policy** — production quotas and compliance set the marginal global supply. Brent, as the international benchmark, is more directly exposed to OPEC+ decisions than WTI, which is anchored to US shale dynamics.
- **Geopolitical risk premium** — Middle East tensions, sanctions (Russia, Iran, Venezuela), and shipping-route disruptions (Strait of Hormuz, Bab-el-Mandeb / Red Sea) feed directly into Brent because the marginal seaborne barrel is priced off it.
- **Global demand** — China and emerging-market growth, OECD refinery runs, and aviation/transport fuel demand drive the demand side.
- **US shale and exports** — rising US production and export capacity narrows the Brent-WTI spread by putting more light sweet barrels onto the seaborne market.
- **Term structure** — contango (oversupply, incentivises storage) vs [[backwardation|backwardation]] (tightness, penalises holding inventory). Brent's structure is closely watched as a real-time gauge of physical balance.
- **US dollar** — oil is priced in dollars, so a stronger DXY is a headwind to Brent and vice versa.

## Brent vs WTI

| Feature | Brent | WTI |
|---------|-------|-----|
| **Exchange** | ICE | NYMEX / CME |
| **Settlement** | Cash (ICE Brent Index) | Physical at Cushing, OK |
| **Geographic anchor** | Seaborne / international | US midcontinent |
| **Pricing role** | ~2/3 of globally traded oil | US benchmark, key for North American crude |
| **Quality** | Slightly heavier, more sour | Lighter, sweeter |
| **Spread driver** | International supply/demand | US inventory, pipeline/export capacity |

The Brent-WTI spread (Brent typically trades at a small premium to WTI) is one of the most-traded oil relationships. It blew out beyond $25/bbl in 2011 when Cushing was glutted before pipeline takeaway and US export capacity expanded; it has generally ranged roughly $3-8/bbl in recent years, narrowing when US exports flow freely and widening on US oversupply or international tightness.

## How Traders Access Brent

- **Futures and options** — ICE Brent (B) is the primary instrument; deep options liquidity for hedging and vol trades.
- **Spreads** — Brent-WTI arbitrage, crack spreads (Brent vs gasoil/diesel), and calendar spreads (term-structure trades).
- **ETFs** — Brent-specific ETFs (e.g. BNO, United States Brent Oil Fund) for retail; USO tracks WTI. Both suffer roll decay in contango.
- **Equities** — energy majors and E&Ps via XLE give equity-style oil exposure with dividends.

## Trading Lesson

Brent is the truer global oil benchmark; WTI can dislocate from world prices when US logistics bottleneck (as in 2011) or when US storage stresses (the April 2020 negative-WTI episode did not occur in Brent, which stayed positive because it is cash-settled and seaborne). Macro traders default to Brent for a clean read on global oil supply/demand and reserve WTI for US-specific plays.
