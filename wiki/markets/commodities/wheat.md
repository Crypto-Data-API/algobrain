Wheat is a **global staple grain** and one of the most geopolitically sensitive agricultural commodities. Traded on the [[cme-group|CBOT]] as soft red winter wheat futures (ZW), with additional contracts for Kansas City hard red winter (KE) and Minneapolis spring wheat (MWE). Each contract represents 5,000 bushels. Unlike [[corn]] and [[soybeans]], which are dominated by the US and Brazil, wheat production is spread across many countries, making it uniquely sensitive to geopolitical events and trade policy. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Wheat Classes

Wheat is not a single commodity -- quality and type matter significantly:

| Class | Ticker | Growing Region | Primary Use |
|-------|--------|----------------|-------------|
| **Soft Red Winter (SRW)** | ZW | Eastern US (Ohio, Illinois, Indiana) | Cakes, cookies, pastries |
| **Hard Red Winter (HRW)** | KE | Central Plains (Kansas, Oklahoma, Texas) | Bread flour (most traded globally) |
| **Hard Red Spring (HRS)** | MWE | Northern Plains (N. Dakota, Montana, Minnesota) | Premium bread flour, blending |
| **Soft White** | — | Pacific NW (Washington, Oregon) | Asian noodles, pastry |
| **Durum** | — | North Dakota | Pasta, couscous |

Quality metrics that affect pricing: **protein content** (higher = more valuable for bread), **falling number** (measures sprout damage), **test weight** (density), and **moisture**. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Production and Global Trade

Global wheat production is roughly 780 million metric tonnes annually, spread across numerous countries:

| Region | Share of Global Production | Notes |
|--------|---------------------------|-------|
| EU | ~20% | France, Germany are top producers |
| China | ~18% | Mostly consumed domestically |
| India | ~14% | Often restricts exports in tight years |
| Russia | ~12% | World's largest exporter |
| US | ~6% | Fourth-largest exporter |
| Canada | ~5% | Premium spring wheat exporter |
| Australia | ~4% | Southern hemisphere exporter |
| Ukraine | ~3-4% | Key Black Sea exporter |

Unlike corn, **most of the world grows wheat**, making it a truly global market. The Black Sea region (Russia + Ukraine) has become the world's largest export zone, accounting for ~30% of global wheat exports. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Winter Wheat vs Spring Wheat

- **Winter wheat:** Planted in fall (September-October), goes dormant over winter, resumes growth in spring, harvested in summer (June-July). Accounts for ~75% of US wheat production. Key risk: winterkill from extreme cold without snow cover
- **Spring wheat:** Planted in spring (April-May), harvested in fall (August-September). Higher protein content, commands a premium. Key risk: late planting from wet springs, summer drought

This dual-cycle structure means wheat has **two distinct growing seasons** per year in the US, each with its own weather risks and report schedule.

## Price Drivers

1. **Geopolitics and trade policy:** Wheat is the most geopolitically sensitive grain. Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine caused wheat to spike ~60% in days as Black Sea exports were disrupted. Export bans by Russia, India, or other major producers move markets instantly
2. **Black Sea production and logistics:** The Black Sea grain corridor (Russia/Ukraine combined ~30% of exports) is a critical chokepoint. See [[geopolitical-risk-premium]]
3. **US Plains weather:** Winter wheat in Kansas/Oklahoma faces drought risk; spring wheat in the Dakotas faces drought and late-planting risk
4. **Global stocks-to-use ratio:** Below 25% signals tightness; above 35% signals surplus
5. **Currency effects:** A strong Russian ruble makes Russian wheat more expensive, benefiting US/EU exporters; a weak ruble does the opposite
6. **Quality premiums:** In years with widespread rain during harvest, protein premiums widen significantly (wet conditions reduce protein and cause sprouting)
7. **Feed wheat substitution:** When wheat is cheap relative to [[corn]], it enters livestock rations as a substitute feed grain

## Seasonal Patterns

Wheat follows [[commodity-seasonality-patterns|seasonal patterns]] driven by its dual growing cycle:

- **October-February:** Winter wheat planting and dormancy. Prices often find support on winterkill concerns
- **March-May:** Spring wheat planting + winter wheat breaking dormancy. Acreage data (March) can move prices sharply
- **June-July:** Winter wheat harvest in US, Russia, and EU. Supply pressure can weigh on prices
- **August-September:** Spring wheat harvest. Northern hemisphere supply cycle completing
- **Southern hemisphere:** Australia harvests in November-January, Argentina in December-January, providing counter-seasonal supply

## Geopolitical Significance

Wheat occupies a unique position among commodities as a **food security staple**. Governments treat wheat supply as a national security issue:

- **Russia's invasion of Ukraine (2022):** CBOT wheat hit $13.63/bushel (all-time high), the Black Sea Grain Initiative was brokered and later collapsed, and wheat became a geopolitical weapon
- **Arab Spring (2010-2011):** Wheat price spikes (partly from Russian export ban after drought) contributed to food price protests across the Middle East
- **India export ban (2022):** Surprise ban on wheat exports removed ~5 million tonnes of expected supply from the global market
- **Russia export taxes/quotas:** Russia regularly adjusts export taxes and quotas to manage domestic food prices, adding policy uncertainty to the market

The "wheat weapon" -- the use of food exports as geopolitical leverage -- is a recurring theme in agricultural geopolitics. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Cash Market, Basis, and Physical Delivery

Wheat is a physically-delivered contract, which ties futures to the cash grain market:

- **Basis** = local cash price - nearby futures. It reflects local supply/demand, transport (rail/barge to export terminals), and storage availability. A strong (positive) basis signals local scarcity or strong export demand; a weak (negative) basis signals a glut. Basis trading and the convergence of cash to futures at delivery underpin [[grain-futures-basis-arbitrage]].
- **Delivery mechanics:** SRW (ZW) delivers against registered warehouse receipts at approved Toledo/Ohio-river and Mississippi-river shipping stations; HRW (KE) and Spring (MWE) have their own delivery geographies. Delivery quality differentials (protein, test weight, falling number) are settled via premiums and discounts.
- **Storage and the carry:** Whether the futures curve covers full carrying charges determines whether commercials store grain or sell it -- the core decision behind [[grain-futures-basis-arbitrage]] and seasonal storage hedges.

## Stocks-to-Use and the Balance Sheet

The single most important fundamental number in any grain market is the **ending stocks-to-use ratio** -- ending inventory divided by total annual use. The [[usda-data|WASDE]] balance sheet flows as: `beginning stocks + production + imports = total supply`, and `domestic use + exports + ending stocks = total demand`. Ending stocks is the residual that markets fixate on:

| Global wheat stocks-to-use | Market read | Typical price posture |
|----------------------------|-------------|------------------------|
| Below 25% | Tight | Risk premium; sensitive to any supply shock |
| 25-35% | Balanced | Range-bound, weather-driven |
| Above 35% | Surplus | Heavy; rallies sell off |

Because wheat is grown nearly everywhere, the *global* stocks-to-use (driven heavily by China's large but largely captive reserves) can mask tightness in the *exportable* balance -- the stocks held by major exporters (US, Russia, EU, Canada, Australia, Argentina). Traders increasingly watch a **major-exporter stocks-to-use** alongside the headline global figure, since China's stocks rarely reach the world market.

## Spread Trading

Wheat offers an unusually rich set of relative-value structures because it trades as three separate futures (SRW/HRW/Spring) against each other and against [[corn]]:

| Spread | Legs | What it measures | Tightens / widens when |
|--------|------|------------------|------------------------|
| **Wheat-corn** | ZW - [[corn\|ZC]] | Wheat's premium to corn (feed substitution) | Tightens (toward feed parity) when wheat is cheap; widens on wheat-specific supply scares |
| **KC-Chicago (Kansas-Chicago)** | KE - ZW | HRW premium to SRW (protein / bread-wheat scarcity) | Widens when protein is scarce or Plains weather is adverse |
| **Minneapolis-Chicago** | MWE - ZW | Spring (high-protein) premium to SRW | Widens on Northern Plains drought / late planting |
| **Old-crop / new-crop calendar** | Jul (N) vs Dec (Z) | Carrying charge; old vs new crop tightness | Inverts when old-crop supply is scarce |
| **Wheat-Kansas-Minneapolis fly** | ZW / KE / MWE | The full protein curve | Used to express a pure quality view |

- **Feed substitution floor:** When SRW wheat trades at less than roughly a $0.50 premium to [[corn]], feed users begin substituting wheat into livestock rations, providing a demand floor. Wide premiums ($2.00+) signal wheat-specific supply concerns rather than a general grain rally.
- **Carrying charges and the calendar curve:** A full-carry market (deferred contracts at a premium covering storage + interest) signals ample supply and incentivizes storage; an inverted curve (nearby above deferred) signals immediate scarcity. These dynamics underpin [[grain-futures-basis-arbitrage]] and [[seasonal-spread-trading]].

## Trading Considerations

- **Three contracts, three markets:** SRW, HRW, and Spring wheat trade independently with different fundamentals. Most global commentary references SRW (ZW)
- **Limit moves:** $0.35/bushel daily limit ($1,750/contract), expanding after a limit day
- **Geopolitical headlines:** Wheat can gap on weekend geopolitical developments more than most commodities
- **Less liquid than corn/soybeans:** Lower open interest and volume, wider spreads, especially in deferred months
- **COT positioning:** [[cot-report-analysis|Commitment of Traders]] data is essential; managed money has historically been net short wheat more often than corn or soybeans

## Advantages of Trading Wheat

- Strong fundamental framework with extensive public data ([[usda-data|USDA]], IGC)
- Geopolitical events create large, tradeable moves
- Multiple classes and spread opportunities
- Two distinct growing seasons per year provide recurring trade setups
- Well-documented seasonal patterns

## Disadvantages and Risks

- Geopolitical risk is difficult to model or predict
- Multiple wheat classes complicate analysis (what's good for SRW may not apply to HRW)
- Less liquid than [[corn]] or [[soybeans]]
- Russia/Ukraine supply disruptions can be sudden and extreme
- Government intervention (export bans, price controls) can override fundamentals
- Quality variability within a single crop year adds complexity
