# Gasoline

RBOB Gasoline (Reformulated Blendstock for Oxygenate Blending) futures are traded on [[cme-group|NYMEX]] under ticker **RB**. Gasoline is the highest-volume refined petroleum product globally and the key component of the [[crack-spread]] (the "2" in the 3:2:1 ratio). Its price is one of the most politically sensitive commodity prices, directly affecting consumer sentiment and election cycles. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## What Is RBOB?

RBOB is the wholesale gasoline blendstock before ethanol is added. Refineries produce RBOB, which is then blended with 10% ethanol (E10) to create the finished gasoline sold at retail pumps. The NYMEX RB contract represents this pre-blended product. Key characteristics:

- **Summer-grade vs winter-grade**: EPA mandates lower Reid Vapor Pressure (RVP) gasoline in summer months (June 1 - September 15) to reduce smog. Summer-grade gasoline is more expensive to produce, creating a seasonal supply squeeze.
- **Regional variations**: Different U.S. regions have different fuel formulation requirements (California's CARB standards are the strictest), creating regional basis differentials.
- **Ethanol blending**: The Renewable Fuel Standard (RFS) mandates ethanol blending, linking gasoline prices to corn/ethanol markets.

## Key Price Drivers

- **[[crude-oil]] prices**: Gasoline is a refined product of crude; crude oil cost represents ~50-60% of retail gasoline price
- **Seasonal demand**: Driving season (Memorial Day through Labor Day) represents peak gasoline consumption in the U.S.
- **Summer/winter blend transition**: The switch to summer-grade gasoline (typically February-May) tightens supply and lifts prices
- **Refinery utilization**: Spring maintenance turnarounds reduce gasoline output just as summer blend requirements kick in — a classic supply squeeze
- **[[eia]] inventory data**: Weekly gasoline stock levels in the petroleum status report (see [[inventory-cycle-analysis]])
- **Consumer behavior**: Gasoline demand is relatively price-inelastic in the short term but responds to sustained high prices
- **EV adoption**: Long-term structural headwind as electric vehicle penetration grows, though the timeline is measured in decades

## Seasonality

Gasoline has the most well-known and tradeable seasonal pattern in commodities:

| Period | Pattern | Driver |
|--------|---------|--------|
| February-April | Prices strengthen ("spring rally") | Refineries switch to costlier summer blend; maintenance reduces output |
| May (Memorial Day) | Seasonal peak begins | Driving season officially starts |
| June-August | Peak demand, elevated prices | Summer driving, vacation travel |
| September (Labor Day) | Demand fades | End of driving season, switch back to winter blend |
| October-January | Prices tend to soften | Lower demand, cheaper winter-grade gasoline |

The **gasoline-heating oil spread** (RB-HO) is a classic seasonal trade: gasoline outperforms [[heating-oil]] from approximately March through August, then underperforms from September through February. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## How It Trades

### Futures and Options
- **RB futures**: Primary contract, 42,000 gallons, physically delivered at New York Harbor
- **Options on RB**: Active market, used for hedging and directional strategies
- **Calendar spreads**: Trade the summer premium vs winter discount directly

### Spread Strategies
- **[[crack-spread]]**: The 3:2:1 crack (3 crude : 2 gasoline : 1 heating oil) isolates refining margins
- **Gasoline crack (1:1)**: Simplified version — long 1 crude, short 1 gasoline
- **RB-HO spread**: Seasonal rotation between gasoline and [[heating-oil]]
- **[[seasonal-spread-trading]]**: Calendar spreads capturing the spring rally

### ETFs
- **UGA** (United States Gasoline Fund): Tracks front-month RBOB futures; suffers from [[contango]] roll costs
- Energy sector ETFs (XLE, XOP) provide indirect gasoline exposure through refiner equities

## Political Sensitivity

Gasoline prices are among the most politically visible commodity prices:

- U.S. presidents have historically released oil from the Strategic Petroleum Reserve (SPR) to combat high gasoline prices ahead of elections
- "Pain at the pump" drives consumer sentiment surveys and can influence Federal Reserve policy considerations
- Fuel taxes and subsidies vary by jurisdiction and can be adjusted for political purposes
- Retail gasoline prices are published daily and tracked obsessively by media

## Advantages

- Strong, well-documented seasonal patterns provide recurring trade opportunities
- Deep liquidity with active options markets on NYMEX
- Fundamental drivers are well-understood and publicly reported (weekly [[eia]] data)
- [[crack-spread]] provides lower-risk relative value trading vs outright directional exposure

## Disadvantages

- Large contract size (~$2.80/gallon x 42,000 gallons = ~$118K notional) requires significant capital
- Summer/winter blend transitions can create unpredictable supply disruptions
- Geopolitical events and government intervention (SPR releases, export bans) can overwhelm fundamental analysis
- Long-term structural demand decline from EV adoption, though timeframe is uncertain
