Ethanol is an exchange-traded agricultural-energy [[commodities|commodity]]: denatured fuel-grade ethyl alcohol, distilled mostly from corn in the United States and blended into gasoline. It trades as a [[futures]] contract (CME/CBOT ticker **EH**) and sits at the intersection of the grain complex (its feedstock is [[corn]]) and the energy complex (its end use is motor fuel), which makes it a hybrid "ag-energy" market driven by both.

## What Ethanol Futures Are

Fuel ethanol is the alcohol blended into US gasoline, almost universally at 10% by volume ("E10"), with higher blends (E15, E85) used in some vehicles. Nearly all US gasoline at the pump contains ethanol, so ethanol demand is effectively a derivative of gasoline demand plus blending policy. Producers ("ethanol plants," largely in the US Corn Belt) buy corn, ferment and distill it into ethanol, and sell the co-product distillers' dried grains (DDGS) as animal feed. Futures let producers, blenders, refiners, and speculators hedge or take a view on the ethanol price.

## Price Drivers

Ethanol is unusual in being pulled by two different complexes at once. The main drivers:

| Driver | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| **[[corn]] prices** | Corn is the feedstock; roughly 2.8 gallons of ethanol per bushel of corn. Corn is the single largest input cost, so ethanol and corn prices are tightly linked. |
| **[[crude-oil]] / [[gasoline]]** | Ethanol substitutes for gasoline as an octane and volume extender; gasoline and crude set the demand-side value. |
| **EPA Renewable Fuel Standard (RFS) / RIN credits** | Federal mandate requiring blenders to mix set volumes of renewable fuel. Compliance is tracked via tradable **RINs** (Renewable Identification Numbers); RIN prices materially affect blending economics. |
| **Blending mandates & waivers** | E10/E15 policy, small-refinery exemptions, and seasonal Reid Vapor Pressure (RVP) rules shift demand. |
| **Export demand** | The US is a major ethanol exporter (Brazil, Canada, etc.); foreign demand and Brazil's sugarcane ethanol supply matter. |
| **Natural gas & operating costs** | Distillation is energy-intensive; plant margins depend on fuel costs. |

### The Corn Crush Spread

The defining trade in ethanol is the **corn crush spread** (or "ethanol crush"), analogous to the soybean crush or the energy crack spread. It captures the gross processing margin of an ethanol plant:

$$\text{Ethanol Crush} \approx (\text{Ethanol price} \times \text{gallons per bushel}) + (\text{DDGS value}) - (\text{Corn price per bushel})$$

A widening crush spread means producers are profitable and likely to ramp production; a negative crush squeezes plants and can force run-cuts or idling. Traders express views on this margin by simultaneously trading [[corn]] futures against ethanol (EH) futures, hedging the input/output relationship rather than betting on either leg outright.

## Trading Relevance

- **Hedging:** Ethanol producers hedge output (sell EH) and input ([[corn]]); blenders and refiners hedge their RFS obligations using a mix of EH and RIN markets.
- **Spread trading:** The corn crush and ethanol-vs-gasoline spreads are the most common professional ethanol trades, since outright ethanol is relatively illiquid compared to corn or crude.
- **Liquidity caveat:** EH futures are far less liquid than [[corn]], [[crude-oil]], or [[gasoline]] (RBOB). Many participants gain ethanol exposure indirectly through the corn and gasoline complexes rather than trading EH directly.
- **Policy sensitivity:** Ethanol is one of the most policy-driven commodities. EPA rulings on blending volumes, RIN prices, small-refinery exemptions, and year-round E15 approval can move the market far more than supply/demand fundamentals.
