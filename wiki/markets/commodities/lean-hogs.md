Lean Hogs futures (HE) trade on the [[cme-group|CME]] and represent the pork market's primary hedging and speculation tool. Unlike [[live-cattle]], lean hogs have a **shorter production cycle** (~6 months vs 2+ years for cattle), which means supply can respond much faster to price signals. HE futures are **cash-settled** (no physical delivery), which simplifies trading but introduces basis risk against the CME Lean Hog Index (a 2-day weighted average of negotiated cash prices). The US is the world's third-largest pork producer after China and the EU. China's pork demand -- the world's largest -- is a key swing factor in global hog markets. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Production Cycle

The hog production cycle is biologically shorter and more manageable than the [[live-cattle|cattle cycle]]:

| Stage | Duration | Details |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **Gestation** | ~114 days (3 months, 3 weeks, 3 days) | Sows farrow (give birth) to litters of 10-14 piglets |
| **Nursery** | ~6 weeks | Weaned piglets grow to ~50 lbs |
| **Growing/Finishing** | ~16-20 weeks | Pigs are raised to market weight (~280 lbs) on [[corn]]-based rations |
| **Total cycle** | ~6 months | From breeding decision to market-ready hog |

This 6-month cycle (vs 2+ years for cattle) means:
- Producers can **expand or contract supply** much faster
- Price cycles are shorter and more volatile
- The market is more responsive to changes in feed costs and demand signals

### Vertical Integration

US hog production is highly **vertically integrated** compared to the cattle industry:
- Large corporate producers (Smithfield/WH Group, Tyson, JBS, Hormel) own or contract the breeding, growing, finishing, and packing operations
- This integration means supply decisions are made centrally by a few large companies
- The "spot" or negotiated cash market has shrunk dramatically as more hogs move under formula pricing contracts

## US Pork Industry

### Production

- The US produces ~120 million head annually (~27 billion lbs of pork)
- Iowa, Minnesota, North Carolina, Illinois, and Indiana are the top producing states
- Iowa alone produces ~33% of US hogs

### Exports

- The US exports approximately 25-27% of its pork production
- Top export destinations: Mexico, Japan, China/Hong Kong, South Korea, Canada
- Export demand is a critical marginal price driver

## The China Factor

China consumes roughly half the world's pork, making its domestic market the most important global variable:

- **African Swine Fever (ASF) 2018-2019:** A devastating epidemic killed an estimated 40-50% of China's hog herd (~200 million pigs). This caused:
  - Chinese pork prices to triple domestically
  - Massive increases in US, EU, and Brazilian pork imports
  - US hog prices surged on export demand
  - Global protein trade flows were restructured
- **Herd rebuilding (2020-2023):** China aggressively rebuilt its herd, overshot, and then faced a domestic surplus that depressed Chinese pork prices
- **Ongoing cyclicality:** China's domestic hog cycle creates waves of import demand and self-sufficiency that ripple through global pork and feed markets
- **Soybean link:** Chinese hog production drives [[soybeans|soybean]] meal import demand (feed), connecting the hog cycle to the soybean complex

## Key Reports and Data

| Report | Frequency | Why It Matters |
|--------|-----------|----------------|
| **USDA Hogs and Pigs** | Quarterly (late Mar, Jun, Sep, Dec) | The most important hog report. Breeding herd, market hog inventory, pig crop, farrowing intentions |
| **Weekly Slaughter** | Weekly | Head count; indicates current supply flow |
| **Cold Storage** | Monthly | Pork in cold storage; demand pipeline indicator |
| **USDA Pork Cutout** | Daily | Wholesale pork prices by primal cut |
| **CME Lean Hog Index** | Daily | Cash settlement price; 2-day weighted average |
| **Export Sales** | Weekly | US pork export commitments |
| **COT Report** | Weekly | [[cot-report-analysis|Speculative and commercial positioning]] |

The **Hogs and Pigs report** is the single most market-moving event for lean hog futures. Surprises in the breeding herd or pig crop figures can cause 3-5% moves on the release day.

## Price Drivers

1. **Hogs and Pigs report:** Quarterly breeding herd and pig crop data set supply expectations for the next 6-12 months
2. **Seasonal demand:** Summer grilling season (May-August) increases pork demand; holiday hams (November-December)
3. **Feed costs ([[corn]], [[soybeans|soybean meal]]):** Feed represents ~55-65% of hog production costs. High feed prices reduce profitability and eventually contract the herd
4. **Export demand:** Mexico and Japan are steady buyers; China swings between large importer and self-sufficient depending on its domestic cycle
5. **Disease (ASF, PRRS, PED):** Disease outbreaks can devastate herds. ASF has not reached the US, but PRRS (Porcine Reproductive and Respiratory Syndrome) and PED (Porcine Epidemic Diarrhea) periodically reduce US pig crops
6. **Packer margins:** Spread between hog prices and pork cutout values; similar to cattle packer margin dynamics
7. **Competing proteins:** Chicken production (faster, cheaper) competes directly with pork at the retail level
8. **Seasonality of production:** Sow productivity tends to be highest in spring (larger litters) and lowest in summer (heat stress reduces conception rates)

## Seasonal Patterns

Lean hogs follow well-documented [[commodity-seasonality-patterns|seasonal patterns]]:

- **January-March:** Winter demand softens. Hog supply from fall farrowing enters the market. Prices often at seasonal lows
- **April-May:** Pre-grilling season. Demand builds; prices begin to rally
- **June-August:** Peak demand (grilling season). Prices tend to be strongest. Summer heat stress can also reduce sow productivity, tightening supply 6 months later
- **September-October:** Post-summer demand decline. Large marketings from spring pig crop. Prices typically weaken
- **November-December:** Holiday ham demand provides some support; but overall seasonal weakness

The 15-year seasonal average shows lean hog prices peak in June-July and trough in November-January. The seasonal range can be $0.15-$0.25/lb, making it one of the more pronounced seasonal patterns in commodities. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Cash Settlement and Basis

Unlike most commodity futures that are physically delivered, lean hogs are **cash-settled** against the CME Lean Hog Index:

- The Index is a 2-day weighted average of negotiated and formula-based cash hog prices
- Cash settlement eliminates delivery logistics but introduces **basis risk** -- the difference between local cash prices and the Index
- As more hogs trade under formula contracts (rather than negotiated), the cash market used to calculate the Index has become thinner, raising questions about price discovery
- Basis patterns vary by region and packing plant proximity

## Trading Considerations

- **Hogs and Pigs report:** The most important event. Set alerts for quarterly release dates (late March, June, September, December). Pre-report positioning and post-report reaction dominate trading
- **Seasonal trades:** The April-July rally and September-November decline are well-documented seasonal setups
- **Spread trading:** Hog-corn spread (profitability proxy), inter-month calendar spreads, and hog-cattle spread all offer opportunities
- **Volatility:** Lean hogs can be extremely volatile, with 3-5% daily moves common around report days and seasonal transitions
- **Limit moves:** $0.0475/lb ($1,900/contract)
- **Options:** Active options market on HE; useful for event-driven strategies around Hogs and Pigs reports
- **Lower open interest:** Lean hogs are less liquid than grain or energy markets; trade in size with caution

## Related Spreads

Lean hogs are rarely traded in isolation by professionals; the contract sits inside a web of inter-commodity and calendar relationships. These spreads isolate a single driver (profitability, protein substitution, or seasonality) and typically carry lower margin than outright positions.

| Spread | Construction | What it expresses | Driver |
|--------|--------------|-------------------|--------|
| **Hog-corn (feeding) spread** | Long HE vs short [[corn]] | Hog producer gross margin proxy | Feed cost vs hog value; high corn squeezes margins and eventually contracts the herd |
| **Hog crush / spot crush** | HE value minus weighted [[corn]] + [[soybeans|soybean meal]] feed cost | Full feeding economics ("crush" analog to the [[soybean-crush-spread|soybean crush]]) | Profitability of finishing a hog; turns negative before herd liquidation |
| **Hog-cattle (protein) spread** | HE vs [[live-cattle]] (LE) | Relative pork/beef value | Substitution at retail, divergent supply cycles |
| **Calendar (inter-month) spread** | e.g., long Jun HE vs short Dec HE | [[seasonality|Seasonal]] demand and supply timing | Grilling-season strength vs winter weakness; carry |
| **Bull/bear calendar** | Front vs deferred within a season | Near-term tightness vs deferred supply | Hogs & Pigs supply outlook |

### Hog-Corn Spread in Detail

Because feed is ~55-65% of production cost, the **hog-corn ratio** (hog price ÷ corn price, in comparable units) is the classic profitability gauge, analogous to the [[hog-corn-ratio|hog-corn ratio]] watched since the 19th-century Corn Belt. When the ratio is high (cheap corn, expensive hogs), producers expand the breeding herd; ~10-12 months later that expansion floods the market and depresses HE — a self-correcting [[commodity-seasonality-patterns|cycle]]. Traders use the spread both as a fundamental signal and as a lower-variance directional position. See [[soybean-crush-spread]] and [[crack-spread]] for the same "processor margin" logic in other complexes.

## Historical Price Events

- **2014 PED virus:** Porcine Epidemic Diarrhea killed millions of piglets, sending hog prices from $0.80 to $1.33/lb -- one of the largest hog rallies in history
- **2018-2019 ASF in China:** US hog prices rallied from $0.45 to $0.85 as Chinese import demand surged following African Swine Fever's devastation of the Chinese herd
- **2020 COVID Packing Plant Closures:** Multiple US packing plants closed due to COVID outbreaks, causing a simultaneous cash price collapse (hogs couldn't be processed) and futures rally (forward supply disruption). The cash-futures disconnect was extreme
- **2022 Seasonal Peak:** Hog prices reached $1.22/lb on tight supply and strong summer demand

## Advantages of Trading Lean Hogs

- Short production cycle (6 months) means supply data translates to price outcomes relatively quickly
- Well-defined seasonal patterns provide recurring trade opportunities
- Cash settlement simplifies trading (no physical delivery logistics)
- Quarterly Hogs and Pigs report provides clear supply framework
- Feed cost linkage creates analyzable spread relationships with [[corn]] and [[soybeans]]

## Disadvantages and Risks

- High volatility; 3-5% daily moves are common
- Thin cash market raises price discovery concerns
- Vertical integration means a few large producers dominate supply decisions
- Disease risk (ASF reaching the US would be catastrophic)
- Less liquid than grain or energy markets
- Chinese demand is unpredictable and policy-driven
- Cash-futures basis can be volatile and difficult to hedge

## Who Trades Lean Hogs and How

- **Hedgers (producers/packers):** Smithfield/WH Group, Tyson, JBS, Hormel and large integrators short HE to lock in margins; packers may use the spread between HE and the pork cutout. Most physical hogs now move under formula contracts referencing the [[usda-data|USDA]] cutout or the Lean Hog Index rather than negotiated cash.
- **Commercial feed buyers:** Use the hog-corn and crush spreads to hedge feeding economics against [[corn]] and [[soybeans]].
- **Speculators / managed money:** CTAs and macro funds trade the [[commodity-seasonality-patterns|seasonal]] pattern and the quarterly Hogs & Pigs surprise; positioning is visible in the weekly [[cot-report-analysis|COT report]].
- **Spread traders:** Run hog-cattle, hog-corn, and calendar spreads (see [[seasonal-spread-trading]]) for lower-variance exposure.
- **Options traders:** Use the active HE options market to position around report risk without taking unlimited directional exposure.

Liquidity is thinner than [[corn]], [[crude-oil|energy]], or equity index futures, so slippage and gap risk around reports are material — size accordingly and prefer limit orders.
