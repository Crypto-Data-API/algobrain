# Iron Ore

Iron ore is Australia's largest commodity export by value and a major driver of the national economy and the [[australian-dollar]]. The iron ore market is dominated by a small number of producers, with [[bhp]], Rio Tinto, and Fortescue Metals controlling a significant share of global seaborne supply. Fred discusses iron ore in the context of sector analysis and Australian market exposure, noting that iron ore price swings flow directly through to ASX materials sector earnings and broader market sentiment. China is the dominant buyer of Australian iron ore, making Chinese construction and infrastructure spending the single most important demand variable for the commodity.

## How Iron Ore Is Traded

Iron ore is not exchange-traded in the traditional sense like [[copper]] or [[aluminum]] on the LME. Instead, it is priced via:

- **SGX (Singapore Exchange) Futures**: The most liquid financial iron ore contract, cash-settled against the Platts 62% Fe fines CFR China index
- **Dalian Commodity Exchange (DCE)**: China's domestic iron ore futures, denominated in RMB
- **Platts/S&P Global Benchmarks**: The industry-standard physical pricing reference is the Platts IODEX — the 62% Fe fines CFR (Cost and Freight) China price, quoted in USD per dry metric tonne
- **[[cme-group|CME]] Iron Ore**: CME also offers iron ore futures, though with lower liquidity than SGX

The benchmark "62% Fe" refers to iron content — higher grades (65% Fe) command premiums, lower grades (58% Fe) trade at discounts.

As of 2026-06-11 the 62% Fe CFR China benchmark traded around **US$101.60/dry metric tonne**, with demand soft/steady — Chinese steel demand remained constrained by a weak property sector and only a limited 2026 recovery (Source: Perplexity sonar / Trading Economics & worldsteel SRO April 2026).

## Supply: The Big Four

The seaborne iron ore market is dominated by four producers who collectively control approximately 70% of global seaborne trade:

| Company | Country | Annual Output | Notes |
|---------|---------|--------------|-------|
| Vale | Brazil | ~300 Mt | Largest single producer; Carajas high-grade ore |
| [[bhp]] | Australia | ~280 Mt | Pilbara operations |
| Rio Tinto | Australia | ~330 Mt | Pilbara operations; largest by volume |
| Fortescue | Australia | ~190 Mt | Lower grade (57-58% Fe); lowest cost |

This oligopolistic structure means supply is relatively disciplined — the Big Four have limited incentive to flood the market. New entrants face extremely high capital costs ($5-15 billion for a greenfield mine) and long development timelines.

## Demand: China Dominates

China consumes approximately 70% of the world's seaborne iron ore for steelmaking. This extreme concentration of demand on a single country means that **Chinese economic data is the single most important set of indicators** for iron ore:

- **Chinese steel production data**: Monthly output from the National Bureau of Statistics
- **Property and infrastructure investment**: China's property sector (construction starts, completions, floor space) and infrastructure stimulus directly drive steel demand
- **Steel mill profitability**: When mills are profitable (hot-rolled coil price minus iron ore and coking [[coal]] costs), they produce more and buy more ore; when margins compress, they cut back
- **Port inventories**: Iron ore stockpiles at Chinese ports (tracked weekly) are a key indicator of near-term supply-demand balance. Rising inventories = bearish; declining = bullish

## Price Dynamics

Iron ore is highly cyclical and tracks China's economic stimulus cycles closely:

- **Stimulus-on**: When China launches infrastructure or property stimulus, iron ore prices surge (sometimes 50-100% in a year)
- **Stimulus-off**: During property sector downturns or environmental production curbs, prices can collapse rapidly
- **Cost floor**: The marginal cost of production (high-cost Chinese domestic mines and smaller seaborne producers) provides a floor — typically $60-80/t for 62% Fe CFR China

The iron ore price has historically exhibited large swings: from $190/t in 2011 to $40/t in 2015, back to $220/t in 2021, and significant volatility thereafter — driven almost entirely by Chinese demand expectations.

## Key Indicators

- Chinese monthly crude steel output
- Chinese property sector data (new starts, completions, investment)
- Chinese port iron ore inventories
- Steel mill profit margins (hot-rolled coil minus inputs)
- Australian and Brazilian export data
- Chinese PMI (official and Caixin)
