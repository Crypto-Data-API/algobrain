# Heating Oil

Heating oil is a refined [[crude-oil|petroleum product]] traded on [[cme-group|NYMEX]] under ticker **HO**. It is a critical component of the energy complex, serving as a key input in the [[crack-spread]] (3:2:1 ratio: 3 barrels of crude yield approximately 2 barrels of [[gasoline]] and 1 barrel of heating oil/diesel). The contract has evolved from pure heating oil to Ultra-Low Sulfur Diesel (ULSD) to reflect regulatory changes, but the "heating oil" name persists in market parlance. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## What It Is

Heating oil and diesel fuel are chemically similar — both are distillate fuel oils derived from the middle of the crude oil distillation process. The NYMEX HO contract is used as:

- **Heating fuel**: Residential heating in the U.S. Northeast (the largest consuming region), where ~5.3 million households rely on heating oil
- **Diesel proxy**: ULSD is the primary transportation diesel fuel; the HO contract is the primary hedge for diesel exposure
- **Jet fuel proxy**: Jet fuel (kerosene) is closely correlated with heating oil; airlines frequently hedge using HO futures
- **Crack spread component**: The "1" in the 3:2:1 [[crack-spread]] represents heating oil/distillate

## Key Price Drivers

- **Seasonal demand**: Winter heating season (November-March) drives peak demand, especially during cold snaps in the U.S. Northeast. A single polar vortex event can spike prices 10-20% in days.
- **[[crude-oil]] prices**: As a refined product, heating oil fundamentally tracks crude with the refining margin ([[crack-spread]]) overlaid
- **Refinery utilization**: When refineries go into maintenance turnaround (typically spring and fall), distillate supply tightens
- **[[eia]] inventory data**: Weekly petroleum status report includes distillate stockpiles — deviations from expectations move the market (see [[inventory-cycle-analysis]])
- **Diesel demand**: Global economic activity drives diesel consumption (trucking, shipping, industrial)
- **Regulations**: IMO 2020 shipping fuel rules increased demand for ULSD; environmental regulations affect refinery output mix
- **Geopolitics**: European diesel market was severely disrupted by Russia-Ukraine conflict and sanctions on Russian refined products (Russia was Europe's largest diesel supplier)

## Seasonality

Heating oil exhibits the strongest seasonality in the energy complex:

| Period | Pattern |
|--------|---------|
| October-November | Pre-winter inventory building; prices tend to strengthen |
| December-February | Peak heating season; cold weather = demand spikes |
| March-April | Shoulder season; heating demand fades, prices soften |
| May-August | Low season; refinery maintenance and summer focus on gasoline |
| September | Inventory rebuilding begins ahead of winter |

The **heating oil-gasoline spread** (HO-RB) has a strong seasonal pattern: heating oil outperforms gasoline from September through February, then gasoline outperforms from March through August. This is a classic [[seasonal-spread-trading]] setup. (Source: [[2026-04-14-commodities-research-framework]])

## Trading Strategies

- **[[crack-spread]]**: Long crude / short heating oil and gasoline to trade refining margins
- **[[seasonal-spread-trading]]**: Long heating oil / short gasoline ahead of winter; reverse for summer
- **Calendar spreads**: Front-month vs deferred heating oil — trades winter premium vs shoulder-month prices
- **Cross-commodity**: Heating oil vs [[natural-gas]] as competing heating fuels (Northeast switching economics)

## Advantages

- Strongest seasonality among energy commodities provides recurring trade setups
- Deep liquidity on NYMEX with active options markets
- Well-defined fundamental drivers (weather, inventories, refinery runs) that are publicly reported
- Useful as a hedge for diesel-exposed businesses (trucking, shipping, airlines)

## Disadvantages

- Large contract size ($2.50/gallon x 42,000 gallons = ~$105K notional) requires significant capital
- Weather-driven volatility can be extreme and unpredictable
- The Northeast U.S. heating market is gradually shifting to natural gas and heat pumps, creating long-term structural demand decline
- Physical delivery at New York Harbor can create basis risk for hedgers in other regions
