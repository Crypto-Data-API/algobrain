# Copper

Copper is a base metal commodity widely considered a barometer of global economic health, earning the nickname "Dr. Copper" — so called because it has a PhD in economics. Copper prices correlate strongly with global GDP growth and manufacturing activity, making it one of the most-watched leading indicators among macro traders.

Fred discusses copper as a key driver in the materials sector, notably highlighting that bhp's sole motivation for its bid on Anglo American was to acquire its copper assets. Copper is classified as a critical transition metal for the net-zero energy transition, with demand expected to grow substantially due to electric vehicles, renewable energy infrastructure, and grid upgrades. Australia is a significant copper producer, making copper prices relevant to [[australian-dollar]] movements and ASX-listed miners.

## How Copper Is Traded

- **LME**: The [[london-metal-exchange|London Metal Exchange]] is the global benchmark for copper pricing. LME copper contracts are quoted in USD per tonne with standard lots of 25 tonnes.
- **COMEX/CME**: The [[cme-group|CME Group]] HG (High Grade) copper futures contract is the primary US venue, quoted in USD per pound. Each contract represents 25,000 pounds.
- **SHFE**: Shanghai Futures Exchange for Chinese domestic trading.
- **Physical Market**: Traded at exchange price plus regional premiums reflecting local supply-demand and logistics.

## Supply

Chile (~27%) and Peru (~10%) are the largest copper producers, together accounting for over a third of global mine supply. China consumes approximately 55% of global copper supply, making Chinese demand the dominant price driver.

| Producer | Share | Notes |
|----------|-------|-------|
| Chile | ~27% | Codelco (state-owned) is the single largest producer |
| Peru | ~10% | Second-largest; growing output |
| Congo (DRC) | ~10% | Rapid growth; Kamoa-Kakula mine |
| China | ~8% | Mostly smelting imported concentrate |
| Australia | ~5% | Relevant to [[australian-dollar]] and ASX miners |

**Supply constraints** are a key long-term theme:
- **Declining ore grades**: Average copper ore grades have fallen from ~1.5% in the 1990s to ~0.6% today, requiring more rock to be processed per tonne of copper
- **Long lead times**: New mine development takes 5-10 years from discovery to first production
- **Environmental permitting**: Increasingly stringent environmental and community approval processes delay projects
- **Water scarcity**: Many major copper deposits are in arid regions (Chile's Atacama Desert, Peru)

## EV and Energy Transition Demand

The EV transition and renewable energy buildout are creating structural demand growth for copper:

- **EVs use 3-4x more copper than ICE vehicles** (~80 kg vs ~23 kg per vehicle) — wiring, motors, battery connectors, charging infrastructure
- **Solar panels**: Each MW of solar capacity requires ~5 tonnes of copper
- **Wind turbines**: Onshore wind uses ~4 tonnes of copper per MW; offshore wind up to ~15 tonnes per MW
- **Grid infrastructure**: Electrification requires massive grid upgrades and expansion

This structural demand growth, combined with supply constraints, has led many analysts to forecast a significant copper deficit by the late 2020s — often called the "copper gap."

## Key Demand Indicator

Chinese PMI data — especially the **Caixin Manufacturing PMI** — is the most-watched demand indicator for copper. A reading above 50 signals manufacturing expansion and typically supports copper prices; below 50 signals contraction. Other closely watched indicators include Chinese copper imports, LME warehouse stocks, and global manufacturing PMI.
