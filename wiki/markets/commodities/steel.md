# Steel

Steel is an alloy of iron and carbon and the most widely used structural metal in the world, with global crude steel production of roughly **1.8-1.9 billion tonnes annually**. As a traded commodity, steel is distinct from its raw input [[iron-ore|iron ore]]: it is a manufactured product whose price reflects the spread between input costs (iron ore, coking coal, scrap, energy) and finished-product demand from construction, automotive, and machinery. Steel derivatives are less liquid than base metals but have grown materially, led by **CME US Midwest Hot-Rolled Coil (HRC)** futures and Chinese rebar/HRC contracts on the Shanghai Futures Exchange.

## How Steel Is Traded

Steel trades both as physical product (via mills, service centers, and distributors) and through financial derivatives benchmarked to specific products:

| Venue | Contract | Notes |
|-------|----------|-------|
| **[[cme-group\|CME]]** | US Midwest HRC (ticker **HRC**) | The benchmark US steel future; cash-settled vs the CME US Midwest Domestic Hot-Rolled Coil Steel (CRU) Index |
| **SHFE** (Shanghai) | Rebar, Hot-Rolled Coil | China's benchmark steel derivatives; among the most liquid steel futures globally |
| **[[london-metal-exchange\|LME]]** | Steel scrap (Turkey CFR), steel rebar | Cash-settled regional contracts |

### CME HRC Contract Specs

- **Contract size**: 50 short tons
- **Quote**: USD per short ton
- **Settlement**: cash-settled against the CME US Midwest Domestic Hot-Rolled Coil Steel (CRU) Index
- **Use**: mills, service centers, and end-users hedge price risk; speculators trade the construction/auto demand cycle

## Production Routes

Two production technologies dominate, with very different cost structures and emissions profiles:

- **Blast Furnace / Basic Oxygen Furnace (BF-BOF)** — the integrated route. Smelts [[iron-ore|iron ore]] with **coking (metallurgical) coal** to produce pig iron, then converts to steel. Capital-intensive, carbon-heavy, dominant in China.
- **Electric Arc Furnace (EAF)** — melts **scrap steel** (and DRI/HBI) using large amounts of **electricity**. More flexible, lower-carbon, dominant in the US (Nucor, Steel Dynamics). EAF economics hinge on scrap and power prices.

The BF-BOF vs EAF split is a structural theme: decarbonization, scrap availability, and electricity costs are shifting capacity toward EAF, especially outside China.

## Inputs and the Steel Margin

Steel is a **spread** business. The price relationship between finished steel and its inputs is analogous to a refining crack:

- **BF-BOF margin** ≈ HRC price − (iron ore cost + coking coal cost)
- **EAF margin** ≈ HRC price − (scrap cost + electricity cost)

Key inputs: [[iron-ore|iron ore]], coking/metallurgical [[coal]], ferrous scrap, electricity, and alloying elements ([[nickel]], manganese, [[zinc]] for galvanizing). Watching iron ore and coking coal prices alongside HRC reveals whether mill margins are expanding or compressing.

## Demand Drivers

- **Construction & infrastructure** — the largest end-market; especially Chinese property and infrastructure, the single biggest macro swing factor for global steel prices.
- **Automotive** — flat-rolled steel for vehicle bodies; sensitive to auto production cycles.
- **Machinery, appliances, energy** (pipe/tube for oil & gas).
- **China property cycle** — China produces roughly **half of world steel output**, so Chinese demand, property starts, and stimulus dominate the global balance.

## Major Producers

- **China** — ~50%+ of global output; **Baowu** (China Baowu Steel Group) is the world's largest steelmaker.
- **ArcelorMittal** (Luxembourg/global), **Nippon Steel** (Japan), **POSCO** (South Korea), **Nucor** (US, EAF leader), **Steel Dynamics** (US, EAF).

## Trade Policy and Catalysts

- **US Section 232 tariffs** — the 2018 national-security steel tariffs remain in place in 2025-26, modified over time via quotas, exemptions, and country-specific deals. They support US domestic HRC prices relative to global benchmarks and are a recurring catalyst.
- **Chinese export flows and stimulus** — Chinese steel exports and property-sector stimulus move global prices sharply.
- **Antidumping/countervailing duties**, EU CBAM (carbon border adjustment), and capacity rationalization.
- **Energy and scrap shocks** — EAF-heavy regions are exposed to electricity price spikes.

## Trading Relevance

- Steel futures let producers, fabricators, and traders hedge or speculate on the **construction/auto demand cycle** directly, rather than through the [[iron-ore|iron ore]] proxy.
- The **iron-ore-to-HRC spread** (or coking-coal-to-HRC) is a tradeable proxy for mill profitability.
- US **HRC** is the most relevant Western contract; **SHFE rebar** is the key China-demand barometer and trades nearly around the clock with deep liquidity.
- Steel is highly cyclical and policy-sensitive (tariffs, Chinese stimulus), making it a macro/regime trade more than a technical one.
