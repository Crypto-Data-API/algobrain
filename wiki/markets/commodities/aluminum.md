# Aluminum

Aluminum (Al) is the most produced non-ferrous metal globally, with annual production exceeding 65 million tonnes. Often described as "solidified electricity" because smelting is extraordinarily energy-intensive, aluminum's cost structure is dominated by power costs rather than ore availability (bauxite is abundant). It is traded primarily on the [[london-metal-exchange|LME]], which sets the global benchmark price.

## Seasonality

Aluminum has weaker seasonality than agricultural commodities, but some patterns recur:

- **Q1 China winter curtailments** — heating-season power rationing and pollution controls in northern China can cut smelter output, supporting prices into spring.
- **Construction season (spring/summer)** — Northern-hemisphere building activity lifts demand for extrusions and sheet.
- **Hydro cycle** — Yunnan province (hydro-powered smelting) sees output swings with the dry season (winter, lower output) vs wet season (summer, higher output), affecting Chinese supply.
- **Energy-price linkage** — European winter power spikes can curtail EU smelters, a recurring seasonal supply risk since the 2022 energy crisis.

## How Aluminum Is Traded

- **LME**: The [[london-metal-exchange|London Metal Exchange]] is the primary trading venue. LME aluminum contracts are quoted in USD per tonne with standardized lots of 25 tonnes. Settlement includes physical delivery to LME-approved warehouses worldwide.
- **COMEX/CME**: [[cme-group|CME Group]] offers aluminum futures (ALI) as an alternative.
- **Shanghai Futures Exchange (SHFE)**: The dominant venue for Chinese domestic aluminum trading, often at a premium to LME due to Chinese import tariffs and VAT.
- **Physical Market**: Traded at LME price plus regional premium (e.g., Midwest Premium for US delivery).

## Supply

China produces approximately 60% of the world's primary aluminum, a dominance driven by access to cheap coal-fired power and large-scale smelter capacity. Other significant producers include India, Russia (Rusal), Canada (hydropower-based smelting), and the Gulf states (gas-powered smelting).

| Producer | Share | Energy Source |
|----------|-------|--------------|
| China | ~60% | Primarily coal |
| India | ~5% | Coal |
| Russia (Rusal) | ~5% | Hydro |
| Canada | ~5% | Hydro |
| Gulf States | ~5% | Natural gas |

Smelting requires approximately 13,000-15,000 kWh of electricity per tonne of aluminum produced. This makes electricity prices the single largest variable cost — smelters in regions with high power costs frequently curtail production, tightening supply. Chinese government policies on energy consumption, carbon emissions, and smelter capacity caps directly affect global supply.

## Price Drivers

- **Energy costs**: Electricity is the dominant input cost. Rising power prices (especially in Europe and China) can force smelter curtailments, reducing supply and supporting prices.
- **Chinese production policy**: Government-mandated smelter capacity caps (~45 million tonnes), environmental inspections, and power rationing directly affect global supply.
- **LME warehouse stocks**: Closely watched as a proxy for physical market tightness. Declining stocks generally bullish; rising stocks bearish.
- **Regional premiums**: The difference between LME price and delivered physical price varies by region and reflects local supply-demand dynamics and logistics costs.
- **Trade policy**: Tariffs, sanctions (e.g., 2018 Rusal sanctions), and export taxes can dislocate global markets.

## EV and Renewable Energy Tailwinds

The transition to EVs and renewable energy infrastructure is structurally supportive of aluminum demand:

- **EV lightweighting**: EVs use more aluminum than ICE vehicles to offset battery weight (battery enclosures, structural components)
- **Solar panel frames**: Aluminum frames are standard for solar panels
- **Grid infrastructure**: Aluminum is increasingly substituted for [[copper]] in power transmission where weight is a factor
