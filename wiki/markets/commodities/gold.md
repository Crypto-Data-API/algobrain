# Gold

Gold (XAU) is a precious metal that has served as a store of value for thousands of years and remains one of the most actively traded commodities in the world. It functions as a safe-haven asset, inflation hedge, and portfolio diversifier, with a daily trading volume exceeding $100 billion across spot, [[futures]], ETF, and OTC markets.

## How Gold Is Traded

- **Spot Market**: XAU/USD — gold priced in U.S. dollars per troy ounce. The most widely quoted gold price.
- **Futures**: COMEX gold futures (GC) on the CME Group are the primary derivatives contract, with each contract representing 100 troy ounces.
- **ETFs**: GLD (SPDR Gold Shares) and IAU (iShares Gold Trust) provide stock-market-accessible gold exposure.
- **Tokenized Gold**: PAXG (Pax Gold) and XAUT (Tether Gold) offer blockchain-based gold ownership, each token backed by one troy ounce of physical gold.
- **Mining Stocks**: Companies like Newmont and Barrick Gold offer leveraged exposure to gold prices.

## Price Drivers

- **Real Interest Rates**: Gold's primary driver. When real rates (nominal rates minus inflation) fall, gold becomes more attractive since it has no yield but no opportunity cost either. [[federal-reserve]] policy is therefore the dominant macro factor.
- **US Dollar Strength**: Gold is priced in dollars, so a weaker dollar typically supports higher gold prices (inverse correlation).
- **Geopolitical Risk and Crisis**: Gold rallies during wars, financial crises, and periods of uncertainty as investors seek safety.
- **Central Bank Buying**: Global central banks have been net buyers of gold since 2010, with purchases accelerating in recent years.

## Trading Relevance

Gold is a core component of [[ray-dalio]]'s all-weather portfolio and a standard hedge against [[tail-risk]] events. It tends to be uncorrelated with equities during normal periods and positively protective during crises. For traders, gold offers high [[liquidity]], extended trading hours, and sensitivity to macro events. Understanding the gold-rates-dollar triangle is essential for any [[macro-trading]] framework.

## Gold vs Silver

Silver outperforms gold in bull markets and falls further in bear markets. The gold-to-silver ratio above 80 historically signals a silver rally — it crossed 80 for the 4th time in early 2024. Silver has unique industrial demand (56% of supply vs 12% for gold) and declining stockpiles. (Source: [[alfred-report-gold-commodity]])

## COT Positioning

Gold [[cot-report-analysis|Commitment of Traders (COT)]] data is one of the most closely watched positioning datasets in all of commodities. The CFTC's weekly COT report breaks down COMEX gold futures positioning into key categories:

- **Commercial hedgers** (miners, jewelers, fabricators): Tend to be net short — they are hedging physical exposure. Extreme commercial short positions can signal that producers expect prices to fall.
- **Managed money** (hedge funds, CTAs): Tend to be trend-followers. Extreme speculative net long positions represent potential reversal risk — when "everyone" is long, there are fewer marginal buyers left.
- **Swap dealers**: Intermediaries whose positioning reflects client flows.

**Contrarian signals**: When managed money net longs reach extreme levels (above the 90th percentile of historical range), gold is statistically more likely to pull back. Conversely, when managed money is flat or net short, it often coincides with major bottoms. However, COT signals work better as confirmation tools than standalone timing indicators — extreme positioning can persist for weeks before reversing.

## Key Technical Indicators

Gold is one of the most technically traded markets, with several indicators widely followed:

- **200-day Moving Average**: The most-watched level for gold. Prices above the 200-day MA are considered bullish; below is bearish. The 200-day MA also acts as dynamic support/resistance, with bounces off the MA frequently triggering trend-following buying.
- **Fibonacci Retracement**: Widely used for entry levels during pullbacks in gold trends. The 38.2%, 50%, and 61.8% retracement levels from major swing moves are standard reference points.
- **[[rsi|RSI]] (Relative Strength Index)**: Used for overbought/oversold readings. Gold RSI above 70 suggests overbought conditions; below 30 suggests oversold. However, in strong trends, gold can remain overbought or oversold for extended periods.
- **Gold-to-Silver Ratio**: A relative value indicator comparing the price of one ounce of gold to one ounce of [[silver]]. The ratio historically averages around 60-65 but has ranged from 15 (1980 peak) to 120+ (2020 COVID). A high ratio (>80) historically signals silver is undervalued relative to gold.

Other key levels: round numbers ($2,000, $2,500, $3,000) act as psychological support/resistance. The [[lbma|LBMA Gold Price]] fix times (10:30 AM and 3:00 PM London) can create intraday volatility patterns.
