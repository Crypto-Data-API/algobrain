# Silver

Silver (XAG) is a precious and industrial metal that trades with significantly higher [[volatility]] than [[gold]], making it attractive to active traders. It occupies a unique position as both a **monetary metal** (store of value, like gold) and an **industrial commodity** (used in electronics, solar panels, and medical equipment). This dual identity is the single most important fact about silver: its price is pulled simultaneously by safe-haven/monetary flows and by the industrial business cycle, which is why silver is often described as "gold on leverage" — it tends to outrun gold in precious-metal bull markets and fall harder in busts.

## How Silver Is Traded

| Instrument | Venue / Ticker | Contract / Unit | Notes |
|------------|----------------|-----------------|-------|
| Spot | XAG/USD (OTC) | Troy ounce, USD | The reference price; quoted per oz |
| Futures (full) | [[comex|COMEX]] **SI** ([[cme-group\|CME Group]]) | 5,000 troy oz | The benchmark futures; a $1 move = $5,000 per contract |
| Futures (micro) | COMEX **SIL** | 1,000 troy oz | Smaller, more accessible for retail |
| Physical fix | [[lbma\|LBMA]] Silver Price | Daily auction | The London benchmark for physical settlement |
| ETF (physical) | **SLV** (iShares Silver Trust) | — | Most liquid silver ETF; holds allocated bars |
| ETF (leveraged) | AGQ (2x), ZSL (-2x) | — | Decay-prone; for short-term tactical use only |
| Mining equities | First Majestic, Pan American Silver, etc. | — | Operationally leveraged exposure |

Because the COMEX full contract represents 5,000 oz, even a modest per-ounce move translates to a large dollar P&L, and the high [[leverage]] of [[futures]] margin is a major reason silver positions can be volatile to hold.

## The Gold/Silver Ratio

The **[[gold-silver-ratio|gold/silver ratio]]** is the number of ounces of silver required to buy one ounce of gold (gold price ÷ silver price). It is the single most-watched relative-value metric in precious metals and the basis of a classic [[mean-reversion]] trade.

- **Historical range**: roughly 15:1 in the bimetallic-standard era, widening to a modern range that has frequently oscillated between about 60:1 and 90:1, with extreme spikes above 100:1 during acute risk-off shocks (e.g., the March 2020 liquidity crisis).
- **Interpretation**: a *high* ratio means silver is historically "cheap" relative to gold; a *low* ratio means silver is "rich." Because silver carries more industrial demand, the ratio tends to **compress** (silver outperforms) during economic expansions and reflationary precious-metal rallies, and **widen** (gold outperforms) during recessions and deflation scares.

**Worked example (illustrative numbers):** Suppose gold trades at $2,400/oz and silver at $30/oz. The ratio is 2,400 ÷ 30 = **80:1**. A trader who believes the ratio is stretched and will mean-revert toward, say, 65:1 can express the view as a *ratio spread* — go long silver and short an equivalent dollar amount of gold. If the ratio later falls to 65:1 (silver outperforming gold by ~23% on a relative basis), the spread profits regardless of whether the absolute price of precious metals rises or falls. The trade is market-neutral to the gold price and isolates the relative-value view. The risk: the ratio can stay stretched or widen further for years, and the position still carries [[basis-risk|basis risk]] and financing cost.

## Price Drivers

- **Gold correlation**: Silver generally moves in the same direction as [[gold]] but with amplified swings. The gold/silver ratio summarises this relationship.
- **Industrial demand (~50–55%)**: A far larger share than gold, dominated by electronics and **solar photovoltaics**. This makes silver more sensitive to global growth, PMIs, and the green-energy build-out than gold.
- **Investment / monetary demand**: Physical coins, bars, and ETF (SLV) holdings swing with retail and institutional sentiment, real interest rates, and the US dollar.
- **Real interest rates & the dollar**: Like gold, silver is a non-yielding asset; rising *real* yields raise its opportunity cost and tend to weigh on price, while a weaker [[us-dollar]] is generally supportive.
- **Supply (inelastic)**: Roughly 70–75% of mined silver is a **byproduct** of [[copper]], lead, and [[zinc]] mining. Because miners do not adjust output based on the silver price alone, supply responds slowly — a structural feature that can amplify both shortages and gluts.

## Industrial Demand

Approximately 50–55% of silver demand is industrial, a far higher proportion than [[gold]] (~10–12%). This dual precious/industrial nature makes silver significantly more volatile than gold and more sensitive to economic growth cycles:

| Industrial Sector | Share of Industrial Demand | Trend |
|-------------------|---------------------------|-------|
| Electronics & electrical | ~30% | Stable; silver's conductivity is unmatched |
| Solar panels (photovoltaics) | ~20% | **Fastest-growing segment**; each GW of solar requires ~20 tonnes of silver |
| Brazing alloys & solders | ~15% | Stable |
| Medical | ~5% | Antimicrobial applications growing |
| Other industrial | ~30% | Chemicals, catalysts, water purification |

**Solar panel demand** is the key growth story for silver. Global solar installations have been growing rapidly, and silver paste is an essential component of photovoltaic cells. While the industry is working on thrifting (reducing silver content per cell), total silver demand from solar continues to rise as installation volumes overwhelm per-unit reductions. Some analysts project solar could consume a fifth or more of total annual silver supply by 2030.

This industrial demand profile means silver tends to outperform [[gold]] when the economy is expanding (industrial demand rising alongside investment demand) and underperform gold during recessions (industrial demand falls, partially offsetting safe-haven buying). The gold/silver ratio typically compresses during economic expansions and widens during contractions.

Silver is also unique among precious metals in that a significant portion of supply comes as a byproduct of [[copper]], lead, and [[zinc]] mining — making silver supply relatively inelastic to silver prices specifically. See also [[platinum]] and [[palladium]] for other precious metals with significant industrial demand profiles.

## Notable Events

- **Hunt Brothers' corner (1979–1980)**: Nelson Bunker Hunt and brother William attempted to corner the physical silver market, driving the price from roughly $6 to nearly $50/oz before the COMEX changed margin rules ("Silver Thursday," March 1980) and the price collapsed. The episode is the textbook case of a speculative corner and remains the reference point for silver-manipulation concerns. See [[hunt-brothers-silver-corner]].
- **2021 "Silver Squeeze"**: A retail campaign originating on [[reddit]]'s r/WallStreetBets attempted to force a short squeeze via mass SLV and physical buying. It spiked prices briefly but failed to break the market — illustrating both silver's susceptibility to coordinated buying and the depth that paper markets provide.
- **March 2020 liquidity crash**: The gold/silver ratio spiked above 120:1 as silver was sold harder than gold in the COVID liquidity scramble, then collapsed back over the following year as silver dramatically outperformed — a textbook ratio mean-reversion.

## How Traders Use Silver

- **Directional precious-metals bet with extra beta**: Traders bullish on the precious-metals complex often choose silver over gold for the amplified percentage move (and accept the larger drawdowns).
- **Ratio trade**: The gold/silver ratio is the classic relative-value/[[mean-reversion]] play (see worked example above).
- **Macro / inflation hedge**: Held as part of a real-asset allocation against currency debasement and negative real rates.
- **Industrial-cycle proxy**: Because of its solar/electronics exposure, silver is sometimes used as a higher-beta expression of a reflation or green-energy thesis, alongside [[copper]].
- **Curve trades**: Watch the futures term structure — [[contango]] (forward prices above spot) is the normal carry state for a storable metal; episodes of [[backwardation]] can signal acute physical tightness.

## Common Pitfalls and Risks

- **Volatility is the point and the danger**: Silver's larger swings cut both ways; position sizing that works for gold can be too aggressive for silver.
- **Leverage**: COMEX SI is a 5,000-oz contract — a small adverse move can trigger margin calls. Micro (SIL) contracts reduce this.
- **Thinner market than gold**: Silver's smaller market capitalisation makes it more prone to sharp moves, squeezes, and manipulation episodes.
- **Leveraged ETF decay**: AGQ/ZSL and similar products suffer volatility decay and are unsuitable for buy-and-hold.
- **Industrial-cycle drag**: A recession can sink silver even when the monetary case looks strong, because ~half its demand is industrial.
- **Premiums and storage on physical**: Retail physical silver carries dealer premiums, spreads, and storage costs that erode returns versus paper exposure.

## Trading Relevance

Silver offers higher volatility and percentage moves than [[gold]], making it preferred by short-term traders seeking amplified returns on precious-metals themes. Its dual nature (monetary + industrial) creates a more complex fundamental picture than gold's. The gold/silver ratio is a popular mean-reversion trade. Traders should be aware that silver's smaller market size makes it more prone to sharp moves and manipulation concerns, and that the high leverage of COMEX futures demands disciplined position sizing.
