The Shanghai Composite Index (SSE Composite, ticker SHCOMP) is the primary stock market [[stock-market-indices|index]] for the Shanghai Stock Exchange, one of mainland [[china|China's]] two major exchanges (the other being Shenzhen). Launched on **15 July 1991** with a base value of 100, it tracks all A-shares and B-shares listed on the SSE, weighted by market capitalization. As of 2025, the index covers ~2,000 stocks with a combined market cap exceeding $6 trillion. It is a **broad, all-share** index — unlike the curated, blue-chip CSI 300 — which makes it heavily weighted toward large state-owned banks and industrials.

## Role in Global Markets

The Shanghai Composite is the most widely watched indicator of Chinese equity market sentiment. Because mainland China's stock market is partially closed to foreign investors (A-shares require QFII/RQFII status or [[china|Stock Connect]] access), the SSE Composite often moves independently of global indices — but when it crashes, the spillover effects are severe.

## Index Construction & Methodology

The Shanghai Composite is deliberately *broad* rather than curated — its design is the opposite of a hand-picked blue-chip benchmark:

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Universe** | **All** A-shares and B-shares listed on the Shanghai Stock Exchange (the entire main board, plus the STAR board) — there is no selection committee paring it to a fixed count |
| **Weighting** | **Total** market-capitalization weighted (historically free-float-adjusted only partially), so large state-owned enterprises with big unfree-floating stakes carry outsized weight |
| **Base** | Set to **100** on **15 July 1991** (base date 19 December 1990) |
| **Maintainer** | The Shanghai Stock Exchange itself |
| **Contrast** | The **CSI 300** (maintained by China Securities Index Co.) is the curated, free-float, 300-name blue-chip benchmark; the **STAR 50** tracks the Nasdaq-style growth board |

Because it is total-cap weighted and all-share, the index is dominated by **large state-owned banks, energy/PetroChina-type names, and "baijiu"/consumer staples** rather than the growth-tech profile foreign investors associate with "China." This structural tilt is why the SSE Composite can diverge sharply from offshore China proxies like the Hong Kong-listed Hang Seng China Enterprises or the Nasdaq Golden Dragon ADR index.

## How It Compares to Other Major Indices

| Index | Market | Construction | Defining feature |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Shanghai Composite** | Mainland China (onshore A/B-shares) | All-share, total-cap weighted | Retail-dominated, state-managed, capital-controlled |
| [[s-and-p-500]] | United States | 500 names, free-float cap | Institution-dominated, open, the global benchmark |
| [[nikkei-225]] | Japan | 225 names, price-weighted | Exporter-heavy, fully open to foreigners |
| CSI 300 | Mainland China | 300 blue chips, free-float cap | The institutional/index-futures benchmark for China |
| Hang Seng | Hong Kong (offshore) | Free-float cap | Fully open; where global investors express "China" views |

The key distinction versus the [[s-and-p-500]] or [[nikkei-225]] is **access and ownership structure**: those markets are open and institution-led, whereas the Shanghai Composite is partially closed, retail-led (~80% of turnover), and subject to direct state intervention. That makes its volatility profile and its sensitivity to *policy signals* fundamentally different from a developed-market index.

## A-Shares vs B-Shares vs H-Shares

A single Chinese company can trade in several share classes across venues, often at large price gaps:

| Class | Where it trades | Currency | Who can buy | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **A-shares** | Shanghai / Shenzhen (mainland) | RMB | Domestic + qualified foreigners (QFII, Stock Connect) | The bulk of the SSE Composite; retail-dominated |
| **B-shares** | Shanghai / Shenzhen | USD (SSE) / HKD (SZSE) | Historically foreigners | Legacy class, now thin and largely irrelevant since Stock Connect |
| **H-shares** | Hong Kong (HKEX) | HKD | Anyone (HK is open) | Mainland firms listed in HK; **not** in the SSE Composite |

The **A-H premium** — the same company's A-shares often trading at a meaningful premium to its Hong Kong H-shares — is a long-running structural anomaly driven by capital controls, a captive mainland retail base, and limited cross-border arbitrage. It is one of the clearest demonstrations that China's onshore and offshore equity markets are segmented rather than unified.

## Notable Crashes

### 2015 Chinese Stock Market Crash

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| 2015-06-12 | SSE Composite peaks at 5,178 — up 150% in 12 months on margin-fueled retail speculation |
| 2015-06-15-Jul | Market falls 32% in 3 weeks as margin calls cascade |
| 2015-07 | Government intervention: PBOC rate cuts, trading halts on ~50% of listed stocks, ban on short selling, state fund buying |
| 2015-08-24 | "Black Monday" — SSE Composite crashes 8.5% in one day, triggering the [[flash-crash-2015-etf|2015 ETF Flash Crash]] in US markets |
| 2016-01 | New circuit breaker system triggers twice in first week, then is abandoned after 4 days |
| 2016-02 | SSE bottoms at ~2,638 — down 49% from the June 2015 peak |

The crash was driven by extreme retail leverage: Chinese retail investors had accumulated $400B+ in margin debt, and many used unregulated shadow margin lending (umbrella trusts, peer-to-peer matching) at leverage ratios of 5:1 or higher. When the market turned, margin calls created a liquidation cascade that no amount of government intervention could stop.

### State Intervention and the "National Team"

The 2015 response defined the modern playbook for Beijing's market management. Authorities deployed the **"national team"** (国家队) — state-backed entities led by China Securities Finance Corp., funded by the PBOC, directed to buy equities directly to put a floor under prices. Alongside the buying, the toolkit included: a ban on selling by major shareholders, suspension of IPOs, criminal investigations of "malicious" short sellers, and PBOC rate and reserve-ratio cuts. At the depth of the crash roughly **half of all listed companies voluntarily suspended trading** to avoid forced markdowns, which paradoxically concentrated selling pressure into the stocks that remained open. This heavy, visible state hand — buying in busts and curbing froth in booms — is a permanent feature of how the SSE Composite trades and a key reason it is poorly described by free-market price-discovery models.

### Global Spillover

The August 24, 2015 crash in Shanghai directly triggered the US [[flash-crash-2015-etf|ETF Flash Crash]]:
- S&P 500 futures hit limit-down before the US open
- DJIA fell 1,089 points within minutes of the opening bell
- Hundreds of ETFs traded 20-35% below NAV
- The crash exposed fragility in the US ETF pricing mechanism during stress

## Market Structure and Trading Mechanics

- **Daily price limit**: ±10% for regular SSE stocks (±5% for stocks under "special treatment"); ±20% for the STAR board (科创板, China's Nasdaq-style growth board).
- **T+1 settlement**: shares bought today cannot be sold until the next session — no intraday round-trips. This is the same [[settlement-risk|T+1]] constraint discussed in settlement cycles, applied here as a deliberate brake on day-trading.
- **Retail-dominated**: roughly **80% of turnover** comes from retail investors (versus ~15-20% in US markets), which is the structural source of the index's boom-bust volatility and its sensitivity to sentiment over fundamentals.
- **Stock Connect**: the Shanghai-Hong Kong (2014) and Shenzhen-Hong Kong (2016) Connect programs give foreign investors capped access to A-shares and mainland investors access to Hong Kong, the main channel by which A-shares entered global indices (MSCI began partial inclusion in 2018).
- **State presence**: the [[china|"national team"]] and SOE-heavy composition mean a large share of the index does not trade on a pure free float.

## Sector Composition

As an all-share, total-cap index, the SSE Composite is structurally heavy in the largest state-owned sectors rather than in growth tech:

- **Financials / banks** — the big state-owned commercial banks (ICBC, China Construction Bank, Bank of China, Agricultural Bank of China) are perennial top weights; their valuations are tied to the property cycle and government net-interest-margin policy.
- **Energy & materials** — PetroChina, Sinopec, and large industrial/materials SOEs reflect the index's old-economy bias.
- **Consumer staples** — premium "baijiu" liquor names (e.g. Kweichow Moutai) are among the most valuable A-shares and a bellwether for domestic consumer sentiment.
- **Industrials & utilities** — infrastructure, construction, and power SOEs.
- **Technology / STAR board** — semiconductors and growth names sit largely on the STAR board (科创板); they are a smaller weight than foreign investors expect, which is why the offshore Nasdaq Golden Dragon / Hang Seng Tech indices behave very differently.

## How It's Traded

- **Onshore A-shares** are the bulk of the index but are *capital-controlled*: domestic investors trade them directly; foreigners need **QFII/RQFII** licences or the capped **Stock Connect** channel.
- **Index futures**: mainland index futures (CFFEX CSI 300, SSE 50, CSI 500 contracts) are the institutional hedging tools, but access is tightly restricted and was throttled after 2015. There is **no deep, freely-traded Shanghai-Composite futures market for foreigners** comparable to [[futures|FESX or ES]].
- **ETFs**: foreign investors typically express "China A-share" exposure through funds that track the **CSI 300** rather than the SSE Composite itself — e.g. US-listed A-share ETFs (ASHR tracks CSI 300) and broad China funds (MCHI, FXI track offshore HK/ADR names). There is no large, liquid US-listed pure SSE-Composite tracker, a direct consequence of the access constraints above.
- **Offshore proxies**: many global investors trade **H-shares (Hong Kong)** and **US ADRs** as their China expression precisely because the onshore market is hard to access — which is what sustains the A-H premium.

## Currency & Macro Drivers

- **PBOC policy & the renminbi**: People's Bank of China rate, reserve-ratio (RRR), and **USD/CNY** management are first-order drivers. A managed-currency regime means FX moves are policy signals, not free-floating prices.
- **Property cycle**: the health of the Chinese real-estate sector (developer solvency, land sales, household balance sheets) flows directly into the heavily-weighted banks.
- **State stimulus & "national team" signalling**: fiscal stimulus announcements and visible state buying can drive sharp rallies independent of fundamentals.
- **US-China relations**: tariffs, export controls (semiconductors), and ADR delisting threats move sentiment and the offshore proxies.
- **Capital flows via Stock Connect**: northbound (foreign-into-A-share) flow data is watched as a real-time sentiment gauge.

## Risks

- **Policy / intervention risk**: the visible state hand (national team buying, trading halts, short-selling bans, IPO suspensions) means prices can be administratively managed, breaking normal price discovery.
- **Capital controls**: foreign capital can be trapped or its access narrowed; repatriation is not guaranteed to be frictionless.
- **Retail-driven boom-bust volatility**: ~80% retail turnover and historical shadow-margin leverage make the index prone to violent sentiment cycles (see 2015).
- **Opacity / governance**: uneven disclosure, audit-access disputes (PCAOB), and SOE-state alignment complicate fundamental analysis.
- **Segmentation**: the index is not a clean proxy for the Chinese economy or for offshore China tech — using it as such is a common mistake.
