The Australian dollar (AUD) is the official currency of Australia and one of the most actively traded currencies globally, ranking fifth by daily turnover in the [[forex]] market with approximately $470 billion traded daily (2022 BIS Triennial Survey). As a commodity-linked currency, AUD correlates strongly with [[iron-ore]] and [[copper]] prices due to Australia's resource-heavy export base. Fred discusses AUD/USD movements as a key factor for Australian investors holding USD-denominated assets, where [[currency-hedging]] decisions can significantly impact total returns. The AUD's value against the [[us-dollar]] is influenced by [[reserve-bank-of-australia|Reserve Bank of Australia (RBA)]] interest rate decisions, Chinese demand for Australian [[commodities]], and global risk sentiment.

## Why the AUD Is a "Commodity Currency"

A commodity currency is one whose nation's economy and export receipts are dominated by raw materials, so the currency tends to track commodity prices. Australia, [[canadian-dollar|Canada]], New Zealand, Norway, and South Africa are the classic examples. The three forces below describe most of the AUD's behaviour.

| Driver | Mechanism | Direction |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Commodity prices** ([[iron-ore]], coal, LNG, [[gold]]) | Higher prices widen Australia's trade surplus and lift export income | Higher commodities → stronger AUD |
| **Interest-rate differential** ([[reserve-bank-of-australia\|RBA]] vs Fed) | Capital chases the higher yield | Higher AU rates vs US → stronger AUD |
| **Global risk sentiment** | AUD is a high-beta "risk-on" proxy | Risk-on → stronger AUD; risk-off → weaker |

The AUD sits in the [[commodity-currencies]] family alongside the [[canadian-dollar|CAD]] (oil), New Zealand dollar (dairy), Norwegian krone (oil), and South African rand (metals). It is frequently grouped with the NZD and CAD as the "commodity dollars" or "comdolls."

## Major AUD Pairs

| Pair | Nickname | What it expresses |
|---|---|---|
| **AUD/USD** | "Aussie" | The benchmark — commodity demand and risk sentiment vs. the world reserve currency; the most liquid AUD pair |
| **AUD/JPY** | — | The classic [[carry-trade]] expression: high-beta risk-on (AUD) vs. low-yield safe haven ([[japanese-yen|JPY]]). A near-pure "risk barometer" that tracks global equities closely |
| **AUD/NZD** | — | A relative-value play on two similar commodity economies; isolates RBA-vs-RBNZ policy and dairy-vs-iron-ore terms of trade |
| **EUR/AUD, GBP/AUD** | — | Cross-rate exposure; used to trade AUD strength/weakness without direct USD exposure |
| **AUD/CNH** | — | Direct read on the China linkage, since China is Australia's dominant trading partner |

Because AUD/USD and AUD/JPY are so sensitive to the global risk cycle, traders use them as **leading or confirming indicators** for broader risk-on/risk-off ([[risk-on-risk-off]]) regimes, often alongside copper and equity-index futures.

## Commodity Currency Dynamics

Australia is one of the world's largest exporters of [[iron-ore]], coal, natural gas, [[gold]], and agricultural products. This resource dependence means the AUD functions as a proxy for global commodity demand -- particularly **Chinese demand**, since China is Australia's largest trading partner, accounting for roughly 30-35% of total exports. When Chinese infrastructure and construction activity accelerates, demand for Australian iron ore rises, trade surpluses widen, and the AUD appreciates. Conversely, slowing Chinese growth or falling commodity prices weaken the AUD. The correlation between AUD/USD and the iron-ore price has been particularly strong since the 2000s mining boom, though it is not mechanical and can diverge for extended periods when interest-rate differentials or risk sentiment dominate.

## RBA Monetary Policy

The [[reserve-bank-of-australia|RBA]] sets the cash rate, the primary short-term interest rate in Australia and a key driver of AUD valuation through interest-rate differentials. When the RBA holds rates above the [[federal-reserve|US Federal Reserve]], the AUD tends to appreciate as capital flows into higher-yielding Australian assets. Historically Australia ran higher rates than most developed economies through the 1990s and 2000s, making the AUD a popular target in [[carry-trade|carry trades]] -- investors borrowed in low-yielding currencies (Japanese yen, Swiss franc) and bought AUD-denominated assets to capture the differential.

## Risk-On Proxy and Carry-Trade Behaviour

The carry-trade dynamic amplifies AUD volatility. During risk-on periods, carry inflows push the AUD higher; during crises (2008, 2020), the unwinding of carry trades causes sharp AUD sell-offs as global investors flee to safe-haven currencies (USD, JPY, CHF). This is why the AUD is treated as a **"risk-on" barometer** -- it behaves like a leveraged bet on global growth, often rising and falling alongside equity indices and copper. The flip side is that the AUD's interest-rate premium has largely evaporated post-2015: when the RBA cut to a record-low 0.10% in 2020-2021 and the Fed later out-hiked it, the AUD lost much of its traditional carry appeal and the differential turned negative.

### Funding vs target currency

In a [[carry-trade]] the trader borrows (sells) a low-yield **funding currency** and buys a high-yield **target currency**, pocketing the [[interest-rate-differential]] as long as the exchange rate is stable or moves favourably.

- **AUD as the historic target.** Through the 1990s–2000s the AUD was the textbook *target* — funded by the [[japanese-yen|yen]] (JPY) or Swiss franc. The AUD/JPY pair *was* the carry trade for a generation of macro traders.
- **AUD as a funding currency (post-2020).** Once the RBA cut to 0.10% and other central banks out-hiked it, the AUD's role partially inverted — at times it became a *funding* currency rather than a target. The role flips with the policy cycle.

**Illustrative carry example (figures hypothetical):**

```
Borrow JPY at 0.10%, convert to AUD, invest at AUD rate 4.10%
Gross carry (rate differential) = 4.10% − 0.10% = +4.00% per year

But: if AUD/JPY falls 5% over the year, the FX loss (−5%)
swamps the +4% carry → net −1%.

The carry is a "picking up pennies in front of a steamroller" payoff:
small, steady gains punctuated by large, sudden losses when the
trade unwinds (risk-off). This is why AUD/JPY gaps down violently
in crises (2008, 2020) as leveraged carry positions are liquidated.
```

The payoff profile is **negatively skewed**: high win-rate, occasional catastrophic loss. Carry returns are therefore best understood as compensation for bearing tail/[[liquidity]] risk, not a free lunch from the rate gap.

| March 2020 | ~0.5510 | COVID crash low |
| 2021 | >0.78 | Commodity-driven recovery |

Since 2015 the AUD has traded mostly between 0.60 and 0.80 as the mining boom faded and Australia's rate premium narrowed.

## How Traders Use the AUD

- **As a risk proxy.** Macro and cross-asset traders watch AUD/USD and especially AUD/JPY as a real-time read on global risk appetite — a sustained AUD/JPY breakdown often coincides with broad equity de-risking ([[risk-on-risk-off]]).
- **As a China / commodity proxy.** Because ~30–35% of exports go to China, the AUD is a liquid, 24-hour way to express a view on Chinese growth and [[iron-ore]]/[[copper]] demand without trading the underlying commodity or onshore Chinese assets.
- **Carry construction.** When the RBA's rate is above funding-currency rates, AUD pairs are built into [[carry-trade]] baskets; the position is sized to the [[interest-rate-differential]] and hedged or trimmed when volatility (and unwind risk) rises.
- **Correlation pairs / relative value.** AUD/NZD isolates RBA-vs-RBNZ policy and relative terms of trade; AUD vs CAD trades iron ore against oil.
- **Hedging decisions for AU-based investors.** Australians holding unhedged US equities effectively run a short-AUD overlay (a falling AUD lifts AUD-converted USD returns) — see [[currency-hedging]] and the implications section below.

## Pitfalls and Risks

- **Correlation is unstable, not mechanical.** The AUD/iron-ore and AUD/rate-differential relationships hold *on average* but break down for long stretches when a different driver (risk sentiment, USD cycle) dominates. Do not treat any single linkage as a hard rule.
- **Carry's negative skew.** AUD carry positions earn small steady gains then lose violently in risk-off unwinds — the AUD/JPY crashes of 2008 and 2020 are the warning. Leverage magnifies the steamroller risk.
- **China concentration risk.** Heavy export dependence on a single partner means Australian-policy or Chinese-policy shocks (trade restrictions, property-sector stress, stimulus surprises) move the AUD sharply and sometimes unpredictably.
- **Lost rate premium.** The AUD's traditional carry edge has largely gone post-2015; assuming a structural yield advantage that no longer exists is a common stale-thesis error.
- **Risk-off "double whammy" for AU global investors.** In a crisis the AUD typically falls *and* offshore risk assets fall — the FX cushion helps unhedged holdings, but a fully hedged AU investor loses that cushion exactly when it would have helped. The hedging choice is itself a risk position, not a neutral default.
- **USD is the other leg.** AUD/USD weakness is sometimes broad USD *strength* (a [[dxy]]/[[capital-flows|flow]] story) rather than AUD-specific — always check whether the move is "Aussie weak" or "dollar strong."

## Implications for Australian Investors

For Australian investors with international equity exposure (particularly U.S. [[stocks]]), AUD/USD movements create a secondary return layer. A falling AUD boosts the value of unhedged USD-denominated holdings when converted back to AUD, while a rising AUD erodes those returns -- which is why a weak-AUD period can flatter unhedged global portfolios. This creates a persistent dilemma around [[currency-hedging]]: hedging removes currency risk but also forfeits the cushion that AUD weakness provides during global sell-offs (when the AUD typically falls and US assets typically rise in USD terms). Fred McNaught's view is that currency-hedging decisions should be considered part of the overall portfolio strategy rather than ad hoc timing calls, since forecasting exchange rates is notoriously difficult even for professionals.
