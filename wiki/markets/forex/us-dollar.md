The US dollar (USD) is the world's primary reserve currency, underpinning global trade, commodity pricing, cross-border lending, and central bank reserves. Roughly 58-60% of global foreign exchange reserves are held in USD (IMF COFER data), and the dollar is on one side of about 88% of all [[forex]] trades by notional. The majority of international commodity transactions -- crude oil, [[gold]], copper, agricultural products -- are denominated in dollars. The dollar's dominance traces back to the Bretton Woods Agreement of 1944, which pegged major currencies to the dollar (itself convertible to gold), and -- after President Nixon closed the gold window in 1971 -- the petrodollar system of the 1970s, whereby oil-exporting nations priced crude exclusively in USD.

## DXY: The Dollar Index

The [[dxy|US Dollar Index (DXY)]] measures the dollar's value against a fixed basket of six major currencies. The weights have been unchanged since the euro's introduction in 1999:

| Currency | Weight | Notes |
|----------|--------|-------|
| Euro (EUR) | 57.6% | Dominates the index; DXY is largely a EUR/USD inverse |
| Japanese yen (JPY) | 13.6% | Sensitive to [[carry-trade]] flows and BoJ policy |
| British pound (GBP) | 11.9% | |
| Canadian dollar (CAD) | 9.1% | Oil-linked commodity currency |
| Swedish krona (SEK) | 4.2% | |
| Swiss franc (CHF) | 3.6% | Safe-haven currency |

Because the euro dominates, DXY is heavily influenced by [[ecb|European Central Bank]] policy relative to [[federal-reserve|Fed]] policy. Traders treat DXY as a macro barometer: a rising DXY signals tightening global financial conditions, risk-off sentiment, or US economic outperformance, while a falling DXY tends to support commodities, [[emerging-markets|emerging-market]] assets, and gold. Note the DXY is a narrow, Europe-heavy basket; the Fed's trade-weighted broad dollar index (which includes the Chinese yuan, Mexican peso, and other partners) is a better gauge of the dollar's true purchasing power.

## What Drives the Dollar

### Real Yields and Fed Policy

The single most important medium-term driver is the **real interest rate** -- the nominal yield on [[treasury-bonds|US Treasuries]] minus expected inflation. Capital chases real yield, so when US real rates rise relative to the rest of the world, the dollar strengthens. The [[federal-reserve|Federal Reserve]] controls the short end of this via the [[fomc|federal funds rate]] and steers the long end through quantitative easing/tightening. The 2022-2023 hiking cycle -- the fastest in four decades -- drove DXY above 114 in September 2022, a 20-year high, as the Fed out-tightened the ECB and BoJ.

### The Dollar Smile

The **dollar smile theory** (Stephen Jen) explains why the dollar can rally in two opposite regimes:

- **Left side of the smile** -- global risk-off / crisis: the dollar rallies as a safe haven as investors flee to US Treasuries and scramble for dollars to service USD-denominated debt (a "dollar shortage," acute in March 2020).
- **Bottom of the smile** -- benign global growth with the US lagging: the dollar weakens as capital rotates into higher-growth foreign and emerging markets.
- **Right side of the smile** -- US economic outperformance: the dollar rallies on superior US growth and higher relative yields.

The dollar is therefore weakest in the "Goldilocks" middle and strongest at both extremes -- a property that makes it an awkward asset to position against.

### USD as Funding & Carry Currency

When US rates are low, the dollar becomes a popular **funding currency** for the [[carry-trade]]: investors borrow cheaply in USD and buy higher-yielding assets like the [[australian-dollar|AUD]] or emerging-market debt. When the Fed hikes or volatility spikes, these carry trades unwind violently, forcing dollar buy-backs and amplifying dollar rallies during stress.

## Impact on Commodities and Emerging Markets

Because commodities are priced in dollars, a stronger dollar mechanically makes oil, gold, and metals more expensive for non-US buyers, typically pressuring prices -- the well-known **inverse dollar-commodity correlation**. Emerging markets are doubly exposed: many EM governments and corporates borrow in dollars, so a rising dollar raises their debt-servicing burden while simultaneously draining capital toward US assets, a recurring trigger of EM crises (1997 Asian crisis, 2013 "taper tantrum," 2018 Turkey/Argentina selloffs).

## The "Milkshake" and De-dollarisation Debate

The **dollar milkshake theory** (Brent Johnson) argues that the structural global demand for dollars -- created by an estimated $13+ trillion of offshore USD-denominated debt and the depth of US capital markets -- will pull global liquidity into dollar assets and strengthen the dollar even as the US runs large deficits. Against this, growing [[us-debt]] levels and persistent fiscal deficits are cited as a long-term debasement risk, and the de-dollarisation push by China, Russia, and the broader BRICS bloc -- bilateral trade settlement in local currencies, gold accumulation by central banks -- represents a slow-moving but potentially structural shift for [[forex]] markets. For now, no rival offers the liquidity, rule of law, and convertibility of the dollar, so reserve displacement has been gradual.

## Implications for Investors

For non-US (e.g. Australian) investors holding USD-denominated equities, the AUD/USD rate adds a second return layer: a falling dollar erodes unhedged foreign returns when converted home, while a rising dollar boosts them. This drives the [[currency-hedging]] decision in [[portfolio-construction|portfolio management]]. The dollar's paradoxical strength during [[market-crashes|market crashes]] -- rallying precisely when fear of US debasement is highest -- makes it the premier defensive currency and a poor instrument to short tactically. Many investors hold [[gold]] as a complementary hedge against long-run USD debasement.
