# FTSE 100

The **FTSE 100** (colloquially the "Footsie", index ticker **UKX**) is the flagship benchmark of the UK equity market: a free-float, market-capitalisation-weighted index of the 100 largest companies listed on the [[london-stock-exchange|London Stock Exchange]] (LSE) by full market value. It is maintained by **FTSE Russell**, the index business of the London Stock Exchange Group, and was launched on 3 January 1984 with a base level of 1,000. Despite being "the UK index," its most important and frequently misunderstood characteristic is that it is only loosely tied to the UK domestic economy — the bulk of its constituents' revenue is earned overseas, making it far more a play on the global and commodity cycle and on [[gbp|sterling]] than on Britain itself.

## What It Tracks / Methodology

FTSE Russell selects the 100 largest eligible companies on the LSE main market and weights each by **free-float-adjusted market capitalisation** (only shares genuinely available to public investors count; large strategic, government, or insider holdings are stripped out). Larger companies therefore dominate index moves. Key methodology points:

- **Quarterly reviews.** The constituent list is reviewed on the first Wednesday of March, June, September, and December, using closing prices from the Tuesday before. Rebalancing takes effect after the close on the following third Friday.
- **The 90/110 buffer rule.** To limit churn, a company is *promoted* into the index if it rises to 90th position or higher by market cap at a review, and *demoted* if it falls to 111th or lower. Companies ranked between the two thresholds keep their existing status. Constituents dropping out of the FTSE 100 typically fall into the mid-cap [[ftse-250]].
- **Free-float bands and liquidity screens.** A company must meet minimum free-float and liquidity requirements to be eligible; nationality/listing rules determine whether a company is treated as UK-eligible.
- **Price and total-return versions.** The headline index most people quote is the *price* index. A separate **total-return** series (which reinvests dividends) exists and — given the FTSE 100's high payout character — diverges substantially from the price index over long horizons.
- **Part of the FTSE UK Index Series.** The FTSE 100 sits alongside the FTSE 250 (the next 250 companies), the FTSE 350 (100 + 250), and the FTSE All-Share.

## The International Character (the key distinctive feature)

The single most important thing to understand about the FTSE 100 is that it is **overwhelmingly international in its revenue base**. A large majority of aggregate FTSE 100 turnover — a figure commonly cited at roughly two-thirds to three-quarters — is generated *outside* the United Kingdom. Constituents such as [[shell]], [[bp]], [[hsbc]], [[unilever]], [[diageo]], [[astrazeneca]], [[rio-tinto]], and [[glencore]] earn most of their money in dollars, euros, and emerging-market currencies, selling oil, minerals, medicines, spirits, and consumer goods globally.

This has a defining consequence for traders:

- **A weaker pound typically LIFTS the FTSE 100.** When [[gbp|sterling]] falls, overseas earnings and dividends translate into *more* pounds, mechanically boosting reported profits and the sterling-denominated index. The relationship is often inverse: the index and the currency frequently move in opposite directions.
- **It is a global-cyclical proxy, not a UK-economy proxy.** The FTSE 100 tells you more about oil, copper, Chinese demand, global interest rates, and the dollar than about UK GDP, high-street spending, or domestic politics.
- **Contrast with the [[ftse-250]].** The mid-cap FTSE 250 derives a much larger share of its revenue domestically and is the better read on the *UK* economy. A classic tell is that the two indices can diverge sharply on sterling moves.

The **June 2016 Brexit referendum** is the textbook illustration: sterling fell sharply against the dollar, and while the domestically-tilted FTSE 250 dropped, the internationally-exposed FTSE 100 recovered its initial losses within days and went on to make new highs — the overseas-earnings translation effect overwhelming the domestic gloom.

## Composition & Sector Character

The FTSE 100 has a distinctly **"old economy," value-tilted** composition that stands in stark relief against the technology-dominated [[s-and-p-500]] and [[nasdaq|Nasdaq]]:

| Sector cluster | Representative constituents |
|----------------|-----------------------------|
| **Energy / oil & gas** | [[shell]], [[bp]] |
| **Materials / miners** | [[rio-tinto]], [[glencore]], [[anglo-american]] |
| **Financials / banks & insurers** | [[hsbc]], Barclays, Lloyds, NatWest, Standard Chartered, Prudential |
| **Consumer staples** | [[unilever]], [[diageo]], British American Tobacco, Reckitt |
| **Healthcare / pharma** | [[astrazeneca]], [[glaxosmithkline|GSK]] |
| **Technology** | Very light — the index has historically had minimal large-cap tech representation |

The largest single constituents in recent years have typically included [[astrazeneca]], [[shell]], [[hsbc]], and [[unilever]], though relative rankings shift with commodity and currency cycles. The near-absence of mega-cap technology is the FTSE 100's defining structural difference from US indices: there is no domestic equivalent of the "Magnificent Seven," which is a large part of why the index trailed Wall Street through the 2010s growth era.

## Character: Value, Income, and Defensiveness

- **High dividend yield.** The FTSE 100 is one of the higher-yielding major equity indices, typically paying a materially larger dividend yield than the [[s-and-p-500]]. Big oil, banks, miners, tobacco, and pharma are cash-generative payers, making the index a favourite of UK income investors. See [[dividend-yield]] and [[dividends]].
- **Value tilt, lower multiples.** The heavy weighting toward cyclicals, financials, and staples — and the light tech weighting — means the index usually trades at a **lower price-to-earnings multiple** than the growth-heavy S&P 500. This "cheapness" is a recurring bull thesis (see [[value-investing]]) and, equally, a recurring value trap, since low multiples reflect lower structural growth.
- **Defensive/commodity blend.** Staples and pharma provide defensiveness; energy and miners inject commodity beta. The net effect is an index that can hold up in risk-off, dollar-strong regimes that punish growth stocks, yet is exposed to the global industrial cycle.

## Ways to Trade / Access It

The FTSE 100 is one of the most heavily traded underlyings in Europe and is accessible across every instrument class:

| Instrument | Examples | Notes |
|------------|----------|-------|
| **ETFs** | ISF (iShares Core FTSE 100), VUKE (Vanguard FTSE 100) | Cheap, liquid, buy-and-hold exposure; accumulating variants (e.g. CUKX) reinvest dividends. See [[etf]] and [[index-funds]]. |
| **Index futures** | FTSE 100 Index Future (ICE Futures Europe) | Standard contract valued at £10 per index point; the primary institutional hedging/directional tool. See [[futures]]. |
| **Index options** | FTSE 100 options | Cash-settled index options for hedging and premium selling — see [[ftse-100-options]] and [[index-options]] / [[options]]. |
| **CFDs & spread bets** | Various UK brokers | Extremely popular retail route in the UK; [[spread-betting]] profits are exempt from UK capital-gains tax for residents. See [[cfd-trading]]. |
| **Tracker funds** | OEICs/unit trusts | Traditional pooled FTSE 100 trackers for long-term savers. |

In portfolio terms the index is used as a **global-cyclical + income proxy**: a way to add commodity and non-US-dollar earnings exposure, and a high-yield sleeve, that behaves quite differently from a US-tech-led allocation.

## Drivers

The FTSE 100's level is driven less by the UK domestic story than by a global set of factors:

- **[[gbp|Sterling (GBP)]]** — the dominant swing factor via overseas-earnings translation; a weaker pound is usually a tailwind.
- **Commodity and [[oil|oil]] prices** — [[brent-crude|Brent crude]], metals, and [[gold]] drive the large energy and mining weights.
- **Global growth and China** — miners and industrials are geared to the global industrial cycle and Chinese demand.
- **[[us-dollar|US dollar]] strength** — a strong dollar boosts dollar-earning constituents in sterling terms and supports commodities pricing.
- **[[bank-of-england|Bank of England]] policy and UK gilt yields** — matter for the domestic banks, insurers, and rate-sensitive names, though less than for the FTSE 250.
- **Dividend sustainability** — because so much of the total return is income, cuts by major payers (as seen across banks and oil majors during 2020) hit the index's core appeal hard.

## History & Notable Episodes

- **1984 launch.** Started on 3 January 1984 at a base of 1,000, replacing the older FT 30 as the UK's headline real-time index.
- **1987 crash, dot-com, and 2008.** The index fell heavily in the October 1987 crash, the 2000–2003 dot-com bust, and the [[2008-global-financial-crisis|2008 Global Financial Crisis]], each time recovering over subsequent years.
- **The lost decade above the dot-com peak.** The FTSE 100 set a peak at the height of the dot-com boom in late 1999 that it did not *decisively* exceed for roughly 15 years, not breaking clearly above it until the mid-2010s — a stark contrast with US indices and a caution against assuming any index only rises.
- **June 2016 Brexit.** The referendum crystallised the sterling/FTSE-100 inverse dynamic (see above), with the internationally-exposed FTSE 100 outperforming the domestic FTSE 250.
- **2020 COVID shock and dividend cuts.** The index fell sharply in the March 2020 crash, and — reflecting its income-heavy character — was hit hard by widespread dividend suspensions and cuts across banks and oil majors.

## Risks / Limitations

- **Not a UK-economy bet.** Investors seeking domestic UK exposure via the FTSE 100 are largely mis-positioned; the FTSE 250 is the better proxy.
- **Commodity and currency concentration.** Heavy energy/mining/financials weights make the index sensitive to a handful of macro variables; a commodity downturn or sharp sterling *strength* can weigh on it.
- **Structural growth deficit.** The near-absence of mega-cap tech has historically capped total returns relative to the [[s-and-p-500]]; the "cheap" multiple can persist for years.
- **Income dependence.** With so much return coming from dividends, sustainability of payouts is central — broad dividend cuts materially impair the thesis.
