XLU is the Utilities Select Sector SPDR Fund, issued by State Street Global Advisors. It tracks the Utilities Select Sector Index — the [[utilities|utilities]] slice of the [[s-and-p-500|S&P 500]] under the [[gics-classification|GICS]] framework. XLU is the most liquid US utilities sector ETF and the standard vehicle for expressing exposure to regulated electric, gas, and water utilities plus independent power producers. It is a classic **defensive, rate-sensitive "bond proxy"** — and, more recently, a vehicle for the [[ai-data-center-power-demand|AI power-demand]] theme.

## What It Tracks

XLU holds the utilities constituents of the S&P 500: regulated electric utilities, multi-utilities (electric + gas), water utilities, gas utilities, and independent power & renewable producers. The sector is **defensive** (electricity demand is inelastic) and **rate-sensitive** — utilities carry heavy debt loads and pay high dividends, so their valuations behave like long-duration bonds, moving inversely to interest rates.

The underlying **Utilities Select Sector Index** is one of the eleven sub-indices that together reconstitute the [[s-and-p-500|S&P 500]] when combined. Each S&P 500 company is assigned to exactly one Select Sector index based on its [[gics-classification|GICS]] sector, so the Select Sector SPDRs are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive — XLU plus its ten siblings ([[xlk]], [[xlf]], [[xlv]], [[xly]], [[xlp]], [[xli]], [[xle]], [[xlb]], [[xlre]], and [[xlc]]) equal the full index. Utilities is the smallest or one of the smallest sectors by index weight, typically only a low-single-digit percentage of the S&P 500, which is why XLU is a mid-size rather than marquee SPDR.

XLU is led by the largest regulated electric utilities and power producers:

| Holding | Role |

NextEra typically carries a large share. The independent power producers (Constellation, Vistra) have gained prominence as data-center electricity demand has surged.

### Concentration Dynamics

Relative to mega-cap-dominated SPDRs like [[xlk|XLK]] (where a handful of names can exceed half the fund) or [[xly|XLY]] (Amazon + Tesla heavy), XLU is **moderately concentrated**: the top holding (usually NextEra) is large but does not dwarf the rest, and the next several regulated utilities carry similar single-digit weights. The most important recent shift is the rise of the independent power producers (Constellation, Vistra) at the expense of the sleepiest regulated names — this changes the fund's character, injecting wholesale-power-price beta and an AI/growth narrative into what was historically a pure rate-and-regulation basket. Quarterly capping rules prevent any one name from breaching diversification limits, but the practical effect is that XLU's beta to the AI-power theme has risen as those producers have re-rated.

## Sector Drivers & Macro Sensitivities

| Driver | Effect on XLU |
|--------|---------------|
| **Long-term interest rates** | The dominant macro factor. Rising 10-year Treasury yields pressure XLU (bond-proxy de-rating, higher financing cost on heavy debt); falling yields lift it. |
| **Rate-case / regulatory outcomes** | Allowed ROE and approved rate-base growth set by state public utility commissions determine regulated-earnings trajectory. |
| **Wholesale power prices** | Drive earnings of independent power producers (Constellation, Vistra) — a newer, more cyclical input. |
| **Electricity demand growth** | Decades of flat US demand kept the sector ex-growth; the [[ai-data-center-power-demand|AI/data-center demand]] surge has reset the outlook. |
| **Commodity (natural gas) prices** | Affect fuel costs and merchant generation margins. |
| **Bond-market risk appetite** | XLU often trades as a risk-off / "flight-to-yield" destination in equity drawdowns. |

Because of its bond-like behavior, XLU's correlation to long-duration Treasuries is frequently higher than its correlation to cyclical equity sectors like [[xli|industrials]] or [[xly|discretionary]]. This makes it a useful diversifier inside an equity sleeve but a poor hedge when rates and stocks fall together (a rate-shock regime).

## Seasonality & Behavior vs the Broad Index

XLU's seasonal pattern is tied less to a calendar and more to the **rate cycle and risk regime**. It tends to attract flows during the late-cycle and risk-off phases of the year, and during the bond-market rallies that often accompany growth scares. Against the [[spy|S&P 500]] it is a **low-beta** instrument (beta well below 1), so in trending bull markets the XLU/[[spy|SPY]] ratio grinds lower (utilities lag), while in corrections and recessions the ratio spikes higher as defensives outperform. The historic tight inverse link to Treasury yields means XLU can decouple from the broad equity tape entirely during pure rate moves — a feature that the AI-power-demand re-rating has partially muddied.

## Methodology & Expense Ratio

XLU follows the standard Select Sector methodology: GICS utilities members of the S&P 500, float-adjusted market-cap weighted with capping rules, rebalanced quarterly. The expense ratio is **0.09%**.

## How It Trades — Liquidity & Options

XLU is liquid with tight spreads and a functioning options market (weekly expirations, $1 strikes near the money, American-style physical settlement). Implied vol is structurally low given the defensive profile, but it has risen with the dispersion between rate-sensitive regulated names and AI-power-demand winners. Options activity picks up around rate moves and the bond market. Traders use XLU as a rate-expression vehicle and a low-beta defensive sleeve.

Because realized vol is structurally low, XLU options carry a relatively modest [[volatility-risk-premium|volatility risk premium]] in absolute terms, though the premium can be attractive on a risk-adjusted basis. Common option uses include: covered-call overlays on a defensive utilities sleeve to enhance the already-high dividend yield; protective collars when long the sector into a rate-decision catalyst (FOMC); and calendar/dispersion trades expressing the regulated-vs-merchant divergence. Skew is generally mild relative to high-beta sectors, but downside skew steepens during rate-shock or risk-off episodes when XLU is sought as a haven.

## AI Power-Demand Angle

A defining recent driver for XLU is [[ai-data-center-power-demand|AI data-center power demand]]. The explosive growth of AI compute has sharply raised forecasts for US electricity consumption after decades of flat demand, re-rating utilities — especially nuclear-heavy independent power producers like Constellation and Vistra that can sell firm, low-carbon baseload power to hyperscalers. This theme has, at times, made parts of the historically sleepy utilities sector trade more like a growth story, partly decoupling XLU from its pure bond-proxy behavior.

VPU covers a broader, all-cap utilities universe than XLU's S&P-500-only basket. XLU remains the most liquid and the standard defensive/rate-rotation building block. Within the SPDR family, XLU sits alongside the other defensives — [[xlp|XLP]] (consumer staples) and [[xlv|XLV]] (health care) — and is most often paired against cyclicals like [[xli|XLI]] in rotation trades. For purer thematic exposure to the AI-power-demand story, traders may favor concentrated independent-power-producer positions or nuclear/clean-energy ETFs over the diluted exposure XLU provides.

## Use in Sector Rotation

In [[sector-rotation|sector-rotation]] frameworks, XLU is a flagship **defensive, rate-sensitive** sleeve. Allocators rotate into utilities during late-cycle slowdowns, risk-off episodes, and especially **falling-rate** regimes (where the bond-proxy gets a valuation tailwind). XLU tends to lag in early-cycle, rising-rate, risk-on environments — except where the AI-power-demand theme provides an offsetting growth tailwind. The XLU/[[spy|SPY]] ratio and XLU's correlation to Treasury yields are watched as defensive/rate signals.

## Risks

- **Interest-rate risk** — the dominant risk; rising long-term rates pressure XLU like long-duration bonds.
- **Regulatory risk** — returns depend on rate cases and allowed ROEs set by regulators.
- **Defensive underperformance** — lags in strong bull markets and rising-rate regimes.
- **Theme reversal** — the AI-power-demand re-rating could unwind if data-center demand forecasts disappoint, hitting power producers like Constellation and Vistra.
