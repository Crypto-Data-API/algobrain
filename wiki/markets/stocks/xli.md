XLI is the Industrial Select Sector SPDR Fund, issued by State Street Global Advisors. It tracks the Industrial Select Sector Index — the [[industrials|industrials]] slice of the [[s-and-p-500|S&P 500]] under the [[gics-classification|GICS]] framework. XLI is the most liquid US industrials sector ETF and the standard vehicle for expressing exposure to aerospace, machinery, transportation, and diversified manufacturers. As an early/mid-cycle cyclical, it is closely watched as a proxy for the manufacturing economy.

## What It Tracks

XLI holds the industrials constituents of the S&P 500: aerospace and defense, machinery, electrical equipment, building products, transportation (rails, airlines, logistics), and diversified industrial conglomerates. The sector is **cyclical** — earnings track capital spending, manufacturing activity, and global trade. XLI is one of the most sensitive sector ETFs to manufacturing PMI surveys (e.g., the ISM Manufacturing index) and to the broader business cycle.

The underlying **Industrial Select Sector Index** is one of the eleven mutually exclusive Select Sector indices that together reconstitute the [[s-and-p-500|S&P 500]]; XLI plus its siblings ([[xlk]], [[xlf]], [[xlv]], [[xly]], [[xlp]], [[xlu]], [[xle]], [[xlb]], [[xlre]], [[xlc]]) equal the full index. Industrials is a mid-weight S&P sector — large enough to make XLI one of the more heavily traded SPDRs, and broad enough that it spans several quite different end-markets (defense budgets, freight cycles, construction capex) under one ticker.

The fund's relatively balanced weighting (top holdings each in the low-single-digit to mid-single-digit percent range) means no one stock dominates XLI's moves the way Exxon does XLE or UnitedHealth does XLV.

### Concentration Dynamics

XLI is among the **least concentrated** of the Select Sector SPDRs. Where [[xly|XLY]] is hostage to two mega-caps and [[xlk|XLK]] to a handful, XLI's top names sit in the low-to-mid single digits and the fund is spread across aerospace, machinery, transport, and conglomerates. The practical upshot: XLI is a cleaner *macro* proxy than most sector ETFs — it expresses the industrial cycle rather than the fortunes of one or two stocks — and idiosyncratic single-name blowups (most notably Boeing) move the fund less than concentrated peers, though aerospace as a *group* still carries weight. This balance is one reason allocators favor XLI for cycle expressions: the signal is the economy, not a single balance sheet.

## Sector Drivers & Macro Sensitivities

| Driver | Effect on XLI |
|--------|---------------|
| **Manufacturing PMI / ISM** | The headline cyclical gauge; rising PMIs are a tailwind, sub-50 contractions a headwind. |
| **Business / capex cycle** | Machinery and electrical-equipment orders track corporate capital spending. |
| **Global growth & trade** | Many constituents are global exporters; trade flows and FX matter. |
| **Defense budgets** | Aerospace & defense names (RTX, defense primes) lean on government spending — a partial counter-cyclical buffer. |
| **Freight & transport volumes** | Rail/air/logistics names track shipping demand; a leading indicator of activity. |
| **Tariffs & supply chains** | Trade policy and input costs directly hit manufacturer margins. |
| **Interest rates** | Higher rates dampen the capex cycle that drives a chunk of industrial demand. |
| **Electrification / reshoring** | Secular tailwinds — grid build-out, factory reshoring, and infrastructure spend support electrical-equipment and building-products names. |

## Seasonality & Behavior vs the Broad Index

XLI is a **high-beta cyclical** (beta around or above 1 to [[spy|SPY]]). Its relative performance is driven by the cycle rather than the calendar: the XLI/[[spy|SPY]] ratio tends to rise during early/mid-cycle expansions and accelerating-PMI windows, and to roll over ahead of slowdowns — making it a watched **risk-on / risk-off** barometer. Industrials often lead the broad index out of recessions (early-cycle outperformance) and lag late-cycle as defensives take over. Because of its global-trade exposure, XLI is also one of the sector ETFs most reactive to tariff and trade-policy headlines, which can drive sharp, news-dependent moves independent of the domestic cycle.

## Methodology & Expense Ratio

XLI follows the standard Select Sector methodology: GICS industrials members of the S&P 500, float-adjusted market-cap weighted, with capping rules for diversification compliance, rebalanced quarterly. The expense ratio is **0.09%**.

## How It Trades — Liquidity & Options

XLI is highly liquid with tight spreads and an active options market (weekly expirations, $1 strikes near the money, American-style physical settlement). Implied volatility tracks the broad market but rises around manufacturing-data releases, trade-policy headlines, and major aerospace/defense news. Because XLI is a clean cyclical proxy, traders use it for macro expressions of the business cycle and as a pair-trade leg against defensives.

Given its broad, balanced basket, XLI options are popular for **macro hedging** — buying puts to hedge a cyclical book ahead of ISM/PMI prints, or selling premium to harvest the [[volatility-risk-premium|VRP]] in a sector whose IV is rarely as extreme as single names. Skew steepens during growth scares and tariff escalations. The XLI/[[xlu|XLU]] and XLI/[[xlp|XLP]] options pairs are used to express risk-on/risk-off views with defined risk, and dispersion trades exploit the gap between XLI's diversified (lower) vol and the higher vol of its aerospace/machinery components.

## Alternatives

| ETF | Issuer | Notes |
|-----|--------|-------|
| **XLI** | State Street (SPDR) | S&P 500 industrials only; balanced large-cap weighting; 0.09% |
| **VIS** | Vanguard | Broader all-cap US industrials index; more mid/small-cap names; low fee |
| **IYJ** | iShares | US industrials; similar large-cap tilt |
| **ITA / PPA / IYT** | iShares / Invesco | Narrower plays (aerospace & defense; transportation) |

VIS captures a broader, all-cap slice of industrials than XLI's S&P-500-only basket. XLI remains the most liquid and the default rotation vehicle. For investors who want only a slice of the sector, the narrower funds matter: **ITA/PPA** isolate aerospace & defense (removing freight and machinery beta), while **IYT** is a pure transportation play (the old "Dow Transports" idea, a classic leading indicator). Within the SPDR family XLI clusters with [[xlb|XLB]] (materials) as the "global industrial cycle" trade and is most often paired against the defensives [[xlu|XLU]], [[xlp|XLP]], and [[xlv|XLV]].

## Use in Sector Rotation

In [[sector-rotation|sector-rotation]] frameworks, XLI is a flagship **early/mid-cycle cyclical**. Allocators rotate into industrials when manufacturing PMIs are rising and the economy is accelerating, and out when growth is rolling over. The XLI/[[spy|SPY]] ratio is a popular cyclical-leadership gauge, and industrials are often paired against defensives ([[xlu|utilities]], [[xlp|staples]]) to express a risk-on / risk-off macro view. XLI also correlates with [[xlb|materials]] as a "global industrial cycle" cluster.

## Risks

- **Cyclicality** — XLI is among the most cycle-sensitive sectors; recessions and PMI contractions hit it hard.
- **Trade & tariff risk** — global manufacturers are exposed to tariffs, supply-chain disruption, and currency swings.
- **Single-name event risk** — aerospace names (Boeing in particular) can carry idiosyncratic operational/regulatory risk.
- **Rate & capex sensitivity** — higher rates can dampen the capital-spending cycle that drives industrial earnings.
