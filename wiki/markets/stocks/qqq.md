# QQQ (Invesco Nasdaq-100 ETF)

QQQ is the Invesco QQQ Trust, tracking the [[nasdaq|Nasdaq-100]] index — the 100 largest non-financial companies listed on the Nasdaq exchange. Launched in March 1999, QQQ is the second-most-traded ETF in the world by volume and the primary vehicle for gaining exposure to U.S. large-cap technology and growth stocks. It is heavily concentrated in the "Magnificent 7" mega-cap tech names, which makes it a higher-beta, longer-duration cousin of [[spy|SPY]].

## What It Tracks / Methodology

QQQ tracks the **Nasdaq-100**, a modified-market-cap-weighted index of the 100 largest non-financial companies listed on Nasdaq. Methodology details that matter for traders:

- **Non-financial only** — banks, insurers, and most diversified financials are *excluded* by construction (they live on the Nasdaq Financial-100 instead). This is a defining difference from [[s-and-p-500|the S&P 500]].
- **Nasdaq-listing requirement** — eligibility requires a Nasdaq listing, which is why some mega-caps (e.g. NYSE-listed names) never appear regardless of size.
- **Modified market-cap weighting with caps** — the index applies a capping methodology so no single name (or the cluster of the very largest names) breaches concentration limits that would violate fund-diversification rules. This is the mechanism behind the **special rebalances** (below).
- **Annual reconstitution (December)** — the *membership* is reset once a year in December based on November-end market caps; this is when stocks are added/dropped.
- **Quarterly rebalancing (Mar/Jun/Sep/Dec)** — *weights* are reviewed quarterly, and a capping check can trigger weight adjustments without changing membership.

### Special rebalances

Because the top handful of names have grown so dominant, the Nasdaq-100's diversification caps have occasionally been breached intra-year, forcing an off-cycle **special rebalance**. The notable example was the **July 2023 special rebalance**, when the combined weight of the very largest constituents exceeded the regulatory diversification threshold and Nasdaq redistributed weight away from the mega-caps. Traders watch these because they create predictable, mechanical buying/selling across the index's components.

## Composition & Concentration

QQQ is *not* a pure tech fund, but tech dominates and the top of the index is extremely concentrated.

### Sector tilt (approximate)

| Sector | Weight (approx.) |
|--------|------------------|
| [[technology\|Technology]] | ~50% |
| [[communication-services\|Communication Services]] | ~16% |
| [[consumer-discretionary\|Consumer Discretionary]] | ~13% |
| [[healthcare\|Health Care]] | ~7% |
| [[consumer-staples\|Consumer Staples]] | ~5% |
| [[industrials\|Industrials]] | ~5% |
| Other | ~4% |

**What's excluded:** [[financials|financials]], [[energy|energy]], [[utilities|utilities]], [[real-estate|real estate]], and [[materials|materials]] are absent or minimal. QQQ is a bet on *innovation and growth*, not the broad economy.

### Top-holdings concentration

The top 10 holdings typically account for **45-55%** of QQQ, making it highly concentrated:

- [[apple|Apple (AAPL)]], [[nvidia|Nvidia (NVDA)]], [[microsoft|Microsoft (MSFT)]], [[amazon|Amazon (AMZN)]], Meta (META), Alphabet (GOOG/GOOGL), Broadcom (AVGO), [[tesla|Tesla (TSLA)]], Costco (COST), Netflix (NFLX)

This concentration means QQQ's performance is driven by a handful of mega-cap names. When the Magnificent 7 lead, QQQ massively outperforms [[spy|SPY]]; when they lag, the underperformance is equally dramatic. Concentration is *higher* than the S&P 500's (already-record) top-10 weight, which is why QQQ is the cleaner expression of the mega-cap-tech trade.

## History & Long-Run Returns

QQQ launched in March 1999, near the top of the [[dot-com-bubble]] — and its history is a vivid lesson in concentration risk and recovery. Because the Nasdaq-100 skews to high-multiple growth, its drawdowns are deeper than the S&P's, but its bull-market returns are larger.

| Episode | Approx. drawdown | Notes |
|---------|------------------|-------|
| Dot-com bust ([[dot-com-bubble]]) | ~-83% | 2000-2002; Nasdaq-100 fell far more than the S&P's ~-49%; took ~15 years to fully recover |
| Global Financial Crisis ([[2008-global-financial-crisis]]) | ~-54% | 2007-2009 |
| 2018 Q4 selloff | ~-23% | Rate-hike / growth-scare correction |
| COVID crash | ~-28% | Feb-Mar 2020; recovered fastest of the major indices |
| 2022 rate bear | ~-35% | Long-duration growth hit hardest by rising real yields (vs SPY ~-25%) |

The takeaway: QQQ delivers higher long-run returns during secular tech bulls (2010-2021 it crushed SPY) at the cost of deeper, longer drawdowns when growth multiples compress. The 2000-2002 ~-83% collapse remains the textbook warning against assuming concentrated growth indices recover quickly.

QQQ is essentially SPY with a growth/tech tilt and no financial or energy ballast. In secular tech bull markets it leads; in rate-hiking environments that punish growth multiples it lags.

## QQQ vs QQQM

Invesco runs a near-identical sibling, **QQQM (Invesco Nasdaq-100 ETF)**, tracking the same Nasdaq-100:

| Feature | QQQ | QQQM |
|---------|-----|------|
| **Structure** | UIT | Open-end ETF |
| **Expense ratio** | ~0.20% | ~0.15% (lower) |
| **Share price** | Higher | Lower (more accessible for small buys) |
| **Liquidity / options** | Deepest; huge options market | Thinner; minimal options |
| **Best for** | Active trading, options, hedging | Long-term buy-and-hold |

The rule mirrors SPY-vs-VOO: **trade QQQ, hold QQQM.** QQQM exists specifically so long-term holders can own the same index at a lower fee, while QQQ retains the deep liquidity and options ecosystem that traders need. (QQQ deliberately keeps its fee where it is rather than competing down, because its value to traders is liquidity, not cost.)

### Options on QQQ

QQQ has the second-most-liquid options market after SPY/[[spx-options|SPX]]:

- **0DTE availability** — daily expirations.
- **Strike increments** — $1 near ATM.
- **American-style, physically settled** — shares delivered on exercise; early-assignment risk on short options.
- **Popular strategies** — covered calls, put credit spreads, and strangles/straddles around the earnings of top holdings (a single [[nvidia|NVDA]] or [[apple|AAPL]] report can move the whole ETF several percent).
- For cash-settled, European-style, 60/40-taxed exposure, traders use **NDX index options** (see [[index-options]]).

## Valuation & Regime Behavior

- **Rate sensitivity (the dominant driver).** As a long-duration equity index — high P/E, earnings far in the future — QQQ is *more* sensitive to interest rate changes than [[spy|SPY]]. Rising real [[treasuries|10-year yields]] compress its multiple disproportionately; falling-rate regimes inflate it.
- **Earnings concentration.** QQQ is disproportionately driven by the earnings of its top 10 holdings; a single beat/miss from a mega-cap can swing the index.
- **Risk-on barometer.** The **QQQ/SPY ratio** is a clean gauge of risk appetite — rising ratio = risk-on (growth leading), falling ratio = risk-off (defensives leading).
- **Higher beta, both ways.** ~1.1-1.2 beta to the broad market means QQQ amplifies both rallies and drawdowns versus SPY.

## Notable Episodes

- **Born at the dot-com top (1999)** — QQQ launched months before the Nasdaq peak and then fell ~83%, the single most important cautionary tale for concentrated growth indices.
- **2010-2021 secular tech bull** — QQQ dramatically outperformed SPY as mega-cap tech compounded.
- **2022 growth-multiple compression** — QQQ fell ~35% vs SPY's ~25%, a clean demonstration of long-duration rate sensitivity.
- **July 2023 special rebalance** — diversification caps forced an off-cycle redistribution of weight away from the largest names.
- **The AI/Magnificent-7 era (2023-2026)** — a handful of AI leaders drove the bulk of QQQ's returns, pushing top-10 concentration to fresh highs.

## Trading Strategies & Uses

- **Growth/tech proxy** — the standard way to express a view on U.S. tech and growth without picking individual names.
- **Pair / relative-value** — long QQQ / short SPY (or vice versa) to express growth-vs-broad-market or tech-vs-value views; one of the most popular macro pair trades.
- **Hedging tech books** — shorting QQQ or buying QQQ puts to hedge a portfolio tilted toward mega-cap tech more precisely than SPY.
- **Premium selling** — selling QQQ options to harvest the [[volatility-risk-premium]]; deep liquidity makes fills easy, though earnings clustering raises gap risk.
- **Momentum expression** — QQQ is a natural vehicle for trend/[[momentum-anomaly|momentum]] systems given its persistent growth leadership in bull regimes.
- **Leveraged day-trading** — TQQQ/SQQQ are among the most-traded leveraged ETFs for intraday directional bets (with the caveat of daily-rebalance decay).

## Risks / Limitations

- **Extreme concentration** — top-10 names ~45-55%; QQQ rises and falls on a few mega-caps, and the 2000-2002 ~-83% drawdown shows how violent the downside can be.
- **No financials/energy/utilities** — QQQ misses entire sectors, so it can badly lag the broad market when value/cyclicals lead.
- **Rate/duration risk** — its high-multiple composition makes it the index most exposed to rising real yields.
- **Earnings gap risk** — clustered mega-cap earnings create outsized single-day moves.
- **Higher fee for holders** — QQQ's ~0.20% is high versus QQQM (~0.15%) and S&P funds; long-term holders overpay if they don't use QQQM.
- **Leverage decay** — TQQQ/SQQQ erode in choppy markets and are unsuitable as long-term holds.
