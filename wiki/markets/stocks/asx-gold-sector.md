The **ASX Gold Sub-Industry** comprises the gold-mining companies listed on the Australian Securities Exchange — roughly 17 names with over A$500M market capitalisation as profiled below. It is a very top-heavy sector — [[northern-star|Northern Star (NST)]] alone has held around 35% of total sector market cap. Many smaller gold companies struggle to maintain consistent profitability (Source: [[alfred-report-asx-gold-sector]]). Australia is one of the world's largest gold producers, and the sector is a core component of ASX resources exposure.

## How Traders Access It

There is a clear ladder from owning the **metal** to owning **leveraged miner equity**:

| Vehicle | Exposure | Beta to gold | Notes |
|---------|----------|--------------|-------|
| Bullion ETFs — [[gld\|GLD]] (US), PMGOLD / GOLD / QAU (ASX) | The metal itself | ~1× | No operational/AISC risk; QAU is AUD-hedged, GOLD/PMGOLD are AUD-unhedged |
| Global miner basket — [[gdx\|GDX]] (VanEck Gold Miners, US-listed) | Global gold-mining equities (incl. some ASX names) | >1× | Diversified miner exposure; AISC and operating leverage |
| ASX resources/miner ETFs — VanEck Australian Resources (MVR) | Broad ASX resources (not pure gold) | varies | Includes gold majors among diversified miners |
| Single ASX names — NST, EVN, PRU, RMS, EMR | Individual Australian producers | highest | Stock-specific cost, jurisdiction, hedging and reserve risk |

- **Single names**: The large producers — NST, EVN (Evolution), PRU (Perseus), RMS (Ramelius), EMR (Emerald) — are traded directly on the [[asx-limited|ASX]].
- **ETFs**: The **VanEck Gold Miners ETF ([[gdx|GDX]])** is the global gold-miner basket (US-listed); ASX-listed options include **VanEck Australian Resources (MVR)** and broad gold-bullion ETFs (PMGOLD, GOLD, QAU) for exposure to the metal rather than miners. To own the metal globally, US-listed **[[gld|GLD]]** is the benchmark bullion ETF.
- **Underlying commodity**: The sector's earnings are leveraged to the AUD gold price — see [[gold]]. Because Australian miners sell USD-priced gold and pay AUD costs, a weaker AUD amplifies margins.

### How it trades vs GLD and global miners

- **Miners vs [[gld|GLD]] (the metal):** ASX gold equities carry **operating leverage** — a 10% move in the gold price typically drives a *larger* percentage move in low-cost producers' earnings (and share prices), because costs (AISC) are broadly fixed. The flip side is that miners *underperform* bullion when gold falls, and add company-specific risks (cost blowouts, mine outages, reserve downgrades, jurisdiction) that [[gld|GLD]] does not. GLD is the clean way to own the metal; the miners are the geared way to own a *gold view*.
- **ASX miners vs [[gdx|GDX]] (global miners):** [[gdx|GDX]] is a globally diversified, USD-denominated basket (North American, African and Australian names). ASX single names add (1) **AUD/USD leverage** — the AUD-cost / USD-revenue structure that boosts Australian margins when the AUD falls — and (2) **jurisdiction/concentration** specifics (e.g. Perseus is Africa-focused, Northern Star dominates the local index). ASX names can therefore diverge from GDX on FX and Australian-specific factors even when global gold is flat.

## Key Drivers

- **AUD gold price**: The dominant earnings driver. As of mid-2026 gold trades around **A$5,000-5,300/oz** — a dramatic rise from the ~A$3,500 level of early 2025 — driven by record USD gold prices and central-bank buying. Australian producers with all-in sustaining costs (AISC) well below spot enjoy very wide margins at these levels.
- **AUD/USD**: A falling Australian dollar boosts AUD-denominated revenue (gold is priced in USD), a structural tailwind unique to Australian miners.
- **Production cost per ounce (AISC)**: The key differentiator between miners — low-cost producers (e.g. Perseus) retain margin even if gold pulls back.
- **Real yields and the US dollar**: Gold (and thus the miners) tends to rise when real interest rates fall and the USD weakens.
- **Operating leverage**: Miner equities move with greater amplitude than the metal itself — a high-beta way to express a gold view.

## Typical Catalysts

- Spot gold breakouts/breakdowns and AUD/USD moves
- Quarterly production reports and AISC guidance
- M&A (the sector consolidates regularly — Northern Star's acquisitions, De Grey takeover interest)
- US Fed rate decisions and real-yield shifts

### Tiering: majors, mid-tier, juniors

ASX gold miners span a spectrum from large, cash-generative producers to pre-revenue explorers — a useful way to frame risk/reward:

| Tier | Profile | Examples (from the table above) | Risk character |
|------|---------|--------------------------------|----------------|
| **Majors / large producers** | Multi-mine, >500koz/yr, index-relevant, dividend-paying | [[northern-star\|Northern Star (NST)]], Evolution (EVN) | Lowest operational risk; valuation/P/E-expansion risk; [[asx-200]] members |
| **Mid-tier producers** | Single-to-few mines, solid production, growth-oriented | Perseus (PRU), Ramelius (RMS), Emerald (EMR) | Cost position and single-asset concentration matter most |
| **Juniors / developers / explorers** | Pre-production or early production, no/low earnings | De Grey (DEG, pre-production) | Highest leverage *and* highest risk (financing, permitting, dilution); many run at negative P/E |

The sector is extremely **top-heavy**: [[northern-star|NST]] alone has held ~35% of total sector market cap, so the "ASX gold sector" beta is heavily a Northern Star beta. The numerous loss-making juniors (7 of 17 names with negative P/E, per the source report) offer the most explosive upside on a gold rally but carry binary financing/execution risk.

### Hedging and costs (AISC)

- **AISC (All-In Sustaining Cost):** the headline cost metric — cash costs plus sustaining capex and corporate overhead, per ounce. The **margin = realised gold price − AISC** is the single most important miner economic. Low-AISC producers (e.g. Perseus, A$1,459/oz production cost in the source data) retain margin even if gold pulls back; high-cost miners are squeezed first in a downcycle. The full cost ladder runs cash cost → AISC → all-in cost (AIC, including growth capex).
- **Hedging:** miners can lock in forward gold prices to secure cashflow (often required by lenders for project finance). In a *rising* gold market, hedged producers **forgo upside** (selling below spot), while **unhedged** producers capture the full price move — a key differentiator in a bull market like 2023–2026. In a falling market, hedges protect cashflow. Whether a name is hedged or running spot exposure materially changes its beta to the gold price.
- **AUD/USD overlay:** because revenue is USD and costs are AUD, the *AUD* gold price (and thus the AUD/USD rate) drives realised margins — a structural advantage Australian producers have over USD-cost global peers in [[gdx|GDX]].
- **Operating leverage:** with costs broadly fixed, profit swings amplify gold-price swings — the reason miner equities are a high-beta way to express a [[gold]] view.

### ASX-200 weighting and index relevance

- Several majors ([[northern-star|NST]], EVN) are [[asx-200]] constituents, so gold-sector strength feeds into the headline index — but gold mining is a *minority* slice of the materials-heavy ASX, which is dominated by iron-ore and diversified majors ([[bhp]], Rio Tinto) and the banks.
- Because [[northern-star|Northern Star]] is so dominant within the gold cohort, its index weight and corporate actions (M&A, production updates) move "the ASX gold trade" more than any peer.
- For passive exposure, broad [[asx-200]] funds give only diluted gold-miner exposure; investors wanting concentrated exposure use single names or the global [[gdx|GDX]]/bullion-ETF routes above.

## Notable Observations

- **Perseus Mining (PRU)** shows the strongest fundamentals: lowest P/E (7.48), highest GP% (59.50%), best ROE (22.06%), zero debt. Operates solely in Africa. 54% institutional ownership (Vanguard, Fidelity, iShares). Lower production cost per oz (A$1,459) than NST (A$1,759).

- **Evolution Mining (EVN)** has a high P/E of 42, low ROE of 5%, and low dividend yield of 1.24% — less attractive than NST despite being the #2 by market cap.

- **7 of 17 stocks** have negative P/E ratios, reflecting the high proportion of unprofitable gold miners on the ASX.

- The sector has benefited from a multi-year gold bull market: AUD gold ran from ~A$2,500/oz (2023) to ~A$3,500/oz (early 2025) and on to roughly **A$5,000-5,300/oz by mid-2026**. At these prices low-cost ASX producers generate exceptional free cash flow; the risk shifts from margin to valuation (P/E expansion) and to a potential gold-price reversal.

- Gold production cost per ounce is a critical differentiator — companies with lower all-in costs have more margin protection if gold price dips.

## Risks

- **Gold-price reversal** — the dominant risk; with the sector having ridden a multi-year bull market, a sustained fall in the AUD gold price would compress the wide margins now embedded in valuations (the risk has shifted from margin to **valuation/P/E expansion**).
- **AUD/USD risk** — a *stronger* Australian dollar erodes AUD-denominated revenue (the mirror image of the structural tailwind).
- **Cost inflation** — rising labour, energy, diesel and consumables push up AISC and erode margins even if gold holds.
- **Operating / single-asset risk** — mine outages, grade/reserve downgrades, permitting delays and geotechnical issues; concentrated single-mine producers are most exposed.
- **Jurisdiction risk** — offshore operations (e.g. Africa-focused producers) add sovereign, fiscal and currency risk on top of mining risk.
- **Junior financing / dilution risk** — pre-production developers and explorers (many running negative P/E) face financing, permitting and equity-dilution risk and can be wiped out on a gold pullback.
- **Concentration risk** — the sector beta is dominated by [[northern-star|Northern Star]]; a single name's stumble disproportionately moves the cohort.
- **Hedging mismatch** — heavily hedged miners forgo upside in a rising market; poorly timed hedges can destroy value.
- **Real-yield / USD regime shift** — gold (and the miners) tend to fall when real interest rates rise and the USD strengthens.
