Aerospace & Defense (A&D) is the industry that designs and manufactures military hardware, weapons systems, and both military and commercial aircraft. It spans **defense primes** (the large contractors that build fighters, missiles, warships, and combat systems for governments), **commercial aerospace** (airliners, engines, and the aftermarket that services them), and the **suppliers and component makers** that feed both. For investors it matters because defense revenue is largely **government-budget-driven and counter-cyclical**, orders are visible years in advance through multi-year **backlogs**, and the group offers a defensive, geopolitically-levered pocket inside an otherwise cyclical sector.

## GICS Placement

Aerospace & Defense is **not a top-level [[gics-classification|GICS]] sector** — it is a single industry sitting inside the **[[industrials|Industrials]]** sector, within the **Capital Goods** industry group. [[industrials]] is the parent sector and covers A&D as one of its sub-industries (alongside machinery, transportation, electrical equipment, and building products).

| GICS level | Name | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Sector | **[[industrials|Industrials]]** | ~9% of the [[s-and-p-500]]; the parent sector |
| Industry Group | Capital Goods | Equipment makers, defense primes, machinery |
| Industry | **Aerospace & Defense** | Defense contractors + commercial aircraft/engines/parts |

Because it is an industry rather than a sector, A&D has no dedicated "select sector SPDR." Traders access it through **thematic industry ETFs** (ITA, XAR, PPA — see below) or through the broad [[industrials|Industrials]] proxy [[xli|XLI]], where the defense primes and aerospace names carry outsized weight.

## Structure of the Industry

The industry breaks into four fairly distinct groups, each with different customers, economics, and cyclicality.

### Defense primes

The large contractors that integrate complete weapons systems — aircraft, missiles, warships, armored vehicles, radars, and combat electronics — and sell them mainly to governments.

- **Examples:** [[lockheed-martin]] (F-35 fighter, missiles, space), [[northrop-grumman]] (B-21 bomber, missiles, space, defense electronics), [[rtx|RTX]] (formerly Raytheon — missiles, defense electronics, plus Pratt & Whitney engines and Collins aerospace systems), [[general-dynamics]] (submarines and surface combatants via Electric Boat/Bath, combat vehicles, plus Gulfstream business jets), [[boeing]] (its Defense, Space & Security arm), and [[l3harris]] (communications, sensors, and mission systems).
- **Economics:** long program lifecycles, deep barriers to entry, and multi-year order **backlogs** that smooth revenue. Government (chiefly the US DoD) is the dominant customer, so growth tracks the defense budget rather than the business cycle.

### Commercial aerospace

The civil side — airliners, aircraft engines, and the high-margin aftermarket (spare parts, maintenance, overhaul) that services the installed fleet.

- **Examples:** [[boeing]] (one half of the airliner duopoly), **Airbus** ([[airbus|Airbus]] — forward link; the other half, Europe-listed), and engine makers including [[rtx|Pratt & Whitney]] (part of RTX), **GE Aerospace** ([[ge-aerospace|GE Aerospace]] — forward link; the engines business spun out of [[general-electric|General Electric]]), and Rolls-Royce and CFM/Safran on the European side.
- **Economics:** driven by air-travel demand, airline profitability, and fleet-replacement cycles rather than government budgets. The **aftermarket** (services on jets already flying) is the durable, high-margin annuity; new-build production is lumpier and more cyclical.

### Suppliers & components

The "picks and shovels" of the airframe and platform — fasteners, castings, actuators, avionics, and specialty materials sold into both defense and commercial programs.

- **Examples:** [[howmet-aerospace]] (engineered castings, fasteners, titanium structures, jet-engine components), [[transdigm]] (highly-engineered proprietary components with a large aftermarket franchise), and a long tail of tier-1/tier-2 suppliers.
- **Economics:** exposure to build rates on both defense and commercial platforms, with the proprietary/aftermarket names ([[transdigm]]) commanding premium margins and pricing power.

### Defense-tech / disruptors

A newer cohort applying software, autonomy, and commercial-tech economics to defense — challenging the traditional primes on cost and speed.

- **Examples:** [[kratos-defense]] (unmanned/target drones, hypersonics, tactical systems), [[palantir-technologies|Palantir]] (data/AI software for defense and intelligence), and **Anduril** ([[anduril|Anduril]] — forward link; still **private**, autonomy and counter-drone systems).
- **Economics:** faster iteration, more software content, and often fixed-price commercial-style contracting — but smaller scale and, for the private names, no listed equity yet.

## Business Characteristics

- **Multi-year backlogs.** Defense programs run for years to decades; primes carry order backlogs that provide unusual revenue visibility and cushion earnings through downturns. Aerospace order books (commercial jet backlogs) can stretch many years of production.
- **Contract structure — cost-plus vs fixed-price.** Development and higher-risk work is often **cost-plus** (the customer reimburses costs plus a fee, capping contractor risk), while mature production tends to be **fixed-price** (the contractor bears cost overruns). Fixed-price development contracts have periodically produced large charges when programs run over budget.
- **Government as dominant customer.** The **US Department of Defense** is the single largest buyer for the primes, with allied governments (NATO members, Indo-Pacific allies) and foreign military sales adding demand. Revenue is therefore a function of appropriations and procurement priorities.
- **High barriers to entry.** Security clearances, decades of program know-how, certified supply chains, and enormous capital and engineering bases make the prime tier extremely hard to enter — reinforcing an oligopolistic structure.
- **Export controls / ITAR.** Defense articles are tightly regulated (in the US, ITAR — the International Traffic in Arms Regulations, plus EAR), constraining who can buy what and adding compliance complexity to foreign sales.
- **Program concentration risk.** A single flagship program (e.g., a marquee fighter or bomber) can drive a meaningful share of a prime's revenue, so program delays, cost overruns, or cancellation are material single-name risks.
- **Free-cash-flow focus.** Because reported earnings are smoothed by long-cycle accounting, investors and management emphasize **free-cash-flow conversion** and cash returns (buybacks, dividends) as the key value drivers.

## Demand Drivers

- **Defense budgets.** The primary driver — US DoD appropriations set the tone, with NATO members' commitments to spend **2%+ of GDP** on defense (and rising targets since 2022) broadening the demand base across Europe and allied nations.
- **Geopolitical conflict and tension.** Active conflicts and rising threat perception (Russia–Ukraine, the Middle East, and Indo-Pacific/Taiwan tensions) accelerate procurement, munitions replenishment, and multi-year budget growth.
- **Munitions and replenishment.** Sustained conflict draws down missile, artillery, and air-defense stockpiles, driving multi-year re-stocking orders.
- **Commercial-air travel recovery & fleet replacement.** Passenger-traffic growth and airlines replacing older jets with newer, more fuel-efficient aircraft drive commercial build rates and the engine/aftermarket cycle.
- **Space.** Government and commercial demand for launch, satellites, missile-warning, and space-based communications is a growing end market for several primes and specialists.

## Cyclicality & Regime Behavior

A&D is really **two cyclical profiles bolted together**:

- **Defense — counter-cyclical / defensive.** Government defense spending is set by budgets and geopolitics, not the business cycle. Backlog visibility and a non-economic customer make the defense primes a **defensive pocket** within cyclical [[industrials]] — they tend to hold up (or rally on geopolitical stress) when the rest of the sector is de-rating. See [[defensive-sectors]] and [[sector-rotation]].
- **Commercial aerospace — cyclical.** Airliner and engine demand tracks air-travel demand and airline profitability, which are pro-cyclical and sensitive to recessions, fuel prices, and shocks (a pandemic grounds fleets; a boom fills order books). The aftermarket is stickier than new-build.

Because of this split, "defense" is often bought as a **counter-cyclical hedge** (long defense / geopolitics), while "aerospace" (Boeing, engines, travel-levered names) trades more like a mid-cycle cyclical. In a [[sector-rotation]] framework, defense names are a place to hide when growth rolls over, whereas commercial-aero build-rate plays lead in recovery.

## Economic Drivers & Sensitivity

| Driver | Direction | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **US DoD budget / appropriations** | Positive (defense) | The dominant demand signal; procurement and R&D lines set prime revenue |
| **NATO / allied defense spending** | Positive (defense) | 2%+ GDP commitments and re-armament broaden the customer base beyond the US |
| **Geopolitical conflict / threat level** | Positive (defense) | Conflict accelerates procurement and munitions replenishment |
| **Air-travel demand / airline profits** | Positive (aero) | Drives commercial build rates and the engine/aftermarket cycle |
| **Fleet age / replacement cycle** | Positive (aero) | Older fleets and fuel efficiency push new-aircraft orders |
| **Business cycle / recession** | Inverse (aero) | Commercial aero is cyclical; defense is largely insulated |
| **Continuing resolutions / budget gridlock** | Inverse (defense) | Stopgap funding and shutdowns delay new-program starts and awards |
| **Program execution** | Idiosyncratic | Cost overruns on fixed-price development trigger charges; delays hit single names |
| **Interest rates / risk appetite** | Mild | Defense is a lower-beta, income-oriented pocket; aero is more rate/cycle sensitive |

Within the cyclical spectrum, defense sits toward the **defensive** end (budget-driven, low economic beta) while commercial aerospace sits in the **mid-cyclical** range — one reason the A&D industry as a whole is steadier than deep-cyclical machinery or transports inside [[industrials]].

## Notable Episodes & History

- **Post-9/11 buildup (2001–2010s).** The wars in Afghanistan and Iraq drove a sustained rise in US defense spending, lifting the primes through the decade.
- **Sequestration (2013).** The Budget Control Act's automatic spending caps ("sequestration") squeezed US defense budgets, pressuring the group and creating a multi-year period of constrained procurement before budgets recovered.
- **737 MAX (2019–2021).** The grounding of Boeing's best-selling narrow-body after two crashes — compounded by the 2020 pandemic collapse in air travel — devastated commercial aerospace, hammered [[boeing]] and its suppliers, and became the defining single-name execution shock in the sector (see [[industrials]]).
- **COVID air-travel collapse (2020).** Commercial aerospace and airlines were among the worst-hit equities as fleets were parked; the defense side proved far more resilient thanks to stable budgets.
- **Ukraine / NATO re-rating (2022– ).** Russia's invasion of Ukraine drove a step-change in European defense commitments, munitions replenishment, and multi-year budget-growth expectations — re-rating the defense primes and ITA/XAR. This defense-spending regime shift remains a central theme.

## Risks

- **Budget / continuing-resolution risk.** Defense demand hinges on appropriations; budget gridlock, continuing resolutions, shutdowns, or a future sequestration-style squeeze can delay awards and cap growth.
- **Program execution.** Cost overruns on fixed-price development, schedule slips, or cancellation of a flagship program are material single-name risks and have produced large charges.
- **Geopolitical de-escalation.** A durable easing of conflict or a peace settlement can deflate the elevated defense-spending expectations that support current valuations.
- **Commercial-aero cyclicality.** The aerospace side is exposed to recessions, air-travel shocks, fuel prices, and airline financial health — and to production/quality problems (the 737 MAX being the archetype).
- **Single-customer concentration.** Heavy reliance on the US DoD (and a handful of flagship programs) concentrates political and procurement risk.
- **Export controls / policy.** ITAR/EAR and shifting export-approval politics can constrain foreign sales and reshape addressable markets.
- **Supply chain & inputs.** Titanium, castings, engine parts, and skilled-labor constraints can throttle build rates on both defense and commercial platforms.
