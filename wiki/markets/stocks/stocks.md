# Stocks

A **stock** (also called a **share** or **equity**) is a security representing fractional ownership of a corporation. Owning a share makes you a part-owner of the business, with a residual claim on its assets and future earnings after all creditors, bondholders, and preferred shareholders have been paid. Stocks are the single most widely held and traded financial asset class; collectively they make up the equity portion of the global capital structure that sits *below* debt in priority but *above* it in long-run expected return.

This page covers the **instrument itself** — what a share is, the rights it confers, and why its price moves. For where and how shares trade, see [[stock-markets]]; for baskets of stocks tracked as a benchmark, see [[equity-index]]; for the navigation hub of this section, see [[stocks-overview]].

## What Equity Ownership Means

When a company incorporates, its ownership is divided into a fixed number of **shares**. Buying one entitles the holder to a proportional slice of everything the company is and earns. Unlike a bond — a *contractual* promise to repay a fixed sum — a share is a *residual* claim: shareholders are last in line in liquidation and have no guaranteed payment, but they capture the entire upside once senior claims are satisfied. This asymmetry (limited downside, unlimited upside) is the defining economic feature of equity.

The three core rights of a common shareholder are:

1. **Residual claim on earnings and assets** — a pro-rata share of profits (paid as [[dividend|dividends]] or retained to grow the business) and of net assets if the firm is wound up.
2. **Voting rights** — a say in corporate governance: electing directors, approving mergers, and voting on major resolutions, typically one vote per share.
3. **Limited liability** — the most a shareholder can lose is the amount invested. Personal assets are never at risk for the company's debts. This legal innovation is what made broad public investment in business possible.

## Common Stock vs Preferred Stock

| Feature | Common stock | Preferred stock |
|---|---|---|
| Voting rights | Yes (usually) | Usually none |
| Dividend | Variable, discretionary | Fixed/stated, paid before common |
| Priority in liquidation | Last | Ahead of common, behind debt |
| Upside participation | Full | Capped (behaves bond-like) |
| Price behaviour | Driven by earnings/growth | Driven by interest rates |

**Common stock** is what most investors mean by "a stock." **Preferred stock** is a hybrid: it pays a fixed dividend and ranks ahead of common shares for dividends and in liquidation, but it usually carries no vote and limited upside, so it trades more like a [[bonds|bond]] than an equity.

## Share Classes and Voting Structures

Many companies issue **multiple share classes** with different voting power. A common arrangement gives founders super-voting shares (e.g., Class B with 10 votes) while the public buys low-vote shares (Class A with 1 vote), letting insiders retain control with a minority economic stake — Alphabet (GOOGL/GOOG), Meta, and Berkshire Hathaway (BRK.A/BRK.B) are well-known examples. Dual-class structures are controversial: they entrench management and are excluded from some [[equity-index|index]] methodologies. Always check which class you are buying; economic and voting rights can differ sharply.

## Capital Return: Dividends and Buybacks

A profitable company can do two things with earnings: **reinvest** them (funding future growth) or **return** them to shareholders. There are two channels for return:

- **[[dividend|Dividends]]** — periodic cash payments, typically quarterly in the US. Measured by [[dividend-yield]] (annual dividend ÷ price) and [[dividend-payout-ratio]] (dividends ÷ earnings). Favoured by income investors and [[dividend-investing]] strategies.
- **Share buybacks** — the company repurchases its own shares on the open market, shrinking the share count so each remaining share owns a larger slice of earnings (boosting earnings-per-share). Buybacks are more tax-efficient and flexible than dividends and have become the dominant form of US capital return.

Both reduce the cash on the balance sheet but, all else equal, transfer value to shareholders. Reinvested dividends are also the largest single contributor to long-run [[equity-index#Price Return vs Total Return|total return]].

## How and Why Stock Prices Move

A share price is the market's real-time estimate of the present value of a company's future cash flows. The classic decomposition is:

> **Price = Earnings × Multiple** &nbsp;(i.e., `P = EPS × P/E`)

So prices move when *either* term changes:

- **Earnings (the fundamentals)** — revenue growth, margins, and profit. Quarterly earnings reports, guidance, and macro conditions (rates, demand) move the **E**. See [[fundamental-analysis]] and [[valuation]].
- **The multiple (sentiment/discount rate)** — what investors will pay per dollar of earnings, driven by [[interest-rates]], risk appetite, growth expectations, and [[sentiment]]. Rising rates compress multiples; optimism expands them.

In the short run, prices also move on supply/demand, positioning, news, and [[market-microstructure|microstructure]] effects. Over the long run, fundamentals dominate — "in the short run a voting machine, in the long run a weighing machine." Frameworks for anticipating moves split into [[fundamental-analysis]] (intrinsic value), [[technical-analysis]] (price/volume patterns), and quantitative/[[momentum]] approaches.

## Equities vs Other Asset Classes

| Asset class | Claim type | Expected return | Volatility | Income | Inflation hedge |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Stocks** | Residual / ownership | Highest (long run) | High | Dividends | Moderate–good |
| [[bonds\|Bonds]] | Contractual / creditor | Moderate | Low–moderate | Coupons | Poor (nominal) |
| Cash / T-bills | Contractual | Lowest | Very low | Interest | Poor |
| [[commodities\|Commodities]] | Real asset | Variable | High | None | Strong |
| Real estate | Real asset + income | Moderate | Moderate (illiquid) | Rent | Good |
| [[crypto\|Crypto]] | Network / speculative | Very high variance | Extreme | Mostly none | Unproven |

Equities sit at the high-return, high-risk end of the spectrum. Combining them with lower-correlation assets is the basis of [[portfolio-theory|diversification]] and asset allocation.

## Total Return and the Long-Run Equity Premium

Stocks generate return through two components, which together define **total return**:

- **Capital gains** — appreciation in share price.
- **[[dividend|Dividends]]** — cash distributions, which when reinvested compound powerfully.

Historically, broad US equities have returned roughly **9–10% nominal (about 6–7% real) per year** over the very long run, meaningfully above bonds and cash. This persistent excess return is the **equity risk premium** — compensation investors demand for bearing equity's higher volatility and [[drawdown]] risk. The premium is *not* free or guaranteed: the [[s-and-p-500]] has fallen 30%+ in 1973–74, 2000–02, 2007–09, and briefly in 2020, and individual stocks can go to zero. The reward exists precisely because the risk is real.

## Risks of Owning Stocks

- **Market (systematic) risk** — the whole market falls; cannot be diversified away (measured by [[beta]]).
- **Idiosyncratic risk** — a single company underperforms or fails; diversifiable by holding many names or an [[equity-index|index fund]].
- **Permanent capital loss** — fraud, bankruptcy, obsolescence can wipe out a position entirely (residual claim = first to be wiped out).
- **Valuation/multiple risk** — paying too high a [[valuation|multiple]] caps future returns even if the business does well.
- **Liquidity risk** — small-cap and thinly traded shares can be hard to exit (see [[liquidity]]).
- **Sequence/behavioural risk** — selling in [[drawdown|drawdowns]] converts paper losses into realised ones.
