# Nikkei 225

The Nikkei 225 (formally the Nikkei Stock Average, ticker **N225**) is Japan's headline equity index, tracking 225 large, actively-traded Japanese companies listed on the Prime market of the Tokyo Stock Exchange. It is compiled and maintained by **Nihon Keizai Shimbun** (the *Nikkei*, Japan's leading business-news publisher), which is why the index carries the newspaper's name. When traders and journalists reference "Japanese stocks" or the mood of Asian markets before the U.S. open, they most often mean the Nikkei.

## What It Tracks

The Nikkei 225 is a barometer of Japan's largest blue-chip corporations, spanning autos, electronics, semiconductors, industrials, banking, retail, and pharmaceuticals. Its constituents are a curated set of 225 highly liquid names selected from the Tokyo Stock Exchange (TSE) Prime market segment. Nikkei Inc. reviews the composition annually (typically an October review, with additional interim changes for delistings, mergers, or bankruptcies) and applies liquidity and sector-balance criteria in a committee-style process rather than a purely mechanical market-cap ranking. This makes it a companion to — and a very different animal from — the broader, market-cap-weighted [[topix]].

## Price-Weighting: The Defining Feature

The single most important thing to understand about the Nikkei 225 is that it is **price-weighted**, exactly like the U.S. [[dow-jones|Dow Jones Industrial Average]] — and unlike the cap-weighted [[s-and-p-500|S&P 500]], [[nasdaq|Nasdaq-100]], or [[topix|TOPIX]].

- **Weighting is driven by share price, not company size.** A constituent's influence on the index is proportional to its per-share price (adjusted by a price-adjustment factor), *not* its total market capitalization. A company with a high nominal share price moves the index more than a much larger company whose shares trade at a lower price.
- **A published divisor keeps the index continuous.** As with the Dow, the Nikkei uses a divisor that is adjusted for [[stock-split|stock splits]], constituent swaps, and other corporate actions so the index level stays comparable over time.
- **High-priced names dominate.** Historically a small cluster of high-priced stocks — notably **Fast Retailing** (the parent of Uniqlo), [[tokyo-electron]], [[advantest]], and [[softbank-group|SoftBank Group]] — has commanded an outsized share of the index's daily moves, regardless of whether they are the largest companies in Japan by [[market-capitalization]]. Fast Retailing in particular has at times been the single heaviest weight in the index despite not being Japan's largest company.

This price-weighting quirk is why the Nikkei and the cap-weighted TOPIX can diverge meaningfully over the same period: a rally in a handful of expensive semiconductor-equipment and retail names can lift the Nikkei even when the broader market (better captured by TOPIX) is flatter.

## Composition & Sectors

The 225 constituents are broad Japanese large-caps, including globally recognised names across several sectors:

| Sector | Representative constituents |
|--------|-----------------------------|
| **Autos & transport** | Toyota, Honda, Nissan |
| **Electronics & consumer tech** | Sony, Panasonic, Nintendo |
| **Semiconductors & equipment** | [[tokyo-electron]], [[advantest]], Renesas |
| **Trading houses / conglomerates** | Mitsubishi Corporation, Mitsui, Itochu |
| **Telecom & tech investing** | [[softbank-group\|SoftBank Group]], SoftBank Corp, NTT |
| **Retail & consumer** | Fast Retailing (Uniqlo), Seven & i |
| **Financials** | Mitsubishi UFJ, Sumitomo Mitsui, [[nomura]] |
| **Industrials & robotics** | Fanuc, Keyence, Komatsu |

Because of price-weighting, the *sector representation* of the Nikkei can differ from its *sector influence* — semiconductor-equipment and retail names punch above their economic weight, while some very large banks and trading houses (whose shares trade at lower nominal prices) carry less index impact than their market cap would imply.

## Key Drivers

The Nikkei is sensitive to a distinctive set of macro and thematic forces:

- **The yen ([[japanese-yen]]).** This is the dominant swing factor. A **weaker yen typically lifts the Nikkei**, because many heavyweight constituents (autos, electronics, machinery, semis) are exporters whose overseas earnings translate into more yen — and become more price-competitive abroad — when the currency falls. A sharply *strengthening* yen tends to pressure the index. The inverse yen/Nikkei relationship is one of the most-watched pair dynamics in Asian markets.
- **[[bank-of-japan|Bank of Japan]] (BOJ) policy.** After decades of ultra-loose settings, the BOJ **ended its negative interest-rate policy in March 2024** — its first rate hike in 17 years — and dismantled its yield-curve-control (YCC) framework. The pace of subsequent normalisation drives the yen and, through it, the Nikkei; hawkish surprises that strengthen the yen are a headwind for exporters.
- **The global tech and [[semiconductors|semiconductor]] cycle.** With [[tokyo-electron]] and [[advantest]] among the most influential price-weighted names, the Nikkei behaves partly as a proxy for the global chip-equipment cycle and AI-driven capex — closely correlated with U.S. semis and the [[nasdaq|Nasdaq]].
- **Corporate-governance reform & buybacks.** The TSE's 2023 push pressing companies trading below book value (price-to-book under 1) to improve capital efficiency — combined with a wave of share buybacks, cross-shareholding unwinds, and dividend increases — has been a structural bull theme for Japanese equities. See [[corporate-governance]].
- **Foreign investor flows.** International investors are the marginal buyers of Japanese large-caps; shifts in foreign sentiment (often expressed through futures) can move the index quickly, making it a sensitive risk-on/risk-off gauge for Asia.

## History & Notable Episodes

- **The 1989 peak and the "lost decades."** The Nikkei reached its record close of **38,915.87 on 29 December 1989**, the apex of Japan's late-1980s asset bubble. The subsequent collapse ushered in a multi-decade bear market and stagnation — the "lost decades" — during which the index spent years far below its bubble high, a canonical case study in financial history of a valuation bubble deflating.
- **Abenomics (from late 2012).** Prime Minister Shinzo Abe's programme of aggressive monetary easing, fiscal stimulus, and structural reform (the "three arrows"), paired with sustained BOJ asset purchases and a weaker yen, drove a powerful multi-year recovery in the Nikkei through the 2010s.
- **Surpassing 1989 at last (2024).** In **February 2024** the Nikkei finally exceeded its 1989 record high for the first time — roughly 34 years later — and pushed above the 40,000 level in the weeks that followed, powered by yen weakness, the semiconductor/AI boom, and the governance-reform theme.
- **The August 2024 plunge.** On **5 August 2024** the Nikkei suffered one of its worst single sessions on record (a roughly 12% drop), triggered by a rapid unwind of the yen [[carry-trade]] after the BOJ hiked and the yen surged. The index rebounded sharply in the following days — a vivid demonstration of how tightly the Nikkei is bound to the currency and to global carry flows.

## Ways to Trade / Access

The Nikkei 225 is a core Asia risk-sentiment barometer and is accessible across instrument classes:

| Instrument | Notes |
|------------|-------|
| **[[futures\|Futures]]** | The most important access route. Nikkei 225 futures trade on the **Osaka Exchange** (part of JPX), on the **CME** (yen- and USD-denominated contracts, useful for global/overnight trading), and on the **Singapore Exchange (SGX)** — giving nearly around-the-clock exposure across time zones. |
| **[[nikkei-options\|Options]]** | Listed Nikkei 225 options (Osaka) are used for hedging and volatility trading; the Nikkei Volatility Index is Japan's equivalent of the U.S. [[vix\|VIX]]. |
| **ETFs** | Domestic Japan-listed ETFs (e.g. Nomura's NEXT FUNDS Nikkei 225) track the index directly. **Note the common trap:** the widely-held U.S.-listed **EWJ tracks MSCI Japan, not the Nikkei 225** — MSCI Japan is a broad, cap-weighted basket, so its holdings, weights, and returns differ from the price-weighted Nikkei. Traders wanting true Nikkei exposure should use Nikkei-specific products, not EWJ. |
| **CFDs / international** | Non-Japanese traders often access the index synthetically via CFDs or via the CME/SGX futures. |

Because Nikkei futures trade during U.S. hours on the CME and SGX, the index is one of the first liquid gauges of how Asia will react to overnight U.S. moves.

## Nikkei vs TOPIX

The two headline Japanese indices are best understood as complements:

| Dimension | Nikkei 225 | [[topix]] |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Weighting** | Price-weighted | Free-float market-cap-weighted |
| **Breadth** | 225 selected large-caps | Broad — historically all/most TSE first-section (now Prime) constituents |
| **Who dominates** | High-priced shares (Fast Retailing, Tokyo Electron, Advantest, SoftBank Group) | Largest companies by market cap (e.g. Toyota, major banks) |
| **Character** | Headline number, media-friendly, futures-heavy | Institutional benchmark, closer to the true economic weight of the market |

Why price-weighting matters: in the Nikkei, a stock split or a big move in a single high-priced name can swing the index in ways that do not reflect the broader market's cap-weighted reality. Serious asset allocators and passive [[index-funds]] often benchmark to TOPIX (or MSCI Japan) for that reason, while the Nikkei remains the number the public and the futures market watch. This is the same price-weighting critique long levelled at the [[dow-jones|Dow]] versus the [[s-and-p-500|S&P 500]] in the U.S.

## Risks / Limitations

- **Price-weighting distortion.** The index over-represents a few high-priced stocks and can misstate the health of the broader market; it is a poor pure gauge of aggregate Japanese equity value versus [[topix]].
- **Currency dependence.** Nikkei returns are heavily entangled with the [[japanese-yen]]; for a foreign investor, a Nikkei rally driven by yen weakness can be partly or wholly offset by currency losses when translated back to their home currency.
- **Concentration in a few names.** A handful of semiconductor-equipment and retail heavyweights drive a large share of moves, so leadership stumbles or a chip-cycle downturn can drag the whole index.
- **Carry-trade fragility.** As August 2024 showed, the index is exposed to sudden global deleveraging when yen-funded carry trades unwind.
