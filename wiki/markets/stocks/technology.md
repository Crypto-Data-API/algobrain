The Information Technology sector is the largest of the 11 [[gics-classification|GICS sectors]], representing approximately **32%** of the [[s-and-p-500]] by market capitalization. It covers companies that develop, manufacture, or distribute technology hardware, software, and services.

## Overview

The IT sector has grown from roughly 15% of the S&P 500 in the early 2000s (after the [[dot-com-bubble]] burst) to over 30% by the mid-2020s, driven by the dominance of mega-cap platform companies and the AI revolution. The sector's outsized weight means its performance largely determines index-level returns — when tech leads, the S&P 500 leads. The listed proxy is [[xlk|XLK]], the Technology Select Sector SPDR.

> **Classification note**: GICS Information Technology does *not* include several companies the public thinks of as "tech." [[alphabet]] (Google) and [[meta-platforms]] (Meta) are in **Communication Services**; [[amazon]] and [[tesla]] are in **Consumer Discretionary**. The "Magnificent Seven" therefore straddle three GICS sectors, which is why XLK behaves differently from a broad mega-cap tech basket like [[qqq|QQQ]].

## GICS Definition & Scope

Under [[gics-classification|GICS]], Information Technology contains three industry groups that resolve into the following industries and representative sub-industries:

| Industry Group | Industries | Sub-Industries (examples) |
|---|---|---|
| **Software & Services** | Software; IT Services | Application Software, Systems Software, IT Consulting, Internet Services & Infrastructure |
| **Technology Hardware & Equipment** | Tech Hardware, Storage & Peripherals; Communications Equipment; Electronic Equipment, Instruments & Components | Computer Hardware, Networking Equipment, Electronic Components |
| **Semiconductors & Semiconductor Equipment** | Semiconductors & Semiconductor Equipment | Semiconductors, Semiconductor Equipment |

The sector splits broadly into **recurring-revenue software** (sticky, subscription, high gross margin) and **cyclical hardware/semiconductors** (capex-driven, inventory cycles). This internal divide matters for trading: software is more rate-sensitive (long-duration cash flows) while semis are more demand-cycle-sensitive (the silicon cycle).

## Key Characteristics

- **High margins**: Software companies often have 70-80%+ gross margins
- **Growth-oriented**: Valuations driven by revenue growth and TAM expansion more than current earnings
- **Cyclical within secular growth**: Semiconductors are cyclical; software is more recurring-revenue
- **AI concentration**: As of 2025-2026, AI-related names ([[nvidia]], [[microsoft]], [[broadcom]]) dominate sector returns
- **Disruption risk**: Incumbents can be displaced rapidly — IBM is the canonical cautionary example

## How Traders Access the Sector

- **Sector ETF**: [[xlk|XLK]] (Technology Select Sector SPDR, ~0.09% expense ratio) is the standard listed proxy for S&P 500 IT exposure; it is market-cap weighted and heavily concentrated in [[apple|AAPL]], [[microsoft|MSFT]], and [[nvidia|NVDA]].
- **Sub-sector ETF**: [[smh-vaneck-semiconductor-etf|SMH]] for the semiconductor complex specifically; SOXX is the iShares alternative.
- **Single names**: deep options chains on [[nvidia|NVDA]], [[apple|AAPL]], [[microsoft|MSFT]], [[broadcom|AVGO]].
- **Relative value**: long XLK / short [[spy|SPY]] to isolate the tech-vs-market spread, or XLK vs a defensive sector ([[utilities|XLU]]) for risk-on/risk-off rotation.

## Key Drivers and Correlations

- **Interest rates** — the dominant macro driver. Tech is the longest-duration equity sector (cash flows weighted far into the future), so it is the most rate-sensitive: it leads when yields fall and lags during tightening.
- **AI capex cycle** — as of 2025-2026, hyperscaler AI spending is the single biggest fundamental driver of semiconductor and infrastructure-software revenue. See [[smh-vaneck-semiconductor-etf|SMH]].
- **Concentration feedback** — because IT is ~32% of the S&P 500, the sector and the index move together with correlation near 1; tech is effectively the market's beta amplifier.

## Trading Relevance

Tech earnings (especially [[nvidia]], [[apple]], [[microsoft]]) are treated as macro events that move the entire [[s-and-p-500]]. The sector offers the deepest [[options]] liquidity outside of index products. [[sector-rotation|Sector rotation]] between tech and value/cyclical sectors is a key macro signal — tech tends to outperform when rates fall and underperform during tightening cycles.

## Economic Drivers & Sensitivity

| Driver | Direction | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Interest rates / real yields** | Strongly inverse | Longest-duration equity sector; cash flows far in the future are discounted hardest — leads when yields fall, lags when they rise |
| **AI capex cycle** | Positive | Hyperscaler spending drives semiconductor and infrastructure-software revenue (see [[smh-vaneck-semiconductor-etf\|SMH]]) |
| **Semiconductor cycle** | Cyclical | Chip demand/inventory swings drive the hardware half of the sector |
| **Enterprise / consumer demand** | Positive | IT budgets and device upgrade cycles |
| **US dollar** | Mild inverse | Mega-caps earn large overseas revenue; a strong dollar trims reported earnings |
| **Regulation (antitrust, export controls)** | Negative skew | Big-tech antitrust, China chip-export restrictions |
| **Risk appetite** | Positive (high beta) | Tech is the market's risk-on amplifier |

Tech's defining macro property is **long duration**: it is the most rate-sensitive sector in the index and sits at the *opposite* end of the inflation-beta spectrum from [[energy]] and [[materials]]. This polarity is the foundation of the [[sector-rotation|tech-vs-energy]] rotation pair.

## Cyclicality & Regime Behavior

Tech is a **secular-growth sector with cyclical sub-components** — it does not map cleanly to the classic cyclical/defensive split.

- **Early cycle / falling rates**: strong **leadership** — long-duration growth re-rates as discount rates fall and risk appetite returns.
- **Mid cycle**: continued participation, especially with a live capex narrative (AI in 2023-2026).
- **Late cycle / rising rates / inflation peak**: classic **laggard** — rate-driven multiple compression; this is the "energy leads, tech lags" regime of 2022.
- **Recession**: software's recurring revenue is relatively defensive; semis and hardware are cyclical and fall harder.
- **Risk-off**: high beta means sharp drawdowns, but mega-cap balance-sheet strength has made parts of tech a paradoxical "safe haven" in some selloffs.

Because IT is ~32% of the index, tech-vs-S&P correlation runs near 1 — the sector is effectively the market's **beta amplifier**. A long [[xlk]] / short [[s-and-p-500|SPY]] position isolates the tech-over-market spread; long [[xlk]] / short a defensive sector ([[utilities|XLU]] or [[xlv]]) is the canonical risk-on/risk-off rotation expression.

## Valuation & Dividend Profile

- **Premium multiples**: Tech persistently trades at the *highest* forward P/E in the index, justified (bulls argue) by superior growth, margins, and returns on capital. Software gross margins frequently run 70-80%+.
- **Growth-over-yield**: Most names reinvest or buy back stock rather than pay large dividends; sector dividend yield is among the *lowest* in the S&P. Mature cash machines (MSFT, AVGO, AAPL) do pay and grow dividends, but yields are modest.
- **Valuation risk**: high multiples mean the sector is acutely vulnerable to rate shocks and growth disappointments — small changes in the discount rate move prices sharply.
- **Concentration premium**: the AI mega-caps carry valuations that can detach from the rest of the sector, creating a two-tier valuation structure.

## Notable Episodes & History

- **Dot-com bubble & bust (1999-2002)**: tech peaked near ~33% of the S&P 500, then the [[dot-com-bubble]] collapse cut the sector to ~15%; the cautionary template for concentration mania.
- **2008-2009**: fell with the market but recovered faster on secular demand.
- **2010s mega-cap rise**: FAANG/platform dominance rebuilt the sector's weight.
- **2020 COVID surge**: work-from-home pulled forward demand; tech led the rebound.
- **2022 rate shock**: the worst tech year since 2008 — aggressive Fed hikes compressed long-duration multiples; energy led, tech lagged.
- **2023-2026 AI boom**: [[nvidia]], [[microsoft]], [[broadcom]] led a historic rally; AI capex became the single biggest fundamental driver and pushed IT above 30% of the index again — reviving dot-com-style concentration concerns.

## Trading & Rotation Strategies

- **Tech vs Energy rotation** — the canonical pair; long [[xlk]] / short [[xle]] expresses a rates-down, growth-over-value, AI-narrative regime; reverse it for an inflation/rate-shock regime ([[sector-rotation]]).
- **Tech vs S&P relative value** — long [[xlk]] / short [[s-and-p-500|SPY]] isolates the tech-over-market spread (near-1 correlation but meaningful tracking difference at the tails).
- **Risk-on/risk-off** — long [[xlk]] / short a defensive sector ([[xlv]], [[utilities|XLU]]) to express risk appetite.
- **Software vs semis** — IGV vs [[smh-vaneck-semiconductor-etf|SMH]] expresses recurring-revenue-vs-cyclical and rate-sensitivity-vs-demand-cycle views.
- **Single-name event vol** — [[nvidia]], [[apple]], [[microsoft]], [[broadcom]] earnings carry index-moving implied moves and the deepest [[options]] liquidity outside index products.
- **Concentration hedge** — given AAPL/MSFT/NVDA dominate [[xlk]], traders hedge single-name risk with XLK options or vice versa (see [[options-concentration-risk]]).

## Risks

- **Rate / duration shock** — the dominant risk; rising real yields compress the sector's premium multiples fastest (2022 is the template).
- **Concentration** — three names drive ~45%+ of [[xlk]]; idiosyncratic news at one mega-cap moves the ETF and, given the sector's index weight, the whole market.
- **AI-capex disappointment** — much of the 2023-2026 rally is priced on sustained hyperscaler AI spending; a capex pause would de-rate semis and infra-software.
- **Disruption** — per Fred, "technology has always been disrupted" and the pace is now extreme; incumbents (IBM the classic example) can be displaced fast.
- **Regulation** — antitrust action against big tech and US-China chip-export controls are persistent overhangs.
- **Valuation air-pocket** — high multiples leave little margin for growth misses.
