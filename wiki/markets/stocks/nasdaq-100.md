# Nasdaq-100

The Nasdaq-100 (index symbol **NDX**) is a modified-market-capitalization-weighted index of the **100 largest non-financial companies listed on the Nasdaq stock exchange**. By rule it excludes financial companies (banks, insurers, and other financials), which — combined with the Nasdaq's historical role as the listing venue for technology and growth firms — makes it the market's canonical "big tech / growth" benchmark. It is distinct from the much broader [[nasdaq-composite|Nasdaq Composite]] (which contains all ~3,000+ Nasdaq-listed common stocks) and from the [[s-and-p-500|S&P 500]] (which spans both exchanges and includes financials). Most traders access it through the [[qqq|Invesco QQQ]] ETF, so "the Q's" and "the Nasdaq" are often used loosely to mean this index.

## What It Is

- **100 largest non-financial Nasdaq listings.** Only companies listed on the Nasdaq exchange are eligible — a firm listed on the NYSE cannot be in the Nasdaq-100 no matter how large. This is a listing-venue index, not a "100 biggest U.S. companies" index.
- **Financials excluded by rule.** Banks, insurers, brokers, and other GICS financials are ineligible. (Historically Nasdaq classified some businesses differently than S&P's [[gics|GICS]] scheme, but the practical effect is that traditional financials are absent, which is a defining structural difference from the S&P 500.)
- **Non-financial only, so it skews growth.** Because the Nasdaq has long been the home of technology and biotech IPOs, the surviving giants are overwhelmingly technology, communication-services, and consumer-internet names. The index is therefore a high-beta, long-duration, growth-tilted expression of the U.S. large-cap market.
- **NDX is the index; QQQ is the tradable proxy.** The index level itself is not investable; exposure comes via ETFs, [[futures]], and options (see below).

## Construction & Methodology

The index is maintained by **Nasdaq, Inc.** ([[nasdaq]]) under a published, largely rules-based methodology:

- **Eligibility.** A security must be listed exclusively on Nasdaq (with limited dual-listing exceptions), be a non-financial company, meet minimum liquidity and trading-volume requirements, have been listed for a minimum seasoning period, and remain current on filings. Only one class of a company's stock is typically included (though some firms with multiple share classes, e.g. [[alphabet]], have historically had both classes represented).
- **Modified market-cap weighting.** Constituents are weighted by market capitalization, but the weights are *modified* by concentration caps so that a handful of mega-caps cannot completely dominate the index. This is the key contrast with a pure cap-weighted index.
- **Annual reconstitution.** Once a year (effective at the December rebalance, using late-autumn ranking data), Nasdaq re-ranks eligible companies and swaps out those that have fallen in size for larger newcomers. This is when constituents are added and dropped.
- **Quarterly rebalancing.** Weights are reviewed and reset quarterly (March, June, September, December) to keep them aligned with current market caps and within methodology limits.

### The special-rebalance ("concentration") rule

To satisfy diversification requirements (rooted in U.S. Regulated Investment Company / RIC tax rules that ETFs must respect), the methodology caps how top-heavy the index can become. In broad terms:

- Securities that individually weigh **more than 4.5%** of the index are collectively monitored. If the **aggregate weight of those >4.5% names exceeds ~48%** of the index, Nasdaq triggers a **special rebalance** that redistributes weight to bring the aggregate of those largest names down (to roughly 40%), trimming the biggest constituents and boosting the rest.
- This mechanism was invoked in a notable **special rebalance in July 2023**, when the surging mega-cap technology names ("Magnificent 7") had pushed concentration to levels that breached the diversification limits. The rebalance reduced the combined weight of the largest holdings without changing the constituent list — a real-world demonstration of the cap in action.

The concentration caps mean the Nasdaq-100 is *slightly* less top-heavy than an uncapped cap-weighted version would be, but in practice it remains one of the most concentrated major equity benchmarks.

## Character & Concentration

The Nasdaq-100's defining trait is **mega-cap technology concentration**. A small group of names — the [[magnificent-seven|Magnificent Seven]] ([[apple|Apple]], [[microsoft|Microsoft]], [[nvidia|NVIDIA]], [[amazon|Amazon]], [[alphabet|Alphabet]], [[meta-platforms|Meta]], and [[tesla|Tesla]]) plus [[broadcom|Broadcom]] — has at times represented roughly **half of the index's total weight**. Compared with the [[s-and-p-500|S&P 500]]:

- **Higher concentration.** The top handful of names carries a larger share of the Nasdaq-100 than of the S&P 500, even after the concentration caps.
- **Higher beta / longer duration.** With no financials and heavy weight in growth/tech, the index is more sensitive to interest rates and risk sentiment than the broader market. It tends to fall harder in risk-off drawdowns and rally harder in risk-on regimes.
- **A "risk-on / growth" proxy.** Traders use the Nasdaq-100 (and QQQ) versus the S&P 500 (SPY) as a relative-value expression of growth-vs-broad-market and risk appetite; a rising QQQ/SPY ratio is a classic risk-on tell.

## Constituents

The Nasdaq-100 is dominated by mega-cap technology and consumer-internet firms, with a supporting cast in semiconductors, communication services, consumer discretionary, consumer staples, biotech, and healthcare. Notable current members that have wiki pages include:

The index also contains large biotech, healthcare, and other consumer names not individually listed here. Constituents change at the annual reconstitution as companies grow into, or fall out of, the top 100 non-financials on Nasdaq.

## Sector Composition

- **Information technology** is by far the heaviest sector, frequently around **half the index** (semiconductors, software, hardware).
- **Communication services** (internet media, streaming) and **consumer discretionary** (e-commerce, autos) add further growth weight.
- **Consumer staples** and **healthcare/biotech** provide the main non-tech ballast.
- **Financials are absent by rule** — the single biggest structural difference from the [[s-and-p-500|S&P 500]], which carries a substantial financials weight.

The net effect is that the Nasdaq-100 behaves like a concentrated bet on U.S. innovation and long-duration growth rather than a broad-economy benchmark.

## Drivers

- **Interest rates and long-duration sensitivity.** Growth stocks derive most of their value from distant future cash flows, so rising [[treasuries|Treasury yields]] and hawkish [[federal-reserve|Fed]] policy compress their valuations more than they do value stocks. The Nasdaq-100 is therefore especially rate-sensitive — its 2022 drawdown was largely a rate-driven multiple compression.
- **The AI capex cycle.** From 2023 onward, spending on AI infrastructure (GPUs, data centers, cloud) has been a dominant earnings and sentiment driver for the semiconductor and mega-cap software names that anchor the index.
- **Mega-cap earnings.** A single report from [[nvidia|NVIDIA]], [[apple|Apple]], [[microsoft|Microsoft]], or [[amazon|Amazon]] can move the whole index given their outsized weights.
- **Risk appetite.** As a high-beta growth proxy, the index leads on the way up in risk-on regimes and tends to lead on the way down in risk-off shocks.

## History

- **Launch (1985).** The Nasdaq-100 was introduced in 1985 alongside a financials-heavy companion index; the non-financial Nasdaq-100 became the technology-and-growth benchmark.
- **Dot-com boom and bust ([[dot-com-bubble]]).** The index rocketed through the late 1990s as internet and telecom valuations soared, peaked in early 2000, then collapsed roughly 80% into 2002 — the defining cautionary tale of concentrated growth investing. It took many years to reclaim the 2000 peak.
- **2010s tech leadership.** The rise of the mega-cap platform companies drove a long bull market in which the Nasdaq-100 consistently outperformed the broader market.
- **2022 drawdown.** As the [[federal-reserve|Fed]] hiked rates aggressively to fight inflation, long-duration growth names led the market lower and the Nasdaq-100 fell sharply — a clean example of the discount-rate channel.
- **2023-2026 AI-led rally.** A powerful recovery led by AI-linked semiconductors and mega-cap software pushed concentration to record levels and triggered the July 2023 special rebalance described above.

## Risks & Limitations

- **Concentration risk.** Even with the caps, a handful of mega-cap names drive the bulk of returns; a stumble in one leader can drag the whole index.
- **Rate / duration sensitivity.** Its growth tilt makes it more vulnerable than the broad market to rising real yields and hawkish policy surprises.
- **Valuation risk.** Growth premiums can be rich; the dot-com bust is the historical reminder that high multiples can compress violently.
- **Sector/style narrowness.** No financials, minimal energy/utilities/industrials exposure, and heavy overlap with the S&P 500's top names mean it is not a diversified "whole market" holding despite its popularity.
