XLV is the Health Care Select Sector SPDR Fund, issued by State Street Global Advisors. It tracks the Health Care Select Sector Index — the [[healthcare|health care]] slice of the [[s-and-p-500|S&P 500]] under the [[gics-classification|GICS]] framework. XLV is the most liquid US health care sector ETF and the standard vehicle for expressing health care equity exposure without picking individual names. It is the largest of the defensive sectors and a core building block of [[sector-rotation|sector-rotation]] strategies.

## What It Tracks

XLV holds the health care constituents of the S&P 500, spanning pharmaceuticals, biotechnology, health care equipment, managed care insurers, life-science tools, and health care services. The sector is generally considered **defensive** — demand for medical care is relatively inelastic to the business cycle, so XLV tends to hold up better than cyclical sectors during recessions while lagging in strong risk-on rallies.

The underlying **Health Care Select Sector Index** is one of the eleven mutually exclusive Select Sector indices that reconstitute the [[s-and-p-500|S&P 500]]; XLV plus its siblings ([[xlk]], [[xlf]], [[xly]], [[xli]], [[xlp]], [[xlu]], [[xle]], [[xlb]], [[xlre]], [[xlc]]) equal the full index. Health care is consistently one of the **largest** S&P sectors by weight, which is why XLV is among the biggest and most liquid sector SPDRs. Unlike pure defensives such as [[xlu|utilities]] or [[xlp|staples]], health care blends defensive demand characteristics with genuine secular **growth** (biotech innovation, GLP-1/obesity drugs, demographics) — giving XLV a hybrid defensive-growth profile rather than a textbook low-beta one.

## Top Holdings (qualitative)

XLV is market-cap weighted, so its largest positions are the mega-cap pharma and managed-care names. Approximate leadership (weights shift with rebalancing):

| Holding | Role |
|---------|------|
| UnitedHealth Group (UNH) | Largest managed-care insurer; often the single largest weight |
| Eli Lilly (LLY) | Large-cap pharma; GLP-1 / obesity-drug leadership has driven outsized weight |
| Johnson & Johnson (JNJ) | Diversified pharma and medical devices |
| Merck (MRK) | Large-cap pharma (oncology, vaccines) |
| AbbVie (ABBV) | Large-cap pharma (immunology) |

The top handful of names typically account for a meaningful share of the fund, so XLV's day-to-day moves are influenced heavily by UnitedHealth and Eli Lilly. Other significant holdings include Thermo Fisher, Abbott, Pfizer, Amgen, and Danaher.

### Concentration Dynamics

XLV is **moderately concentrated** — less extreme than [[xly|XLY]]'s two-name dominance, but more top-heavy than the balanced [[xli|XLI]]. The key recent dynamic is **Eli Lilly's re-rating**: GLP-1 / obesity-drug success has pushed LLY's weight up sharply, while UnitedHealth's managed-care scale keeps it perennially near the top. Because these two carry very different risk profiles (LLY = pharma-pipeline/growth beta; UNH = managed-care policy/regulatory beta), XLV's behavior depends heavily on which of its mega-caps is in favor. Pharma and managed-care together dominate the basket, so biotech and tools — despite being the sector's most innovative pockets — exert proportionally less influence on XLV than their headlines suggest.

## Sector Drivers & Macro Sensitivities

| Driver | Effect on XLV |
|--------|---------------|
| **Drug-pricing & reimbursement policy** | The dominant policy risk — legislation (e.g., Medicare negotiation), pricing reform, and managed-care regulation can re-rate the sector abruptly. |
| **Election / political cycle** | Health care is a perennial political target; "Medicare for All" type proposals or pricing crackdowns drive headline risk, especially in US election years. |
| **FDA approvals & clinical-trial outcomes** | Binary catalysts for pharma/biotech weights; a single readout can move large names. |
| **Patent cliffs / loss of exclusivity** | Major drugs going off-patent cut earnings for the affected mega-cap. |
| **Demographics** | Aging populations underpin secular demand — a structural tailwind largely independent of the cycle. |
| **Innovation cycles** | GLP-1/obesity, oncology, gene therapy, and AI-in-drug-discovery drive growth pockets. |
| **Managed-care medical-loss ratios** | Insurer profitability (UNH, etc.) hinges on utilization trends and cost control. |
| **Interest rates** | Minor relative to other sectors — biotech (long-duration, pre-revenue) is the most rate-sensitive corner. |

## Seasonality & Behavior vs the Broad Index

XLV's most reliable non-fundamental pattern is **election-year policy risk**: US election cycles routinely inject drug-pricing and health-policy headline risk that can pressure the sector regardless of earnings. Against [[spy|SPY]], XLV is a **lower-beta** holding (beta below 1) but not as low as utilities or staples, reflecting its growth component. The XLV/[[spy|SPY]] ratio is a recognized **defensive-leadership** gauge — it tends to rise during late-cycle and risk-off phases and lag during early-cycle, cyclical-led rallies. A nuance versus pure defensives: in a growth-led melt-up, XLV's pharma-innovation names (notably Lilly) can let it keep pace better than [[xlu|XLU]] or [[xlp|XLP]], blurring the "defensive lag" pattern.

## Methodology & Expense Ratio

XLV uses the same Select Sector methodology as its SPDR siblings: it takes the GICS health care members of the S&P 500, weights them by float-adjusted market cap, and applies capping rules to keep the fund compliant with diversification requirements. Rebalancing is quarterly. The expense ratio is **0.09%**, the standard low fee across the Select Sector SPDR lineup.

## How It Trades — Liquidity & Options

XLV is one of the more liquid sector SPDRs, with tight bid-ask spreads and a deep, actively traded options market (weekly expirations, $1 strikes near the money, American-style physical settlement). Implied volatility typically runs below the broad market because of the sector's defensive profile, though single-name binary events (FDA decisions, managed-care policy headlines, drug-pricing legislation) can drive sharp idiosyncratic moves that lift sector vol. XLV options are used for sector hedges, rotation overlays, and as a lower-beta complement to higher-vol sector exposures.

The interesting structural feature of XLV options is **event-driven, lumpy vol**: index IV is calm most of the time but episodically jumps on policy headlines (drug-pricing bills) or large-name binaries (an FDA decision or trial readout in Lilly/Merck). This creates dispersion and calendar opportunities — selling the [[volatility-risk-premium|VRP]] in quiet windows while staying alert to known catalyst dates. Skew is generally moderate but steepens around election-year policy risk and managed-care headlines. XLV puts are a favored *lower-cost* hedge for broadly long books because the sector tends to drawdown less than cyclicals.

## Alternatives

| ETF | Issuer | Notes |
|-----|--------|-------|
| **XLV** | State Street (SPDR) | S&P 500 health care only; mega-cap concentrated; 0.09% |
| **VHT** | Vanguard | Broader — all-cap US health care index; lower fee; more small/mid-cap names |
| **IYH** | iShares | US health care; similar large-cap tilt |
| **IHI / IBB / XBI** | iShares / SPDR | Narrower sub-sector plays (devices, biotech) |

VHT and IYH offer broader coverage than XLV (which is restricted to S&P 500 members), so they include more mid- and small-cap health care names. XLV remains the most liquid and the standard rotation building block. The sub-sector funds matter for targeting: **IHI** is medical devices, **IBB** large-cap biotech, and **XBI** *equal-weight* biotech — XBI in particular behaves very differently from XLV (high-beta, small/mid-cap, rate-sensitive, M&A-driven) and is the go-to for pure biotech-risk expression. Within the SPDR family XLV is grouped with the defensives [[xlp|XLP]] and [[xlu|XLU]] for rotation, though its growth component makes it the least "bond-like" of the three.

## Use in Sector Rotation

In [[sector-rotation|sector-rotation]] frameworks, XLV is a classic **defensive** sleeve. Tactical allocators rotate into health care (alongside [[xlp|staples]] and [[xlu|utilities]]) during late-cycle and risk-off regimes, when investors favor stable earnings and inelastic demand. XLV tends to underperform during early-cycle recoveries when cyclicals lead, and outperform during slowdowns. The XLV/[[spy|SPY]] ratio is a common gauge of defensive leadership.

## Risks

- **Policy risk** — drug-pricing legislation, Medicare/Medicaid reimbursement changes, and managed-care regulation can hit the sector hard and suddenly.
- **Concentration** — a few mega-cap names (notably UnitedHealth and Eli Lilly) drive a large share of returns; single-name shocks ripple through the fund.
- **Patent cliffs & pipeline risk** — major drugs losing exclusivity or trial failures can cut large weights.
- **Defensive underperformance** — in strong bull markets, XLV typically lags cyclical sectors.
