The **S&P/ASX 200** (ticker: XJO) is the benchmark stock-market index for Australia, comprising the 200 largest companies listed on the [[asx|Australian Securities Exchange]] by float-adjusted market capitalization. It is the most widely quoted measure of the Australian equity market and serves as the basis for index funds, ETFs, the [[spi-200-futures|SPI 200 futures]] contract, and institutional portfolio benchmarks. Its defining structural features are a heavy concentration in **financials and materials**, an unusually high **dividend yield** amplified by Australia's [[franking-credits|franking-credit]] system, and a close link to commodity prices and Chinese demand.

## Overview

The ASX 200 is maintained by S&P Dow Jones Indices and weighted by float-adjusted market capitalization, with quarterly rebalances. Because it captures roughly 80% of total ASX market capitalization, it functions as the country's de facto large-cap benchmark — the Australian analog of the [[sp500|S&P 500]] in the US or the [[ftse-100]] in the UK. For international investors, returns are a joint bet on the index level and on the [[australian-dollar]]: AUD is a commodity-linked "risk-on" currency, so a falling AUD often coincides with falling equities, compounding USD-denominated drawdowns.

## Index Construction & Methodology

The S&P/ASX 200 is built and maintained by S&P Dow Jones Indices under the broader **S&P/ASX index family** (which also includes the ASX 20, ASX 50, ASX 100, ASX 300, and the All Ordinaries). Key methodology features:

- **Selection universe** — eligible securities are drawn from ASX-listed stocks screened for size and **liquidity** (a minimum relative-liquidity threshold), so a company must trade actively enough, not merely be large, to qualify. This liquidity filter is why some large but thinly-traded names are excluded.
- **Weighting** — **float-adjusted market capitalization**: only the freely-tradeable share float counts, so strategic and government holdings are removed from the weight. Larger free-float companies carry proportionally larger index weight.
- **Rebalancing** — reviewed and rebalanced **quarterly** (effective on the third Friday of March, June, September, and December), with constituent additions/deletions and weight adjustments. Buffer rules reduce turnover by not swapping names that are only marginally over/under the size threshold.
- **Index family ladder** — the ASX 200 sits between the [[asx]]-listed narrower benchmarks (ASX 20/50/100) and the broader ASX 300; it is the institutional benchmark of choice because it balances coverage (~80% of market cap) against the investability of its constituents.
- **Corporate-action handling** — divisor adjustments for splits, rights issues, buybacks, and constituent changes keep the index level continuous across events, in standard S&P DJI fashion.

A consequence of the **quarterly-rebalance + liquidity-screen** design is that index inclusion/exclusion events (notably promotions into the ASX 200 from below) generate measurable [[index-reconstitution|reconstitution]] flows as passive ASX 200 trackers buy added names and sell removed ones around the effective date.

## Sector Composition

The ASX 200 is heavily concentrated in financials and materials, reflecting Australia's economic structure as a resource exporter with a large, profitable banking oligopoly:

| Sector | Approximate Weight | Notable constituents |
|--------|--------------------|----------------------|
| **Financials** | ~28–32% | Big Four banks: [[commonwealth-bank\|CBA]], [[westpac\|WBC]], [[nab\|NAB]], [[anz\|ANZ]], plus Macquarie |
| **Materials** | ~20–25% | BHP, Rio Tinto, Fortescue (iron-ore heavy) |
| **Healthcare** | ~8–10% | [[csl-limited\|CSL]] dominates |
| **Real Estate** | ~5–7% | Goodman Group, Scentre |
| **Energy** | ~4–6% | Woodside, Santos (LNG) |
| **Technology** | ~3–5% | WiseTech, Xero, NEXTDC |
| **Other** | Remainder | Consumer staples (Woolworths, Coles), industrials |

This composition makes the ASX 200 a **resource-and-bank-heavy index** — highly sensitive to commodity prices ([[iron-ore]], gold, LNG), to Australian interest rates set by the Reserve Bank of Australia, and to Chinese economic activity, since China is Australia's largest trading partner and the dominant buyer of its iron ore.

## SPI 200 Futures

The [[spi-200-futures|SPI 200 futures]] contract (ASX code AP, traded on ASX 24, formerly the Sydney Futures Exchange) is the primary derivative for hedging and leveraged exposure to the index:

| Spec | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Underlying** | S&P/ASX 200 index |
| **Multiplier** | A$25 per index point |
| **Notional at 8,000** | ~A$200,000 per contract |
| **Settlement** | Cash, to a Special Opening Quotation on the third Thursday of the expiry month |
| **Quarterly expiries** | March, June, September, December |
| **Hours** | Day and overnight sessions — provides offshore-hours hedging while the cash market is closed |

The overnight SPI 200 session is closely watched as a leading indicator for the next Australian cash open, since it reacts to US and European moves after the ASX has closed. A **Mini SPI 200** at a smaller multiplier exists for smaller accounts (verify the current spec sheet for exact sizing).

## XJO Index Options and Other Derivatives

Beyond the [[spi-200-futures|SPI 200]] future, the ASX lists a full derivatives suite on the index:

- **XJO index options** — European-style, cash-settled options on the S&P/ASX 200 index itself, listed on the ASX. They are the primary listed vehicle for index-level hedging and income (covered-call, collar, and put-protection programs run by Australian super funds and institutions). As cash-settled European options on the cash index, they avoid the cheapest-to-deliver and early-exercise complications of US-style products.
- **Options on SPI 200 futures** — options whose underlying is the SPI 200 future rather than the cash index, used by traders who already run the futures as their base instrument; these inherit cost-of-carry through the future (the [[option-on-future-vs-option-on-index|option-on-future vs option-on-index]] distinction).
- **Sector and single-stock options** — the ASX also lists options on major single names (the banks, miners, [[csl-limited|CSL]]) used for the single-name legs of [[dispersion-trading|dispersion]] against the index.
- **Mini SPI 200 future** — a smaller-multiplier future for retail-scale and fine-tuning of hedges (verify the current spec sheet for exact sizing).

Australian index-vol is benchmarked by the **S&P/ASX 200 VIX (A-VIX)**, the local analog of the [[vix|VIX]], computed from XJO option prices and used to read the Australian implied-vol regime. A-VIX tends to track global risk-on/risk-off conditions but carries an idiosyncratic overlay from commodity and China-demand shocks specific to the index's composition.

## Dividends and Franking

The ASX 200's headline characteristic for income investors is its **high dividend yield** — historically ~4% (gross of franking, the cash yield runs lower) versus roughly 1.3–1.5% for the S&P 500. Two structural forces drive this:

1. **Payout culture** — Australian large-caps, especially the banks and miners, return a high share of earnings as dividends rather than reinvesting.
2. **[[franking-credits|Franking credits]]** — under Australia's dividend-imputation system, companies attach franking credits (representing corporate tax already paid at 30%) to dividends. Australian-resident shareholders can use these credits to offset their own tax, and certain investors (notably super funds in pension phase) can receive them as **cash refunds**. This makes fully-franked dividends worth materially more after tax to domestic holders than the cash amount alone, and it structurally incentivizes high payout ratios.

For **foreign investors**, franking credits are generally not usable, so the after-tax yield is closer to the raw cash yield. This creates a domestic/foreign valuation wedge: Australian dividend payers are priced partly off a tax benefit that overseas buyers cannot capture. Total-return comparisons of the ASX 200 against foreign indices must specify whether they include the grossed-up value of franking.

## Trading the ASX 200

- **ETFs**: A200 (Betashares), STW (SPDR), IOZ (iShares) — the primary route for passive ASX 200 exposure.
- **Futures**: [[spi-200-futures|SPI 200]] on ASX 24, for leveraged, hedging, and overnight access.
- **Options**: index options (XJO) listed on ASX for hedging and income (covered calls, collars, put protection).
- **CFDs**: offered by [[ig-markets|IG Markets]], [[cmc-markets|CMC Markets]] and others for leveraged short-term trading.

## How the ASX 200 Trades vs Other Indices

| Dimension | **S&P/ASX 200** | [[sp500\|S&P 500]] | [[ftse-100]] | Nikkei 225 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Constituents | 200 | 500 | 100 | 225 |
| Weighting | Float-adj. cap | Float-adj. cap | Float-adj. cap | **Price-weighted** |
| Dominant sectors | **Financials + materials** | Tech + comms + healthcare | Resources + financials | Industrials + tech |
| Dividend yield | **High (~4% gross, franking-amplified)** | Low (~1.3-1.5%) | Moderate-high | Low-moderate |
| Currency overlay (for USD investor) | **AUD — commodity/risk-on** | None | GBP — intl-revenue inverse | JPY — carry-linked |
| Key external driver | **China demand / iron ore** | US/global growth, tech cycle | Global commodities, EM revenue | Yen-carry / BoJ |
| Tech weight | **Low** | High | Low | Moderate |
| Cash trading hours | 10:00-16:00 AEST | 09:30-16:00 ET | 08:00-16:30 UK | 09:00-15:30 JST |

The ASX 200's behaviour relative to peers is shaped by three things: a **commodity/China-demand beta** through its materials sleeve, a **bank-concentration beta** through the Big Four, and a **currency amplification** effect where AUD weakness in risk-off episodes compounds (rather than hedges) equity drawdowns for foreign investors. As a value/commodity-tilted, low-tech index, it tends to **lag in tech-led bull markets and outperform in commodity- or value-led regimes** — a near-mirror of the [[nasdaq|Nasdaq]]'s profile.

## Characteristics and Risks

- **Bank concentration** — the Big Four banks alone are ~20% of the index, creating single-sector concentration risk; a credit cycle or housing shock hits the index disproportionately.
- **Commodity / China sensitivity** — performance correlates strongly with iron-ore prices and Chinese growth; a Chinese property or steel slowdown drags BHP, Rio, and Fortescue, and through them the whole index.
- **Currency effect** — for USD-based investors, [[australian-dollar|AUD/USD]] moves can dominate returns; AUD weakness in risk-off episodes tends to amplify equity drawdowns rather than hedge them.
- **Rate sensitivity** — high-payout financials and REITs are sensitive to RBA policy and bond yields, giving the index a partly rate-driven, "bond-proxy" character.
- **Lower tech weight** — the index has a small technology sleeve versus the [[nasdaq|Nasdaq]] or S&P 500, so it tends to lag in tech-led bull markets and outperform in commodity/value-led regimes.
