The bond market (the fixed-income market) is where debt securities are issued and traded. It is significantly larger than the global equity market, with an estimated $130+ trillion in outstanding debt worldwide. Bonds are the primary mechanism through which governments and corporations borrow: an investor lends principal (the "face value") and in return receives periodic interest (the "coupon") plus repayment of principal at maturity.

## Major Segments

| Segment | Issuer | Risk | Notes |
|---------|--------|------|-------|
| **[[treasuries\|US Treasuries]]** | US federal government | "Risk-free" benchmark | Bills (<1yr), Notes (2-10yr), Bonds (20-30yr); see [[treasury-bonds]] |
| **[[corporate-bonds\|Corporate bonds]]** | Companies | Investment-grade → high-yield | Rated by [[credit-rating-agencies]] AAA down to junk |
| **Municipal bonds** | US states & local governments | Low-moderate | Interest often exempt from federal (and some state) income tax |
| **Sovereign / EM debt** | Foreign governments | Varies widely | Priced as a [[credit-spread\|spread]] over Treasuries or Bunds |
| **Agency MBS** | Fannie Mae, [[freddie-mac\|Freddie Mac]], Ginnie Mae | Low (gov't-backed) | Pools of residential mortgages; prepayment risk |

The US Treasury market alone is roughly $27 trillion and is the deepest, most liquid market on earth -- which is why Treasury yields serve as the reference "risk-free rate" against which nearly every other asset is priced.

## Primary vs Secondary Market

- **Primary market** -- new issuance. The US Treasury raises money through regular **auctions**; corporations issue new bonds via underwriting syndicates. Auction demand (the bid-to-cover ratio) is watched closely as a gauge of appetite for government debt.
- **Secondary market** -- existing bonds trade between investors, overwhelmingly **over-the-counter (OTC)** through dealer banks rather than on a central exchange. Government bonds are highly liquid; many corporate and municipal bonds trade infrequently, so liquidity and bid-ask spreads vary enormously by issue.

## Key Concepts

### Price–Yield Inverse Relationship

Bond prices and yields move in opposite directions. A bond paying a fixed 4% coupon becomes less attractive if new bonds yield 5%, so its price must fall until its effective yield matches the market. This is the central mechanic of fixed income: **when yields rise, existing bond prices fall, and vice-versa.**

### Yield Curve

The [[yield-curve]] plots yield against maturity. A **normal** curve slopes upward (longer maturities yield more, compensating for time and inflation risk). An **inverted** curve -- short rates above long rates -- has preceded every US recession of the past ~50 years and is the bond market's most-watched recession signal. The 2s10s spread (10-year minus 2-year) is the standard gauge.

### Duration

[[duration|Duration]] measures a bond's price sensitivity to interest-rate changes, expressed in years. A bond with a duration of 7 loses roughly 7% of its value for a +1% (100bp) move in yields. Longer-maturity, lower-coupon bonds have higher duration and thus more price risk -- the reason 2022's rate-hiking cycle inflicted historic losses on long-duration Treasury holders.

### Credit Spread

The [[credit-spread]] is the extra yield a non-government bond pays over a comparable-maturity Treasury, compensating for default risk. Spreads **widen** when investors demand more compensation (recession fears, stress) and **tighten** when confidence returns -- making high-yield spreads a real-time barometer of credit-market stress.

## The Bond Market and Other Assets

The bond market is deeply interconnected with everything else. Rising Treasury yields raise the discount rate used to value future cash flows, pressuring [[stocks|equities]] (especially long-duration growth stocks), tend to strengthen the dollar in [[forex]], and tighten overall financial conditions. [[quantitative-easing]] -- central-bank purchases of bonds -- operates directly through this market, suppressing yields to stimulate the economy; quantitative tightening reverses it.

## Why It Matters for Traders

The bond market is often called the "smart money" market because it tends to lead equities and reflects collective macroeconomic expectations -- growth, inflation, and central-bank policy. Watching yields, the shape of the [[yield-curve]], and [[credit-spread|credit spreads]] provides essential context for trading any asset class, even for those who never buy a bond.
