**Corporate bonds** are debt securities issued by companies to raise capital. The investor lends the firm money and in return receives regular interest (coupon) payments and the return of principal at maturity. Corporates issue bonds rather than equity because debt does not dilute ownership and the interest is usually tax-deductible to the company.

Corporate bonds are rated by [[credit-rating-agencies]] (S&P, Moody's, Fitch) from investment-grade (AAA to BBB-/Baa3) down to high-yield/junk (BB+/Ba1 and below). The [[credit-spread]] -- the yield difference over a comparable government bond -- reflects default risk and widens during market stress.

## Investment Grade vs High Yield

The dividing line at **BBB-/Baa3** (the lowest investment-grade rung) is one of the most consequential thresholds in finance. Many institutional investors -- pension funds, insurers -- are mandated to hold only investment-grade paper. When a company is downgraded from BBB- to BB+ it becomes a **"fallen angel,"** and forced selling by constrained holders can drive sharp price declines and opportunities for high-yield specialists.

| | Investment Grade | High Yield ("Junk") |
|---|---|---|
| Rating band | AAA to BBB-/Baa3 | BB+/Ba1 and below |
| Typical buyers | Pensions, insurers, IG funds | HY funds, hedge funds, CLOs |
| [[credit-spread\|Spread]] over Treasuries (typical) | ~0.5-2.0% | ~3-8%, blowing out to 10%+ in crises |
| Default risk | Low | Materially higher |
| Reference index | Bloomberg US Agg Corporate | Bloomberg US Corporate High Yield |

**High-yield bonds** (junk bonds) offer significantly higher coupons to compensate for greater default risk. The market was pioneered by Michael Milken at Drexel Burnham Lambert in the 1980s and is now a trillion-dollar asset class. High-yield spreads are watched closely as a barometer of credit-market stress and broader economic health -- they gapped above 10% in the 2008 GFC and again, briefly, in March 2020 before central-bank backstops compressed them.

## Spread, Yield, and Pricing

A corporate bond's total yield decomposes into the risk-free [[treasury-bonds|Treasury]] yield plus the credit spread. Two distinct risks therefore move the price:

- **Interest-rate risk** -- captured by [[duration]]; the bond falls when the underlying Treasury yield rises, regardless of credit.
- **Credit (spread) risk** -- the bond falls when its spread widens on deteriorating creditworthiness, even if Treasury yields are flat.

Investment-grade bonds behave more like rate instruments (Treasury yields dominate); high-yield bonds behave more like equity (spread/credit risk dominates and correlates with stock markets).

## Bond Structures

| Feature | What it means | Effect |
|---------|---------------|--------|
| **Callable** | Issuer can redeem early, usually after a set date | Caps upside for the investor; issuers call when rates fall to refinance cheaper |
| **Putable** | Holder can force early repayment | Protects the investor; rare |
| **Convertible** | Can convert into the issuer's stock at a set ratio | Lower coupon; gives equity-like upside, common in growth/tech |
| **Secured vs unsecured** | Backed by specific collateral or not | Secured bonds recover more in default |
| **Floating-rate** | Coupon resets off a benchmark (e.g. SOFR) | Low duration; protects against rising rates |

## Capital Structure and Default

Corporate bonds are **senior to equity** in a company's capital structure. In bankruptcy, bondholders are paid from remaining assets before stockholders. This seniority makes bonds less risky than the same issuer's stock, but **recovery rates** vary widely -- senior secured bonds may recover 60-80 cents on the dollar, while subordinated unsecured bonds often recover far less, sometimes near zero.

## How They Trade

Corporate bonds trade primarily **over-the-counter** through dealer banks rather than on an exchange, and liquidity varies enormously: a newly issued large IG benchmark trades tightly, while a small off-the-run high-yield issue may not trade for days. Investors gain diversified, liquid exposure through **bond ETFs** (e.g. LQD for IG, HYG/JNK for HY). [[credit-default-swaps]] (CDS) let traders take or hedge credit exposure synthetically without owning the underlying bond, and CDS indices (CDX IG, CDX HY) are a common way to express macro credit views. Analysts evaluate corporate bonds through credit spreads, relative value against comparable issuers, [[yield-curve|term-structure]] positioning, and fundamental credit analysis of leverage and cash flow.
