# Freddie Mac

**Freddie Mac** (Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corporation, ticker FMCC) is a U.S. government-sponsored enterprise (GSE) that buys mortgages from lenders and packages them into [[mortgage-backed-securities|mortgage-backed securities (MBS)]]. Chartered by Congress in 1970 to compete with Fannie Mae and broaden the secondary mortgage market, Freddie Mac is one of the two pillars of U.S. housing finance. Since September 2008 it has operated under federal **conservatorship**, and together with Fannie Mae it backs the large majority of new U.S. residential mortgages, making agency MBS one of the deepest and most liquid segments of the global [[bond-market]].

## Role in the Housing Market

Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae (chartered 1938) form the backbone of the U.S. housing finance system. Their primary function is to buy "conforming" mortgages from banks and other lenders, pool them, and issue [[mortgage-backed-securities|MBS]] sold to investors worldwide. This provides liquidity to lenders, who can recycle capital into new loans rather than holding mortgages on their balance sheets.

By guaranteeing timely principal and interest on the MBS they issue, the GSEs transfer the **credit risk** of the underlying mortgages from individual lenders to themselves (and, implicitly, to the U.S. taxpayer). This guarantee is what makes the **30-year fixed-rate, freely prepayable mortgage** — a product essentially unique to the U.S. — economically viable and keeps borrowing costs lower than they would otherwise be.

### Conforming vs. non-conforming

Freddie Mac can only buy loans that meet FHFA criteria — the "conforming loan limit" (a baseline of $766,550 for a single-family home in 2024, higher in high-cost areas), documentation standards, and maximum loan-to-value ratios. Loans above the limit are "jumbo" loans, which banks hold themselves or securitize privately. This conforming/jumbo split is a key structural feature of the U.S. mortgage market.

## Agency MBS Issuance

Agency MBS — securities guaranteed by Freddie Mac, Fannie Mae, or Ginnie Mae — trade largely through the **TBA (To-Be-Announced) market**, one of the most liquid fixed-income markets after U.S. Treasuries. Key features relevant to traders:

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Credit risk** | Effectively eliminated by the GSE guarantee; agency MBS are quasi-sovereign credits |
| **Prepayment risk** | The dominant risk: borrowers refinance when rates fall, returning principal early ("negative convexity") |
| **Single Security (UMBS)** | Since 2019, Freddie and Fannie issue a fungible Uniform MBS, merging their once-separate TBA markets |
| **Benchmark spread** | The agency MBS "current coupon" spread over Treasuries is a closely watched gauge of mortgage-market stress and Fed balance-sheet effects |

Freddie Mac also publishes the long-running **Primary Mortgage Market Survey (PMMS)**, the standard reference for the U.S. average 30-year fixed mortgage rate.

## The 2008 Crisis and Conservatorship

In the lead-up to the [[2008-global-financial-crisis|2008 Global Financial Crisis]], both GSEs accumulated enormous exposure to [[subprime-mortgage]] and Alt-A loans and held thinly capitalized retained portfolios. When house prices collapsed, losses mounted rapidly and threatened both firms' solvency. On **September 7, 2008**, the newly created Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA) placed both into **conservatorship**, with the U.S. Treasury committing support through Senior Preferred Stock Purchase Agreements (initially up to $100B each, later raised to $200B+ each).

Under the later "net worth sweep," the Treasury swept nearly all GSE profits to taxpayers. Freddie Mac has remained in conservatorship ever since, and the question of how to **reform, recapitalize, or release** the GSEs from government control has been a recurring (and politically charged) policy debate. Together, Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae guarantee on the order of **$7 trillion** in mortgage debt, making them systemically important to both the housing market and the broader [[bond-market]].

## Trading Relevance

1. **Rate sensitivity and convexity hedging.** Because of prepayment-driven negative convexity, GSE and MBS investors hedge by selling Treasuries/receiving swaps when rates fall and buying when rates rise — a mechanical flow that can amplify Treasury-market moves during fast rate selloffs.
2. **Policy headline risk.** Any credible move toward GSE privatization or guarantee changes can reprice agency MBS spreads and the FMCC/FNMA common and preferred shares (which trade on speculation about conservatorship exit).
3. **Mortgage-rate transmission.** The agency MBS market is the channel through which Fed policy and QE/QT reach household mortgage rates; Fed MBS purchases compress, and runoff widens, the current-coupon spread.
