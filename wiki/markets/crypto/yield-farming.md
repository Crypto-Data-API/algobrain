Yield farming is a [[defi|decentralized finance]] strategy in which users deposit or stake crypto assets into smart contract protocols in exchange for rewards, typically paid in the protocol's governance token or a share of transaction fees. Often called "liquidity mining," yield farming became the dominant DeFi activity during the 2020-2021 boom, with aggregate total value locked (TVL) across DeFi protocols exceeding $180 billion at its November 2021 peak.

## How It Works

At its core, yield farming involves supplying capital that a protocol needs to function. The most common forms are:

1. **Liquidity provision** — Depositing token pairs into automated market maker (AMM) [[liquidity-pools|liquidity pools]] (e.g., ETH/USDC on [[uniswap|Uniswap]] or Curve). The depositor earns a share of swap fees generated by traders using the pool.
2. **Lending** — Supplying assets to lending protocols like [[compound|Compound]] or [[aave|Aave]]. Borrowers pay interest, which is passed to suppliers.
3. **Staking** — Locking governance tokens or LP tokens in a protocol's staking contract to earn additional token rewards.
4. **Incentive programs** — Protocols distribute their native tokens to users who provide liquidity, effectively paying users to bootstrap the protocol's utility. This was popularized by [[compound|Compound's]] COMP distribution in June 2020.

### APY vs. APR

Yield farming returns are typically quoted as APY (Annual Percentage Yield) or APR (Annual Percentage Rate). The distinction matters:

- **APR** — Simple interest. A 50% APR means you earn 50% of your principal over a year, without compounding.
- **APY** — Compound interest. A 50% APR compounded daily equals roughly 64.8% APY.

Advertised APYs in DeFi can be misleading because they often assume continuous compounding, stable token prices, and constant reward rates — none of which are guaranteed. A pool showing "500% APY" typically reflects a brief period of high rewards with new token emissions that will dilute rapidly.

## Risks

Yield farming carries significant risks beyond standard market price risk:

### Impermanent Loss
When providing liquidity to an AMM pool, if the prices of the deposited tokens diverge significantly, the depositor may end up with less value than simply holding the tokens. See [[impermanent-loss]] for a detailed treatment. In a 50/50 pool, a 2x price change in one token results in roughly a 5.7% loss versus holding.

### Smart Contract Risk
DeFi protocols are only as safe as their smart contract code. Exploits, bugs, and flash loan attacks have caused billions in losses. Notable examples include the $600M Poly Network hack (August 2021) and the $325M Wormhole bridge exploit (February 2022).

### Rug Pulls
Unaudited or anonymous projects may have backdoors that allow developers to drain deposited funds. This is particularly common with new "fork" protocols advertising extremely high APYs.

### Token Price Risk
Rewards are often paid in a protocol's governance token. If that token's price falls — as frequently happens when emissions are high and sell pressure mounts — the realized yield may be far lower than the quoted APY, potentially resulting in a net loss.

### Regulatory Risk
The legal status of yield farming varies by jurisdiction. Reward token distributions may constitute securities in some regulatory frameworks, creating legal exposure for both protocols and users.

## Yield Compression

DeFi yields have compressed significantly since the 2020-2021 boom. Stablecoin lending rates that once exceeded 20% APY fell to 2-5% by 2023-2024, reflecting increased capital competition, lower demand for leverage, and reduced token emission subsidies. This pattern is consistent with any market: as more capital chases a finite set of opportunities, excess returns diminish toward the [[risk-management|risk-free rate]].
