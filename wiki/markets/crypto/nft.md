Non-fungible tokens (NFTs) are unique digital assets recorded on a [[blockchain]] that represent verifiable ownership of items such as art, collectibles, music, gaming assets, and virtual real estate. Unlike fungible tokens where one [[bitcoin]] or [[ethereum|ETH]] is interchangeable with another, each NFT carries a unique token ID and metadata that distinguishes it from every other token. NFTs emerged from experimental projects on Bitcoin in 2014, exploded into a multi-billion-dollar market in 2021, crashed violently in 2022, and have since settled into a smaller but persistent market where early "blue-chip" collections retain significant cultural and financial value.

## Timeline of the NFT Market

### 2014-2016: Proto-NFTs

The concept of unique digital assets on a blockchain predates [[ethereum]] entirely. The earliest experiments occurred on [[bitcoin]] using **Colored Coins** (2012-2013), a protocol that "colored" specific satoshis with metadata to represent real-world assets. Colored Coins were limited by Bitcoin's scripting language and never achieved mainstream traction, but they proved the concept.

**Counterparty** (launched January 2014) built a more capable layer on top of Bitcoin, enabling the creation of custom tokens and assets. In 2015-2016, communities began using Counterparty to create and trade digital trading cards and memes. The most significant project was **[[rare-pepes|Rare Pepes]]** (September 2016) — a collection of ~1,774 unique Pepe the Frog trading cards created by ~300 artists. Rare Pepes are arguably the first true NFTs: unique digital art assets with provable scarcity on a blockchain. The "RAREPEPE" Series 1, Card 1 later sold for over $500,000 in 2021, and the famous "Homer Pepe" card sold for $320,000 at the first-ever live NFT auction at the Rare Digital Art Festival in January 2018.

Other Counterparty experiments included **Spells of Genesis** (2015), one of the first blockchain-based trading card games, and **Force of Will** cards.

### 2017: The Genesis Year

2017 was the year NFTs as we know them were born on [[ethereum]]. Multiple projects launched that would later be recognized as foundational:

**[[curio-cards|Curio Cards]]** (May 2017) — Often overlooked, Curio Cards launched before CryptoPunks and represents one of the first art NFT projects on Ethereum. Thirty unique card designs by 7 artists, with ~2,000 editions per card. The project used a custom smart contract that predated the ERC-721 standard. Curio Cards were largely forgotten until their rediscovery during the 2021 boom.

**[[cryptopunks|CryptoPunks]]** (June 2017) — Created by Larva Labs (Matt Hall and John Watkinson), CryptoPunks are 10,000 unique 24x24 pixel art characters that were originally **free to claim** — holders only paid Ethereum gas fees. The project used a custom contract that inspired the later ERC-721 standard. CryptoPunks became the iconic NFT collection and remain the cultural benchmark for the entire space. Alien Punk #7523 sold for $11.75 million at Sotheby's in June 2021, and Punk #5822 sold for $23.7 million in February 2022.

**[[mooncats|MoonCats]]** (August 2017) — 25,600 procedurally generated pixel cats that were free to "rescue" (mint). The project was abandoned by its creators shortly after launch, then spectacularly rediscovered in March 2021 when the remaining cats were all rescued within hours.

**[[etherrock|EtherRock]]** (December 2017) — 100 JPEG images of clipart rocks on Ethereum. Created as an experiment, forgotten for years, then rediscovered in August 2021 when floor prices briefly exceeded $1 million per rock — becoming a mainstream media story about NFT speculation.

**[[cryptokitties|CryptoKitties]]** (November 28, 2017) — The first NFT project to go truly viral. Created by Dapper Labs, CryptoKitties let users breed, collect, and trade unique digital cats. The game became so popular it congested the Ethereum network, spiking gas prices by over 600% and grinding transactions to a halt. The Genesis kitty "Dragon" sold for 600 ETH (~$170,000 at the time). CryptoKitties proved NFTs could generate mainstream media attention and real economic activity, but also exposed Ethereum's scaling limitations — directly motivating [[layer-2]] development.

The **ERC-721 standard** was proposed by Dieter Shirley (CryptoKitties lead developer) in late 2017 and formalized in January 2018, giving NFTs a common technical standard on Ethereum.

### 2018-2019: The Quiet Years

The crypto winter of 2018-2019 decimated NFT trading volumes along with the broader market. CryptoKitties activity dropped over 95% from its December 2017 peak. However, important infrastructure was built during this period:

**SuperRare** launched in April 2018 as a curated marketplace for 1/1 digital art, establishing the model of artist-centric NFT platforms. **KnownOrigin** and **MakersPlace** followed with similar concepts.

**Autoglyphs** (April 2019) by Larva Labs (the CryptoPunks creators) became the first "on-chain" generative art — the artwork itself is generated and stored entirely on the Ethereum blockchain, not just a pointer to an external image. Only 512 exist, and they later sold for hundreds of ETH each.

**OpenSea** (founded December 2017, gained traction in 2018-2019) became the dominant NFT marketplace, offering a general-purpose platform for trading any ERC-721 or ERC-1155 token. By providing a simple interface and broad collection support, OpenSea positioned itself to capture the eventual boom.

The **ERC-1155 standard** (2018) introduced "semi-fungible" tokens — allowing both unique and fungible tokens in a single contract, reducing gas costs for gaming and collectible use cases.

### 2020: The Mainstream Gateway

2020 saw NFTs begin to break out of the crypto-native bubble:

**NBA Top Shot** (October 2020) — Built by Dapper Labs (CryptoKitties creators) on their new Flow blockchain, Top Shot let users buy, sell, and trade officially licensed NBA highlight clips as NFTs. It was designed for mainstream sports fans, not crypto users — you could buy with a credit card. Top Shot generated over $230 million in sales by February 2021, with a LeBron James "Cosmic" moment selling for $208,000.

**Rarible** launched its governance token $RARI in July 2020, pioneering the concept of "marketplace mining" — rewarding users with tokens for trading, which incentivized volume and liquidity.

**Beeple** (Mike Winkelmann) made his first NFT sale in October 2020, selling a collection for $66,666. This was a prelude to his record-shattering 2021 sale.

The first NFT sales exceeding $100,000 became regular occurrences. Total NFT market volume for 2020 reached approximately $250 million — a 300% increase from 2019 but still tiny compared to what was coming.

### 2021: The Supercycle

2021 was the year NFTs entered mainstream consciousness. The numbers were staggering:

**March 2021: The Beeple Moment** — On March 11, Christie's auctioned Beeple's "Everydays: The First 5000 Days" for **$69.3 million**, making it the third most expensive work by a living artist. The buyer was crypto investor Metakovan (Vignesh Sundaresan). This single sale generated more mainstream media coverage for NFTs than everything before it combined.

**March 2021: MoonCats and EtherRocks Rediscovered** — The community rediscovered [[mooncats|MoonCats]] in March and [[etherrock|EtherRock]] in August, sparking a broader trend of "NFT archaeology" — hunting for old, forgotten contracts on Ethereum.

**April 2021: Bored Ape Yacht Club (BAYC)** — [[bored-ape-yacht-club|BAYC]] launched on April 30 with a mint price of 0.08 ETH (~$220). The 10,000 unique ape PFPs came with commercial rights and membership in an exclusive community. BAYC floor prices eventually exceeded 100 ETH ($300,000+) and the collection spawned an entire ecosystem including Mutant Apes, Bored Ape Kennel Club, and eventually ApeCoin.

**Summer 2021: Art Blocks and Generative Art** — [[art-blocks|Art Blocks]], a platform for on-chain generative art, exploded. Projects like **Fidenza** by Tyler Hobbs (999 pieces, floor price peaked above 100 ETH), **Ringers** by Dmitri Cherniak, and **Chromie Squiggles** generated massive demand. Art Blocks demonstrated that algorithmically generated art could command fine-art prices.

**August 2021: EtherRock Mania** — [[etherrock|EtherRocks]] briefly traded above $1 million each, becoming a viral mainstream news story. The absurdity of million-dollar rock JPEGs captured the speculative frenzy perfectly.

**Celebrity and Brand Adoption** — Visa purchased a CryptoPunk for $150,000 (August 2021). Steph Curry bought a BAYC for $180,000. Snoop Dogg, Eminem, Paris Hilton, Jimmy Fallon, and dozens of other celebrities bought or endorsed NFTs. Adidas, Nike, and luxury brands launched NFT initiatives.

**Market Peak Numbers:**
- Monthly NFT trading volume exceeded **$5 billion** in August 2021
- OpenSea monthly volume hit **$3.4 billion** in August 2021
- OpenSea's valuation reached **$13.3 billion** by January 2022
- Total 2021 NFT market volume: approximately **$25 billion**
- Estimated 2.5 million active wallets traded NFTs

### 2022: Peak and Crash

2022 began with extraordinary highs and ended in devastation:

**January-February 2022: Final Peak** — [[cryptopunks|Punk #5822]] (alien) sold for $23.7 million (February 12), the most expensive CryptoPunk ever. [[bored-ape-yacht-club|BAYC]] floor prices exceeded 100 ETH. LooksRare launched as an OpenSea competitor with aggressive token incentives, generating artificial volume that briefly exceeded OpenSea's.

**March 2022: Yuga Labs Acquires CryptoPunks** — Yuga Labs (BAYC creators) acquired the CryptoPunks and Meebits intellectual property from Larva Labs for an undisclosed sum. They granted commercial IP rights to individual Punk and Meebit holders, aligning with the BAYC model. Yuga Labs also launched **ApeCoin ($APE)** and announced **Otherside**, a metaverse project, raising $320 million in a chaotic mint that cost participants $180 million+ in gas fees alone.

**April 2022: Azuki and the Zagabond Controversy** — [[azuki|Azuki]] launched as a 10,000-piece anime-inspired PFP collection, reaching a floor of 20+ ETH. However, founder Zagabond's blog post revealing he had previously abandoned multiple NFT projects (CryptoPhunks, Tendies, CryptoZunks) caused the floor to crash over 50% in hours — an early signal of eroding trust.

**May-June 2022: The Collapse Begins** — The Terra/LUNA crash in May 2022 and subsequent crypto contagion (Three Arrows Capital, Celsius, Voyager) devastated crypto markets broadly. NFT floor prices began a relentless decline:
- BAYC floor dropped from ~100 ETH to ~60 ETH by June
- Many collections lost 50-70% in weeks
- New mint activity slowed dramatically

**November 2022: FTX Collapse** — The [[ftx|FTX]] bankruptcy in November 2022 accelerated the NFT decline. NFT trading volume in November fell to approximately $400 million — down 92% from the August 2021 peak. By year-end, most PFP collections had lost 80-99% of their peak floor prices.

### 2023: Bitcoin Ordinals and the Reset

**January 2023: Bitcoin Ordinals Launch** — Casey Rodarmor launched the Ordinals protocol, enabling NFT-like inscriptions directly on the Bitcoin blockchain by embedding data in individual satoshis. This was controversial among Bitcoin maximalists but generated significant activity:
- Over 60 million inscriptions were created by December 2023
- BRC-20 tokens (fungible tokens via Ordinals) briefly spiked Bitcoin transaction fees above $30
- Bitcoin Ordinals volume briefly rivaled Ethereum NFT volume in May 2023

**February 2023: Blur Overtakes OpenSea** — **Blur**, a "pro trader" NFT marketplace launched in October 2022, overtook OpenSea in trading volume by February 2023. Blur's key innovations: zero marketplace fees, optional creator royalties, and the $BLUR token airdrop that rewarded active traders. This triggered the **creator royalty wars** — as Blur and competing marketplaces raced to reduce fees, NFT creators saw their secondary sale royalties (typically 5-10%) effectively eliminated. OpenSea responded by making creator royalties optional in August 2023, effectively ending the royalty model that had been a key selling point for artists.

**Market State:** Monthly Ethereum NFT volume settled between $300-600 million through most of 2023, a fraction of 2021 peaks but still significant. Blue-chip collections (CryptoPunks, BAYC, Art Blocks) retained relative value but were down 70-90% from peaks.

### 2024-2026: Current State

The NFT market has contracted significantly from 2021 peaks but has not disappeared:

- **Blue-chip resilience**: [[cryptopunks|CryptoPunks]] continue to trade as the benchmark "store of value" NFT collection. While far below 2022 peak floors, they remain the most recognized NFT brand globally.
- **Institutional digital art**: Christie's, Sotheby's, and Phillips continue to host NFT art auctions, albeit at lower volumes. Generative art (Art Blocks, fxhash) has found a niche audience among art collectors.
- **Bitcoin Ordinals maturation**: The Ordinals ecosystem has developed its own marketplaces (Magic Eden, Gamma), standards, and collector communities, adding a second major blockchain for NFTs alongside Ethereum.
- **Reduced speculation, more utility**: The market has shifted away from pure speculation toward NFTs with tangible utility — gaming assets, membership tokens, event tickets, and brand loyalty programs.
- **Monthly volume**: Ethereum NFT trading volume has stabilized in the $200-500 million range through 2025-2026, with periodic spikes around new collections or Bitcoin Ordinals activity.

## What Made NFTs Valuable

The NFT boom was driven by a convergence of factors:

**Scarcity**: Fixed supply collections (10,000 or fewer) created artificial scarcity. CryptoPunks (10,000), BAYC (10,000), and EtherRock (100) all benefited from supply constraints. Rarity within collections (alien Punks, golden apes) created additional price tiers.

**Provenance and History**: On-chain provenance — verifiable creation date, ownership history, and authenticity — gave early collections an advantage that cannot be replicated. A 2017 CryptoPunk carries historical significance that a 2022 derivative never can.

**Community and Identity**: PFP collections became tribal identifiers. Using a BAYC or CryptoPunk as a social media avatar signaled membership in an exclusive community. Celebrity adoption amplified this dynamic. Twitter's NFT profile picture verification feature (hexagonal borders) further embedded NFTs into social identity.

**Cultural Status**: At peak mania, owning certain NFTs became a status symbol comparable to luxury goods. The difference was that NFT prices were publicly verifiable on-chain, making them a uniquely transparent form of conspicuous consumption.

**Speculation**: The dominant driver for most participants. Stories of 100x-1000x returns on free mints or low-cost early purchases created a gold-rush mentality. The vast majority of NFT buyers were speculating on price appreciation, not collecting art.

## Lessons for Traders

**Early trend identification**: The NFT cycle offered classic signals of an emerging market — start with a small community of enthusiasts (2017-2020), breakthrough mainstream moment (Beeple, March 2021), parabolic adoption (April-August 2021), celebrity endorsement (late 2021), and eventual saturation and collapse (2022). Traders who recognized the pattern from previous cycles (ICOs in 2017, DeFi summer in 2020) could time entries and exits.

**Market cycle timing**: NFT floor prices followed crypto cycles with amplified [[volatility]]. The 80-99% drawdowns in most collections mirror patterns seen in altcoins during bear markets. The lesson: NFTs are among the highest-beta assets in an already volatile crypto market.

**Liquidity risk**: Unlike fungible tokens, NFTs cannot be sold instantly at the current "floor price." During crashes, liquidity evaporated — sellers could not find buyers at any price. This is the fundamental risk of NFT trading vs. token trading. [[wash-trading]] further distorted apparent liquidity on platforms using token incentives.

**Provenance premium**: Older collections consistently commanded premiums over newer ones, even when newer collections had objectively "better" art. Historical significance and provable age on-chain created a moat that new projects could not replicate.

**Exit timing**: The best NFT traders treated collections as momentum trades with defined exit points, not long-term holdings. Those who sold during 2021 highs captured enormous gains; those who held through the crash often lost 90%+ of paper wealth.

## Key Marketplaces

| Marketplace | Launched | Chains | Model / Fee | Status |

| Rarible | 2020 | Multi-chain | Pioneered $RARI "marketplace mining" | Niche |

**[[opensea|OpenSea]]** — The original dominant NFT marketplace. Founded in 2017, OpenSea became synonymous with NFT trading. At peak, it processed over $3 billion in monthly volume and was valued at $13.3 billion. Market share eroded significantly after Blur's launch. OpenSea charged a 2.5% marketplace fee and initially enforced creator royalties before making them optional in August 2023.

**[[blur|Blur]]** — Launched October 2022, overtook OpenSea by February 2023. Designed for professional traders with zero marketplace fees, a bid-based interface, and aggregated listings. The $BLUR token airdrop strategy rewarded active traders, concentrating volume. Blur's dominance effectively ended the creator royalty model on Ethereum.

**[[magic-eden|Magic Eden]]** — Originally the dominant Solana NFT marketplace, Magic Eden expanded to support Ethereum, Bitcoin Ordinals, and Polygon. Became the leading marketplace for Bitcoin Ordinals.

**[[looksrare|LooksRare]]** — Launched January 2022 with aggressive $LOOKS token incentives. Generated high headline volumes but much was [[wash-trading]] to farm token rewards. Volume declined sharply after initial airdrop enthusiasm faded.

## Key Metrics Traders Watch

NFT markets are illiquid and notoriously noisy — these are the gauges that matter for trading and valuation.

| Metric | What It Means | Caveat |
|---|---|---|
| **Floor price** | The cheapest listed item in a collection — the de-facto "quote" price | You usually **cannot** sell instantly at the floor; bids sit well below it in a downturn |
| **Best bid / bid depth** | The highest standing offer and how many bids exist below it | The true exit price; floor minus bid spread can be large |
| **24h / 7d volume** | Trading activity | Heavily distorted by [[wash-trading]] on incentive-driven venues ([[looksrare]], early [[blur]]) |
| **Unique holders / holder distribution** | Decentralisation of ownership; whale concentration | A few whales can crash the floor by dumping |
| **Listed ratio (% supply listed)** | Sell-side pressure | A rising listed ratio often precedes floor declines |
| **Sweep / sales count** | Number of distinct sales vs volume | Filters out a few high-value sales inflating volume |
| **Royalty enforcement** | Whether creator royalties are paid | Post-2023, mostly optional — affects creator economics, not floor |
| **Mint gas cost** | [[gas-fees\|Gas]] to mint during hot drops | Gas wars can cost more than the mint itself (Otherside: $180M+ in gas) |

**Liquidity is the defining risk.** Unlike fungible tokens, an NFT's floor price is aspirational — during the 2022 crash, sellers found **no bids at any price**. Always read the bid side, not just the floor.
