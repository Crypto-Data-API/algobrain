**Autoglyphs** are the first fully "on-chain" generative-art [[nft|NFTs]] on [[ethereum]], created by [[larva-labs|Larva Labs]] (the creators of [[cryptopunks|CryptoPunks]]) in April 2019. Unlike the vast majority of NFTs — which store artwork off-chain on IPFS or centralized servers — Autoglyphs are both *generated* and *stored* entirely inside the Ethereum smart contract itself; the art is produced deterministically by on-chain code rather than uploaded. Only **512 pieces** exist, and the generating contract was permanently switched off after the 512th mint, making the collection fully closed and immutable. They are widely regarded as the "grandfather of on-chain generative art" and a direct conceptual ancestor of [[art-blocks|Art Blocks]].

## How Autoglyphs Work (On-Chain Generative Art)

The defining technical feature of Autoglyphs is **on-chain generation and storage**:

- **Algorithm lives in the contract**: Rather than referencing an image file, the Autoglyphs contract contains the generative algorithm. Calling the contract's draw function returns the artwork as a deterministic ASCII-style character grid derived from each token's seed. The art is *computed*, not *retrieved*.
- **No external dependencies**: There is no IPFS hash, no centralized server, no metadata file that can rot or be rehosted. If every server in the world went offline, an Autoglyph could still be regenerated from the Ethereum state. This is the strongest form of "on-chain permanence" in NFTs and the main reason collectors prize the collection.
- **Deterministic symbol schemes**: Each piece belongs to one of several "symbol scheme" categories (different character sets used to render the pattern). Because output is deterministic from the seed, rarity is fixed at mint and fully verifiable on-chain.
- **Closed generator**: The contract minted exactly 512 glyphs and the generator was then permanently disabled — there is no possibility of a 513th. Supply is hard-capped and immutable.
- **Royalties / utility**: As a 2019 Larva Labs contract, there is no enforced on-chain creator royalty and no utility, governance, or roadmap. Value rests on historical significance and on-chain purity, not promised features.

This contrasts sharply with off-chain peers: [[etherrock|EtherRock]] and [[curio-cards|Curio Cards]] store ownership on-chain but the images off-chain, while [[cryptopunks|CryptoPunks]] originally referenced an off-chain composite (later moved on-chain). Autoglyphs were on-chain from day one.

## History

Autoglyphs launched on **April 10, 2019**, during a period when the NFT market was still in its infancy and years before the 2021 NFT boom. Larva Labs, already known for pioneering the PFP NFT concept with [[cryptopunks|CryptoPunks]] in 2017, designed Autoglyphs as an experiment in fully on-chain generative art. The project was far ahead of its time -- most collectors and traders did not yet understand the significance of on-chain storage or generative algorithms.

In **March 2022**, [[yuga-labs|Yuga Labs]] acquired both CryptoPunks and Autoglyphs from Larva Labs, consolidating ownership of three of the most historically significant NFT collections (alongside [[bored-ape-yacht-club|BAYC]]) under one company. The acquisition did not change the Autoglyphs smart contract, which remains immutable on Ethereum.

| **Mint price** | 0.2 ETH (~$35 at time of mint) |
| **Storage** | Fully on-chain -- art is generated and stored in the smart contract |
| **Generator** | Smart contract that creates ASCII-style visual patterns |
| **Status** | Generator permanently closed after 512 mints |

Each Autoglyph is an ASCII-like visual pattern generated by an algorithm written into the Ethereum smart contract. The patterns fall into several "symbol scheme" categories, and each piece is entirely deterministic based on its on-chain data. There are no external dependencies -- if every server in the world went offline, the art would still exist on the blockchain.

### Collection Economics

- **Mint**: 512 pieces minted at 0.2 ETH each in April 2019 (~$35 at the time). Proceeds were donated by Larva Labs to the charity 350.org, a detail often cited as part of the project's "art-first" ethos.
- **Supply permanence**: Hard cap of 512; generator disabled. No inflation, no expansions, no derivative editions from the original contract.
- **Royalties**: No on-chain creator royalty enforced by the 2019 contract; secondary fees are whatever the marketplace of execution charges.
- **IP**: After the March 2022 sale to [[yuga-labs|Yuga Labs]], the IP sits with Yuga, though (unlike its CryptoPunks/BAYC moves) Yuga has not built significant utility around Autoglyphs — they remain a pure art-historical asset.

## How & Where It Trades

- **Marketplaces**: As a standards-compliant ERC-721, Autoglyphs trade natively on [[opensea|OpenSea]] and [[blur|Blur]] without wrappers (unlike pre-721 peers EtherRock and Curio Cards). Larva Labs historically operated its own marketplace interface as well.
- **Liquidity profile**: Among the most illiquid blue-chips in NFTs. With 512 units held mostly by long-term art collectors, weeks or months can pass between sales. There is no meaningful "floor" to tap at size — a quoted floor often reflects one outlier listing.
- **Price discovery**: Driven by occasional high-value sales and the prevailing ETH-denominated risk appetite. Because so few pieces trade, a single transaction can re-anchor perceived value in either direction.

## Narrative, Category & Catalysts

Autoglyphs anchor the **on-chain generative art** category. Their narrative is "permanence + provenance": the purest on-chain art with an unrepeatable 2019 origin. Historical catalysts include (1) renewed appreciation for fully on-chain art during NFT-cycle upswings, (2) Art Blocks / generative-art waves that draw attention back to the progenitor, and (3) Yuga/Larva ecosystem news. In the mid-2026 bear tape, none of these catalysts are active, and demand for illiquid art-historical assets is correspondingly muted.

## Risks

- **Extreme illiquidity**: 512 units, mostly long-term-held. Exit at a quoted price is rarely possible; realized prices can diverge widely from "last sale."
- **Sentiment reflexivity**: Even pure art-historical assets are subject to the broad NFT/crypto cycle. Extreme-Fear regimes (e.g., 2026-06-24) thin out bids.
- **Thin-market price signal / wash-trading**: With so few trades, a small number of transactions (or self-dealing) can set a misleading floor. Treat headline figures skeptically.
- **Concentration & whale risk**: A handful of large holders can move the entire market by listing or selling.
- **IP / steward risk**: IP now sits with [[yuga-labs|Yuga Labs]]; strategic decisions (or neglect) by the steward can affect perceived value, though the on-chain art itself is immutable.
- **No royalty enforcement**: The 2019 contract enforces no creator royalty, so secondary economics depend entirely on the marketplace of execution.

## Cultural Significance

Autoglyphs are considered the **"grandfather of on-chain generative art"** and hold a unique place in NFT history for several reasons:

- **Fully on-chain**: At a time when virtually all NFTs pointed to off-chain images, Autoglyphs proved that art could live entirely on the blockchain, with no external dependencies
- **Generative process**: The art is created by code at mint time, not pre-designed -- each piece is a unique output of an algorithm, a concept that directly inspired [[art-blocks|Art Blocks]] and the broader generative art NFT movement
- **Historical significance**: As the first on-chain generative art collection, Autoglyphs are considered canonical in NFT art history, alongside [[cryptopunks|CryptoPunks]] as a foundational project
- **Scarcity**: Only 512 pieces with no possibility of expansion gives them a rarity profile that even CryptoPunks (10,000) cannot match
- **Provenance**: Created by Larva Labs before NFTs were mainstream, they carry the weight of being "early" in a way that later projects cannot replicate

## Why It Has Held Relatively Well

Blue-chip generative art (Autoglyphs, [[art-blocks|Fidenza]]) has historically held value better than PFP collections during downturns because its valuation rests on **historical significance and on-chain purity** rather than community hype or utility promises — there is no team making roadmap promises that can be broken, and the art simply exists on-chain. That said, "relatively resilient" still means subject to broad NFT-market gravity; in deep bear regimes even blue-chip art repriced lower and traded rarely.
