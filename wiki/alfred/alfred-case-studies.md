A collection of [[fred-mcnaught|Fred McNaught's]] real-world stock analysis examples extracted from 26 recorded sessions with [[sam-deering|Sam Deering]]. Each case study demonstrates Fred's decision-making process in action — the metrics he checked, the reasoning he applied, and the conclusions he reached. These serve as teaching cases for [[alfred|Alfred]].

## Case Studies

### EVN Capital Raise — Intuition Detecting Unpriced Risk

**Date:** December 2023
**Stock:** Evolution Mining (EVN:ASX)
**Decision:** Participate with minimum amount ($2,500), not the usual $7,500+

Fred evaluated a capital raise by Evolution Mining for a mine acquisition. The prospectus painted an optimistic picture, but Fred's assessment:
- The capital raise price ($3.80) only reflected the increase in share count, not the risk of assimilating a new mine
- Fred believed fair value was closer to $3.50 — nearly 8% lower
- The company was "pumping up the prospects far too much"
- After the raise closed, the company released negative information to the ASX that was not in the original prospectus

**Lesson for Alfred:** When a company's capital raise pricing only reflects dilution without risk premium, the deal may be unfavourable. Watch for information asymmetry — companies are incentivised to present optimistic scenarios before capital raises. Intuition here was pattern recognition: the price-to-risk gap.

(Source: [[fred-sam-session-2023-12-05]])

### PFP — Patient Accumulation of a Quality Company

**Date:** December 2023 — February 2024 (multiple sessions)
**Stock:** [[propel-funeral-partners|Propel Funeral Partners (PFP:ASX)]]
**Decision:** Buy and hold, accumulate on dips

Fred walked through his PFP buy thesis across multiple sessions:
- Evaluated PE ratio relative to funeral services sector average
- Assessed revenue, EPS, dividends, ROE, D/E ratio
- Identified PFP was borrowing to increase market share — a deliberate growth strategy
- Used PFP as the worked example for [[alfred-fundamental-analysis|Alfred's metrics framework]]

**Lesson for Alfred:** Start with a small position in a quality company. Accumulate over time. Use the company's data across multiple reporting periods to build confidence in the thesis. Data discrepancies between platforms are normal — always cross-reference.

(Source: [[fred-sam-session-2023-12-28]], [[fred-sam-session-2024-02-02]], [[fred-sam-session-2024-02-09]], [[fred-sam-session-2024-02-15]], [[fred-sam-session-2024-02-22]])

### NST — Fully Priced, Don't Buy

**Date:** April 2024
**Stock:** [[northern-star|Northern Star Resources (NST:ASX)]]
**Decision:** Do not buy — put on watch list

Fred's worked fundamental analysis:
- **PE 24.2** — red flag (too high)
- **ROE 7%** — red flag (too low)
- D/E 15.6 — acceptable for commodity stock
- EBITDA margin 27.6% (5yr avg) — excellent
- Dividend yield 1.8% (5yr avg) — dividend payer
- **Conclusion:** "Fully priced given current gold price. Purchasing would mean relying on gold price increase." — decided not to proceed

**Lesson for Alfred:** Even when macro thesis is compelling (government debt, geopolitical uncertainty → gold), the individual stock must be attractively priced. "Fully priced" means the upside depends on external price movements you can't control, not on the company's fundamentals improving.

(Source: [[fred-sam-session-2024-04-25]])

### Apple at $8 — Missed Opportunity Through Friction

**Date:** Referenced in April 2024 session
**Stock:** Apple (AAPL)
**Decision:** Did not buy — paperwork friction prevented action

Fred tells the story of when Apple was trading around $8 per share and he considered buying but was deterred by the complexity of purchasing US stocks from Australia at the time (pre-SelfWealth era).

**Lesson for Alfred:** Reduce friction in the investment process. Great opportunities are time-sensitive. Fred's subsequent move to SelfWealth for US stocks was partly motivated by this missed opportunity.

(Source: [[fred-sam-session-2024-04-11]])

### Brambles — Management Quality as Decisive Factor

**Date:** January 2024
**Stock:** Brambles (BXB:ASX) — wooden pallets/logistics
**Decision:** Evaluated management team

Fred discussed Brambles as an example of how management quality and SWOT analysis of the leadership team influences investment decisions. Also referenced the Telstra stock management change story as a counter-example of how management transitions can destroy value.

**Lesson for Alfred:** Management quality is not a secondary consideration — it is a primary investment filter. Assess the management team's track record, strategic direction, and incentive alignment before buying.

(Source: [[fred-sam-session-2024-01-05]])

### Poseidon Bubble 1969 — Historical Warning

**Date:** January 2024 (Fred recounting 1969-70 events)
**Stock:** Poseidon NL (Australian nickel mining)
**Decision:** Historical lesson, not a trade

Fred recounts the [[poseidon-bubble|Poseidon bubble]] — a nickel discovery in Western Australia that sent the stock from ~$0.80 to over $280, taking the entire speculative mining sector with it before collapsing.

**Lesson for Alfred:** Speculative manias are recurrent. The dominant narrative always sounds compelling. Prices overshoot because people cannot be prevented from making excessively optimistic assumptions. When Fred says "boom themes are always false" — Poseidon is the archetype.

(Source: [[fred-sam-session-2024-01-05]])

### Australian Banks — Utility, Not Growth

**Date:** June 2024 (sector analysis)
**Stock:** CBA, NAB, ANZ, WBC (Big Four Australian banks)
**Decision:** Long-term core hold since the 1980s

Fred has held bank stocks since the early 1980s. His thesis:
- Banks are at the heart of the economy
- Four major banks operate as an oligopoly — a structural moat
- Technology disruption is a risk but the big four have resources to adopt new technology (CBA led the cloud migration)
- Treat banks as a **utility** — collect franked dividends and reinvest in growth stocks elsewhere

**Lesson for Alfred:** Not every core holding needs to be a growth story. Dividend-paying utilities with structural moats provide portfolio stability and cash flow for reinvestment into higher-growth opportunities.

(Source: [[fred-sam-session-2024-06-05]], [[fred-sam-session-2024-01-12]])

### Coles Demerger — Demergers Always Worth a Look

**Date:** January 2024
**Stock:** Coles (COL:ASX) — demerged from Wesfarmers
**Decision:** Bought at demerger

Fred's principle: "Demergers are always worth a good look." When Wesfarmers divested Coles:
- Greater management focus on the divested division
- Capital available for the new entity to expand
- "Coles stocks have performed much, much better than anticipated"

**Lesson for Alfred:** Demergers create value through focused management and dedicated capital allocation. They are almost always worth investigating — Fred says he "generally takes up his entitlement."

(Source: [[fred-sam-session-2024-01-18]])
